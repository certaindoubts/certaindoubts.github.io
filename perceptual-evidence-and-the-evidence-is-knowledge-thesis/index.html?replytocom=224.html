Perceptual evidence and the “Evidence is Knowledge” Thesis

Posted on July 1, 2004 by Jon KvanvigJune 20, 2012

Among the many truly interesting and novel ideas in Williamson’s book is his claim that your evidence just is your knowledge. He argues for the view, but then considers what he takes to be the strongest counterargument–what to say about perceptual evidence.

Here I think he makes a mistake. He considers two cases, first, cases of veridical experience and second, cases of illusory experience. He takes the second case to be the harder case, and argues that in such cases, what you know is a claim about how you are appeared to. He responds to the claim that we don’t ordinarily consider claims about how we are appeared to by claiming that we know lots of things that we don’t explicitly consider (we know we are walking when we are even though we don’t consider the proposition that we are walking).

I don’t think this response is sufficient. It might be that we do know things that we don’t consider, but what we need is an argument that, in every case of illusory experience in which we don’t explicitly consider how we are appeared to, we still know how we are appeared to. Pointing out that sometimes we know things that we don’t consider isn’t sufficient to establish this point.

My real concern, however, is with the treatment of veridical experience. In such cases, Williamson argues that what we know is the truth of the content of the experience. This strikes me as a mistake, however. Our evidence is not the content of the experience, but that content under the modality in question, the modality of experience.

It’s easy to see this point when we think of experience having a qualitative character but no content. In such a case, it is the qualitative character of the experience that makes evident the belief in question in the case of veridical experience. It is not the quality itself, somehow surgically removed from the experience and transplanted into some other mental modality, that is one’s evidence.

If we think experiences have content, this point doesn’t change. It is the content of the experience under the modality of experience that makes evident, and known, that content (or some suitably related content). To remove the content from its original modality and transplant it into some other realm, gets things wrong.

It is for this reason that, when queried about how I know there is a tree in my backyard, I don’t say, “There’s a tree in my backyard.” I say, instead, “I can see it!” My response cites a content, if experience has content, under a certain modality, not the content abstracted from the experience and presumed now to be among my items of belief. It is the content-as-experienced that makes evident to me that there is a tree in my backyard.

So, the idea I’m hoping to get some reactions on is this. Whether experience is veridical or illusory, I don’t think Williamson’s responses are adequate in defense of the idea that your evidence just is the collection of things you know.

  5. uriah on July 3, 2004 at 2:20 am said:

    Because W’s is such a cool thesis (too cool to be true?), here is a desperate attempt to save it from Jon’s objection.

    We tend to work with an attitude/content and modality/content distinction in philosophy of mind. But suppose instead we worked with a model that builds the attitude or modality into the content. On this model, there aren’t belief boxes, desire boxes, vision boxes, etc. in minds. Rather, the mind is just full of representations, and these have contents that betray what we normally think of as the attitude or modality. Perhaps they have contents of the form “believably, p”, “desirably, p”, “visibly, p”, etc. Let’s call this the heterodox view of contents and attitudes/modalities.

    In the just sketched version of the heterodox view, I built the attitudes/modalities into the content by making them some kind of operators. But there might be other ways the heterodox view could play out – there could be other ways of building the attitude/modality into the content.

    This attempt to save the cool thesis may fall short in two ways: 1) the heterodox view may be deemed overly implausible, or 2) it may turn out that Jon’s problem can be adapted to the heterodox view.

  6. jon kvanvig on July 3, 2004 at 8:08 am said:

    Uriah, great to see you here–the Brown background requires these interests, you know!

    I like the heterodox view, but I see two issues. First, on W’s view, knowledge is a state of mind, so we’d have to have the heterodox view applied to K as well. But then the treatment of veridical perception has no chance of going as W wanted it to go. Recall that what he does is search for a content to experience that is one’s evidence, and then argue that this content is already the content of one’s knowledge. But if one’s knowledge reaches beyond the mind, this won’t be true.

    The second problem is how to get knowledge of the external world here. It looks like what would happen is that the veridical case would be treated like the illusory case: in both cases the object of knowledge would at best be the hetorodoxical combination of modality and content.

    One might hold, of course, that knowledge of the external world is indirect, and that right after knowing the experiential claim in question, we have an epistemic principle that allows us to strip off the experiential operator (we’d need similar principles for every mental modality by which we can come to know their contents stripped of the operator). Quite indirect, I would think.

    Perhaps your idea that experience is always also self-representational would help avoid the indirect path? I’m not sure about this, so I’ll wait to see if you think so.
