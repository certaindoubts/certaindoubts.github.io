X-Phi Data on Contrasts

Posted on February 3, 2009 by J. SchafferFebruary 3, 2009

Joshua Knobe and I have conducted some new experiments testing for contrast sensitivity in knowledge ascriptions. We wanted to share our results with you. We also wanted to invite potential criticisms of our methodology, and especially to encourage alternative non-contrastive explanations of our data. We are very much open to alternative explanations, and hope that you might help us see more alternatives than we could see by ourselves.

It may be worth noting that our experiments are the first that we know of to find any sort of contextual or stakes-based effects on knowledge ascriptions. Strikingly, previous experimental studies by (i) Ram Neta and Mark Phelan, (ii) Wesley Buckwalter, (iii) Joshua May, Walter Sinnott-Armstrong, Jay G. Hull, and Aaron Zimmerman and (iv) Adam Feltz and Chris Zarpentine have all failed to find any statistically significant effect of stakes on intuitions about knowledge, and the studies from Buckwalter and from May et al. both found no effect of explicitly mentioning an alternative. So—at least to the extent that one takes survey data as a decent guide to intuitions—the empirical results so far have seemed to undermine the case for either contextualism or subject-sensitive invariantism.

Joshua and I wanted to look to different sorts of cases in which different questions and contrasts are salient. We conducted three experiments, using three different methods of manipulating contrasts, and found statistically significant differences in every case.

The first two experiments used the following vignette:

Last night, Peter robbed the jewelry store. He smashed the window, forced open the locked safe, and stole the rubies inside. But Peter forgot to wear gloves. He also forgot about the security camera.
Today, Mary the detective has been called to the scene to investigate. So far she has the following evidence. She has been told that there was a theft, she has found and identified Peter’s fingerprints on the safe, and she has seen and recognized Peter on the security video, filmed in the act of forcing open the safe. She has no further information.

In the first experiment, we tried to manipulate contrasts using ‘rather than’ clauses, and subjects were therefore randomly assigned to receive one of the following two sentences:
(1a) Mary now knows that Peter rather than anyone else stole the rubies
(1b) Mary now knows that Peter stole the rubies rather than anything else
They were then asked whether they agreed or disagreed with the sentence they had received. We found a statistically significant effect such that subjects tended to agree with sentence (1a) but not with sentence (1b).

In the second experiment, we manipulated contrasts using knowledge-wh constructions, with subjects being randomly assigned to receive one of the following two sentences:
(2a) Mary now knows who stole the rubies
(2b) Mary now knows what Peter stole
Again we found a statistically significant difference, such that subjects tended to agree with the first sentence but to disagree with the second one.

For the third experiment we wanted to manipulate contrasts not by changing the words of the actual knowledge ascription but by changing the conversational context. Subjects in this third study were randomly assigned to receive one of the following two cases:

(3a)

Last night, someone stole the rubies from the jewellery store. But who did it? The thief smashed the window, forced open the locked safe, and stole the rubies inside. But the thief forgot to wear gloves. The thief also forgot about the security camera.
Today, Mary the detective has been called to the scene to investigate. So far she has the following evidence. She has been told that there was a theft, she has found and identified Peter’s fingerprints on the safe, and she has seen and recognized Peter on the security video, filmed in the act of forcing open the safe. She has no further information.

The news reporter is about to write an article on Mary. He is wondering if Mary has found out who did it. Please tell us whether you agree or disagree with the following:
Mary now knows that Peter stole the rubies

(3b)

Last night, Peter stole something from the jewellery store. But what did he steal? Peter smashed the window, forced open the locked safe, and stole something from inside. But Peter forgot to wear gloves. He also forgot about the security camera.
Today, Mary the detective has been called to the scene to investigate. So far she has the following evidence. She has been told that there was a theft, she has found and identified Peter’s fingerprints on the safe, and she has seen and recognized Peter on the security video, filmed in the act of forcing open the safe. She has no further information.

The news reporter is about to write an article on Mary. He is wondering if Mary has found out what Peter stole. Please tell us whether you agree or disagree with the following:
Mary now knows that Peter stole the rubies

Just as in each of the previous studies, we found a significant effect such that subjects tended to agree with the knowledge ascription in the first condition but not in the second.

Our current explanation for the differences between the (a) and (b) cases across surveys 1-3 is that (i) the different ‘rather than’ clauses, different wh-complements, and different preceding questions produce different contrasts, and (ii) knowledge ascriptions are contrast sensitive. More specifically, in all the (a) cases the contrasts involve alternative thieves, and Mary’s evidence (Peter fingerprint’s, Peter’s face on the security video) identify Peter as the thief. But in the (b) cases the contrasts involve alternative loot, and Mary’s evidence does not identify the rubies as the loot.

We would very much value any feedback as to whether this seems to be a reasonable explanation for the data, and whether any alternative explanations might be available.

    These are some very interesting experiments. Thanks for letting us know about them! For what it’s worth, here’s one alternative explanation that I think is fairly plausible. I’d like to see what people think.

    I think we can explain the change in agreement with the knowledge attribution by appealing to whether partcipants are giving the relevant claim in question what we might call a “de re” versus “de dicto” reading.

    To illustrate, consider the following Cat Case:

    Suppose my wife and I aren’t supposed to have cats in our complex. Despite this policy, we have a cat named “Hemingway” that we try to keep indoors. Suppose Hemingway gets outside and our landlord, Tom, sees him. Tom, however, doesn’t know who the cat belongs to at first. But, later on, he finds out it’s ours. Tom doesn’t know the name of our cat or think of him in any other way than “the stupid cat that’s running around” or (later on) as “Josh and Stephanie’s cat.” But suppose I say sadly to my wife “Tom now knows that Hemingway is ours.”

    I would think that my wife and anyone else who knows the name of our cat (and the details of the situation) would likely agree with that last statement, presumably because they’d be giving the knowledge attribution a de re reading (something like: “Tom now knows of Hemingway that he’s ours”).

    It seems that the Schaffer/Knobe (a)-cases are relevantly similar to the cat case, whereas the (b)-cases are not. On this proposal, people tend to read the (a)-cases as de re and the (b)-cases as de dicto. Yet we needn’t hold (as contrastivism does) that the contrastive features show up in the logical form of the knowledge attributions, affecting the change in agreement with the relevant attribution. This alternative explanation would yield the (presumably nice) result that people are agreeing to something true in the (a)-cases and disagreeing with something false in the (b)-cases as they should be.

    To try to clarify, here’s how I’m proposing that people may be reading these things (thanks to AZ for help in clarifying):

    EXPERIMENT 1:

    (1a) Mary now knows of the rubies that Peter (rather than anyone else) stole them.

    Or: The rubies are the x such that Mary now knows that Peter (rather than anyone else) stole x.

    This, combined with participants’ knowledge of:

    (ii) “the rubies = what was stolen last night”

    yields their agreeing with a true statement.

    (1b) Mary now knows that: Peter stole the rubies (rather than anything else).

    This de dicto claim is false, because Mary doesn’t have any evidence about the identity of the loot. So people don’t tend to agree with the claim.

    Likewise for the rest of the experiments…

    EXPERIMENT 2:

    (2a) Mary now knows of the rubies that Peter is who stole them.

    (2b) Mary now knows that: the rubies are what Peter stole.

    EXPERIMENT 3:

    (3a) Mary now knows of the rubies that Peter stole them.

    (3b) Mary now knows that: Peter stole the rubies.

    I may not be displaying the intended readings of these statements very well, but hopefully they’re not too far off.

    Q: But why would people be inclined to switch between de re and de dicto readings in this way?

    Well, first off, subjects in the third experiment aren’t even in a position to give the (b)-statement a de re reading because they aren’t at all privy to the identity of the loot. As for the other experiments: Perhaps the explanation here is that people tend to go with the reading that makes the claim true, when it can be interpreted in a natural way in which it comes out true. Perhaps, too, the contrasts contributed to motivating the various readings. So, in the (a)-cases (in which the contrast between Peter and other thieves is made more salient), people tend toward the de re reading that makes the claim true. However, in the (b)-cases (in which the contrast between the rubies and other jewels is made more salient), Mary’s lack of relevant evidence about the loot is likewise made more salient, and so people tend toward the de dicto reading on which the claim is false.

    Also, this proposal would explain why there’s a difference to be observed in these cases but not in the bank cases, because the bank cases don’t involve such issues (viz. of the reader being aware of some relevant that the protagonist is not).

  6. Michael Huemer on February 4, 2009 at 11:16 am said:

    I’ll just focus on 1a and 1b:

    The results could be explained by a standard contextualist view, rather than contrastivism. The “rather than …” clauses alter the context in different ways by making the audience think of different alternatives. When “rather than something else” is included in 1b, Mary now has to have evidence to rule out alternatives involving other things being stolen. But she does not need to rule out those same alternatives in the context of 1a. You could test this by giving subjects both questions at the same time, presumably causing both to be evaluated in the same conversational context.

    I also note that the story does not actually say that nothing other than rubies was stolen. So of course Mary couldn’t know that the rubies were stolen *rather than anything else*. To know that rubies were stolen rather than anything else, I take it, is at least to (1) know that rubies were stolen, and (2) know that nothing else was stolen. But on no view of knowledge would Mary know (2), *even if* she knew (1). You could fix this by stipulating, in the story, that Mary knows that exactly one thief was involved, and exactly one type of precious stone was stolen.

    When you add that, the results could also be explained by an anti-closure theory. If you reject the closure principle, then even if Mary knows that Peter stole the rubies, and even if she knows only one thing was stolen, she might still not know that Peter didn’t steal anything other than rubies. (Granted, this is a bizarre closure violation.)

    Lastly, the audience in 1a might have been assuming that Mary knew what was stolen. True, the story says “She has no further information,” but that can’t be taken literally–surely Mary knows things other than what was explicitly stated in the story. (Presumably, for instance, Mary knows what a fingerprint is, she knows some basic logic, she knows what a safe is, etc.) The audience might have interpreted “no further information” to mean “no further evidence as to who did it”. You could fix this potential problem by stipulating, in the story, that no one told Mary what was stolen. Then see whether they would still agree that “Mary knows that Peter, rather than anyone else, stole the rubies.”

  8. Aaron Zimmerman on February 4, 2009 at 1:56 pm said:

    I have an alternative explanation to Josh May’s. It’s also neither contrastivist (nor contextualist). Josh has subjects in the first case making the de re reading of “Mary knows that Peter stole the rubies” and truly reporting that the rubies are an x such that Mary knows that Peter stole the x. (Mary knows that Peter stole what was in the jewelry store’s safe, where the rubies=what was in the jewelry store’s safe.) Then something in the second description leads them to the de dicto reading on which the sentence is false because Mary does not know that rubies were stolen. Presumably, the shift from the de re to the dicto is induced by the explicit question “What was stolen?” and other similar devices absent in the first case. Subjects consequently report that Mary does not know that Peter stole the rubies as “Mary knows that Peter stole the rubies” is false on its (more natural) de dicto reading.

    In contrast, I give “Mary knows that Peter stole the rubies” the standard de dicto reading throughout–so that it is logically equivalent to “Mary knows that rubies were stolen and Peter stole them”–and then accuse subjects of unjustly assuming that Mary knows rubies were stolen in case 1–an assumption they make because the phrasing in the first scenario induces the relevant presupposition. “Mary knows that Peter stole the rubies” encourages subjects to (unwarrantedly) presuppose that Mary knows that rubies were stolen. (It’s not as sharp an effect as the presupposition that Mary beat her husband that marks “Has Mary stopped beating her husband?” but I think the presupposition effect is nevertheless there.) I predict that subjects wouldn’t make the relevant assumption in the first case (and so wouldn’t grant Mary knowledge that Peter stole the rubies) if the first case asked “Does Mary know both that rubies were stolen and that Peter is the thief?” (Note that Josh May’s explanation predicts this too because he thinks the change in phrasing would force subjects away from the de re reading.)

    Again, contrastivism is resisted on both Josh’s proposal and my own. (Contra Heumer, I take it that the contrastivist does *not* think “Mary knows p rather than q” is equivalent to “Mary knows p and not-q.”) That is, we can resist the contrastivist explanation of the cases either by saying with Josh that subjects move from the (true) de re to rejecting the (false) de dicto because of a change in the language of the cases, or if (as I suspect) subjects have the de dicto reading in mind throughout, the results can still be explained as the move from an unwarranted assumption or presupposition not really forced by the text, to the lack thereof when “What was stolen?” in case 2 alerts subjects that Mary cannot know that Peter stole the rubies unless she has evidence that rubies were stolen–evidence not attributed to her in the description of the case.

    My immediate reason for preferring my explanation to Josh’s is the suspicion that subjects will admit to making a mistake with regard to case 1 once it is drawn to their attention that Mary doesn’t have evidence with regard to what was stolen. If they had the de re reading in mind when initially encountering case 1, then (holding all else equal) they shouldn’t admit to having made an error even when it is drawn to their attention that the description gives Mary no evidence as to what was stolen. (Again, “The rubies are an x such that Mary knows that Peter stole x” is entailed by “Mary knows that Peter stole what was in the safe” and “The rubies are what was in the safe”; these sentences are all true in case 1 even though Mary has no evidence that rubies were stolen.)

    It’s a cool experiment, and we may try to test Josh’s hypothesis and my own by first seeing whether we get the same results as Schaffer/Knobe, and then seeing whether or not subjects describe themselves as making an error with regard to case 1 once it is drawn to their attention that the description gives Mary no evidence as to what was stolen. I think the main interest of the study isn’t a defense of contrastivism but a somewhat unexpected presupposition effect. I.e. it is interesting that so may subjects read “Does Mary know Peter stole the rubies?” and assume from it that Mary knows rubies were stolen.

  18. jennifer nagel on February 5, 2009 at 2:23 pm said:

    Hi Jonathan, Josh,

    Interesting work.

    I want to give you one more thing to worry about.

    Aaron remarks: ‘it is interesting that so may subjects read “Does Mary know Peter stole the rubies?” and assume from it that Mary knows rubies were stolen.’ If your subjects are picking up the presupposition of the question as if it were content delivered in the original scenario, they are exhibiting a pretty well-known pattern of behavior. The classic on this is Muscio’s 1916 article “The influence of the form of a question”, in the British Journal of Psychology. Having shown a series of short films to his subjects, he asked them questions (varied between subjects) such ranging from A. “Did you see a gun?” to B. “Did you see the gun?” to Z. “Was the gun a revolver?” And yeah, we get really unreliable when the questions look like Z (you’ll be interested in all 3 of the variables he was able to manipulate with different question forms — caution, suggestiveness and reliability).

    Loftus and Zanni did some work along similar lines in the mid-1970s, showing subjects a film of a car accident and asking them a bunch of questions about it, some of which concerned non-existent elements. Phrasing had some interesting effects here too. Some subjects got asked “Did you see a broken headlight?” (7% said yes); others were asked “Did you see the broken headlight?” (15% said yes — and some of their follow-up work got even larger reports for non-existent objects). Loftus thinks (and this is the mainstream view now) that our representation of the scenario we are judging is constructed out of a mixture of what we originally encoded and material being supplied at the point of judgment, including material presented in the cue or question.

    Sometimes the line between the story and the question about the story is not as sharp as an epistemologist might wish.

  19. Jonathan Schaffer on February 5, 2009 at 4:11 pm said:

    Jennifer (and also Kenny and Aaron): Great point! The possibility that the participants in the (a)-cases are presupposing that Mary has been told that the rubies were stolen is a troubling confound. Especially given that we have evidence of a propensity for just this sort of error. (Jennifer: I didn’t know about these studies — thanks for bringing these to my attention!)

    What is especially troubling about this confound, from my perspective, is that Joshua and I are in effect trying to test whether “knows” is semantically sensitive to the distinction between (i) the foreground information in focus for which alternatives are considered, and (ii) the background information that functions as a presupposition. So we went looking for empirical data showing a presupposition effect in knowledge ascriptions. But now it turns out that there is an antecedently established presupposition effect on performance. So how can we tell whether we are seeing a presupposition effect IN THE SEMANTICS??

    Offhand I can think of three ways to try to get around this problem:

Here subjects differ in their risk-seeking/risk aversion choices when presented with different presentations of the same choice.     A related but distinct set of phenomena is priming.

    Priming may be roughly characterized as an instance in which a stimuli at time t has an effect in the cognitive reception of a stimuli at time t+1 (this characterization is overly rough – it provides neither necessary nor sufficient conditions – but this is a blog-post : ).

    Repetition priming effects occurs when subjects have been exposed to, say a list of words, and are then later given the task of constructing words out of fragments. Subjects complete more fragments of the words included on the list than of words not included (Tulving, Schacter and Stark 1982). Other sorts of priming than repetition (emphasis etc.) have similar effects.     I do by no means suggest that these phenomena alone make for a generality objection to drawing a conclusion about the truth-conditions of ‘knows’ from your data. But I think that the putative analogies with these well-established and rather general sorts of psychological phenomena should be considered with some care.

    I think that’s the right basic picture, Joshua. Note that there’s a funny sort of methodological flip here, compared to what usually goes on in psychology. Usually in a bit of psychological research, one is interested in the general fact about human psychology, and the last thing you want to be the case is that you’ve just uncovered some oddity about a bit of English verbiage. Here it’s just the opposite: the epistemologist wants to make sure that what has been found is exactly about an oddity of a (philosophically important) bit of English verbiage.

    There seems to me a general methodological point here, in that philosophers are very used to their default interpretation of an outcome of a thought-experiment being indicative of some underlying semantics, with the one big exception of a well-delineated set of pragmatic effects. And I suspect that that default generally fails, or at a minimum, some serious work needs to be done to determine when it does hold up and when it doesn’t. So in general, I think that this kind of check-for-the-hypothesized-effect-in-other-places research needs to be adopted as a necessary component of the overall research methodology.

    In this particular instance, it seems to me that two major factors point away from interpreting the contrast effect as revealing part of the logical structure of the “knows” relation . Second, that contrastive devices are found all over the darn place in our discourse, and not at all just in epistemic contexts, should significantly tilt the scale away from a specifically-epistemological reading of these phenomena, on the initial weighing.

But if we do get sloppy on “knows” in difficult contexts we may want to be cautious about reading the meaning of “knows” off the case responses of laymen.

    Yes, I think that in our paper (‘Philosophy’s New Challenge: Experiments and Intentional Action’) we (me, Nick Smith, Shyam Nair, Peter Marchetto and Cecilea Mun) gave a recipe for testing whether an asymmetry between answers (such as 1a and 1b) is due to ‘thoughtlessness’ as you put it.

    First, you can use the Shane Frederick’s CRT test to measure subject’s intelligence and see if ‘smarter’ people exhibit less asymmetry.(CRT is a three question test that correlates well with other measures of intelligence and it only takes 5 minutes to complete). We showed a reduction of the Knobe Effect for higher scoring people. Similarly, Frederick showed that people who do well in this test exhibit less “reflection” effects (from Prospect Theory), for example. Alternatively, you can ask people what they got on their SAT scores.

    Second, we gave some evidence that if you take the CRT and you get questions right you are temporarily more thoughtful in your answers, so if you immediately give them a question they will think harder about it. (Knobe Effect is completely gone for the highest scoring group in this condition). Basically, getting questions right on the CRT is disfluent (because the CRT is designed in such a way that the “intuitive” answer is incorrect–so you think twice before giving your first gut instinct answer once you catch on). A similar effect may be reached by using “bad font” as Jennifer Nagel suggested.

    Finally, if you get the percentage of people who say TRUE for 1a and the percentage of people who say FALSE for 1b, then with the help of some elementary probability theory you can deduce the minimum percentage of people N who have the following two dispositions: The disposition to say TRUE for 1a when given by itself and the disposition to say FALSE for 1b when given by itself.
    If you then give another group of people both questions at once, then if the percentage of people who give TRUE to 1a AND FALSE to 1b is less than N, then it looks like giving people BOTH questions is helping some people “change their minds” by catching their error. We found a reduction for the Knobe asymmetry with this probe also.
