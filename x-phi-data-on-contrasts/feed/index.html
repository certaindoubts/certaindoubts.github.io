Yes, I think that in our paper (‘Philosophy’s New Challenge: Experiments and Intentional Action’) we (me, Nick Smith, Shyam Nair, Peter Marchetto and Cecilea Mun) gave a recipe for testing whether an asymmetry between answers (such as 1a and 1b) is due to ‘thoughtlessness’ as you put it.

First, you can use the Shane Frederick’s CRT test to measure subject’s intelligence and see if ‘smarter’ people exhibit less asymmetry.(CRT is a three question test that correlates well with other measures of intelligence and it only takes 5 minutes to complete). We showed a reduction of the Knobe Effect for higher scoring people. Similarly, Frederick showed that people who do well in this test exhibit less “reflection” effects (from Prospect Theory), for example. Alternatively, you can ask people what they got on their SAT scores.

Second, we gave some evidence that if you take the CRT and you get questions right you are temporarily more thoughtful in your answers, so if you immediately give them a question they will think harder about it. (Knobe Effect is completely gone for the highest scoring group in this condition). Basically, getting questions right on the CRT is disfluent (because the CRT is designed in such a way that the “intuitive” answer is incorrect–so you think twice before giving your first gut instinct answer once you catch on). A similar effect may be reached by using “bad font” as Jennifer Nagel suggested.

Finally, if you get the percentage of people who say TRUE for 1a and the percentage of people who say FALSE for 1b, then with the help of some elementary probability theory you can deduce the minimum percentage of people N who have the following two dispositions: The disposition to say TRUE for 1a when given by itself and the disposition to say FALSE for 1b when given by itself.
If you then give another group of people both questions at once, then if the percentage of people who give TRUE to 1a AND FALSE to 1b is less than N, then it looks like giving people BOTH questions is helping some people “change their minds” by catching their error. We found a reduction for the Knobe asymmetry with this probe also.

I should say I also find Josh May’s proposal an attractive one; the performance error worry was a supplement to it, and not a rival. You suggest that a performance error theory would have trouble explaining why the definite article would shift performance. Hmm. Who knows how well these things are explained, but there is experimental work on this. The Muscio article I mentioned before finds a sharp drop in both caution and reliability when we switch to the definite article (i.e. from “Did you see a dog?” to “Did you see the dog?”). Presumably this is driven by our ordinary tendency to trust our interlocutors; it would be interesting to test the effects of explicitly priming and canceling conversational norms here, or by alerting subjects to the possibility that the questioner may be asking leading questions or attempting to mislead.

Meanwhile, I’m still wondering about the degree of semantic processing we are devoting to ‘knows that’ when focus is elsewhere in the sentence. Here’s a problem case from a very nice talk Adam Sennet gave here last year: the scenario is the standard Havit/Nogot Ford case. Here’s the question: “Does Jones know that it is a Ford, rather than a Jaguar, that is owned by someone in his office?” Sennet finds — and anecdotally, this works for my undergrads too — it’s much easier to get a “yes” on that question than on the standard question “Does Jones *know* that someone in his office owns a Ford?” Together with the focus on the “someone in his office” about whom Jones has the false belief, the stress on “know” should increase the level of semantic processing of that term, making it easier to get the right answer. Or that’s what I think is going on there — I don’t know if Sennet would be inclined to buy the shallow-processing explanation of his results. But if we do get sloppy on “knows” in difficult contexts we may want to be cautious about reading the meaning of “knows” off the case responses of laymen.
