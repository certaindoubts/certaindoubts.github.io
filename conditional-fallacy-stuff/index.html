Conditional Fallacy Stuff

Posted on February 12, 2005 by Kvanvig JonFebruary 12, 2005

A word of caution: it will be very hard to find a conception of epistemology on which this post counts as that. It’s on the conditional fallacy, but the context of discussion is the nature of dispositions. So I’m going to put the content of the post below the fold. My defense at violating the epistemological purity of the site is that so many instances of the fallacy crop up in epistemology that clarity about the fallacy (if there be such a fallacy) is important in epistemology.

I posted earlier linking to the paper by Bonevac, Dever, and Sosa on the fallacy, and then emailed David about it, and he’s amenable to discussing the paper here on the blog so others can join in as well if they choose, or just enjoy reading. So here goes.

Suppose we have the following proposal:

COND: d iff c counterfactually implies m

For example, let d be the claim that a given wire is live, and let the counterfactual analysis be that the wire would pass electricity to a conductor if that conductor were to come into contact with the wire.

I have two questions about central elements in the BDS account. The first is their reaction to counterexamples to such an account, especially to their charge that certain ways of understanding counterexamples amount to begging the question against COND. So consider such a counterexample, one suitably adjusted to the life experiences of the person posting: There are two kinds of electric fence designed to keep cattle in a pen. In one kind the electric meter is constantly whirring because the wire is live all the time. In the other case, the electric meter stands still, except when a cow touches the wire, at which time the meter begins to run. The reason it begins to run is because it and the wire are connected to a monitoring device that passes electricity through the wire upon being contacted by a conductor (in this case, the poor cow). In both cases, the counterfactual in question is (always) true, but in only one of the two cases is the wire (always) live.

About such a case, Bonevac, Dever, and Sosa (hereafter BDS) say the following (I’ve changed the quote slightly to fit the above example):

But this example alone points to no fallacy on the part of the conditional analyst, and to no immediate problem for the conditional analysis. What the example attempts to offer is simply the possibility of a divergence in truth value of the two sides of the biconditional in COND. Of course the falsity of COND would follow immediately from this; but the conditional analyst will simply take this implication as reason to reject the above characterization of the situation. If the wire is indeed such that if it were touched by a conductor then electrical current would flow, then it is a live wire. One does not expose a fallacy in the conditional analysis merely by bruiting the possibility of a thing being such that not d, but also such that c counterfactually implies m.
But another, more persuasive, argument can be extracted from this example, especially from his description of that example in the first paragraph quoted above.We can drop the question-begging insistence that the wire be both dead and such that if it were touched by a conductor, electricity would flow, and instead say only that the wire is both dead and such that if it were touched by a conductor, it would be live. This description of the electrofink requires no direct rejection of the conditional analysis, but instead only the rather modest claim that dispositional properties can come and go under various circumstances – including, crucially, the circumstance of the presence of the conditions of manifestation of the disposition. So far, there seems to be nothing the conditional analyst ought to, or plausibly could, object to. But if it were then to follow, in conjunction with COND, that the wire was in fact live, a problem for the conditional analysis would have emerged.

BDS wish to contrast a question-begging objection with a non-question-begging one. The question-begging one seems to be this: the bare assertion that d could be false while the counterfactual in question is true. That’s certainly an objectionable kind of response to COND, whatever name we wish to call it. The contrasting case, however, is described as one in which an argument of a certain kind is provided. The argument goes like this: 1. the wire is dead, 2. the wire, if touched by a conductor, would be live, 3. COND, so 4. the wire is in fact live.

I wouldn’t have thought that this is the best way to interpret the example. If asked to enumerate premises, I would have said (about the dead wire case): 1. the wire is dead (as shown by the fact that the meter is standing still), 2. the wire, if touched by a cow (conductor), would result in electricity passing through the wire, 3. by COND, 2 implies the negation of 1, so 4. COND is false.

What’s the difference, one might ask. Let’s use COND together with the schematic letters it contains. As BDS represent the argument, it goes:
1. ~d
2. c counterfactually implies d
3. d iff c counterfactually implies m
4. therefore, d

I would have thought that the argument is as follows (regarding the case where the meter is presently standing still):
1. ~d
2. c counterfactually implies m
3. if d iff c counterfactually implies m, then if 2 is true, 1 is false.
4. so, it is false that d iff c counterfactually implies m.

This difference is important because their analysis of this case sets the stage for their inquiry into the logic of the conditional fallacy as follows:

We thus offer the following general characterization of the conditional fallacy. Suppose the following two conditions are met:
1. There is some set S of claims such that S in conjunction with COND entails some conclusion P which makes some assertion about the possession of a dispositional property (so, paradigmatically, P is either d or ¬d). This is the inferential condition.
2. S together with ¬P form an independently plausible scenario. This is the possibility condition.
If both the inferential and the possibility conditions are met, we have reason to reject COND – the co-possibility of S and ¬P is incompatible with the argument S, COND |- P. The fallacy committed by the conditional analyst is that of neglecting the (perhaps obscure, until brought to explicit attention) co-possibility of S and ¬P – thus the “overlooking” and “failing to notice” terminology used by Shope.

It’s the inferential condition that I’m having trouble with. Their characterization of the motivating argument described above does indeed have COND as a premise, but that argument can’t be right. My version of the argument doesn’t have COND as a premise, but is valid and true to the example. Moreover, my example doesn’t have either d or ~d as a conclusion, but I don’t think it should have such a conclusion, since the argument is supposed to undermine COND. So I don’t think the inferential condition can be quite right.

So here’s my diagnosis: BDS are just too nice. They imagine a counterfactual theorist resistant to a particular Shope counterexample. The resistance takes the form of an assertion: “You’re example just begs the question!” Rather than replying, “Well, then, begging the question, in this sense, is rather ubiquitous among sound and compelling refutations,” they kindly decide to try an indirect reply. They are willing to retreat to safer ground, noting that dispositions can come and go, so even a recalcitrant counterfactual theorist must admit that a wire could be dead and yet become live when touched by a conductor. And then they think that, at least in some cases, one can soundly infer that the wire is in fact live, given the counterfactual account.

What I don’t see, however, is why this rhetorical strategy should be taken to uncover the nature of the fallacy in question. In general, what one does in response to resistance to a claimed fallacy shouldn’t be taken to define the fallacy.

This first issue concerns how to construe the inferential condition. My second question concerns the possibility condition itself. The possibility condition states that the set of premises used to undermine COND must be co-possible with whatever conclusion is derived that presents a problem for COND. That claim is surely right. They also give a Canonical Test that allows one to derive the conclusion that the possibility condition fails. Here’s how the test goes. Where P is the conclusion that is supposed to undermine COND, and S the set of premises which together with COND implies P, we need some argument J in addition which aims to show that S&P is possible. Then the test runs as follows:

Let J’, S’, and P’ be the result of replacing all mention of the dispositional property with mention of the purported analyzing condition in COND. Then, if J’ suffices to make plausible the conjunction S’&P’, then the conditional fallacy argument fails.

BDS argue for this requirement by examples, but I think I’m not convinced. Suppose we generalize this canonical test to other kinds of analyses. Suppose, for example, that you have an argument against the utilitarian analysis of rightness. Can’t the utilitarian reply that if you replace all talk of rightness in the argument with talk of maximizing overall utility, the resulting claims will all be perfectly acceptable? In fact, this is just what bright undergraduates do when confronted with counterexamples to utilitarianism. They say, “if we assume utilitarianism, then we can replace all talk of rightness here with talk of maximizing utility, and then everything said is obviously true.” The conclusion to draw is that this approach doesn’t provide a good test for determining whether the counterexample is successful. So my question is: how is the Canonical Test above different?

    But we are out for bigger game than the refutation of a particular conditional analysis of a disposition. Instead, we are interested in the claim, made by multiple people (we cite some examples in the paper), that there is a generic way of showing that conditional analyses of dispositional properties *always fail*. An important initial move is that, whatever this generic way is, it can’t be the way you attack the analysis of “live”. What you do is describe a situation in which the analysans applies but the analysandum does not. That of course defeats the particular case (supposing that the description is acceptable), but it can’t generalize as a compelling strategy. We can’t remove conditional analyses from the philosophical table by saying, “Look, there’s no point in attempting a conditional analysis of a dispositional property, because if you do I’ll just describe a situation in which the analysans applies but the analysandum does not.”

    Importantly, we try to emphasize throughout that we are not concerned with defending any particular conditional analysis of any particular property (or even that a given property could be given a conditional analysis). Rather we are concerned to respond to the apparently-pervasive thought that conditional analyses are *generally* doomed. The idea is that there is a generic *recipe* for producing counterexamples to *any* attempted conditional analysisâ��-that giving a conditional analysis is in general a *fallacy*. We find this an interesting and philosophically fertile, but ultimately mistaken, view.

    Again, any particular application of the recipe will, for all we say, succeed. But (unless we are proceeding by way of a sort of meta-induction) the question remains about whether we should not expect a refined conditional analysis to have better prospects. Giving an example that may (or may not) show that *live* is not to be analyzed as *would conduct electricity*, or that fragile is not to be analyzed as *would break if struck*, does not really engage our point.

    Suppose someone attacks utilitarianism by describing a situation in which total utility is maximized but in which the acts are not right. That’s fine as a way of attacking utilitarianism, but if the attacker goes on to say, “So no form of consequentialism in ethics can succeed,” we need to ask how the strategy generalizes. If the answer is just, “Whenever I’m given a specific form of consequentialism, I’ll describe a situation in which the relevant method of weighing consequences is satisfied, but the act is not right,” then we should be unmoved thus far. We need more than a commitment to produce a counterexample; we need as well a reason to think that a counterexample is in fact forthcoming.

Our attempts to make precise the structure of the conditional fallacy argument and our examination of the details of the mimicking and masking arguments are all directed at this end.

    The validity of the Canonical Test is intended to rest on straightforward logical and methodological grounds.
