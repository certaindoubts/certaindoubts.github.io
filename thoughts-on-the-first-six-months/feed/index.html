So suppose that view is true. What is a valid argument? One that is truth-preserving? What is a sound argument? One that is truth-preserving with true premises? My understanding of your view is that you must replace these accounts with something referring to sophisticated forms of persuasion. But above, you don’t.

On the surface, it looks as if you should say: there can be a sound argument against epistemic noncognitivism, but there couldn’t be one in favor of it.

On your last point. I’m sure there are senses of “the rational thing to do” that don’t sustain my claim. My claim was about being in a context abstracting from practical considerations and focusing on purely intellectual ones. In that context, if you tell me you’re about to engage in a sophisticated form of persuasion only, then the rational thing to do is to walk away. You have nothing to say that would interest me.

That’s probably too strong, however, so I recant. Any situation, even noises from a non-linguistic entity can be used as an occasion for pursuing reflective equilibrium. For example, if a lizard starts emitting noises that sound like sentences in English, and have apparent logical connectives in them, I can interpret the sounds as sentences in English and use the occasion to notice incoherences among my commitments. With the noncognitivist, I can do more. I can treat him or her as having a bad theory, and so interpret him or her as actually giving arguments which may or may not be sound. What I couldn’t do is come to the conclusion that the noncognitivist is correct, at least not without forgetting some of what the position implies.

All of this has a huge proviso attached, however. I think a noncognitivist in ethics shouldn’t accept alethic characterizations of the view, according to which there are no true evaluative claims, etc. At least, they shouldn’t do so without being forced by good arguments to do so. They should try to adopt some minimalist notion of truth which allows them to grant that evaluative claims are alethically assessable.

]]> By: Joe http://certaindoubts.com/thoughts-on-the-first-six-months/#comment-1122 Thu, 16 Dec 2004 22:17:30 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=205#comment-1122 Jon, you have put the issue in sharper focus here, but my worries remain. The
first thing that strikes me is that the three problems you pose are not
ones that arise at once, but are in fact different responses, or perhaps
descriptions, to three different situations. Let me explain.

1. I do not mean to imply it would necessarily be irrational for someone
to believe that noncognitivism were true if there were a sound argument
against it. Clearly that does not follow. My point rather was what do we say
when I am presented with a sound argument against it? Consider the two
possible responses we might expect that I could have in such a situation.

A. I grasp the argument and appreciate its soundness. Now if continue to
maintain that noncognitivism is true will you still call me rational? I
would be surprised if you did. Consider an analogous case.

If the Red Sox won the world series this year then the Yankees didn’t.
The Red Sox won the world series this year.
So, the Yankees didn’t win the world series this year.

This, we will agree, is a valid argument and–thank God,I can’t believe it
happened in my lifetime–sound. Now, would you call someone rational if
they accepted its soundness yet maintained the Yankees won the world series?
I doubt we would. For a person to be plausibly construed as rational while
believing the Yankees won this year’s series, we would demand that they
not believe that the Red Sox won. In which case, they do not accept the
soundness of the argument. And this we can live with, since we also think
it not very difficult, at least in most cases, to get them to believe the
blessed truth of the matter.

Now when we move back to philosophical matters, things are a bit more
difficult. Now clearly it is plausible that I can be considered rational
while believing that noncognitivism is true while also having the mistaken
belief that a proposed argument against that theory is unsound. But here
is where the trouble starts. What happens when you start facing resistance
in your attempts at getting me to revise my beliefs in the face of the
‘evidence,’ a resistance that would not be very suprisising in the
philosophical case though quite in the baseball case? Am I being
reasonable in that resistance, or stubbornly unreasonable? Or am I simply
incapable, for some reason, of really grasping the truth in this matter? Will
you still call me reasonable/rational then?

Now we might complain here that the two cases are importantly different in
some way. Philosophical positions, after all, are not very easy to prove,
unlike past winners of the world series. I admit that, but why is it? Is
the truth of philosophical theories simply hard to track down? Or is it
deeper than this? One way of allaying the suspicion that there is
something deeper would be to show instances of sound philosophical arguments.
But is there one single, untendentious example of one? I confess to not being
able to think of one at the moment. In the absence of examples, one might
think that it is really hard to generate one. And the defender of
noncognitivism (or what have you) may wonder if there really is a sound
argument against them. Now if both sides are unsure of the existence of a
sound argument against either view, we reach a new circumstance, namely

2. where we are quite comfortable saying, as you put it, that ‘rational
people can disagree and yet both hold rational opinions.’ Indeed, its not
clear what else there would be to say in such a situation, particularly
since the differing parties hold different beliefs and we generally take
ascriptions of rationality to be relative to the content of those beliefs.
But if we were to be so bothered by the dearth of sound philosophical
arguments that we came to believe that none were possible, given the
nature of philosophical discourse, we might say something stronger, namely

3.’rational disagreement can be irresolvable,’ in which case we have
little problem (again, modulo the believer’s beliefs) saying that both
noncognitivists and realists are rational in believing there respective
accounts. But if, by whatever route, we were to arrive at this stage,
would there be much to reccomend realism? Is it saying too much that a
realist view implies the possiblity of a sound argument in its favor? This
was the problem with which I begun.

Finally, a comment on Nobis (by the way, is the paper at issue the one
about Stevenson and Ayer and their epistemological emotivism? Or is there
another that I didn’t see?). It strikes me as rather obvious that to say that
it would be rational to walk away from the forthright noncognitivist is to
employ a rather loaded sense of ‘rational,’ and one that I am not at all
sure I understand. If rationality is instrumental, then the rationality of
walking away is purely contingent on the agent’s ends, and clearly for
some it will be rational to stay and listen. If rationality is something more
than instrumental, one would like to know just what it is. Presumably if
such an account could be produced and clearly presented, it would be
trivially true that no rational person would hold noncognitivism to be
true.

First, suppose there are sound arguments against non-cognitivism. That doesn’t imply that anyone is irrational in believing non-cognitivism to be true. Second, rational people can disagree and yet both hold rational opinions. Of course, at least one has a false belief, but nothing follows about rationality from that. Third, rational disagreement can be irresolvable as well.

The basic problem Nobis pinpoints, however, is that if you are trying to convince me of non-cognitivism and also tell me that you’ll do it using a sophisticated form of persuasion, the rational thing for me to do is walk away. Nothing you have to say will have any significance for my intellectual viewpoints, conceived of from a purely intellectual point of view.
