A Technical Issue with ‘S is rational to prefer as if p’ in Fantl/McGrath

Posted on February 29, 2012 by Kvanvig JonFebruary 29, 2012

The end of chapter 5 in F/M is devoted to responding to Weatherson’s challenge to F/M’s pragmatism, a challenge that attempts to explain the pragmatism in terms of a pragmatism about belief rather than one about justification. I like the arguments here very much, though I think the expression that appears in the title is characterized in a way that won’t quite work.

Here is what F/M say: S is rational to prefer as if p iff for all states of affairs A and B, it is rational to prefer A to B iff it is rational to prefer A&p to B&p. (If you want to see this using the cool LaTeX coding that one of our plugins allows us to use in posts, check out the formula at the end of this post.)

To simplify, let’s recognize that states of affairs and propositions are correlated: for each distinct one of one type, there is a matching one of the other type. So I’ll just treat A, B, and p as of the same type in what follows.

The problem that concerns me arises from noticing that rational preferences are defeasible.

You can rationally prefer A to B, but have this preference order switched when adding something else to both. Suppose that something else is p. Then you rationally prefer A to B, but you don’t rationally prefer A&p to B&p. In such a case, the account above implies that you don’t rationally prefer as if p (and hence aren’t justified in believing p, on F/M’s position).

But that’s too strong a result I think. I rationally prefer as if today is Wednesday (b/c I get to work on epistemology stuff on Wednesdays). I believe it and I’m justified in believing it as well. And F/M, in their (2002), allow the inference from being justified in believing to being rational to prefer as if. So the account should allow that I rationally prefer as if today is Wednesday, but I think it doesn’t. I prefer showing up to class on time to not showing up to class on time, but that preference is erased with I conjoin each side to the claim that today is Wednesday: I never teach on Wednesday and so have no preference for showing up to class on time on Wednesday over not showing up to class on time on Wednesday. Or again: I prefer it being Thursday to it being Friday (because my fun seminar is on Thursday), but I don’t prefer it being both Wednesday and Thursday to it being both Wednesday and Friday.

Once one starts looking for such defeat possibilities, they are easy to find. Suppose I’m justified in believing that Obama will win the next election. That is compatible with rationally preferring that Ron Paul be the Republican nominee to Mitt Romney being the nominee (because of the chances of each beating Obama). But when I conjoin the Paul nomination with Obama winning and compare it to the Romnee nomination with Obama winning, I no longer have a rational preference for the former over the latter. The winner is all that matters to me, and I’m rationally neutral on this comparison. Hence, it appears that this account fails to have me preferring as if p on the account above, even though I am (assumed to be) justified in believing that Obama will win.

We can change this case so that I don’t believe Obama will win, and still get the worrisome result. Suppose I don’t believe this, but have plenty of justification for believing it. All the preferences will remain the same, and it looks like it isn’t rational to prefer as if Obama will win.

In short, defeasibility is ubiquitous. I’m tempted to think the following. For any proposition, there is some rational preference ordering that a fully rational, idealized agent (one with a complete set of preferences) that would be defeated by that proposition. But even if that universal quantification is too strong, there are too many defeating situations to use the universal quantification in the formula in characterizing the phrase in the title of this post.

The F/M principle formalized:

$$!RP^*_{Sp} \Leftrightarrow \forall A \forall B\boldsymbol{(}R_S(P_SA > P_SB) \Leftrightarrow R_S(P_SA\&p > P_SB\&p)\boldsymbol{)}$$

  2. Matt McGrath on February 29, 2012 at 10:41 pm said:

    Hi Jon,
    We imposed restrictions on the principle in our 2002, one of which is similar to the one you’re suggesting in your comment (it’s footnote 8 of that paper). We restricted the principle to A/B/p combinations such that the subject has no reason to think that whether A as opposed to B obtains will causally affect whether p is true. However, this restriction might not be strong enough to rule out all the problematic cases, and something more of the order of your proposal might be needed. It was partly because of worries like these about how exactly to restrict the principle that we turned to proposals connecting knowledge and reasons in the book. I still like the core idea: if you know that p, then the chance of not-p is idle in an important sense. We’d now spell this out in terms of its not standing in the way of p’s being a reason you have for belief and action, rather than in terms of the chance that not-p making no difference to one’s rational preference orderings.
