Nondeviant reasoning

Posted on February 25, 2009 by John TurriFebruary 25, 2009

I’ve been studying Ralph Wedgwood’s excellent paper “The Normative Force of Reasoning” (Nous 40:4 (2006), 660 – 686). Ralph presents and defends a causal theory of reasoning, and one main task is to explain causal nondeviance. I’m very sympathetic to his basic proposal. But I have doubts about one necessary condition he proposes, namely: “the causal chain in question must consist purely of manifestations of the reasoner’s dispositions” (p. 665, bold emphasis added). This post explains those doubts.

Dispositions (abilities, powers, etc.) manifest themselves in events, states and processes — what we can generally call “outcomes.”

I dropped my favorite coffee cup yesterday. It crashed to the floor and broke. The outcome here was the cup’s breaking. This outcome manifested both the cup’s fragility and the floor’s rigidity.

During a riveting soccer match, a midfielder navigates through traffic and launches a masterful pass across to the striker, who effortlessly taps it in past the prostrate goalie. The outcome here — the goal — manifests not only the striker’s abilities, but also the midfielder’s. (That’s why the midfielder gets an assist.)

Good coaching manifests itself in many ways. Here’s one way: when a player performs as instructed and thereby succeeds, the performance and outcome manifest not only the player’s abilities, but also the coach’s coaching skill. (That’s part of the reason why coaches get congratulated on their players’ good performances, and why coaches take pride in their players’ achievements.)

Now consider an intellectual case. The teacher trains the student to spot certain patterns, cultivating good intellectual habits. When the student recognizes a pattern on a later occasion, the habit activates and the student makes the relevant inference. The reasoning and resulting inferential belief manifest not only the student’s intellectual abilities, but also the teacher’s teaching skill, since he trained the student to perform that way.

Now go back to Ralph’s necessary condition on nondeviant causation for reasoning: “the causal chain in question must consist purely of manifestations of the reasoner’s dispositions.” I think this threatens to rule out too much because the student’s performance and resulting belief might manifest the teacher’s skills too, and so won’t consist purely of manifestations of the student’s dispositions. But it needn’t strike us as deviant because of that. In fact, many cases of perfectly normal inference proceed just this way.

Well, maybe there’s a way to understand the condition as stated that avoids my worries. Maybe we should read it like so: “each step of the causal chain must manifest the reasoner’s dispositions.” That is, there must be no step that doesn’t manifest the reasoner’s dispositions. This allows for any step to manifest someone else’s dispositions too. Call this the weaker reading.

The stronger reading — the one that most naturally occurred to me when I read the paper, and also the one that causes trouble — would be something like this: “no step of the causal chain manifests something other than the reasoner’s dispositions.”

Of course, Ralph introduces the necessary condition to rule out certain problem cases (e.g. see the Mallett case on p. 663), and we should want to know whether the weaker reading can rule them out as effectively as the stronger reading can. I agree — that’s definitely a relevant consideration. But setting that aside for the moment, I’m wondering whether my discussion above creates at least some pressure to opt for the weaker reading.

    Your example of the midfielder’s pass seems much more intuitive to me than the coach/teacher example. Consider the following example: I learned to spot simple arithmetical patterns from my teacher in elementary school. Now suppose that she has already passed away many years ago. What sense does it make to say that my present arithmetical calculations manifest the skills of my teacher? That is, I doubt that someone who does not exist anymore can still manifest her dispositions in my present arithmetical performances. But doesn’t this suggest that my teacher’s skills would, in some sense, be causally inessential to my present performance even if she were still alive and well? This is different in the case of the midfielder and the striker, for to score a goal like that is arguably impossible without the presence of someone who launches it (in close temporal proximity). But off the top of my hat, I cannot think of a really analogous case in the domain of reasoning. So, maybe Wedgewood’s condition is a plausible necessary condition for reasoning even in the strong sense, although it would give rise to counterexamples if it were applied to other human performances.

    To take a slightly different example (inspired by Thomson’s “Time of a Killing”), suppose an assassin shoots a leader. The assassin is killed by law enforcement almost immediately. The leader dies later of his injuries. I think that the leader’s death is a manifestation of the assassin’s malevolent intentions and dark art.

    I suspect this all points to an important distinction in ways dispositions manifest themselves.
