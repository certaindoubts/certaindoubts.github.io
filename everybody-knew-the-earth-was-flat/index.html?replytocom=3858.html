“Everybody knew the earth was flat”

Posted on August 22, 2006 by Ralph WedgwoodAugust 22, 2006

According to today’s NY Times, in the movie “Men In Black”, when the character played by Will Smith is still in shock after his first encounter with aliens, the character played by Tommy Lee Jones says to him: “1500 years ago everybody knew the earth was the center of the universe. 500 years ago, everybody knew the earth was flat. And 15 minutes ago you knew that people were alone on this planet. Imagine what youâ€™ll know tomorrow.”

Like most epistemologists, I’m inclined to say that what this character says is strictly speaking false. Nobody ever knew that the earth was the centre of the universe — falsehoods can’t be known. (Even though I’m a card-carrying contextualist, I draw the line at falsehoods!) Still, the point that this character is making by using the word ‘know’ in this way is surely quite true.

What is the best way for our philosophy of language to explain how this particular utterance of a false sentence conveys a truth? Is it that this is a sort of ironic use of ‘know’, mimicking the way in which the people 1500 years ago would treat their conviction about how the earth was the centre of the universe exactly as if it were knowledge? Or is there some better theory about what’s going on when people use the term ‘know’ in this way?

  8. Jonny Blamey on August 22, 2006 at 9:27 am said:

    I think there is a good way of characterising this non factive knowledge. It is a belief which is certain in such a way that certainty rests on intersubjective agreement (considerably fleshed out). The “intersubjective agreement” is necessary because the quote doesn’t intend to include mad people or radical thinkers or minority cults; and it doesn’t intend to capture people who either had no interest in the shape of the world or people who had arbitrary unjustified beliefs. “Knowledge” I believe, is a universal concept, and non factive intersubjective certainty maps the universal usage better than a timeless factive analysis that occurs in mainstream analytic philosophy. The trouble with factivity is that we are doomed into thinking that we know nothing since it is unlikely that any contemporary theories will never be improved upon. Hence we might be inclined to say “We now know the earth goes around the sun” but does this really make sense in the absents of an absolute frame of reference? From our perspective on earth, the sun goes around the earth. If we were to radically translate a completely foreign culture’s language, we would be better off translating as “Knowledge” the word they used to refer to intersubjective agreement in certainty; rather than say they had no word corresponding to knowledge, because they were certain of so many things we consider false and had no special word that picked out the true subset of these. I don’t think such a move would entail a slide into relativism because the intersubjective agreement is agreement within a set of people, and the relevant set of people is set by whatever it is you are trying to communicate. So we can say with confidence that the world is not flat, and that when people thought it was, they failed to know it was because it is not flat and knowledge is factive. But there is a sense in which, were we to restrict ourselves to discussing them at their time, this is what passed as knowledge, as opposed to true belief or thought or guess work.

  22. Tim McGrew on August 24, 2006 at 4:22 pm said:

    I can’t resist …

    Lest history swerve, we should note that 500 years ago — in fact, we could go back nearly two and a half millennia on this one — virtually no educated person believed that the earth was flat. Aristotle (De Caelo II, 13 & 14) takes the sphericity of the earth as an established fact and provides a theoretical account of it and a critique, based on the indistinguishability of likelihoods, of an argument against sphericity. Ptolemy spends several chapters in book I of the Almagest laying out the evidence that the earth is (to an excellent approximation) spherical. Many medieval thinkers quoted and discussed Aristotle’s claim, often asking not whether it is true — they knew that from the shape of the shadow in lunar eclipses, for example — but in how many distinct ways it may be demonstrated. (See, e.g., Adam de Wodeham, Lectura Secunda d. 1, q. 3.)

    The idea that there was widespread belief in the flat earth arises from two principal sources. The first is a historical fraud perpetrated by Washington Irving, who claimed, in the 1820’s, to have discovered primary sources indicating that Columbus had to convince the Portuguese scientists of his day that the earth was not flat. The second is Andrew Dickson White’s tendentious History of the Warfare of Science with Theology (1896). White quotes Lactantius and, in particular, Cosmas Indicopleustes in support of his thesis, but he never gets around to mentioning that the impact of these two worthies on scientific thinking was utterly negligible. Philoponus paused to slam Cosmas in the 6th century, and that was more or less the last word on the subject. Cosmas’s speculations were so inconsequential that the were not translated into Latin until the eighteenth century. Several other histories of science follow White here — a painful reminder of why reference borrowing is not a substitute for going to the primary sources.

    Doubtless one could, by an assiduous search, find a few more theologians straying into the claim of a flat earth. To spare you the labor, I believe Cyril of Jerusalem, Diodorus of Tarsus, and Theodosius of Mopsuestia are three, all of them fairly minor figures. It is frightening to think of the picture of science in the 21st century that might be put together by the selective quotation of a few strange birds who live among us still.

    Anyone interested in following up on this might want to consult Owen Gingerich’s article “Astronomy in the Age of Columbus,” Scientific American, November 1992, or Jeffrey Burton Russell’s book Inventing the Flat Earth (New York: Praeger, 1991). (I am also told that the sphericity of the earth and the antiquity of this knowledge were the subject of a scientifically and historically accurate lecture on Captain Kangaroo. But I have been unable to verify this latter claim.)

    Looks like I’m a few months late on this, but:

    1. Christoph Kelp & Duncan Pritchard have a paper saying more or less what (it sounds like) Stjernberg says, called “Anti-Realism, Factivity, and Fitch”:
    http://www.philosophy.stir.ac.uk/staff/duncan-pritchard/documents/FactivityAndFitch.pdf

    2. Stjernberg’s point (“we do not have arguments for the factivity of knowledge â€” we just assume it”) is probably the most important thing that people suspicious of ‘factivity’ (like me) want to argue for.

    3. On that: the important thing about cases like “People knew the earth was flat” isn’t that they are sufficient evidence that ‘S knows p’ doesn’t entail p. The assertibility of one sentence, in context, isn’t conclusive evidence for that. (Though I happen to think it’s pretty good evidence!) The important thing that these cases is that they show that there isn’t conclusive linguistic evidence that ‘S knows p’ entails p. At best the linguistic evidence is inconclusive. That’s important if you think the linguistic evidence is relevant to the question. (If you don’t, the reply from critics of ‘factivity’ is going to be: define your own “knows” however you like!)
