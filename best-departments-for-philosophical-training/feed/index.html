I know no program should be held to the standard of being able to get everyone to an acceptable level of competence, but I think it is irrational for students choosing a graduate school to envision themselves as one of the brilliant elite. So a crucial issue they should consider is how well they will be prepared for the professional demands they’ll face at whatever placement level their abilities take them, and that means they need to consider how well-trained something like the average student is from the departments they want to join. It is unfortunate that this issue is so impenetrable to rational considerations. It would be fun and intriguing to require every new PhD at every program in the country to submit their best couple of papers for blind reviewing to some standing quality assessment panel, and see what the results look like each year. My suspicion is that there are some programs that are consistently good, regardless of which faculty member you write your dissertation with, and many more where things are too unpredictable. When you’re looking for brilliance in the context of a hiring committee, this doesn’t matter much; but when you’re choosing a graduate program, it’s critical information.

By the way, this idea isn’t quite as ridiculous as it might appear; quite a few undergraduate programs are required to have papers by their majors assessed by some outside reviewer every year. The information is for internal use only, but a suitably proactive national organization could easily ask graduate programs to do something similar as part of our collective responsibility to our discipline. Of course, there’d be lots of eastern-division-like haranguing…

]]> By: jon kvanvig http://certaindoubts.com/best-departments-for-philosophical-training/#comment-882 Fri, 15 Oct 2004 12:11:17 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=160#comment-882 Here’s what I’d look for before advising a student about a program. The issue of faculty accessibility is one point to examine, since without it, nothing else the program does will make up for this failure. But accessibility by itself isn’t enough. The question is whether the program is structured in such a way that students who go through the program master the professional side of the discipline. What singles out the programs I mentioned is that students come out of those programs doing philosophy in a particular way, and having mastered the demands of the profession in terms of how to succeed at publishing. My opinion on this is that there really aren’t very many programs that make this skill an explicit part of their programs. One model for developing such skills is Keith Lehrer’s: you make students rewrite until they find a thesis that’s worth defending and come up with an argument that’s plausible. There are simply too many young faculty who come out of grad school from very good places who lack these skills. They get hired in departments with decent teaching loads; they are quality philosophers, but they flounder when confronted with the quantitative publication requirements for success. My sense is that most manage to make it somehow, but it’s a preposterous situation for them. The skills in question are foundational for good philosophy; even students who are not gifted enough to make original contributions to philosophy can master them. And on the basis of having been on over 20 search committees and reading some 500 writing samples from candidates from Leiter’s top 15-20 programs, I’ve concluded the following. In the rhetorical style of British understatement, I’ve concluded that such skills are not ubiquitously taught. And the reason I singled out UMass, Syracuse, Arizona, and Brown, is that candidates from such schools, regardless of the year of the search or the specialization sought, uniformly sent writing samples that showed mastery of these skills.
