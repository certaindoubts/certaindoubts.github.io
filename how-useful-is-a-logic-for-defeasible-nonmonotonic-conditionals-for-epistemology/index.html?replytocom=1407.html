How useful is a logic for Defeasible / Nonmonotonic Conditionals for Epistemology?

Posted on February 14, 2005 by hawthorneFebruary 14, 2005

The posting “Jim Hawthorne on the Logic of Nonmonotonic Conditionals” took a rather technical turn (which was my fault–sorry!!!). This posting is addressed to readers of the blog more generally. In it I will describe much about defeasible conditionals that experts already know, though they may disagree with the interpretative spin I’ll give. This posting is intended to provide one take (my take) on what certain kinds of defeasible conditionals represent that may be of interest to epistemologists. I’d be interested in whatever anyone thinks of this.

With regard to the logic of defeasible conditionals, I think that the most central issues for epistemologists are:

1. What “exactly” are defeasible or nonmonotonic conditionals such as those in system R (the rational consequence relations) supposed to represent? and how precisely are they supposed to be relevant to knowledge or belief? and

2. How does each particular semantic basis or model theory (e.g. Popper functions, Popper measures (which I’ve been calling “vF functions”), preferential models, etc.) illuminate whatever it is that these conditionals are supposed to represent (in a way that is useful to epistemology)? Skip the technicalities — what is it supposed to represent, intuitively? And does it succeed?

I’ll take a stab at part of this. I’ll focus on the R conditionals.

Consider a conditional –> that satisfies the rules of R. Each “conditional statement” of form B–>A, may be interpreted as saying, “among the possible worlds where B is true, A is almost certainly true”. (In this posting the capital letters all represent statements.) Here the “almost certainly” may be understood in the mathematicians sense — e.g. among the worlds where B is true, those that make ~A true have “measure 0”. There may be such ~A worlds, but they are so unlikely that we need not concern ourselves with them at all, unless we learn something else, C, that makes them loom much larger. We may indeed learn something new, C, that brings the ~A worlds to be worth considering. That is, although B–>A, it may be that (B&C)-/->A (i.e., (B&C) does not make A almost certain), and it may even happen that (B&C)–>~A. That is, the B&C worlds, which is a subset of the B worlds, may not give A measure 1, and may even give ~A measure 1. But, as the rule called “Rational Monotony” says, when B–>A holds, learning that C is true can interrupt that support (i.e. (B&C)-/->A) only when C itself has measure 0 among the worlds where B is true — i.e. only when B–>~C. And this process can iterate. That is, suppose that (B&C)–> ~A. Then learning anything new, D, will continue to make ~A almost certain, unless (B&C)–>~D, in which case we might have (B&C&D)–>A again (or we might still have (B&C&D)–>~A, or it might be that (B&C&D)-/->A and (B&C&D)-/->~A — the logic itself doesn’t force any one of these on us — it only requires that each given conditional –> in R does one of these three things).

So, on this reading, “B–>A” says that that among the worlds in which B is true, A is true in almost all (measure 1) of them. And, in general, more information C will leave this strong support of A by B intact, unless C itself is almost certainly false in the B worlds, in which case learning C can “re-invigorate” a subset of the possibilities that were considered “almost certainly impossible” on the basis of B alone.

However, there may be statements A that, according to a particular conditional, –>, are considered “absolutely certain” among the B worlds (not just “almost certain”), and that cannot be undermined, no matter what is learned. So, even if we “learn” ~A, statement A is still considered certain on B. That is, B–>A, and for every possible C, (B&C)–>A, and even (B&~A)–>A. This last conditional, (B&~A)–>A, is the mark of B making A absolutely certain. The idea is that among the B worlds, absolutely none of them make ~A true, so (B&~A) is a “conceptual contradiction” — no worlds make (B&~A) true. So, A is true among “all of the worlds” that make (B&~A) true. Thus, whereas ‘B–>A’ says that B makes A “almost certain”, ‘(B&~A)–>A’ makes the stronger claim that B makes A “absolutely certain”.

What a conditional statement B–>A in R says about belief is this: if the agent is “certain of B”, then she should be “almost certain of A”. So suppose a particular conditional –> in R represents a particular agent’s “belief supporting conditional”. And suppose that she is certain (for now) that C, and suppose that C–>B holds for her. Should she then be “almost certain that B”? Yes, provided that there is no additional claim D of which she is certain and such that C–>~D. So, C need not consist of “everything she is certain of”. C suffices to support “almost certainty” in B provided that she is not also certain of some claim D that C implies (according to –>) is almost certainly false.

Notice, we must keep claims like C that the agent is “certain of” distinct from claims like B that the agent is (only) “almost certain of”. That is, after B is inferred on the basis of C, one cannot then just throw B into the same pot with C. If one did so, then one would lose track of what to retract when one learns some new information D that (together with C) should undermine B.

Thus, the way to think about these conditionals is this. One needs to keep two “lists of statements”, which I call “the given list” and “the inferred list”. The “given list” is the “certain list”, the list of claims on which the agent is certain. The “inferred list” for conditionals in R is the “almost certain list”. The given list contains everything the agent “believes with certainty” (at a given time, anyway — she may later change the given list, but that is not part of the defeasible logic that the conditionals –> are about). The inferred list contains every statement B such that C–>B holds, provided that C is presently on the given list and provided that there is no claim D presently on the given list such that C–>~D. (If there is such a D, then B can only be on the inferred list if (C&D)–>B.) That is, B’s support from members of the given list must be undefeated. The inferred list represents the claims of which the agent is “almost certain”, given her certainty in whats on the given list. (Notice that C–>C always holds — so the inferred list contains everything on the given list as well — if C is “certain”, then it is “almost certain”, too.)

Notice what this does to transitivity in belief updating. In R we don’t in general have that “if C–>B and B–>A, then C–>A”. What we have instead is this: if C–>B and B–>A, then C–>A, provided that B-/-> ~C. That is, if C is on the given list (i.e., the “certain” list), and if by virtue of that, B is on the inferred list, then any statement A that B would support (were B to be placed on the given list) can be placed on the inferred list as well, provided that B itself doesn’t imply that C is almost certainly false. This kind of transitivity permits the inferred list to be updated with whatever its members defeasibly imply, provided those member of the inferred list don’t “bit the hand” of the member of the given list that put them there. Notice, too, that if B–>~C does hold, then we can still update the inferred list, but only based on (C&B) — because if the given list has C and the inferred list has B (because of inference from C), then the inferred list has both B and C, and so has (B&C). So although B on its own may give measure 0 to C, we are now reasoning “within the set of worlds” (B&C) (that had measure 0 on B alone).

One more point. What about cases where C supports neither B nor ~B — i.e. where C-/->B and C-/-> ~B. That is, neither B nor ~B has measure 0 or 1 among C worlds — that is, when the agent is certain of C, she is neither “almost certain” of B nor is she “almost certain” of ~B. Does the agent have to simply leave it at that? It is rather natural for an agent to take C to support some such statements B more strongly than other such statements. If we model the agent as having “belief strengths” on such statements, scaled between 0 and 1, and if these belief strengths follow some very simple, obvious rules that are compatible with how the logic of “almost certainty”, R, works, we get the Popper functions!!!

For a Popper function P, P[A|B] = r may be read as “among the worlds where B is true, A is true in proportion r of them (according the way that P measures worlds). Thus, if a particular Popper function P represents an agents belief strengths, then when she is certain of B (and B represents everything she is certain of that is relevant to A), her belief strength for A should be r. But Popper functions also have R embedded in them. That is, although P[~C|B] may be 1, P[A | B&C] is still perfectly well defined. That is, although among B worlds C has measure 0, if C were learned, the (B&C) worlds may still provide a perfectly coherent measure of the plausibility of A. Indeed, each conditional –> in R is the probability 1 part of a Popper function, and the probability 1 part of each Popper function is a conditional in R. That is, if we define a conditional –} such that B–} A iff P[A|B]= 1, then –} must satisfy the rules of R. And, conversely, each conditional –> that satisfies the rules of R can be expanded into a Popper function P by assigning P[A|B]= 1 whenever B–>A and assigning some number less that 1 (but greater than or equal to 0) to all other pairs of sentence (in accord with certain restrictions). For any given –> satisfying R, there is always a way to do this (and generally many different ways) — different ways to expand the conditionals in R to incorporate “belief strengths”.

This naturally gives rise to the question, are there other interesting conditionals for which C–>B represents, for example, “among worlds where C is true, B is true in at least proportion r of them”, or where the conditional may be read as “when the agent is certain that C, and C is the strongest claim of which she is certain that is relevant to A, then her degree of confidence in A is (or should be) at least r”? The systems Q in the posting that started this discussion (i.e. “Jim Hawthorne on the Logic of Nonmonotonic Conditionals”) are an attempt to investigate the qualitative rules for such a logic of “uncertain” (not even “almost certain”) belief at various levels r.

So, for those of you who know about such logics, what is your reading of these logics? Does it differ from mine?

For the epistemologists more generally: Does such a logic for Defeasible / Nonmonotonic Conditionals seem helpful/useful to Epistemology? Your thoughts about how! — or about why not!

Your comments and reactions, please!

    I have a few worries about the use of a measure on the space of possible worlds as a model for epistemic concepts. Let me just sketch one of them here, regarding the source of the measure.

    When physicists talk about events in a phase space, their talk is constrained by a huge number of boundary conditions that make it plausible to speak of a unique measure on the space. Once the measure is in place, they can talk about events with measure 0 and move from that to talk about what is “almost certain.” Sometimes the choice of measure is justified in a rough-and-ready way by sheer frequencies: we haven’t seen any events of type X, so that’s a “pretty good” fit with a measure that ascribes to them a vanishing proportion of the space.

    It’s not clear to me where we can get such conditions to constrain a measure on the space of possible worlds, which surely has at least as great a cardinality as that of the continuum. (Think of all the possible laws, all the possible initial conditions.) If we start with a Euclidean (flat) measure we seem to run into normalizability problems, in which case it isn’t clear we’re justified in moving from measure to any probabilistic or epistemic concepts. We might be forced to go the direction of NSA, which is (IMHO) epistemically tricky. But what measure on possible worlds is more reasonable, ab initio, than a Euclidean measure?

  7. Gregory Wheeler on February 15, 2005 at 2:37 pm said:

    One question is whether rational belief is constrained by probabilities–or another way to put it, whether there are such things as doxastic probabilities. One view on this is to think that agents have doxastic probabilities ‘in their heads’ and so the job for a logic is strictly that for keeping these degrees of beliefs consistent. There are a variety of views that crop up on this approach about what the correct theory of probability is–whether probability should be primitively defined as a monadic function, a diadic function, defined over events, defined over sentences directly, how to extend the measure to events that aren’t strictly defined by the measure, and whether the weaker additivity properties on various extended measures are appropriate by the lights either of the general program of providing doxastic constraints or the more restricted purpose of combining probabilities.

    The question of whether a theory for defeasibile / non-mon conditionals is useful for this project should not be identified with the question of whether such a theory is useful for epistemology. The former is a specific project within (formal) epistemology. Perhaps rational belief is instead constrained by statistical information about relative frequencies in addition to the numerous other sources of evidence agents receive and probabilism is false. This option shouldn’t rule out a role for a theory of defeasible/non-mon conditionals.

    But how might a theory be helpful to this broader conception of epistemology? One idea is that the study of non-mon logics might yield some insight into fallibilism–the claim (roughly) that it is rational to believe a proposition that may be false. Nearly every contemporary theory of justification embraces some form of fallibilism. But, the manner in which justification comes and goes hasn’t moved much beyond black-box descriptions of properties which have been developed with an eye toward avoiding skeptical arguments. (There are notable exceptions, one of which is relevant to the present line: Pollock (1993 AIJ) for one thinks that non-mon logics cannot model defeasibility.) It would be nice to see more than descriptions of support relations, in other words, when evaluating theories of justification. Non-monotonic logic might become an important analytical tool for thinking about these notions. (Pereira and I argued in our JAL 2(4) 2004 paper that a useful consequence of this stance would be to generate applicable, testable theories. )

  31. Jim Hawthorne on February 19, 2005 at 1:46 am said:

    Greg,

    It seems to me that if each of two (or any number, for that matter) of possibilities Bi is prima facie strong reason to believe A, then their disjunction should be as well. That holds even if the disjunction is a tautology. However, “prima facie strong reason to believe A given a tautology” just amounts to “prima facia reason to believe A on the basis of whatever other presuppositions are brought to the context that the conditional represents”.

    That is, think of a conditional –> as analogous to a conditional probability function P[*|*]. The probability of A on a tautology, P[A|taut], is not usually taken to be the probability of A relative to “no background beliefs at all”. Rather, when we use probabilities (even conditional ones) to represent belief strengths, we usually suppose that the probability function already presupposes a lot of background beliefs. The idea then is that P[A|B] represents how strongly the agent believes A when B is added to everything else the agent knows (or believes). The same idea applies for how a defeasible conditional –> is supposed to work. So, for a specific conditional –>, “taut–>A” represents prima facie strong belief that A, given some implicit set of background beliefs. If we then also have B–>~A, for example, this means that adding B to those background beliefs provides prima facie strong reason to believe ~A.

  33. Jim Hawthorne on February 20, 2005 at 3:27 pm said:

    Andy,

    Good questions!!! You make me realize I didn’t say enough about how these axioms (and the conditionals in R more generally) are often supposed to be “applied”. So here is one such account (though not the only possible account) of how these conditionals are supposed to function in an agent’s doxastic economy.

    The usual idea is that the agent has a “belief base” (what artificial intelligence researchers often call a “knowledge base”). Let’s call it ‘K’. The beliefs represented in K (as statements) are some sort of “more basic beliefs” on the basis of which other beliefs are supposed to be inferred. The agent is then supposed to employ some conditional — for our purposes, a conditional that satisfies the rules of R — for defeasibly inferring (or updating to) additional beliefs based on her belief basis K. There may be many possible such conditionals, just as Bayesian agents, for example, are supposed to have available a number of possible “degree of belief functions”. But (like the Bayesian analogy) each (ideal) agent is presumed to employ just one of the possible R conditionals. So let ‘–>’ be the conditional for a given agent whose belief basis is K. Now read ‘B–>A’ to say that if B were added to K, then this (i.e. B together with K) is prima facie strong reason to believe A. Notice that we will be keeping track of two separate “belief lists”, the basis K and an inferred list, which I’ll call ‘DK’ — the list of Defeasibly Inferred beliefs based on K. As new information B is added to K we get a new basis K+{B} (K union {B}) and a new inferred list DK+{B}, which contains just those sentences A such that B–>A.

    By the way, before updating, DK contains just those sentence A such that ‘tautology–>A’ — which are just the sentences that the agent is taken to have prima facie strong reason to believe based on K alone, before any (additional) updating (though K itself may have come from updates to a previous belief basis). This DK, based only on K, will include, of course, all sentences in K, and usually much more.)

    With this understanding of –>, let me address your questions.

    Axiom 1: ‘A–>A’ just says that all basic beliefs in K also go on the “defeasibly inferred list” DK. Also, if a new basic belief A is added to K, it also goes on the new inferred list. In other words, “based on a given (more basic) belief that A, the agent has prima facie strong reason to believe A”

    Axiom 2: I take your point. The idea is that the agent has prima facie strong reason to believe the logical implications of her beliefs — provided she is aware of (or in a position to be aware of) those logical implications. The logic of the conditionals in R tends to be aimed at “logically ideal” agents. But perhaps these conditionals can be interpreted in a way that takes account of the fact that real agents are less than logically ideal by building the appropriate qualification into the interpretation of the conditional itself.

    Axiom 3: roughly the same issue as raised for axiom 2

    Axiom 4: seems OK to you for now

    Axiom 5: See if it might sound even better given the above explanation. This is a version of a rule that logicians call CUT.

    Axiom 6: The way R is usually understood, no nesting of the conditional is permitted. So there is no “exportation” from ‘C&B–>A’ to ‘B–>(C–>A)’. Indeed, –> is usually thought of as a metalinguistic relation (just as logical entailment is metalinguistic). So even expressions of form ‘C&(B–>A)’ are not taken to be proper expressions, just as C&(B|=A) is considered an improper mixing of object language and metalanguage. The closest to ‘C&(B–>A)’ one might come is to say (metalinguistically) “C is in K (C is a more basic belief) and B–>A (adding B to the more basic beliefs would constitute prima facie strong reason to believe that A)”.
