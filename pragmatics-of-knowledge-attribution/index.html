Pragmatics of knowledge-attribution

Posted on December 8, 2004 by Kvanvig JonDecember 8, 2004

Here’s an issue that I’m not sure how to begin thinking through. If we think about theories of knowledge in terms of a semantics/pragmatics distinction regarding the use of knowledge ascriptions, we encounter the problem of determining which aspects of use to explain semantically and which to explain pragmatically. The same issues will recur if we wish to cast the discussion more in terms of the nature of knowledge rather than in terms of meaning, but I’ll just do things in terms of meaning for now.

Consider, then, the way in which knowledge and practice are related. Ascriptions of knowledge typically convey the idea of closure with respect to further inquiry, and thus when the practical stakes get raised, ascriptions of knowledge tend to be inappropriate. Contextualists and Subject-Sensitive Invariantists generate a semantic account of this feature of knowledge ascriptions, but I am unaware of arguments to the conclusion that this feature is semantic in character rather than pragmatic.

I’m thinking here of arguments by semantic skeptics and those in favor of semantic modesty about the role semantic content plays in the philosophy of language and, derivatively, in generating our philosophical accounts. My usual approach to trying to sort between semantic and pragmatic features adverts to the possibility of canceling the implication by explicit denial. (So, if I say “there’s a gas station on the corner,” a pragmatic implication of this claim is that you can buy gasoline there, but I can cancel this implication by adding “but it’s closed”.) On this test, the above feature of knowledge ascriptions may look semantic: it is hardly coherent to say “I know that p, but I ought to check further.” But consider third-person ascriptions: “Mary knows the plane will arrive at 11:00, but because it’s so important that she be correct, she still ought to check further.” Is this statement incoherent? I don’t see it that way, and if that’s right, then there is some evidence that the connection between practical stakes and knowledge ascription may be more of a pragmatic phenomenon than an semantic one.

But I don’t know the literature well enough on semantic skepticism and semantic modesty to be sure how to sort these things, and my ear for ordinary language may be corrupted as well (especially given the inductive argument from the many others ways in which I have been corrupted)…
