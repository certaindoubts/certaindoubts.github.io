]]> By: Gregory Wheeler http://certaindoubts.com/pragmatics-of-knowledge-attribution/#comment-1105 Thu, 09 Dec 2004 13:01:32 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=198#comment-1105 Hi Jon- I might not have understood your last comment. I thought the idea was that the knowledge ascription, S knows that p, typically means that S doesn’t need to continue collecting evidence for p, setting aside for the moment what type of modality ‘doesn’t need to’ is. The thought experiments we’ve been discussing are exceptions, cases where S knows p but that it is rational for S to continue collecting evidence for p.

I take it that an important feature of the example is that the knowledge ascription holds even after Mary considers the stakes for being wrong. What prompts her to action is not a shift in context that makes her doubt Bill’s intentions more, but, rather, she considers an action based upon p where being wrong is very expensive. Some actions seem reasonable to undertake based on her knowledge, like buying the magazine, while others don’t. This suggests that there are reasonable circumstances that may prompt an agent to continue epistemic inquiry into matters they already know.

I’m not sure what theoretical tool to apply to the case. I was interested in seeing how the example worked. The case is not quite like the skeptic’s challenge and so an appeal to a standard contextualist semantics to explain the non-monotonic behavior of Mary’s epistemic inquiry doesn’t suggest itself as a direct answer. However, there is some attraction to providing semantic models for pragmatic features too–which is another camp at Rochester–but this depends on what you want to do with such structures. I prefer sparse semantic structures because I’ve an interest in formal reasoning; it is cleaner in my mind to treat inquiry closure as a pragmatic feature.

]]> By: Gregory Wheeler http://certaindoubts.com/pragmatics-of-knowledge-attribution/#comment-1103 Wed, 08 Dec 2004 20:35:40 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=198#comment-1103 Hi Jon,

Ok, consider (2) then. Mary’s evidence about Bill’s intentions are sufficient for Mary to believe, and to believe truly, that Bill will propose. She may well perform some actions based upon this knowledge (buy a bridal magazine, say), but refrain from performing other actions, such as completely ending other prospects.

Couched in evidentialist terms, we’re supposing that Mary has good (but fallible) evidence that Bill’s intentions are to propose to her. And we’re supposing that her evidence is such that she is justified to believe that Bill’s intentions are to propose to her. Moreover, we’re supposing that her belief is true and that there aren’t any irregularities in her justificatory state to proclude her from knowing that Bill’s intentions are to propose to her.

Still, from Mary’s perspective she could be mistaken–and who hasn’t been about love or marriage? So, by granting that (2) is coherent I understand us to be granting that it is reasonable for Mary to continue epistemic inquiry when the stakes are raised high enough for her to have second thoughts about suffering the penalty of being wrong. Being out $5 for a copy of Today’s Bride is nothing compared to losing Bill and Joe.

If all this runs, then why worry about the first person case? It isn’t that your belief changes, that you are committed to believing that p and believing that not-p, as (it seems) would be required for Moore’s paradox to surface; rather, the cost of being mistaken is what has significantly changed.

Cheers from Lisbon, Greg
