]]> By: Pat Bondy http://certaindoubts.com/1-its-3-oclock-in-lingustics-and-in-philosophy-2-a-truism-3-an-argument-for-hard-ass-semantics/#comment-15218 Fri, 18 Feb 2011 15:49:28 +0000 http://el-prod.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=2452#comment-15218 I’m no expert on pragmatics or the like, but here’s an intuitively correct (i.e. I thnk it’s right) take on ordinary claims about the time: they are precise to the minute given. So “it’s 3 o’clock” means that it is after 2:59:59, but before 3:01. I think that with this account in hand, we can handle all sorts of cases. In ordinary cases, if you say to John that Mary arrived at 3 o’clock, and it was 3:00:15, your claim is literally true. But if instead she arrived at 3:01, your claim would be literally false. In cases that require greater precision like the space launch case, seconds count, so it’s not an ordinary case. In that case, “it’s 3 o’clock” would be literally false, if it was 3:00:15, because in that case “it’s 3 o’clock” is elliptical for “it’s 3:00:00.”

(This account doesn’t appeal to the speaker/sentence meaning distinction, since it says that “it’s 3 o’clock” can be literally true, even if it’s 3:00:15. The account appeals, rather, to the view that a given context can allow a sentence to express a proposition that is more or less precise than the sentence appears on its face.)

That doesn’t help with your questions, but perhaps it can offer a mediating viewpoint.
