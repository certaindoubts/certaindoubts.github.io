Some Epistemological Metatheory

Posted on April 2, 2005 by Kvanvig JonApril 2, 2005

The discussion here about pragmatic encroachment in epistemology, as well as the renewed interest on approaches to epistemology that depend on results in the philosophy of language, has led me to reflect a bit about different approaches to epistemology. So suppose we adopt a functionalist approach to the concept of knowledge, one that identifies some human need or interest that is satisfied by speaking a language that expresses this concept. Never mind for the moment the identity of the concept.

One such function is that identified by Craig–we need such a concept to track reliable sources of information. (I won’t argue with details of proposals here, though I think this one needs some precisification.) If that were the only functional factor available, there’d be no need for pragmatic elements in a theory of knowledge.

A different function is more relevant to this issue. It has to do with the experience of closure with regard to further inquiry.

We use the concept of knowledge to communicate that closure has been (legitimately) achieved. We find awkward and perhaps something close to unintelligible remarks such as “I know that the gym opens in an hour but I ought to check the schedule to make sure.” Here I’ll say that closure is experienced when it has been legitimately achieved and the cognizer’s appetite on the question is sated. For some items, such as ordinary perceptual ones, I assume that the design of our cognitive equipment generates the closure experience by default–just look to see if there’s a tree in your backyard, and you’ll have the closure experience if you see one. In other areas, things are not quite that simple.

It is this function that, I think, gives a basis for pragmatic elements in epistemology. For it is obvious that whether closure is experienced depends on what is at stake. DeRose’s bank case, Cohen’s plane case, and the train case of Fantl&McGrath are cases in which the experience of closure is absent either for the context of the speaker or of the agent in question. And so the language of knowledge comes to be used in such a way that is sensitive to pragmatic factors (either directly, as for F&M, or indirectly, for DeRose and other contextualists). I’ll say that there is pragmatic encroachment in epistemology whenever the closure experience depends on pragmatic factors, either directly or indirectly.

Now, there are different stories to tell about how language works to encode both of these functions. The first function is purely alethic, the second somewhat pragmatic. One of the two could be the dominant function, with the second function being viewed as an irritating corruption of our pristine scenery. One such view I’ll call Rortyism, and leave it as an exercise to the reader which function is primary and which a corruption. For those who don’t like pragmatic encroachment, something like the first function is primary, with the second as an irritating corruption. Those who like pragmatic encroachment treat knowledge in the way you’d expect from those who are egalitarian about the two functions.

Once we see the landscape of epistemology in this way, there are two quite different approaches we might take toward theory construction. One way is to focus on the output of the functions in question, attending to the nuances of language and what explanatory part is played by the semantics of the language versus its pragmatics. Call this the linguistic approach to epistemology. The other way is to focus more on the inputs of the system, asking more about what functions are played by epistemic language and which of these functions are the important ones. Call this the value-driven approach.

There is one significant advantage to the latter approach that I’ll mention here. The linguistic approach tends to be conservative about our epistemic language, in the following sense. It provides little incentive to alter the language or jettison it, except when the investigation shows it to be thoroughly incoherent. Since I doubt the segment of our language that is epistemic is thoroughly incoherent, the linguistic approach tends to leave the linguistic world as we found it.

When one approaches cognition and its achievements from the value-driven approach, one’s approach to epistemology will be less conservative. Such an approach can sort between the various functions our epistemic language answers to and give an underlying explanation, perhaps in a new technical vocabulary, for some of the data about linguistic usage as well as some of the conflict of intuitions experienced by competing theorists.

Though the recent history of epistemology has not been articulated in these terms, it provides an explanatory grid that helps us understand some of the disputes that are occurring. For example, some virtue epistemologists avow little interest in the differences between justified true belief and knowledge; others have adopted pluralist views about epistemic theories, granting that many different such theories talk about something worth talking about; and quite earlier, some epistemologists said they weren’t interested in what epistemic language means as much as what properties or relations it picks out. These inclinations away from the linguistic approach require an alternative perspective, and close attention to their language suggests strongly that the approach they are reaching for, perhaps in the dark, is the value-driven approach.

  12. jon kvanvig on April 3, 2005 at 6:11 pm said:

    Robert, some very nice and helpful distinctions here. Trying to honor intuitions in the Rawlsian sense would not commit one about which of the two approaches one is taking, as you point out; but it would display some of the conservative tendencies characteristic of approaches that try to honor linguistic intuitions in theory construction. The significance of the value-driven approach becomes clearer when one thinks about the domain of epistemology, not in terms of the theory of knowledge, but rather in terms of cognitive achievements of a purely theoretical (i.e., non-practical) sort. On this perspective, we should theorize about X to the extent that it is an important cognitive achievement, and the implications about this for the theory of knowledge are, I think, that we should be more interested in theorizing about justification, rationality, true belief (and perhaps calibrated degrees of belief, though I haven’t thought about that one as much as I should), cognitive virtues or excellences, and understanding than about knowledge. The bottom line is that I think philosophy is at its best when it refuses to take the world as we find it, even if there is a lot to be learned by theorizing about that.
