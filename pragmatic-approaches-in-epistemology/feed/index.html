]]> By: jon kvanvig http://certaindoubts.com/pragmatic-approaches-in-epistemology/#comment-1620 Sun, 03 Apr 2005 23:11:05 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=291#comment-1620 Robert, some very nice and helpful distinctions here. Trying to honor intuitions in the Rawlsian sense would not commit one about which of the two approaches one is taking, as you point out; but it would display some of the conservative tendencies characteristic of approaches that try to honor linguistic intuitions in theory construction. The significance of the value-driven approach becomes clearer when one thinks about the domain of epistemology, not in terms of the theory of knowledge, but rather in terms of cognitive achievements of a purely theoretical (i.e., non-practical) sort. On this perspective, we should theorize about X to the extent that it is an important cognitive achievement, and the implications about this for the theory of knowledge are, I think, that we should be more interested in theorizing about justification, rationality, true belief (and perhaps calibrated degrees of belief, though I haven’t thought about that one as much as I should), cognitive virtues or excellences, and understanding than about knowledge. The bottom line is that I think philosophy is at its best when it refuses to take the world as we find it, even if there is a lot to be learned by theorizing about that.

As small thought. The conflict between Hare and Rawls over the status of moral intuitions seems to be an example of this sort of meta- conflict in philosophy. Hare took pretty much what you describe as the ‘value driven’ approach in ethcal theory and was appalled by the status Rawls gave intuitions. These were, however, intuitions about whether something is just, right, fair, etc., not linguistic intuitions, though I don’t know whether that makes a difference to your grid. I suppose there could be a very similar sort of conflict, without appeal to ‘what we would say’, but only to whether this seems to be a case of knowing or not. Whatever we might say *in* the circumstances, there are also different things we might say *about* the circumstances, regarding whether someone knows or not. And philosophers might disagree over whether those intuitions too are given too little or much weight.
