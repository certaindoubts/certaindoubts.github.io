Testimonial knowledge

Posted on July 8, 2004 by Jon KvanvigJune 20, 2012

Suppose you think that knowledge by testimony requires knowledge on the part of your source of information (even if you think that justification can be transmitted even when your source has no justification). Brian Weatherson recently claimed on his blog that if the chain of testifiers is long enough, it doesn’t matter that the first link in the chain was, e.g., gettiered.

That case is interesting, but what about a case like this. You have multiple testimonial sources for p. None of them have knowledge, since none of them have a total body of evidence sufficient for epistemic justification. But each of them has some evidence for the claim, which is why each of them tells you that p is true. They are all correct: p is true. And you believe them, so you have a true belief. Moreover, if you combine all of their reasons for believing p, the combination is a secure, ungettiered epistemic justification for believing p. So if the quality of their reasons is somehow behind their epistemic authority, can the multiplicity of sources cover over the epistemic flaw that each of them displays in reporting to you something they don’t know to be true? In short, even though they don’t know, do the details about their grounds for belief allow for you to know on the basis of these multiple testimonial sources?

  15. Sandy Goldberg on July 9, 2004 at 2:25 pm said:

    Sorry I missed getting into this one earlier. Jon a while back raised the issue of what counts as a pure case of acquiring a belief on the basis of testimony. Some papers do address this. Audi talks about it in his 97 APQ paper “The Place of Testimony in the Fabric of Knowledge and Justification.” It lurks in several of Peter Graham’s testimony papers (often discussed in connection with how testimony spreads knowledge). I tried my hand in my 01 PQ paper “Testimonially based knowledge from false testimony” (first third of the paper). I have a better version coming out in a paper “Reductionism and the Distinctiveness of Testimonial Knowledge”, in the forthcoming OUP volume on testimony (edited by Jennifer Lackey and Ernest Sosa). Basically, the idea is that a pure case of knowledge through testimony is a case involving (i) knowledge which both (ii) causally implicates the hearer’s (correct and warranted) apprehension of a piece of testimony and (iii) depends for its status as knowledge on both (a) the reliability (or safety or sensitivity) of the testimony and (b) the hearer’s epistemic right to rely on that testimony. This formulation (which is only rough!) is meant to be neutral on the key issues in the epistemology of testimony (reductionism vs. anti-reductionism). At the same time, it speaks to many of the issues that arise between Fritz and Jon: the relevance of understanding, the epistemic dimension of relying on another’s word, and so forth.

    Also, for what it’s worth — I think this one is to Fritz — it turns out to be more controversial than you might think that KNOWLEDGE through testimony requires that one’s source, or someone in the line of communication, knows. I thought Lackey’s 1999 PQ paper “Testimonial Knowledge and Transmission” has a nice counterexample to this thesis. Also, Peter Graham claims to have one too, in one of his recent papers (either in PPR or Nous, I have forgotten which), but I remember thinking that as a counterexample the case depended on an arguable sort of misunderstanding of the content asserted by the speaker. Anyway, the general thesis that knowledge must be had in the chain prior to the hearer is controversial.

    For what it’s worth. …

  23. Jennifer Lackey on July 10, 2004 at 8:52 pm said:

    I just saw the discussion on testimonial knowledge and thought that I would jump in.

    First, I’m not sure the participants in the testimony literature are taking past one another in the way suggested. For in order for the thesis that speaker-knowledge is a necessary condition for testimonial knowledge to have any plausibility, it has to be read as applying only to cases of pure testimonial knowledge. Otherwise, counterexamples would simply abound, since supplementing a speaker’s testimony with further background information can easily result in knowing something in part through testimony that a speaker fails to know. Hence, as far as I can tell, the speaker-knowledge thesis is always (or at least should always) be read as applying *only* to cases of knowledge acquired purely through testimony.

    But even with respect to cases of pure testimonial knowledge, it is not difficult to construct counterexamples. There are at least three types of cases: first, a speaker may not believe (and hence not know) that p herself, but may nonetheless reliably report that p. For instance, suppose that a teacher who is a Creationist is required to teach evolutionary theory to her students. She may, then, be a reliable testifier to her students that a certain dinosaur fossil was found in a certain place (suppose she acquired this information from reliable evolutionary texts), despite not believing this herself. Hence, her students can acquire knowledge that p, even though she does not have this knowledge herself. Second, a speaker may be a radically yet consistently unreliable knower with respect to a certain domain of propositions and also be a radically yet consistently insincere testifier with respect to this same domain of propositions. When these combine in the right sort of way, a hearer can come to know that p purely on the basis of a speaker’s testimony that p despite the fact that the speaker does not know that p herself. (For notice that even though the speaker is an unreliable knower and a compulsive liar, she can nonetheless be a highly reliable testifier. For instance, her reports may be both Nozick-sensitive and Sosa-safe, even though her beliefs are Nozick-insensitive and Sosa-unsafe.) Third, a speaker may possess a defeater for believing that p that prevents her from knowing that p. When this speaker reports that p, a hearer who lacks such a defeater may come to know that p on the basis of the speaker’s testimony despite the speaker’s lack of the knowledge in question. Now in all three of these types of cases, the hearer is neither engaged in any sort of obvious inference nor is she supplementing the testimonial belief in question with background information. Hence, they are cases of pure testimonial knowledge if anything is.

    By the way, examples that are similar to the case of a hearer coming to know that p through multiple speakers reporting that p (each of whom fails to know that p) can be found in Plantinga’s chapter on testimony in Warrant and Proper Function and in Martin Kusch’s book, Knowledge by Agreement.

    Let me elaborate a bit on what I think should be Robert A’s strategy against the counterexampe recipes. (Robert A, for those who don’t knwo, is one defender of the view that for one to come to know that P via testimony the testifier must know that P).

    Take, for example, the defeater cases. The testifier testifies that P but doesn’t know P because he has an undefeated for P. It turns out that the defeater is, all things considered, misleading and the testifier is correct in believing and asserting that P. Intuitively it’s clear that testifier doesn’t Kp (in believing it he is being overly bold in the face of the defeater). One receiving the testimony, however, might not have the defeater (so the recipe says) and so might get only the good epistemic stuff from the testifier. In some cases, says the recipe, this good epistemic stuff will (together with truth and belief) suffice for knowledge.

    Reply: the case splits into two. Dilemma — either hearer doesn’t have the undefeated defeater for P becaues hearer has an overrider for the defeater or hearer doesn’t have the undefeated defeater for P because testifier hasn’t shared it with hearer. The defeater for testifier’s knowledge also defeats hearer’s knowledge — lean here on luck considerations: hearer is *lucky* the defeater didn’t get passed along. One liking the defeater counterexample recipe might think that defeaters defeat only when believed.   50. Sandy Goldberg on July 19, 2004 at 7:35 am said:

    I want to go back to the dilemma Fritz introduced at 27 (and which Jennifer L. picked up on in 29). The first horn was: the hearer acquries testimonial knowledge (which the speaker herself lacked) for having a defeater-defeater. Jennifer agrees with Fritz that this would make the knowledge unacceptable as a case of pure testimonial knowledge.

    I guess I agree that if we allow the presence of defeater-defeaters to rule out cases of “pure” testimonial knowledge, then “pure” testimonial knowledge will be either practically impossible or difficult to come by.

    But might it be helpful nevertheless to distinguish cases in which one accepts testimony and has a defeater-defeater, from cases in which what one comes to know is something other than what was presented-as-true in the testimony?

    I guess the idea might be this. We should distinguish capital-‘P’ Pure cases from lower-case-‘p’ pure cases of testimonial knowledge. A case can be pure (but not Pure!) even if one has a defeater-defeater for a defeater that one’s source cannot defeat. The idea of pure testimonial knowledge (as distinct from Pure testimonial knowledge) would be that these are cases in which ALL of the epistemic support the hearer’s belief that p enjoys pertains, either to the reliability (safety, sensitivity) of the testimony, or to her (the hearer’s) entitlement to accept the testimony. True, some of this support comes in the form of a defeater-defeater. But even so the case contrasts with other cases, e.g. I come to know that Jon has limited baseball acumen by observing his utterance of “The Yankees are not a good baseball team.” So it seems to me that one can have pure testimonial knowledge, but not Pure testimonial knowledge (the sort of knowledge that would be ruled out if one’s knowledge depends on having a defeater-defeater).

    I don’t know whether the distinction between Pure and pure testimonial knowledge will be of interest to anyone, but I think it might capture a notion of “pure testimonial knowledge” on which the issue whether we have “pure” testimonial knowledge need not await the outcome of the debate regarding the role of defeaters in testimony.

    I have forgotten to mention two other worthwhile papers on the topic of “pure” testimonial knowledge. One will be familiar to those who follow the testimony literature: E. Fricker’s 1987 paper (in Aristotelian Society) “The Epistemology of Testimony”, where she has a couple of things to say about this (although her discussion is not very involved there.) The other is a paper by Julie Jack, entitled “The Role of Comprehension,” in the 1994 book KNOWING FROM WORDS (eds. Matilal and Chakrabarti). Fricker’s paper has one drawback in that she simply stipulates, rather than argues for, a restriction on testimonial knowledge on which one can only have such knowledge — we might say, can only have a pure case of such knowledge — if what one believes = what was attested. But perhaps others won’t find this an arbitrary stipulation. And for its part, Jack’s paper is restricted in its interest to the role of linguistic comprehension in testimonial knowledge, but she does formulate a notion (I think she calls it “radical communication”) that gets at what I think people are after with respect to “pure” testimonial knowledge.

    Hi everyone,

    I thought I’d put these thoughts here, since I advertised this thread at the Pacific. In my paper, I defend the idea that, contrary to what Lackey says, the epistemic dishonor of defeaters should be charged to the recipients of testimony, just as it should be charged to the testifier. This fits well if we see the testifier/recipient relationship as like the agent/principal relationship in the law.

    Several people at the Pacific (I recall Brian Weatherson, Chase Wrenn, and Sanford Goldberg, but there may have been be others) suggested a counterexample, but I think the legal analogy can handle it in a way that fits with our epistemic intuitions.

    When I say that testifiers창?? defeaters come along for the testimonial ride, I mean that the portion of their epistemic dishonor pertaining to their testimony that p, rather than the actual defeaters–the p-undermining beliefs themselves. Suppose Testifier tells Recipient that p, but Testifier has an uncommunicated belief, d, that would defeat both p and q. (d might be ~p&~q.) Suppose both p and q are true. And suppose that Recipient has independent, perfectly sound basis for believing that q. Now, if the defeater d comes along for the testimonial ride, then it might seem that Recipient can창??t know that q.

    So, suppose Testifier believes, erroneously, that a desk in his office is empty (call this belief d). And suppose that Recipient believes that there is a pen in the desk (call this belief q), because he saw it there a few minutes ago. And suppose through some malfunction or breach of epistemic duty, Testifier also believes, without abandoning his belief that d, that there is a pencil in the desk (call this belief p), and tells Recipient that p. Testifier is misbehaving, because he also believes that d, which contradicts p, and on my view Recipient cannot therefore come to know testimonially that p–that there is a pencil in the desk. However, Recipient is not charged with the full collateral consequences of believing d, just because that happened to be the defeater for Testifier창??s belief that p. Recipient can still keep his knowledge that q창??that there is a pen in the desk. Rather, Recipient is only charged with the same epistemic debt pertaining to his testimonially-based belief that p that is owed by Testifier.

    It is not plausible to think that my belief that there is a pen in a desk is defeated, simply because you tell me that there is a pencil, and your belief is defeated by your erroneous contradictory belief that there is nothing in the desk. A potent skeptical defeater might do as well: if d is Testifier창??s belief that all humans may well be just brains in vats, the mere fact that I receive testimony from Testifier does not charge me with that defeater and so destroy all of my knowledge.

    These intuitions are surely right. The law, however, has a simple, compelling reply: only those actions that my agent takes within the scope of his employment are charged to my account. If Testifier has a super-powerful skeptical defeater d, it puts a epistemic stain on all of Testifier창??s other beliefs. When Testifier simultaneously believes that p, he is misbehaving, and if I accept that p on Testifier창??s say-so, there is a similar stain on my belief that p. But I have not employed Testifier regarding other beliefs like q. Testifier창??s misbehavior in believing p was, given the testimony, performed on my behalf and for my benefit, but his misbehavior in believing the super-defeater d was not.

    Think of an analogous situation with an employee. I hire my employee to clean up the floors of my store. This morning, he uses drugs, which causes him both (a) to do a very poor job of cleaning up the floors of my store, and (b) to damage his neighbor창??s landscaping by driving his car into it. Now, I am responsible for the results of my employee창??s drug use, insofar as they cause him to do a very poor job of cleaning up my store. But I am not responsible for the other results of that drug use. I am only responsible for the results within the scope of his employment.

    The fact that, on reflection, we want to limit the transmission of defeaters to recipients of testimony in this way fits quite well with the fact that the law limits the transmission of liability to principals to actions performed in the course and scope of employment. It may be very difficult to determine exactly how much of a testifier창??s misbehavior should be charged to the recipient of testimony, and this, too, fits with the law of principal and agent, which features many hard cases distinguishing 창??frolics창?? (which are outside the course and scope of employment) from 창??detours창?? (which are within it).
