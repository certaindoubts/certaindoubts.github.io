The Perils of In-House Reviewing

Posted on November 6, 2004 by Kvanvig JonNovember 6, 2004

Our own Sandy Goldberg is here in town, and gave a very nice paper yesterday on testimony. Since you ask, yes, he’s no longer needing anti-depressants over the ignoble decline of the Yankees, taking refuge, as is the wont of such fans, in the hope of buying a better team for next year…

Anyway, back to epistemology, where there’s much less to gloat over. In his talk, Sandy referred to an article on testimony in the illustrious journal edited by his alma mater, a journal known for running the risks inherent in in-house reviewing. The article is by Paul Faulkner, and it’s a very nice attempt to provide a hybrid theory of testimony. Here’s his view:

I propose the following theory of testimony.

(A) An audience is warranted in forming a testimonial belief if and only if he is justified in accepting the speaker’s testimony.
(B) If the audience is warranted in forming a testimonial belief, then whatever warrant in fact supports a speaker’s testimony continues to support the proposition the audience believes.

Let me call this the hybrid theory of testimony. It is hybrid because it is composed of two principles, one internalist and the other social externalist. I named (A) the principle of assent; it articulates an internalist claim. It is internalist because the formation of a warranted testimonial belief requires a justifying argument that articulates the audience’s reasons for believing. This argument must be based on the audience’s own experiences; in the fundamental case, the audience’s reasons for acceptance will be his experience of what testimonies are credible. Let me name (B) the principle of warrant; it articulates a social externalist claim. It is externalist because in accepting testimony an audience frequently believes propositions whose warrant could not possibly be equated with whatever justification the audience has for its acceptance.

There’s an interesting theory in the neighborhood of the conjunction of (A) and (B), but not as formulated. (A) is supposed to be an internalist constraint on knowledge by testimony and (B) a social externalist constraint. Notice, however, that the conditions as formulated logically guarantee that if you’ve satisfied the internalist constraint, you’ve satisfied the social externalist constraint. The mistake is easy to correct, however: just change the connective in (A) from ‘iff’ to ‘only if’.

So what does this have to do with the in-house reviewing at JP? Maybe nothing, but here’s a possibility (which I’m inclined to bring up because I think in-house reviewing is a bad idea). Most of us are familiar with the errors people make when deciding how to test a hypothesis about cards with numbers on one side and geometric shapes on the other. (The case involves showing four cards, two with the numbers showing and two with the shapes showing, and you get to turn over two of the cards to test the hypothesis, say, that any card with an even number on one side has a triangle on the other side. If the cards show a 3, an 8, a triangle, and a square, the most common responses are to turn over the 8 and turn over the triangle.) What’s interesting, I’m told, is that if you change the example to objects with which one is more familiar, mistakes are no longer made as often. For example, if you change to letters with return addresses on the back either present or absent, with stamps on the front either absent or present, and then ask subjects to test whether any stamped enveloped will have a return address on the back, mistakes rarely occur in testing this hypotheses. (If one wishes, the envelope case can be made more complex, involving two kinds of stamps and return addresses either handwritten or in the form of a printed label, and the results are still better than with the card case.)

So here’s the possibility. The mistake in Faulkner’s formulation is an obvious enough one that I suspect it would be caught by a significant percentage of people who work in the area. But if the data about the localization of logical expertise is correct, we might expect people not expert on testimony to show a greater tendency to miss the mistake.

Of course, defenders of in-house reviewing can always offer the panglossian possibility that competent philosophers have been freed by their training of the localization phenomenon…

There are interesting questions about those conditions, but they are not at the heart of the R/AR dispute. (And I say this as a proponent of AR.)

    I was never sure I understood Faulkner’s article even while I was reading it, but I don’t think Faulkner would see the entailment as a problem. This is partly because–I think–Faulkner uses “warrant” in a somewhat eccentric way, in which your warrant for believing something can be different from your justification for believing it. So, even when we’ve established what someone’s justification is, we haven’t established what her warrant is.

    So I don’t think Faulkner would object to the conclusion that, if you are justified in believing a speaker’s testimony (internalist), then you are warranted in believing the speaker’s testimony (social externalist). The point is that, if a speaker tells you that p, and you are (internalistically) justified in believing her, then the warrant you have for believing that p is the same warrant she has for believing that p. Which will be external to you, and (typically?) will be very different from the factors that justify you in believing her.

    Here’s an example: Maria tells me that every 3-manifold can be represented by a framed link. She is warranted in believing that every 3-manifold can be represented by a framed link because she carried out the proof of that in her introduction to knot theory class. I am justified in believing Maria’s testimony because I have ample evidence that Maria is a mathematics professor who knows this sort of thing, and I have ample evidence that she is trustworthy and warrant–which evidence is internal to me. But now my warrant for believing that every 3-manifold can be represented by a framed link is the proof that Maria carried out–and that proof is external to me. If Maria had carried out a different proof, I would have a different warrant, even though the same factors would justify me.

    (It seems to follow from this that if Maria never carried out a proof, I would have no warrant, even though I am justified. That seems like a problem for Faulkner, on my reading. I’ll have to think about it.)

    Part of what Faulkner is trying to do, I think, is to establish that our knowledge of mathematics can still be a priori (or a priori-ish) even when it’s partly based on testimony, and our reasons for accepting the testimony are a posteriori.

    Anyway, that’s what I see Faulkner as up to. As I said, I’m not sure I understood his article. But I think (B) isn’t supposed to be an additional constraint on knowledge; rather it’s a claim about what the basis of your knowledge is, given that you have knowledge.

  14. jon kvanvig on November 11, 2004 at 2:28 pm said:

    Sandy, Faulkner makes the point that it is wholly implausible to think that justification exhausts warrant, and you can’t make that point without taking the ‘any’ in “any type of positive epistemic evaluation” to be merely an existential quantifier. But I think the more I reflect on principles A and B, the fuzzier all of this is getting.

    Note that your talk of increasing warrant isn’t in either of Faulkner’s principles. All B says is that if your testimonial belief is warranted, it’s content is warranted by whatever warranted the speaker in the first place. This warrant of content is not one the holder of the testimonial belief has, since in the ordinary case, you’re not privy to the speaker’s basis for assertion. But that’s a problem, since you have propositional warrant for a claim only when you possess the warrant. In the case of evidential warrant, the content of your belief is not warranted simply because sufficient evidence exists to belief it–you have to have the evidence in order for the content to be warranted by that evidence. (There’s also the further stage of basing your belief on the evidence in order to have doxastic warrant, but that’s not relevant to the present point about the distinction between there being evidence for a claim and one’s having evidence for that claim.)

    Understood in this way, all B requires is that the warrant hasn’t ceased to exist during the transmission of belief from speaker to hearer. But if it is possible for the warrant possessed by the speaker to disappear, it’s also possible for one to have the same justification for accepting the speaker’s testimony even though the speaker’s warrant has ceased to exist. But then the conjunction of A and B is inconsistent. And note that the inconsistency could be removed just by replacing the “iff” in principle A with “only if”.
