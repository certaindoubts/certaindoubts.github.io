]]> By: Sandy Goldberg http://certaindoubts.com/the-perils-of-in-house-reviewing/#comment-972 Thu, 11 Nov 2004 20:15:16 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=182#comment-972 Talk of increasing warrant isn’t in any of the principles as stated, but it is in Faulkner’s understanding of ‘warrant’. Proof by quotation: “Let me define the extended warrant of a proposition presented by testimony as the conjunction of the warrant possessed by the original speaker for believing this proposition TOGETHER WITH ANY FURTHER WARRANT PROVIDED BY THE CHAINS OF COMMUNICATORS. … Our justifications for acceptance, as it were, refer to the FURTHER, MORE COMPLETE WARRANTS that we take to support our testimonial beliefs” (p. 591). So I think I’m not adding anything to his view by talk of “increasing warrant”.

Now you note that as long as warrant is not lost from speaker to hearer, then all that a hearer needs, in order for her testimonial belief to be warranted, is that she accepted (whether with reasons or not) a piece of testimony which itself was warranted. Your point appears to be: as long as warrant isn’t lost in the communication, then the hearer is warranted — even when she’s not justified in accepting the testimony. In which case we have a problem for (A), since the biconditional doesn’t hold.

My sense is that Faulkner would take his reductionism to entail the following: one’s testimonial belief is not warranted (at all) unless one is justified in accepting the testimony. So he would deny that merely accepting warranted testimony gives warrant to one’s testimonial belief. As it were: you have to ENTITLE yourself to the testimony’s warrant, and this you do by being justified in accepting it. If you’re not justified in accepting the testimony, then even though the testimony itself is warranted, your testimonial belief does not inherit that warrant. This is a very standard reductionist position to take; Lizzie Fricker takes the same view in her recent writings (see esp. her paper in the forthcoming Lackey/Sosa volume THE EPISTEMOLOGY OF TESTIMONY), and I also note this possibility in my contribution to that volume.

In sum, given that Faulkner accepts the reductionist thesis, that one is justified in accepting testimony iff one has positive (undefeated) reasons for regarding the testimony as credible, he has reason to reject your thesis that the mere acceptance of warranted testimony gives one’s testimonial belief (some) warrant. And without that thesis, I don’t think the difficulty you raise arises.

]]> By: jon kvanvig http://certaindoubts.com/the-perils-of-in-house-reviewing/#comment-971 Thu, 11 Nov 2004 19:28:51 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=182#comment-971 Sandy, Faulkner makes the point that it is wholly implausible to think that justification exhausts warrant, and you can’t make that point without taking the ‘any’ in “any type of positive epistemic evaluation” to be merely an existential quantifier. But I think the more I reflect on principles A and B, the fuzzier all of this is getting.

Note that your talk of increasing warrant isn’t in either of Faulkner’s principles. All B says is that if your testimonial belief is warranted, it’s content is warranted by whatever warranted the speaker in the first place. This warrant of content is not one the holder of the testimonial belief has, since in the ordinary case, you’re not privy to the speaker’s basis for assertion. But that’s a problem, since you have propositional warrant for a claim only when you possess the warrant. In the case of evidential warrant, the content of your belief is not warranted simply because sufficient evidence exists to belief it–you have to have the evidence in order for the content to be warranted by that evidence. (There’s also the further stage of basing your belief on the evidence in order to have doxastic warrant, but that’s not relevant to the present point about the distinction between there being evidence for a claim and one’s having evidence for that claim.)

Understood in this way, all B requires is that the warrant hasn’t ceased to exist during the transmission of belief from speaker to hearer. But if it is possible for the warrant possessed by the speaker to disappear, it’s also possible for one to have the same justification for accepting the speaker’s testimony even though the speaker’s warrant has ceased to exist. But then the conjunction of A and B is inconsistent. And note that the inconsistency could be removed just by replacing the “iff” in principle A with “only if”.

We agree also that justification is a species of positive epistemic evaluation. So if “any” in “any species …” is the existential quantifier, then to be justified is to be warranted.

You write that “Faulkner needs a universal, not an existential, quantifier here”. I guess I don’t see why. My sense is that what he’s after, and what’s implicit in his use of “warrant”, is a single term under which to group all of the epistemically-relevant support (or grounds) a belief has. The more support (or grounds), of any of the various kinds of support, the more Faulkner-style warrant one has. If this is the view, then one has warrant (to some degree) whenever one has some kind of epistemically-relevant support — emphasis on SOME.

Finally, you write that “if we change the view so that principle B is read as involving only the existential quantifier, it is completely redundant, and so there is no hybrid position left at all.” I don’t see this either. Principle (B) is the warrant condition. It say in effect that you inherit the warrants that are upstream (in the communication process) from you, that these warrants go towards increasing your warrant. This holds even if we say that all that you need to be warranted (to some degree or other) is one type of positive epistemic evaluation. The point of the upstream warrants is not to warrant you (this you already are if you are justified in accepting the testimony) but to INCREASE the warrant your belief has. I don’t see how (B), understood in this way, is redundant. Can you clarify?
