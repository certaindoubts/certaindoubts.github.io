]]> By: Gordon Barnes http://certaindoubts.com/a-little-meta-theory-on-sellars-problem/#comment-134 Thu, 24 Jun 2004 18:00:11 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=29#comment-134 Ok, fair enough. Here is one more argument, and then I’ll quit. This argument will highlight a particular instance of Sellars’ problem, with respect to introspection. If I am sensing redly, then I can be justified in believing that I am sensing redly. Moreover, it is the experience itself, the event of sensing redly, that justifies me in believing that I am sensing redly. Now, here comes a particular instance of Sellars’ problem. Either my experience of sensing redly has the semantic content that I am sensing redly, or it does not. If it does not have this content, then it cannot justify my belief that I am sensing redly, since the experience does not even “claim” that I am sensing redly. But my experience DOES justify my belief that I am sensing redly. Therefore my experience must have the semantic content that I am sensing redly.

Maybe this is really just to say that, if you don’t like my solution, then I think that you are still stuck with Sellars’ problem.

I think that your reply proves too much. If we agree that experiences have semantic content (correct?), then it must be possible for a belief to contradict an experience. Generally speaking, to have semantic content implies having truth-conditions, and thus a truth-value, and if an experience has a truth value, then a belief can have the opposite truth value, in which case that belief would contradict the experience. However, what you say above is that beliefs never really contradict experiences per se; rather, a belief can only contradict the FACT THAT an experience is occuring. But if you agree that experiences have semantic content, then you don’t want to say that. If you agree that experiences have semantic content, then you must concede that it is possible for a belief to contradict the very content of an experience, and not just the fact that the experience is occurring.

Nonetheless, I can certainly understand if my latest argument for the view doesn’t convince. I still think it a very large advantage of this view that it solves Sellars’ problem. Other solutions really seem to dismiss the problem, rather than solve it, whether they want to admit it or not.

]]> By: Gordon Barnes http://certaindoubts.com/a-little-meta-theory-on-sellars-problem/#comment-127 Wed, 23 Jun 2004 16:40:50 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=29#comment-127 I’m glad that you raised this question. I have an argument for the claim that experiences are only self-representational. If I have an experience as of something red (I sense redly), but I believe that I am not sensing redly, then my belief contradicts my experience. I do not just mean that my belief contradicts the fact that the experience occurs. I mean that my belief that I am not sensing redly contradicts the content of the experience itself. If a belief that Not-P contradicts an experience, then that experience must have the semantic content that P. So my experience when I sense redly must have the semantic content that I am sensing redly. However, suppose that I know that my sensation is caused by a drug that I have taken, and that there is nothing in my external environment that is either the cause or the object of my sensation. Then I think I would not be contradicting myself to believe that I am not sensing anything external to myself. But if this belief (that I am not sensing anythinge external to myself) does not contradict my experience alone, then my experience must not represent anything external to myself.

That’s my argument for this seemingly-crazy view.
