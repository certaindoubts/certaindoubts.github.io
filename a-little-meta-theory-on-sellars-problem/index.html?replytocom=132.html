A Little Meta-Theory on Sellars’ Problem

Posted on June 21, 2004 by Jon KvanvigJune 20, 2012

I’m dissatisfied with the usual metatheory of distinguishing internalism and externalism, and want to try out a slightly different way of thinking about what I think is the same issue, but in response to Sellars’ problem. My thoughts are on the vague and inchoate side at this point, so I’m hoping for some input here…

In a nutshell, Sellars’ Problem concerns the relationship between experience and belief. Sellars wanted to know how experience could justify belief, and there are two options here that reveal the nature of the problem. Either experiences have semantic content or they do not. If they do not, they cannot make the truth of a belief intelligible in the way justification is supposed to make the truth of a claim understood and seen intellectually. If these experiences do have propositional content, then that content can be either appropriate or inappropriate to the context in question, in which case such contents are themselves epistemically evaluable, and a regress problem looms. The Sellars’ Problem is, thus, a problem arising from the question of whether experiences have semantic content.

Some quite common approaches to the problem are utter failures. It won’t help, for example, to say that experience doesn’t justify belief, but only prods belief. If we suppose that there are some beliefs that are directly caused by experience, the question remains whether those beliefs are justified, and if so, how. The standard reply here is coherentist in nature: they are justified by their relation to an entire system of beliefs. This reply avoids Sellars’ problem, but only at the expense of creating another. For if such beliefs are justified solely by their relation to a system of beliefs, they’d still be so justified in the absence of the experiences or in the presence of quite different experiences.

It will not do, either, so say that it is only our beliefs about our experiences that form the beginning point for justification. If I believe that my experience is of a red apple, that may justify my believing that there is a red apple on the table, but only if my original belief is appropriate to my experience. Such an account is psychologically implausible–most of us don’t normally form beliefs about the character or content of our experiences–but even apart from that point, the view simply doesn’t address Sellars’ question about how experience makes certain beliefs appropriate in a given context.

These approaches, common as they are in the history of epistemology, give cause for concern. Their failure calls for a reflective distancing of ourselves from the fray of epistemological theorizing. They call us to meta-theory to try to figure out what can be done to assuage our discomfort in the face of Sellars’ problem.

As I see it, the meta-theoretical options that have developed are these. One option is to jettison the presupposition in my characterization of Sellars’ problem that the notion of justification (or whatever notion is thought to close the gap between true belief and knowledge) is connected with the notions of intelligibility, insight into truth, and the cognitively understood, seen, or grasped. On such an approach, it is better to characterize the cognitive machine more on the model of inputs and outputs, where the outputs are beliefs and the inputs can be whatever the particular theorist wishes to hold generates epistemic value for beliefs. Intelligibility and understanding are jettisoned, and the new language uses notions such as reliability of the mechanism, proper functioning of the system, and production by systems that get us to the truth at least most of the time.

The other approach refuses to give up on the notions of intelligibility and understanding in the theory of justification, hoping to find some way in which experience can play a role in justification without succumbing to Sellars’ problem. The difference between this approach and the above approach is this: the present approach tries to solve Sellars’ problem, granting that it is a problem that must be solved, while the above approach denies a presupposition of the problem and thereby holds that there is no problem here to be solved. The latter approach holds that what is thought by the former camp as a distinctive feature of rational beings–the sense of intellibility and understanding when rationality is displayed–is nothing more than an occasional (or perhaps more general) artifact of cognition. Even if this aspect were a universal accompaniment of displays of rationality, it is a mere epiphenomenon of the material which it is the job of epistemological theory to characterize properly, on one view, and it is the substance of epistemological theorizing itself on the other.

Thoughts?

  4. Gordon Barnes on June 21, 2004 at 8:54 pm said:

    Jon,
    You want to be careful not to build the problem of the external world right into Sellars’ problem. There are two, distinct problems here: (1) how can experience justify belief if it has/lacks semantic content, without itself needing justification; (2) how could experience justify a belief about an external object. A solution to the first problem need not solve the second problem as well. For what it’s worth, here is my own suggestion concerning Sellars’ problem. I think that conscious experience has semantic content, but what it represents isn’t the external world. Rather, conscious experiences are SELF-representational. To experience redly is, ipso facto, to represent oneself as experiencing redly. The self-representation of a conscious experience is not a second-order belief about the experience. Rather, the self-representation of a conscious experience is constitutive of that very experience–it is “built-in” to the experience. Now, here is how this solves Sellars’ problem: experiences have semantic content, but since this semantic content is self-representational, it is infallible, and hence this semantic content is self-justified, because it literally represents ITSELF. I think that this is one way of developing the idea that Chisholm used to express by saying that some states are “self-presenting,” though Chisholm never spelled it out the way that I’m doing here.

    Finally, this won’t solve the problem of the external world, but I don’t think it’s fair to expect a solution to Sellars’ problem to solve the problem of the external world as well. Surely this solution to Sellars’ problem must be COMPATIBLE WITH a stance on the problem of the external world, but I think that this solution to Sellars’ problem is compatible with a variety of proposals on the problem of the external world.

  12. Gordon Barnes on June 23, 2004 at 11:40 am said:

    I’m glad that you raised this question. I have an argument for the claim that experiences are only self-representational. If I have an experience as of something red (I sense redly), but I believe that I am not sensing redly, then my belief contradicts my experience. I do not just mean that my belief contradicts the fact that the experience occurs. I mean that my belief that I am not sensing redly contradicts the content of the experience itself. If a belief that Not-P contradicts an experience, then that experience must have the semantic content that P. So my experience when I sense redly must have the semantic content that I am sensing redly. However, suppose that I know that my sensation is caused by a drug that I have taken, and that there is nothing in my external environment that is either the cause or the object of my sensation. Then I think I would not be contradicting myself to believe that I am not sensing anything external to myself. But if this belief (that I am not sensing anythinge external to myself) does not contradict my experience alone, then my experience must not represent anything external to myself.

    That’s my argument for this seemingly-crazy view.
