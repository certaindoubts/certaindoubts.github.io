  8. Branden Fitelson on February 1, 2006 at 3:11 am said:

    I’m puzzled by Jon’s example. It’s not true that (p –> q) & ~q entails Pr(-p)=0. First, (p –> q) & ~q entails ~p. So, if anything, (p –> q) & ~q is conclusive evidence for ~p. But, it does not follow from this that (p –> q) & ~q entails that ~p has ANY probability. We need to be careful not to conflate

    (1) p entails q [hence Pr(q | p) = 1]
    (2) p entails Pr(q) = 1.

    (1) and (2) are different. Just let p = q. Then, Pr(q | p) = 1. But, p does not entail that p has probabilty 1 (it only implies that p is actually true). And, if p has probability less than 1, then it’s even false that p -> Pr(p) = 1.
