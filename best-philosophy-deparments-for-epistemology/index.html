Best Philosophy Departments for Epistemology

Posted on October 13, 2004 by DeRoseOctober 13, 2004

It’s that time of year: Seniors interested in graduate school are deciding which programs to apply to. Some of them are interested in which are the best departments for certain areas of philosophy. Perhaps because of some material I have on-line, every year I get some e-mails asking about the best programs for epistemology. Rather than answering lots of e-mails, I’ve taken to putting some of my thoughts on the topic on my on-line Epistemology Page (section 3). But maybe other have comments, either about what I say, or directly about the question. I’ll paste what’s on my E-page here, below the fold. (That way, those who find such discussions distasteful can avoid most of it.)

So here is the “update” I recently added:

UPDATE 10/6/04: The new edition of the Philosophical Gourmet Report should be out in not too long, and I’ll wait for that before really over-hauling this section. In the meantime, though, just a few comments about how things have changed since I wrote the below, in light of the moves listed at the bottom of this section. All the moves listed are significant, but in virtue of various circumstances (mostly having to do with who else is in the relevant departments), a few of them have a substantial impact on which are the the top programs.

Jason Stanley’s work has been more focused on philosophy of language than on epistemology, but his recent work in epistemology is excellent. Add him to Rutgers, and especially in light of the facts that John Hawthorne has just put out an important book in epistemology (Knowledge and Lotteries) and that the main epistemological competition for Rutgers has taken significant hits, and I think Rutgers is now pretty clearly the best program for epistemology, not merely edging out the competition (as I suspected was already the case before the recent changes), but opening up a bit of a gap between themselves and their nearest competition. Oxford and NYU are still excellent, but Oxford’s loss of Brewer and NYU’s loss of Peacocke do hurt. At NYU, much depends on how much to count Crispin Wright, who is there only part-time. (I see from my PGR survey that Wright is listed there as being only quarter-time at NYU.)

Moving up: Princeton was already a good choice in epistemology, with Harman and Pryor. Though he appears to be untenured, Thomas Kelly seems to be an excellent young epistemologist, and his addition to Princeton, by giving them a certain critical mass in epistemology, may be a significant help there. Kelly’s move helps Princeton more than it hurts Notre Dame, it seems to me, because ND has a good-sized group of epistemologists even without Kelly, and so doesn’t face the same “critical mass” issue. So I don’t see Notre Dame falling much because of this. I should add, however, that there seems to be a serious possibility that Pryor will be leaving Princeton to go to NYU, in which case the net effect of the changes will be Princeton dropping quite substantially in epistemology, rather than nudging up a bit.

With the addition of Bealer, and also of Jonathan Dancy, Texas looks very strong.

And here are the main comments, which the above updates:

From time to time I am asked, mostly by philosophers who have an undergraduate student who is interested in going on to do graduate work in philosophy, which are the best departments to go to for epistemology. For what it’s worth, here are my thoughts on the subject.

A good resource for anyone interested in graduate work in philosophy is Brian Leiter’s Philosophical Gourmet Report. Particularly helpful is Leiter’s “Breakdown of Programs by Specialties” which, for many particular areas of philosophy, including epistemology, evaluates the top departments that are strong in that area as “excellent”, “good”, or “also notable”. His ranking of the best departments in epistemology is helpful. In the most recent (2002-2004) PGR, Leiter lists (* indicates non-U.S. program; within each category, departments are listed alphabetically):

-NYU, Oxford*, and Rutgers as “excellent” in epistemology;

– Brown, Princeton, Arizona, Notre Dame, Rochester, UWashington (Seattle), and Yale as “good”;

-and he lists the following as “also notable”: Arizona State, Columbia, Indiana (Bloomington), Johns Hopkins, Loyola University (Chicago), Ohio State, UC-Berkeley, UCLA, UC-Santa Barbara, UColorado-Boulder, Iowa, UMass-Amherst, Melbourne*, Miami, Missouri-Columbia, Nebraska, North Carolina, Oklahoma, Pittsburgh, Reading*, Sheffield*, and St. Andrews*.

The PGR epistemology listings are now much more in line with my own opinions than they were in past years. (I am on the advisory board of the PGR, so I do have some input on the listings. Still, I’m just one voice among several, and it’s quite possible for my own opinions to diverge from the final verdict reached at the PGR.) This is especially so about the very top of the list, which I think the PGR gets just right now. At least in the English-speaking world (and beyond that I’m not qualified to comment), the three programs the Leiter lists as “excellent” do seem to me to stand above the rest in epistemology. Unsurprisingly, those three — NYU, Oxford, Rutgers — are also among the very best over-all programs in philosophy in the English-speaking world. For prospective epistemology students, that’s both good news and bad. Good because it’s important to go to a program with good over-all strength, and not just one good in your own area of specialization. Bad because, being among the very top programs, they are no doubt highly selective in admissions and therefore tough to get into.

Boasting several very prominent epistemologists on its faculty, Rutgers is probably now the best program in epistemology. Alvin Goldman, John Hawthorne, Peter Klein and Stephen Stich are full-time at Rutgers, and Ernest Sosa is splitting time between Rutgers and Brown, and seems to be quite available to graduate students at both schools. Additionally, and importantly, Rutgers also has several excellent faculty members who, though epistemology is not their main area of research, have done or do good work in the area: Brian Loar, Barry Loewer, Colin McGinn, Brian McLaughlin. A graduate student could easily put together an outstanding dissertation committee for a wide variety of dissertation topics in epistemology at Rutgers.

Oxford University features two epistemologists who have written two of the best and most important books in the area to come out in recent years: Timothy Williamson (Knowledge and Its Limits, Oxford UP, 2000) and Bill Brewer (Perception and Reason, Oxford UP, 1999). Also at Oxford is Ralph Wedgwood who has written some excellent papers in epistemology. This is an impressive team. Update: Brewer has now accepted an offer from the University of Warwick.

NYU‘s department contains three excellent epistemologists, Paul Bogghossian, Richard Foley, and Christopher Peacocke. (Foley is serving as a Dean at NYU, as we was at his previous job at Rutgers, as well as being a member of the philosophy department, so there is significant competition for his time and energy.) But NYU also has some other outstanding philosophers who, though epistemology might not be among their current main area of research, would be very good to work with there. Most notably, Peter Unger, though he now works mainly in other areas, used to be primarily an epistemologist (and an excellent one, too). And there are several other faculty members at NYU whom I think it would be very exciting to work with in epistemology. Hartry Field’s important work often takes him deeply into epistemological territory, and he could be an excellent guide to some of this territory. And, though they don’t list epistemology as one of their areas (at least on NYU’s faculty web page), Stephen Schiffer and Thomas Nagel have each done some fairly recent work there. (I should divulge that I may be biased in favor of NYU: My first job was there, and I know some of the people there very well. Still, I think my advice here is sound.) Update: Peacocke has now accepted an offer from Columbia University.

After those three, several of the other programs Leiter lists would also be good choices. More detailed opinions here are available upon e-request to prospective graduate students who really face a choice here. When I look at the programs listed as “good” (other than Yale, about which I shouldn’t pronounce due to bias), I guess I’d pick Brown as the best of the goods. But in choosing among the “good”, “also notable,” and programs not listed in the PGR, much will depend, of course, on how well your approach, style, and interests match up with the faculty at the various programs. On that score, you might do well to read some of the published papers of the relevant faculty, and find someone whose work interests you. As a start, you can check out the depatments’ and individuals’ web sites, to which I’ve provided some links (in this section, for the departments, and below for many of the individual epistemologists).

Hope this is of some help, future colleagues in epistemology. Remember that it’s just one epistemologist’s opinion. Talk to your adivsors about it.

Epistemologists on the Move: Potential graduate students interested in epistemology should take note of these upcoming moves of epistemologists:

-George Bealer of the University of Colorado has accepted an offer from the University of Texas
-Bill Brewer at Oxford University has accepted an offer from the University of Warwick.
-Anthony Gillies at Harvard University has accepted an offer from the University of Michigan.
-Mark Heller of Southern Methodist University has accepted an offer from Syracuse University
-Robin Jeshion of Yale University has accepted an offer from the University of California, Riverside
-Thomas Kelly at the University of Notre Dame has accepted an offer from Princeton University.
-Christopher Peacocke at New York University has accepted an offer from Columbia University.
-Jason Stanley at the University of Michigan has accepted an offer from Rutgers University
-Brian Weatherson at Brown University has accepted an offer from Cornell University.

    One factor that ought to play a stronger role in thinking about where to go to grad school is the quality of the training that occurs in the program. Over the course of my career, a few departments stand out in this regard, though the evidence here is admittedly anecdotal, but UMass, Syracuse, Arizona, and Brown are at the top of my list of departments that train philosophers exceptionally well. Of these, only Syracuse doesn’t make Leiter’s list (I assume because Alston had retired), but with the addition of Heller, maybe they deserve to be thought about for epistemology as well.

    This point is, to my mind, vastly underrated by potential graduate students, and the Leiter report, focused as it is on faculty quality, encourages such underestimation of importance (even if we grant general reliability of the report). I’m a fan of the Leiter report, but potential graduate students need to know which star faculty are known for accessibility and which aren’t, and which are known for strong training abilities and which aren’t.

    I’m not sure how deep the disagreement is here, but I think it’s clear that Keith and I see things differently. I think deciding which schools to apply to is a function of several things, two of which are faculty quality and quality of training. I’d probably say the first should be weighted more than the second. But my concern here is that quality of faculty ought to correlate with the kind of job one first gets out of graduate school (or at least, it will correlate with perceived faculty quality), but getting tenure and succeeding in one’s research career ought to be more a function of quality of training. I’ve no data to this effect, only educated guesses, but I would say that there are some first-rate programs with reputations for undercutting the prospects of success by their graduates–with the proviso that this reputational generalization is not exceptionless, as Keith himself demonstrates–and others with the opposite reputation. Students with this information might reasonably go to a school 15-20 notches down on the Leiter report to avoid a program that behaves in this way. Given this point, and the limited funds students typically have for applying to graduate programs, the quality of training needs to be a factor in deciding which programs to apply to as well as in deciding which offer to accept.
