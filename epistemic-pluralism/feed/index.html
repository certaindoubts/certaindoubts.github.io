]]> By: Jason Stanley http://certaindoubts.com/epistemic-pluralism/#comment-1166 Sun, 02 Jan 2005 18:18:41 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=217#comment-1166 I don’t agree with David’s points, or rather I think that David’s reading is a misunderstanding of what the contextualist anti-skeptical response is (or at least how I was understanding it). On my understanding of the contextualist response to skepticism, it’s a way to explain away the psychological pull of the skeptical argument. The idea is that ordinary people (that is, undergraduates) find the skeptical argument compelling because they are not aware of the context-shifts involved in knowledge attributions. So, the purpose of the semantics is to give a psychological explanation of the force of skeptical arguments, one which, if correct, removes the normative force of such arguments (if the contextualist is right about why ordinary people find the skeptical argument compelling, then the fact that they find it compelling is no evidence at all that it is a sound argument).

The anti-skeptical contextualist believes that the skeptical argument is not sound. So, she needs an account of why we (falsely) believe it to be sound. contextualism comes in at the very same place at which it comes in with the contextualist about (say) the sorites paradox, as an account of why we falsely believe an unsound argument to be sound. In other words, contextualism in epistemology, like contextualism about the sorites paradox, is an answer to what Delia Graff (in her paper “Shifting Sands”), calls “The Psychological Question”. In the case of vagueness, it is ‘If the universally generalized sorites sentence is not true, why were so inclined to accept it in the first place?’ In the case of skepticism, the psychological question is ‘If the skeptical argument is not sound, why were we so inclined to accept it in the first place?’ Contextualism in epistemology is specifically designed as a response to this, nothing more.

Now, what I don’t understand about David’s points is that he seems to be saying that contextualism is irrelevant to epistemology, because it doesn’t answer some fundamental questions in epistemology (such as ‘what is the value of bearing K1 to some propositions?’). But I’m puzzled about why he would think it should address these questions. The semantic apparatus is there to respond to the psychological question; if it’s correct, it explains why we find an unsound argument so compelling. The other epistemological work is done to show that the argument *is* unsound.

You can compare the contextualist point here to the major contribution of Tim Williamson’s vagueness book. I take it that the major contribution of Williamson’s book isn’t the argument that there is a sharp boundary. Rather, the major original move is in Chapter 8, with the margins of error arguments. There, Williamson argues that *even if there were sharp boundaries, we still would think there were no sharp boundaries* (or, more precisely, even if there were sharp boundaries, we couldn’t know what they are — so Williamson is more specifically responding to an epistemological, rather than a psychological concern). So, before Williamson, the major response to epistemicism was ‘Well, if there are sharp boundaries, why is it impossible for us to figure out where they are?’, and Williamson gives an brilliant response to this objection. As far as Williamson’s *arguments* for the sharp boundaries, well, he has some original and interesting ones, but the main force of his book lies in his answer to the above objection to epistemicism.

One could imagine a David* (of vagueness) responding by saying ‘Williamson’s book doesn’t make a contribution to the study of the sorites. Responding to the sorites involves explaining what is unsound about the sorites argument, and there Williamson’s response isn’t really genuinely new or original.’ But surely that position would be absurd. Williamson’s main original contribution in the book is in explaining why we find a false premise in an argument so intuitively compelling. That certainly counts as hard work in philosophical logic on the sorites problem. Similarly, the contextualist seeks to explain why we find an unsound argument in epistemology so compelling. Surely, that is hard epistemological work, which, whether successful or not, is a great contribution to epistemology and our understanding of the force of skeptical arguments.

Maybe Moore was right about why the skeptical argument is unsound (i.e. maybe there is no really deep interesting answer to why the skeptical argument is unsound). Then, all the work has to go into explaining why we falsely believe it to be sound. If that’s correct, the contextualism is one of the deepest attempts to respond to skepticism.

(I am not a contextualist, and don’t agree with much of the contextualist response to skepticism — but I also am deeply perplexed by the ‘it isn’t really epistemology’ line of criticism).
