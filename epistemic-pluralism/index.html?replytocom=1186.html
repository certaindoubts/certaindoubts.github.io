Epistemic Pluralism

Posted on December 28, 2004 by Kvanvig JonDecember 28, 2004

Philosophers can be, at times, inordinately dense. I’m in that state now, I fear. It’s engendered by considering the interesting email exchange copied to me by Jonathan Schaffer and David Sosa. The discussion is a follow-up to a previous post here about Ernie Sosa’s charge that contextualism is not a theory of knowledge, and Keith DeRose’s granting the point while rebutting its negative connotations for contextualism.

A central issue in the discussion is whether contextualism is evidence for (without entailing) a position they call epistemic pluralism. At first blush, there are three positions here: epistemic eliminativism, which says there are no knowledge relations; epistemic monism, which says there is just one knowledge relation; and epistemic pluralism, which says there are many knowledge relations.

Apoplexy befalls me at these characterizations. I’m inclined to think I’m just being dense here, but let’s see.

The issue between Sosa and contextualists, as I would put it, is about the connection between a metaepistemological viewpoint (contextualism) and various epistemological viewpoints. And I take the response by Keith to grant that contextualism is only a metaepistemological view but to insist that it has implications for, or constitutes evidence for or against, certain epistemological viewpoints.

I’m not sure how to reconcile this understanding with the above chacterizations of eliminativism, monism, and pluralism. On the understanding above, these terms should name various epistemological positions, not metaepistemological positions, and I’m not sure they do. First, here’s how I understand the eliminativist position that there are no knowledge relations. It’s like emotivism in ethics: people use the word ‘know’ and its variants, but no content is ever expressed by such language. But then eliminativism is a metaepistemological position, not an epistemological one. Is there a way to explain the view that makes it an epistemological thesis as opposed to a metaepistemological one?

If we think of eliminativism in the philosophy of mind, we can be misled into thinking that epistemological eliminativism is obviously an epistemological rather than metaepistemological position. That’s because eliminativism about beliefs, for example, is really a first-order claim, not a meta-theoretical claim. But things and properties (relations) are different in precisely this way. It’s a first order claim to deny the existence of knowledge (that’s skepticism), but I don’t see how it’s anything but a bit of metaepistemology to deny that there is a knowledge relation.

Since the same points apply fairly straightforwardly to monism and pluralism, I’ll cut to the chase. Either I’ve mischaracterized the dispute, or am misunderstanding three positions in question. I can’t tell which…

    Here’s an argument that might have the properties Jason mentioned. “‘S knows that P’ is usually false in high-standard contexts, but it’s often true in ordinary contexts, so the skeptical challenge is answered.” (I don’t claim that this is an actual contextualist argument, but it’s at least an argument-parody.) I think this sort of argument is uncompelling, unless we’re clear on just what epistemic relation K1 (between subjects and propositions) is expressed by ‘know’ in the low-standard contexts.

    For example, if it turns out that K1 is just true belief (nothing turns on whether this is true, though I take it that it’s not out of the question that at least some uses of ‘know’ work this way), then the premises may be true, but the conclusion is plausibly false. In this case, the skeptic will reasonably hold that the truth of ‘S knows that P’ in ordinary contexts counts for nothing in answering the skeptical challenge: true belief is just irrelevant to that challenge. Or if you think that true belief is pretty cool, substitute K1 = confident belief.

    If this is right, it looks like we need to know what K1 is in order to assess the anti-skeptical force of the claim that ordinary utterances of ‘S knows that P’ are true (and also in order to assess the truth of the claim, of course). But now it looks like we’re back to a fairly familiar pre-contextualist dialectic. An anti-skeptic (let’s make them an epistemic pluralist who’s not hung up on the word ‘know’) might hold that there are multiple epistemic relations, K1 and K2, and that while we don’t stand in K2 (say, certainty) to many propositions, we do stand in K1 (say, justified true belief) to many propositions, and standing in K1 is good enough to remove skeptical worries. In response, the skeptic will either deny that we stand in K1 to many propositions (e.g. when K1 is justified true belief), or will deny that K1 is enough to remove skeptical worries (e.g. when K1 is true belief, or when K1 is truth in all relevant alternatives where the skeptical alternatives are stipulated to be irrelevant). I think that once the contextualist is clear about their K1 and K2, we’re in more or less the same situation. But now it looks like the semantics of ‘know’ is largely epiphenomenal to the anti-skeptical point, which really turns entirely on the status of the relation K1.

    I take it that this isn’t a million miles away from Sosa’s point. It also connects to the recently-discussed point about the value of knowledge. If much of the force of skeptical worries is that they threatens to remove something that we value, then much of the force of this sort of anti-skeptical reply turns on whether K1 can deliver what we value.

    This comment is a bit orthogonal to the issue of this thread, but I think it pertains to issues raised about ‘metaepistemology’ in many of the threads on this blog. Professor Kvanvig writes:

    ‘I also recognize your point: who the hell cares about boundaries like this? So I face a perpexity: what is it that is bothering Ernie?     But Kantianism and utilitarianism are positions about the correct fundamental normative principle of morality, not metaethical theories about the semantics, metaphysics, and epistemology of moral judgments. The Kantian argues that all of our correct moral judgments about right action can be derived from the categorical imperative or the principle of humanity, while the utilitarian argues that all of our correct judgments about right action can be derived from the principle that pleasure and only pleasure ought to be maximized. Now these positions may or may not be in conflict, but neither position says anything metaethical about the semantics, metaphysics, or epistemology of moral judgments (although of course, philosophers who are utilitarians and Kantians have taken stances on these issues as well). For example, one can be an expressivist about the metaethical questions and accept a utilitarian or Kantian position about which is the fundamental normative principle of morality.

    Similarly, taking an evidentialist or a pragmatist view about justified belief doesn’t commit one to any particular view about the semantics, metaphysics, and epistemology of judgments within normative epistemology such as ‘One ought to believe only that for which one has sufficient evidence’.

    There is thus a point to distinguishing normative ethics from metaethics. Doing so allows us to distinguish inquiries that aim to answer different questions. In a slogan, metaethics aims to tell us what it is to make a normative judgment, not which normative judgments to make.

    Nishi Shah

    How much skeptical distress remains behind? That, it seems, depends on how important it is that we know according to “absolute” standards. If one thinks that is all-important, one will find almost no comfort in the contextualist solution. But for my part, once the skeptical strategy is seen to have no tendency to show that any of my claims to know — except those very rare ones made in settings governed by “absolute” standards — are in any way wrong, and once I start to get a clear look at what it would take to “know” according to the skeptic’s absolute standards, I find the distress caused by my failure to meet those standards to be minimal at best — perhaps to be compared with the “distress” produced by the realization that I’m not omnipotent.

  23. Jason Stanley on January 2, 2005 at 1:18 pm said:

    I don’t agree with David’s points, or rather I think that David’s reading is a misunderstanding of what the contextualist anti-skeptical response is (or at least how I was understanding it). On my understanding of the contextualist response to skepticism, it’s a way to explain away the psychological pull of the skeptical argument. The idea is that ordinary people (that is, undergraduates) find the skeptical argument compelling because they are not aware of the context-shifts involved in knowledge attributions. So, the purpose of the semantics is to give a psychological explanation of the force of skeptical arguments, one which, if correct, removes the normative force of such arguments (if the contextualist is right about why ordinary people find the skeptical argument compelling, then the fact that they find it compelling is no evidence at all that it is a sound argument).

    The anti-skeptical contextualist believes that the skeptical argument is not sound. So, she needs an account of why we (falsely) believe it to be sound. contextualism comes in at the very same place at which it comes in with the contextualist about (say) the sorites paradox, as an account of why we falsely believe an unsound argument to be sound. In other words, contextualism in epistemology, like contextualism about the sorites paradox, is an answer to what Delia Graff (in her paper “Shifting Sands”), calls “The Psychological Question”. In the case of vagueness, it is ‘If the universally generalized sorites sentence is not true, why were so inclined to accept it in the first place?’ In the case of skepticism, the psychological question is ‘If the skeptical argument is not sound, why were we so inclined to accept it in the first place?’ Contextualism in epistemology is specifically designed as a response to this, nothing more.

    Now, what I don’t understand about David’s points is that he seems to be saying that contextualism is irrelevant to epistemology, because it doesn’t answer some fundamental questions in epistemology (such as ‘what is the value of bearing K1 to some propositions?’). But I’m puzzled about why he would think it should address these questions. The semantic apparatus is there to respond to the psychological question; if it’s correct, it explains why we find an unsound argument so compelling. The other epistemological work is done to show that the argument *is* unsound.

    You can compare the contextualist point here to the major contribution of Tim Williamson’s vagueness book. I take it that the major contribution of Williamson’s book isn’t the argument that there is a sharp boundary. Rather, the major original move is in Chapter 8, with the margins of error arguments. There, Williamson argues that *even if there were sharp boundaries, we still would think there were no sharp boundaries* (or, more precisely, even if there were sharp boundaries, we couldn’t know what they are — so Williamson is more specifically responding to an epistemological, rather than a psychological concern). So, before Williamson, the major response to epistemicism was ‘Well, if there are sharp boundaries, why is it impossible for us to figure out where they are?’, and Williamson gives an brilliant response to this objection. As far as Williamson’s *arguments* for the sharp boundaries, well, he has some original and interesting ones, but the main force of his book lies in his answer to the above objection to epistemicism.

    One could imagine a David* (of vagueness) responding by saying ‘Williamson’s book doesn’t make a contribution to the study of the sorites. Responding to the sorites involves explaining what is unsound about the sorites argument, and there Williamson’s response isn’t really genuinely new or original.’ But surely that position would be absurd. Williamson’s main original contribution in the book is in explaining why we find a false premise in an argument so intuitively compelling. That certainly counts as hard work in philosophical logic on the sorites problem. Similarly, the contextualist seeks to explain why we find an unsound argument in epistemology so compelling. Surely, that is hard epistemological work, which, whether successful or not, is a great contribution to epistemology and our understanding of the force of skeptical arguments.

    Maybe Moore was right about why the skeptical argument is unsound (i.e. maybe there is no really deep interesting answer to why the skeptical argument is unsound). Then, all the work has to go into explaining why we falsely believe it to be sound. If that’s correct, the contextualism is one of the deepest attempts to respond to skepticism.

    (I am not a contextualist, and don’t agree with much of the contextualist response to skepticism — but I also am deeply perplexed by the ‘it isn’t really epistemology’ line of criticism).
