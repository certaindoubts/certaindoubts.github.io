The neighborhood semantics employs a three-place closeness relation; the Williamson view gets by with a two-place closeness relation. If the three-place relation doesn’t cause you to balk at neighborhood semantics, I don’t see why the two-place relation is somehow more mysterious or problematic. It perhaps would be if we had a good account of the three-place relation, but we don’t.

No, I don’t think it’s a necessary truth that the grain of sand change renders that world closer than one in which a belief is absent. And my point is that the semantics simply doesn’t guarantee anything here. So you’d have to hope for some metaphysical guarantee, and if you posit one, I’d want an argument. After all, how much of a change it would be for one to fail to have a belief that one actually has varies greatly. For example, the probability that one was dead instead of having any belief at all could be .9999999; in that case, a world where one doesn’t hold the belief in question is, by my lights, more distant than one in which the Sahara has an extra grain of sand. Or suppose you’ve got advanced stage Alzheimer’s disease, having had no belief formations at all for many years, and suddenly through an enormously improbable coalescence of indeterminacies, one believes that someone is at the door (and someone really is). The world where you don’t hold that belief is incredibly close, and I see no reason to think that the one-more-grain-of-sand world is closer.

Note as well that finding a closest world in which the belief isn’t held is only one of the problems for the neighborhood theory. More generally, all that’s needed is to find some cases in which a world where the belief is absent is at least as close as worlds where the belief is still present.

]]> By: Juan http://certaindoubts.com/what-is-safety/#comment-866 Tue, 12 Oct 2004 21:18:45 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=158#comment-866 Jon, the most straigthforward modification of the Lewis/Stalnaker semantics for conterfactuals that allows for false (and not trivially true) subjunctives with actually true antecedents and consequents is as follows. Define the p-neighborhood of the actual world as the set of worlds that starts with the first world where p is true (this can be the actual world) and ends just before the first world where p is false. A subjunctive p -> q is true iff q is true in the p-neighborhood of the actual world. In your case, there is a world where both p and q are false. Let’s call it W17. According to the definition of p-neighborhood, then, no world that is beyond W17 can count as being in the p-neighborhood of actuality, so the safety conditional is not true in your example. And this doesn’t assume that all close worlds are equally close. This, by the way, is pretty much the semantics that Nozick gives in a footnote for his p -> Bp conditional.

]]> By: jon kvanvig http://certaindoubts.com/what-is-safety/#comment-865 Tue, 12 Oct 2004 19:54:15 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=158#comment-865 Juan, I still think there’s a mistake here. Suppose all the following are true: p, q, and ~p counterfactually implies ~q. If we interpret safety according to the standard Lewis/Stalnaker semantics, then the truth of p and q alone imply safety. But suppose we adopt Sosa’s preferred approach, according to which safety means something like this: in close q worlds p is true as well. That claim could be false even though p is true, q is true, and ~p counterfactually implies ~q. All you need is for one of the close worlds to be a world in which q is true and p is false, and that can happen even though there is a closer world in which both p and q are false (as would be required for ~p to counterfactually imply ~q). The only way to avoid this possibility is to assume in addition that all close worlds are equally close, but that assumption doesn’t follow from the assumptions of p, q, and the sensitivity conditional.
