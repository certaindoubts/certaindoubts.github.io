What is safety?

Posted on October 12, 2004 by Kvanvig JonOctober 12, 2004

The notion of safety, to epistemologists in the know, is supposed to be simple: where Bp is the belief that p is true, and p is some proposition, safety is supposed to be the conditional:
Bp → p,
where ‘&#8594’ is the symbol for counterfactual implication.

In describing the safety condition, however, Sosa uses a number of different claims. He says that S’s belief that p is safe iff:
S1: S would believe that p only if it were so that p.
S2: S would not believe that p without it being the case that p.
S3: as a matter of fact, though perhaps not as a matter of strict necessity, not easily would S believe that p without it being the case that p.

The question is: which, if any, of these formulations is properly represented by my canonical formulation of safety above?

If we treat this translation problem along the lines that we teach to logic students, here’s what we’ll get. First, when my ear hears “only if”, I’m inclined to change order and add negations: “It’s raining only if there’s clouds” doesn’t sound like “rain implies clouds”, but sounds instead like, “no clouds, no rain.” And I don’t find the sound changing to my ear just because the sentences are in the subjunctive mood. That makes S1 sound to my ear like:
FS1: ~p → ~Bp.

What about S2? To my ear, ‘without’ is a conjunction-indicator term. “You can’t have rain without clouds” comes out as “it impossible to have rain and no clouds”. Using that guide, S2 would look like:
FS2: ~(Bp & ~p).

Well, that’s got to be wrong, since S2 is formulated subjunctively, and FS2 doesn’t capture that feature. To honor that feature, perhaps we should treat S2 as expressing a conditional. The same could be done for “You can’t have rain without clouds”, in which case we’d say, again, I think, “No clouds, no rain”, with a stronger conditional than the material conditional. On that model, the consequent for a formalization of S2 will be ~Bp, so S2 should be represented by FS1.

Finally, the same point applies to S3, but with an operator to capture the idea of a certain conjunction not easily occurring. I’m not sure this is quite accurate, but let’s suppose that the “not easily” operator is just a probability claim. If we ignore the subjunctive feature of S3, we get:
FS3: ~Pr(Bp & ~p).

And if we honor the subjunctive feature by formulating S3 as a subjunctive conditional, we have to place a probability operator somewhere in FS1. The plausible options are:
FS1a: Pr(~p → ~Bp).
FS1b: ~p → Pr(~Bp).

Both readings include the tendentious claim that ‘easily’ is best understood as a probability operator. Since I’m somewhat a fan of keeping syntax and semantics separate, I will balk at attempts to translate ‘easily’ into the language of possible worlds. If that’s a better direction to go, I think we should be able to find some translation in English that doesn’t simply resort to some preferred semantical apparatus in this way. So, I use the notion of probability since I haven’t been able to come up with a preferred translation in English. Maybe others have better ideas than I on this score, though…

All of these formalizations are interesting proposals concerning the nature of knowledge, and each can plausibly be claimed to be similar to a Nozickian sensitivity requirement on knowledge (one so similar as to be identical!). But none of them are logically equivalent to my canonical formulation of safety. FS1, it’s probabilistic subtypes, and FS3 are obviously not equivalent to it, and FS2 is implied by the canonical formulation but does not itself imply it.

In light of this multiplicity, much more is needed to undermine appeals to safety beyond giving counterexamples to the canonical formulation of it, on the assumption that all of the above formulations are claims along the same theoretical line. But perhaps we should reserve the notion of a safety approach to those formulations that are logically distinct from a sensitivity approach, in which case, not all of the formulations above count as safety approaches.

    Juan, I still think there’s a mistake here. Suppose all the following are true: p, q, and ~p counterfactually implies ~q. All you need is for one of the close worlds to be a world in which q is true and p is false, and that can happen even though there is a closer world in which both p and q are false (as would be required for ~p to counterfactually imply ~q). The only way to avoid this possibility is to assume in addition that all close worlds are equally close, but that assumption doesn’t follow from the assumptions of p, q, and the sensitivity conditional.

    Jon, the most straigthforward modification of the Lewis/Stalnaker semantics for conterfactuals that allows for false (and not trivially true) subjunctives with actually true antecedents and consequents is as follows. According to the definition of p-neighborhood, then, no world that is beyond W17 can count as being in the p-neighborhood of actuality, so the safety conditional is not true in your example. And this doesn’t assume that all close worlds are equally close. This, by the way, is pretty much the semantics that Nozick gives in a footnote for his p -> Bp conditional.
