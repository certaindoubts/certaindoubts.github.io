Journal Horror Stories!

Posted on July 19, 2009 by John TurriJuly 19, 2009

There’s this journal. It’s very well respected — a top five journal by most measures. And there’s this paper of mine. I think it’s good. So I send it in to this journal. Then I wait for a decision. And wait … and wait … and wait …

So after a couple years without getting even an initial decision, I write the journal to pull the paper. Then I wait for a response … and wait … and wait.

So after a couple months they respond and now I can send the paper elsewhere. They apologize, they regret, etc. I’m told this won’t happen again. But then I did something foolish. I sent them a different paper — one of my best. This time I was given a six-month time frame.

“Great,” I think to myself, “there’s no way that will happen again. They’ll feel obliged to get to my paper quickly.”

Think again. After six months, I had heard nothing. So I send an email inquiring. No response. I wait a while; send another email. No response. I wait longer. Still no response. Finally, I wrote, told them I’m pulling the paper, and sent it to another journal ten minutes later.

All told, two papers spent approximately thirty-five months sitting at this journal, waiting to be read … or, actually, waiting to not be read, as it turned out.

As of now, I can’t imagine ever sending another paper to this journal. And this whole business got me seriously wondering whether we should collectively adopt some kind of policy that would sanction double-submission after, say, six months. The second journal would have first right to publish the paper if it wanted (provided it decided within six months!), and the first journal would get to publish it only if the second journal rejected it.

Anyway, that’s my horror story. Care to share yours?

  24. admin on July 21, 2009 at 12:30 pm said:

    One other point worth noting on the issue of comments from referees. In some cases, lack of comments is laziness, but there are other explanations worth noting. Sometimes the comments are so caustic that no responsible editor would send them along. And other times, the lack of comments signals that the paper is so badly structured that nothing helpful can be reported.

    This latter possibility underlies the philosophical writing course that I teach every fall to incoming grad students. It is quite demanding, but nobody gets out of the course not knowing what a properly structured paper looks like. One of the motivators I use in the course is horror stories of sending a paper to journal after journal and never getting any feedback. In such cases, one needs to get honest feedback from caring friends who are successful in the profession, as to whether one’s work is the sort that referees simply won’t waste their time interacting with. Sad to say, but the percentage of such work that crosses my desk from journals asking for reports is embarrassingly high. In such cases, I still try to say something that might help, but I fully understand the reaction of those who simply won’t waste their time doing so.

    I also recently had a bad experience with Mind: no response for over a year, then when I pestered them some hasty, unhelpful comments from a referee. And I had thought the change of editor would make a difference! CJP was very fast with the same paper.

    Of course a journal can be fast with some papers and slow with others, so it’s risky to generalize from one case or even a few. But having edited a journal for a decade or so, I think a key factor is this: when referees are slow to report, as they often are, how promptly and how persistently does the journal get after them? This is a purely administrative matter, requiring the journal to keep track of which referees’ reports are due when and send reminders when deadlines aren’t met. Well-run journals do this efficiently; badly-run journals don’t. Mind told me their referee was taking a long time, but they obviously weren’t getting after him to take a short time.

    Lastly: I’ve refereed for Nous and they use two stages. First you read the paper and recommend, within a week or two, whether it should be seriously looked at or rejected right away. If the recommendation is to reject, you don’t right more than a sentence or two of comments. If you recommend that it be seriously looked at, you may and often will be one of the serious referees and you’ll write substantive comments. But all this means there can be rejections without comments, if they happen at the first stage. But I think the system is a good one. For one thing, people are much more likely to agree to referee if the first request is for just a keep-looking vs. reject-now decision with only a line or two of comments. And then, if the paper merits it, those people will write more substantive comments.

    A brief response to one of the comments on my remark.

    Saying that journal X does better than journal Y is of no significance, if the comparison is uncontrolled, as these are. A point I was trying to make is that we have to take account of each particular journal’s infrastructure and management if we are to understand how they operate, with whatever strengths and weaknesses they have. So, for instance, it is difficult to compare journals that are still largely print based that are trying to cope financially with the switch to on-line access with journals that are on-line from the get-go. If you have a journal that has traditionally been financed from print-based subscriptions, and which is running a shoe-string operation on that basis, switching to on-line access with potentially a substantially reduced revenue flow may be daunting.

    Having said this, in my view the single major factor accounting for delays in receiving answers from journals is reviewers getting reviews done. If you the reviewer do not get the job done quickly, then the author will not get the answer quickly. That is where the fault lies. Apparently some journals do a better job of hounding reviewers than others. OK, so the style of some journals is to hope that reviewers will be responsible and do their reviews on time, while others start with the presumption that inherently people won’t do them on time, and so have to constantly pestered. So what?

    A couple of remarks on blind reviewing.

    First, it seems to me that if you post a paper on your web-site, then you are willingly letting it be known to the general public that you are the author of the paper. Hence, if you do that do not expect to retain anonymity from reviewers! They are just as much part of the general philosophical public, and you have no reason to expect that they will not know who wrote the paper. (It is irrelevant whether they have googled it or not.)

    Second, I found the point that the editor of the journal should not know who the author is in selecting a reviewer bizarre. The editor should have this information so that they can choose a proper reviewer – e.g. an author may have a contentious history with someone who would not be an appropriate reviewer.
