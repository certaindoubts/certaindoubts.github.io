Since he is necessarily omniscient, he is incapable of believing falsehoods, and thus it is irrelevant to him whether his beliefs are true or not and he can have no value for truth.

]]> By: Jonny Blamey http://certaindoubts.com/606/#comment-3804 Fri, 11 Aug 2006 10:49:42 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=606#comment-3804 Rodrigo, I’m trying to put forward a pragmatic view of belief and truth such as there is a third truth value: irrelevance. A proposition then is true, false or irrelevant. Nothing that you are inquisitive about will be irrelevant. But as you point out in your example, someone’s interest can wane, a chemistry student turned gardener may no longer care whether a certain archane chemical hypothesis is true or false. If the gardener is genuinely no longer inquisitive about chemistry, he will no longer be able to value true chemical propositions. So he falls outside the set of people capable of valuing the hypothesis. Therefore his indifference doesn’t provide a countereaxample to the claim that *anybody* capable of valuing the hypothesis will value it if it is true. He is no longer capable of valuing this particular truth. If the set of people capable of valuing a particular sentence is empty then the sentence could be said to be completely irrelevant, and its truth value questionable. These latter completely irrelevant sentences are often used as counterexamples to excluded middle. Is anyone curious to know whether “this sentence is a lie” is true or false? Or where the exact boundary between red and blue is? Or whether I feel cold mid morning as the room gradually warms up? One may say that philosophers are interested in such questions; but if there was a surefire answer to these questions then philosophers interest would vanish. The test then for whether a sentence is truth apt is to ask “of what interest could it possibly be to anyone whether this sentence is true or false?” Or more succintly “who cares?”. If the answers are none and no one, then there is no value in the truth of this sentence.

There are several ways to go:
1. Minimalist way: “is true” just generalizes over particular instances of truth-schema.
So,the value of “truth” is grounded in value of particular truths for the inquisitive learner.
Believing that echidna is oviparous is valuable for a would-be zoologist, since echinda is oviparous.
Believing that Cetinje is the capital of MOntenegro is valuable for a would-be geographer since Cetinje is (indeed) the capital of Montenegro.
The minimalist says the value of truth is just generalization of all this. See Horwich in the last Nous, No2 2006.

2.I prefer a slightly more substantial line: suppose truth is a substantial higher order property common to all these instances. A functional property, say. Then truth in each particular case will satisfy the particula curiosity. And THIS IS THE GROUND OF ITS VALUE for the curious.
3. Finally, there is the tough correspondence line: Truth=Correspondence
and correspondence is valuable because it “attunes” our beliefs to reality, and the curius value the attunement.

As I said, my favorite is no 2.
