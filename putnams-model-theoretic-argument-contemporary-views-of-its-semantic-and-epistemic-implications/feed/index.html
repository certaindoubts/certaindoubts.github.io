One preliminary question is—which model theoretic argument are we thinking of? The one that uses Loweinheim-Skolem technology is very relevant to contemporary debates about unrestricted quantification—e.g. the Rayo/Uzquiano volume, and various of McGee’s papers, are addressed to it. I guess the idea that (determinately) unrestricted quantification doesn’t work has some genuine advocates, but there are all sorts of upshots (and problems) for maintaining that view.

Then there are other Putnamian arguments, like the permutation stuff from *Reason Truth and History* (which goes back through Davidson, Wallace, Quine, Jeffrey…). That’s likely to have a different kind of impact (and I suppose one trend that’s been influential is the view that this is a paradox, from which we learn stuff about metasemantics—i.e. that the story contains something-or-other that blocks the argument). At least in metaphysics, this gets a lot of airplay as a motivation for the natural/non-natural property distinction, a la Lewis. That’s kind of an indirect contemporary resonance, but a very active one. (Of course, the two puzzles are unrelated, but it’s at least open that we find a way to block permutation based indeterminacy arguments while still having quantifier indeterminacy in place).
