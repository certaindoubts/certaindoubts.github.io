Lewis on Belief and Knowledge

Posted on April 4, 2008 by Kvanvig JonApril 4, 2008

In “Elusive Knowledge,” Lewis says,

Our definition of knowledge requires a sotto voce proviso. S knows that P iff S’s evidence eliminates every possibility in which not-P–Psst!–except for those possibilities that we are properly ignoring.

Like most others, it has always been the “Psst!” qualifier that got my attention here. But, as Ryan Byerly pointed out to me today, the definition leaves open knowing what you don’t believe to be true. Lewis would be fine with that implication, since later in the article he explicitly endorses the idea that knowledge doesn’t require belief, as in the case of a timid student who “knows the answer but has no confidence that he has it right,” citing Woozley and Radford in support.

But the definition has stronger, and worse implications: it doesn’t rule out believing ~P, even being certain of ~P. Even those impressed with cases such as the timid student case shouldn’t be happy about this implication.

  10. kvanvig on April 6, 2008 at 7:49 am said:

    Roger, I don’t think this will work. First, nobody’s irrational because of evidence they don’t have. Rationality or justification is perspectival in just the way needed to rule this out. If rationality requires further inquiry, that is because something about your own perspective on the world tells you it is required.

    But even if we let this issue pass, it still doesn’t address the second point. The second point covers lots of cases, including cases where people believe contrary to evidence they possess.

    On the lack of confidence point, I also think it is a mistake to identify belief that ~P with certainty that ~P. I’m attracted to a Lockean thesis here that requires for belief a degree of confidence above a threshold, but since we are more confident about some things we believe than other things we believe, the threshold can’t be so high as to require certainty for belief.

    Of course, once DL agrees with Woozley and Radford that belief isn’t required, he won’t want to add a belief clause. But maybe he could have added a clause denying belief in the opposite.
