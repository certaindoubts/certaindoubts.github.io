I should say that Jon’s VERY charitable reading is not in response to a worked out paper — just a quick post on Weatherson’s blog. My main idea there is that, though it does seem, as you say, possible to imagine a student defending the claim that the past Europeans knew whether the earth was flat, my experience is that most students are in fact MUCH less willing to defend that claim than they are to defend the claim that the past Europeans knew that the earth was flat. And they are even less willing to say that those Europeans knew the shape of the earth.

So, both of the following conditionals seem true:

If the Europeans knew that the earth was flat, then they knew whether the earth was flat.

If the Europeans knew that the earth was flat, then they knew the shape of the earth.

But it’s much psychologically harder to maintain belief in the consequents than it is to give in and deny the antecedents. Perhaps it’s just a way of teaching students competency with the term “knows”. But you shouldn’t be looking for a fully fledged paper on this, or even an argument on Weatherson’s blog that’s in as good a shape as what Jon attributes to me.

Thanks, by the way, to Jon for making the argument look (to my eyes) so good.

]]> By: kvanvig http://certaindoubts.com/fantls-argument-about-the-factivity-of-knows/#comment-2268 Mon, 01 Aug 2005 20:31:50 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=393#comment-2268 That’s a good point, Steve. Here’s the way the argument is supposed to be understood. We have a case where S knows whether p. That gives us two options if “knows whether” is factive: p is true and S knows that, or p isn’t true and S knows that. If “knows whether” isn’t factive, then you can know whether p by knowing that p is false even though it’s true, or vice-versa.

The point of the argument is that whereas there’s some linguistic data in support of “knows that” not being factive, Jeremy claims that there is none for “knows whether”. That is, take any case where it is reported that S knows that p, but p is false, and ask the reporter “does S know whether p?” Jeremy claims this question gets a negative answer. So the “knows whether” question never yields an answer in which the reporter competently says “yep, he knows whether p” when p is false but the original report was that he knows that p.
