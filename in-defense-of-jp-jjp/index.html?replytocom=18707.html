In defense of (Jp –> JJP)

Posted on September 27, 2011 by Trent DoughertySeptember 27, 2011
I am now more attracted (than ever, not than not) to the idea that when a proposition is justified for one, so is the proposition that that proposition is justified.  This would be a significant change in perspective if it continued (it might even reverse my views on some matters of religious epistemology (reflection upon which brought me to this post)).

Below the fold, I make some clarifications and discuss Greco’s objections to Jp → JJp.  I’m interested in what people take to be the best kinds of proposed counter-examples to the principle, though Williamson’s rejection depends on E=K, and I’d prefer to focus on objections with less controversial bases.

First, I am talking about propositional justification, not doxastic justification: “Jp” is to be read as “The proposition p is justified for S.”

The driving intuition is Chisholm’s: “Whatever justifies a subject in believing is available to the subject to justify her in believing that she is justified in believing.”

It is unfortunate he uses “available” here, because it is ambiguous between “available in principle” or “actually available psychologically.”  The version I want to endorse is the former.

Since I’m interested in the JJ principle as a principle of propositional justification, the objections Greco makes in his very, very interesting (every internalist should read it) “Internalism and Epistemically Responsible Belief” (Synthese 85, 1990) don’t apply (at least not without modification I don’t see).  He objections that (i) most ordinary folk don’t hold beliefs about justification, (ii) people can’t have an infinite number of beliefs, (iii) some further issues about cognitive limitations for a modified principle, and (iv) that a further revised version doesn’t serve the purpose of motivating internalism.

(i) doesn’t apply for propositional JJ, because belief isn’t required, only the possession of evidence is required.  (ii) Doesn’t apply, because my evidence can support infinitely many propositions (without batting an eye lash!).  (iii) doesn’t apply for essentially the same reasons (ii) doesn’t.  (iv) doesn’t apply, because I’m not interested in how such a principle relates to internalism.  I have a philosophy of religion application in mind.

Finally, since I’m inclined to think that (a) the kind of probability relevant to epistemic justification is epistemic probability and (b) that epistemic probabilities do not always obey the probability calculus, the status of JJ may be independent of the following principle.

[Pr(p/E) > k] → Pr([Pr(p/E) > k]) > k

Higher-order probabilities are a mess anyway.

So, all that having been said, what do we think of JJ?

    I’m quite sympathetic to JJ, though I take that to be very much a minority position.

    One strategy for motivating it involves reflecting on claims with the following Moore-paradoxical flavor (I think Michael Huemer discusses such cases, also in defense of a principle in the neighborhood of JJ, in “The Puzzle of Metacoherence”) :

    P but I have no idea whether I’m justified in believing that P.

    It seems (at least to me) like it could never be reasonable/felicitous to utter a claim like the one above. Standard stories about Moore’s paradox can’t explain this infelicity in the same way that they can explain the infelicity of uttering claims like “P, but I don’t know that P”; in the absence of something like JJ, there’s no obstacle to my knowing/having justification for P, while also knowing/having justification for believing that I have no idea whether I have justification to believe that P.

    Examples like this, I think, function similarly to the “Skeptic’s student” example that you mentioned is discussed at Rochester a lot (and which comes up in Rich Feldman’s “Respecting the Evidence.”) While we could keep looking for some broadly pragmatic explanation, I’m inclined to think the right answer involves appealing to something in the neighborhood of JJ. Roughly, the story would say that if one can truly utter a claim like the above, then one either lacks justification to believe that P (and so one is going wrong in believing that P), or one has justification to believe that one has justification to believe that P (and so one is going wrong by failing to take advantage of one’s justification to believe this higher order claim).

  8. Martin Smith on September 28, 2011 at 6:26 am said:

    I’ve wondered for a while whether Williamson’s argument against the KK-principle (in section 5.1 of KAIL) could be adapted for use against a JJ-principle of the sort under consideration here – but I’ve never really thought through the details. Here, very roughly, is how the argument might go:
    Suppose Mr. Magoo is staring out of his window at a tall tree on the other side of his garden. Suppose that, when estimating heights at this distance, Magoo is accurate to within a margin of about 10cm, but can’t discriminate more finely– his eyesight is too poor. Presumably Magoo has justification for the proposition that the tree is taller than 1cm (t1), that it’s taller than 5cm (t5) etc. It’s kind of plausible, in the case described, that if the tree is exactly n+1 cm tall, then, given his limitations, the proposition that the tree is taller than n cm wouldn’t be justified for Magoo. We have the following ‘margin for error’ principle: Jtn -> tn+1. If Magoo is sufficiently aware of his own discriminative limitations, then he could also have justification for every instance of this principle: J(Jtn -> tn+1).
    Suppose, finally, that J satisfies the (K) axiom schema: J(P -> Q) -> (JP -> JQ). This, in effect, amounts to a kind of closure principle for justification. If, in addition to all this, we have the JJ-principle: JP -> JJP then we’ll be able to prove an absurd conclusion – namely, Jtn for any n.
    Both the closure principle and the margin for error principle are no doubt very debatable – but one of these would, I think, have to be rejected if the JJ-principle were to be maintained.

So, on my view, understanding a proof shoudn’t be understood on the model of having a perceptual experience that provides me with new evidence. Rather, I have justification to believe that there is no greatest prime just by virtue of the existence of the proof. But of course my belief that there is no greatest prime is not justified unless it is based on my understanding of the proof. And so understanding the proof puts me in a better position to form a justified belief because it enables me to satisfy the basing condition.

    I think this helps to bring out a disagreement between Trent and me in the way that we’re thinking of evidence and justification as determined by mental states. I accept that evidence and justification supervenes on the mental in the sense that mental duplicates are duplicates with respect to which propositions comprise their evidence and which propositions they have justification to believe. But I deny the stronger thesis that, for every proposition one has justification to believe, one has justification to believe that proposition in virtue of being some mental state. On my view, mental states are justificational difference-makers. But there are certain a priori propositions that everyone has justification to believe whatever mental states they happen to be in.

    So glad this thread is going on, since on Friday at MEW I’m going to be defending (roughly) the thesis that it’s irrational to have false beliefs about the requirements of rationality! That puts me largely on Declan’s side here.

    A couple of comments: I think what Declan and I need is that the a priori propositions in question are indefeasibly justified, not maximally justified. Of course, you might think that in order for something to be indefeasibly justified it must be maximally justified, but you also might not. For example, suppose you thought that (appearances notwithstanding) it’s impossible to get any evidence against certain types of propositions. Then it would be possible for such a proposition to become more justified for an agent but impossible for disbelief in that proposition to ever be justified.

    Second, on standard subjective Bayesian pictures agents are required to have maximal credence in logical truths. But that’s consistent with their degree of justification (in that maximal credence) going up and down. Just like two agents can be rationally required to assign a particular coin flip a 50/50 credence, but one agent might have much more justification than the other for that assignment. I think Bayesianism only prescribes maximal justification in logical truths if “epistemic probabilities” are probabilities in the mathematical sense and those “probabilities” are degrees of justification.

    In fact, it was thinking about (subjective) Bayesian logical omniscience that got me to my thesis about rationality. I think there’s a very tight tie between the way credences in logical truths work within a Bayesian model and the way a priori beliefs about what one is justified in believing work in general.
