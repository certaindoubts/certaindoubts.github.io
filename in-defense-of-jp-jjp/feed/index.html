A couple of comments: I think what Declan and I need is that the a priori propositions in question are indefeasibly justified, not maximally justified. For example, suppose you thought that (appearances notwithstanding) it’s impossible to get any evidence against certain types of propositions. Then it would be possible for such a proposition to become more justified for an agent but impossible for disbelief in that proposition to ever be justified.

Second, on standard subjective Bayesian pictures agents are required to have maximal credence in logical truths. But that’s consistent with their degree of justification (in that maximal credence) going up and down. Just like two agents can be rationally required to assign a particular coin flip a 50/50 credence, but one agent might have much more justification than the other for that assignment. I think Bayesianism only prescribes maximal justification in logical truths if “epistemic probabilities” are probabilities in the mathematical sense and those “probabilities” are degrees of justification.

In fact, it was thinking about (subjective) Bayesian logical omniscience that got me to my thesis about rationality. ]]> By: Declan Smithies http://certaindoubts.com/in-defense-of-jp-jjp/#comment-18713 Wed, 28 Sep 2011 19:33:08 +0000 http://el-prod.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=2997#comment-18713 Jeremy – no, I definitely don’t like the Platonist option! That option might seem forced on me if you’re think of having reasons or justification to believe a proposition as a matter of being in a certain kind of mental state, e.g. representing some information. But while that picture seems apt for a posteriori justification, I don’t think it applies to a priori justification. So, on my view, understanding a proof shoudn’t be understood on the model of having a perceptual experience that provides me with new evidence. Rather, I have justification to believe that there is no greatest prime just by virtue of the existence of the proof. But of course my belief that there is no greatest prime is not justified unless it is based on my understanding of the proof. And so understanding the proof puts me in a better position to form a justified belief because it enables me to satisfy the basing condition.

I think this helps to bring out a disagreement between Trent and me in the way that we’re thinking of evidence and justification as determined by mental states. I accept that evidence and justification supervenes on the mental in the sense that mental duplicates are duplicates with respect to which propositions comprise their evidence and which propositions they have justification to believe. But I deny the stronger thesis that, for every proposition one has justification to believe, one has justification to believe that proposition in virtue of being some mental state. On my view, mental states are justificational difference-makers. But there are certain a priori propositions that everyone has justification to believe whatever mental states they happen to be in.
