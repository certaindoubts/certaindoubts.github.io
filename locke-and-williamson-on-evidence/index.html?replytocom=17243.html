Locke and Williamson on Evidence

Posted on July 19, 2011 by Kvanvig JonJuly 19, 2011

Dani Rabinowicz sent me this idea finding a precursor in Locke of the Williamsonian idea that E=K, asking it to be posted if it would interest readers here:

Many have balked at some of the central claims Timothy Williamson defends in his Knowledge and its Limits. Of particular note in this respect is his claim that “one’s total evidence is simply one’s total knowledge” (p. 9). The claim E=K is part of Williamson’s challenge to the phenomenal conception of evidence. E=K also serves another function—it is central to Williamson’s treatment of justification: “justification is primarily a status which knowledge can confer on beliefs that look good in its light without themselves amounting to knowledge” (ibid.). It is knowledge that does the justifying, which amounts to a radical reversal of the tradition in the post-Gettier period that seeks to analyze knowledge in terms of “justified true belief + …” conditions for knowledge.

It is interesting, then, to note that Plantinga offers us a reading of Locke’s epistemology which seems, on the surface at least, to share some of the above features. In his Warrant: The Current Debate, Plantinga writes (p. 19-20):

“Locke holds that it is my duty to regulate my belief in such a way that I believe only what I have good reasons for, that is, only what is epistemically probable with respect to my total evidence.35 … what makes an act of believing permissible or right is its being appropriately supported by the believer’s total evidence.

Footnote 35: That is, the body of beliefs that are certain for me.”

To my ear that sounds quite Williamsonian. Here’s why. Recall that Locke is traditionally read as being an infallibilist about knowledge. So only those true beliefs of mine that have the property of certainty count as knowledge for me. So, for Locke, “the body of beliefs that are certain for me” = knowledge. One might then paraphrase Plantinga’s reading of Locke like this: According to Locke a belief is justified in proportion to the degree that it is probable on one’s knowledge. (cf. Essay IV: XIV, XV)

Now there are obvious differences between Locke and Williamson. For one, Williamson is a fallibilist about knowledge. And whilst Williamson does accept some norms, e.g. knowledge as the norm of belief (p. 11), it is not clear how that squares with Locke’s deontology. Lastly, Locke’s “internalism” seems opposed to Williamson’s (anti-luminous) “externalism.” These differences may impede any fruitful comparison between Locke and Williamson. Alternatively, such differences may offer new avenues for elucidating their respective epistemologies. Perhaps some of the positions defended by Williamson problematize some aspects of Locke’s epistemology, and vice versa.

In this post I merely wish to draw our attention to another possible defender of E=K and its role in the concept of justification. I recognize that the reading of Locke that Plantinga offers us may be inaccurate, in which case this comparison is doomed. But if E=K indeed has an older and more traditional pedigree than initially thought, then this realization might encourage some to reconsider their response to Williamson.

  2. Declan Smithies on July 19, 2011 at 3:03 pm said:

    I don’t know if this is Locke’s view, but it’s certainly possible to combine a phenomenal conception of evidence with a version of the E=K thesis. Consider a view on which (i) one’s total evidence is the total set of propositions that one knows, or one is in a position to know, with certainty, i.e. epistemic probability 1; and (ii) the only propositions that one knows, or one is in a position to know, with certainty are propositions about one’s phenomenal mental states. Williamson denies (ii), since he thinks that all our knowledge is certain in the sense that it has epistemic probability 1, but he denies the skeptical view that our knowledge is restricted to propositions about our own phenomenal mental states. The question at issue here is whether we are in any better epistemic position with respect to our own mental states than we are with respect to anything else. And, of course, Williamson argues that we’re not.

    Still, it’s quite interesting and perhaps a bit surprising to note that the phenomenal conception of evidence is consistent with E=K. I think the phenomenal conception of evidence is much less easily combined with another thesis that Williamson seems to accept, i.e. factive mentalism: the thesis that facts about which propositions comprise one’s evidence are grounded or determined by facts about one’s factive mental states. On the view sketched above, it’s much more plausible to think that facts about which propositions comprise one’s evidence are grounded or determined by facts about one’s phenomenal mental states, which are presumably non-factive.

  7. Jennifer Nagel on July 20, 2011 at 4:39 pm said:

    Hi Dani,

    The proposal that E=K might be traced back to Locke is an interesting one, although I think considerable textual gymnastics would be required to make it work (on the bright side, I think considerable textual gymnastics are required to make almost anything work in Locke, given the various conflicting remarks on any given topic of interest in the Essay).

    One passage to worry about is 4.15.6, in which Locke lays out the formula for the regulation of probable judgment: ‘as the conformity of our Knowledge, as the certainty of Observations, as the frequency and constancy of Experience, and the number and credibility of Testimonies, do more or less agree, or disagree with it, so is any Proposition in it self, more or less probable.’ Locke is frequently redundant, and indeed the ‘certainty of Observations’ would in his view fall within knowledge, but it would take more work to argue that the rest of that list — especially the way it goes on to be elaborated in 4.16 — fits with a general identification of evidence with knowledge.

    In answer to Jon’s question, yes, Locke does maintain that everything that is certain is known, e.g. at 4.6.3 and 4.15.3-4, although he also says some things about faith that are somewhat hard to reconcile with the notion that knowledge has a monopoly on certainty (for example, that faith “as absolutely determines our Minds, and as perfectly excludes all wavering as our Knowledge itself” 4.16.14). The fact that faith “leaves no manner of room for Doubt or Hesitation” does not necessarily mean it affords us certainty in Locke’s technical sense, but such passages do make us wonder what precisely Locke did mean by “certain”.

  10. William Rowley on July 22, 2011 at 12:50 pm said:

    If you read Locke’s account of probable belief as an analogue of Williamson’s account of justification, you might at least be tempted to think that all the grounds of probable belief must be known, so you get one direction of that biconditional. Wolterstorff interprets Locke this way in his “John Locke and the Ethics of Belief” and what he takes to be a “rational reconstruction” of Locke’s views on rational belief – what Wolterstorff calls the “principle of evidence”: roughly, this is that one’s evidence for forming beliefs should consist in only what one knows (see JL&EB p. 67).

    Wolterstorff’s been taken to task for this by a few critics*, and the Essay seems to support his critics (though maybe not Locke’s later Conduct of the Understanding, which, in fairness, Wolterstorff chiefly cites in support of his interpretation). What they’re zeroed in on is Locke’s inclusion of testimony as a ground for probable belief – note that it’s mentioned in the passage Jennifer cited (4.15.6) and in Locke’s most concise statement of the grounds of probable belief:

    “The grounds of it are, in short, these two following: First, the conformity of any thing with our own Knowledge, Observation, and Experience. Secondly, the Testimony of others, vouching their Observation and Experience.” (4.15.4)

    The problem for interpreting the grounds of probable belief in consisting only in what one knows is that it is hard to see how testimony could fit Locke’s characterization of knowledge (I think this may be part of what Jennifer meant by the difficulties in dealing with the rest of the 4.16.6 quote): first, it’s definitely a fallible source of information, second, it does not appear to fall under any of the three kinds of knowledge Locke recognizes: intuitive (immediate perception of the agreement or disagreement of ideas), demonstrative (stepping by intuitions to perceive the agreement or disagreement between ideas one cannot immediately perceive), and sensitive (sense perception of particulars). If p is in your justifying (or probabilizing) grounds for q because you’ve seen S testify that p, then p must be something you don’t know and your belief that q is justified (or probable) on partially non-knowledge grounds. And that would be a textual counterexample to a Lockean version of E=K.

    * See De Jong and Van Woudenberg “Testimony and John Locke’s Principle of Evidence” 1996 Locke Newsletter 28 and David Owen’s review of John Locke and the Ethics of Belief in the same journal vol 30 1999
