]]> By: Keith DeRose http://certaindoubts.com/epistemic-modals-relative-truth-and-contextualism/#comment-340 Wed, 14 Jul 2004 19:39:33 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=54#comment-340 While I am (unsurprisingly) in basic agreement with Jason here, I do think that Brian is right that epistemic modals behave a bit differently in certain situations of embedding than do other context-sensitive terms.

So, for a contrast with epistemic modals, note how the context-sensitive “tall” behaves in various situations of embedding. As we go, I’ll also note something Brian won’t like to hear: that in these various ways “knows” behaves like “tall.” So, suppose that in a relatively “low-standards” setting, Alice has just said to you, “Ben is tall” or “Ben knows that John was at the office yesterday.” But now you suddenly find yourself in a context governed by markedly higher standards — now, for instance, you’re discussing the 6’5″ Ben as a potential NBA center with some NBA scouts, and you’ve all admitted, “Ben is not tall,” or you’re discussing whether John was at the office with the police, where you’ve admitted that you yourself “don’t know” that John was the office, though you have the same good grounds that sufficed for claiming to know in the low-standards setting you were just in with Alice. (Suppose you & Alice & Ben all heard from the same very reliable source that John was in, and that, in the low-standards context, that was enough for you to ascribe knowledge to all of you. But in your more highly-charged conversational setting with the police, who are looking for witnesses who can definitely place John at the office yesterday, you are saying that subjects, including yourself, “don’t know” that John was in if all they have to go by is such a reliable report; you’d only say that you or they “know” if they had much stronger grounds — if, for instance, the subject in question had themselves seen John at the office. So you are now saying “Ben doesn’t know that John was in,” though, in the lower-standards context, you, like Alice, were saying “Ben knows.”) Even though Alice has sincerely said “Ben is tall” / “Ben knows”, here, in your much more highly-charged contexts, it seems somehow wrong to issue these belief-reports:
“Alice believes that Ben is tall”
“Alice believes that Ben knows that John was in”.
It likewise seems somehow at least a bit problematic to issue these simple said-that-reports:
“Alice said that Ben is tall”
“Alice said that Ben knows that John was in”.
Among the things one clearly can correctly say are the above said-that-reports with an appropriate follow-up:
“Alice said that Ben is tall, but she wasn’t speaking of him as a potential NBA center when she said that”
“Alice said that Ben knows, but she was speaking casually”.
Lots of controversial matters lurk nearby, like whether the things I say above are somehow wrong or problematic would be false if they were inappropriately said or just misleading, what the function of the follow-ups is, etc. But on the surface, at least, things seem the same: the “behavior” of the terms is here very similar. (I discuss such cases at a *bit* more length toward the end of the last section of my “The Ordinary Language Basis for Contextualism…”, on line here: pdf and here: word.)

But — now for the point — notice how very differently epistemic modals can behave. You’ve just heard Alice say, “It’s possible George has that book” — by which she simply means roughly that she doesn’t know otherwise. Now you are talking with Ben, and, as both of you know perfectly well that George doesn’t have the book in question, the two of you are saying it’s “not possible” that George has it. In your context, your modal statements are made relative to a completely different set of people — so there is a very marked difference in this parameter from Alice’s statement. Yet it seems — at least to me — fine to issue even the simple belief report,
“Alice believes it’s possible that George has the book”.

But as Jason points out, we shouldn’t overstate this difference. Sometimes epistemic modals behave like “tall” (and, I’d add, like “knows”) in the ways currently under discussion. Here’s a case that illustrates that — a case where the parameter of content that’s changing is not the simple matter of which people are relevant to a modal claim.

Suppose that it would be very significant if a certain recently discovered fossil is from the Cambrian period. Nobody knows that it isn’t from the Cambrian — or that it is. But tomorrow, it will be subjected to simple test, that can conclusively rule out, but cannot conclusively establish that, it is from the Cambrian: If the results of the test are “negative,” that will conclusively rule out it’s being from the Cambrian, but “positive” results won’t settle it that it is from the Cambrian.

You are speaking with Alice, who doesn’t even know about the test that’s about to be performed, but does know that nobody knows that the fossil isn’t from the Cambrian. The two of you are both saying that “it’s possible that” it’s from the Cambrian, by which you mean only that you don’t know otherwise, or perhaps that nobody knows otherwise.

You leave Alice, and start up a new conversation with Betty, a fellow-scientist who is, like you, very interested in how the test will come out tomorrow. Now, Betty and you can choose to use epistemic modals in a way similar to how Alice and you were speaking earlier — in such a way that nobody’s knowing otherwise is sufficient for the “possibility” of the fossil being from the Cambrian — in which case you’ll simply forthrightly assert that “it’s possible” that it is. But since at least the time of G.E. Moore, it has been recognized that sometimes epistemic modals can be used in such a way that something can be “impossible” even though nobody knows otherwise. In our case, you can exploit that aspect of the context-sensitivity of epistemic modals and speak in such a way that it “isn’t possible” that the fossil is from the Cambrian if it is such that it will yield negative results in tomorrow’s test. Suppose that’s the route you and Betty take, and you say such things as “I can’t believe that they delayed the test until tomorrow. Now we’ll have to wait til then to know whether it’s possible that the fossil is from the Cambrian”, and “I wonder whether it’s possible.” To a colleague who isn’t yet aware that the test has been postponed until tomorrow, and has breathlessly asked whether it’s “possible” that the fossil is from the Cambrian, supposing that you already know the results of the test, you can say, “Sorry, we won’t know whether that’s possible until tomorrow: The test has been postponed.” Notice how all this compares with you earlier talk with Alice, in which you forthrightly asserted that, and thereby represented yourself as knowing that, it is “possible.” Now you’re admitting that you don’t know whether it’s “possible” — though we may suppose you will say, “I suspect it is possible,” because you suspect that the fossil really is from the Cambrian, and therefore that the tests will come out “positive.”

In this conversational setting, it seems somehow wrong to tell Betty, “Alice believes it’s possible that it’s from the Cambrian.” Alice has no opinion about whether it’s from the Cambrian, and doesn’t even know that a test is being run. Her only grounds for saying “it’s possible” were that nobody knows otherwise. In this setting, the epistemic modal behaves like “tall” (and “knows”): It is *somehow* wrong to issue the belief-report, at least seemingly in the same way and to roughly the same degree that it’s wrong to issue the simple belief-reports involving “tall” and “knows”, above. Again, in exactly what way these are wrong — whether they are false or just misleading — is a controversial matter. But the “behavior” is the same.

You’re saying this, I take it, because the idea would be that the contextual index related to ‘might’ would be for a particular knower (this is how I treat epistemic modals in my section on epistemic modals in “Context, Interest-Relativity, and Knowledge”). So, ‘It might rain’ expresses the proposition that it rains is consistent with x’s knowledge. You’re right that if this is how the context-sensitivity works, then my explanation for why we often refer to the character doesn’t work.

But if the way the context-sensitivity of epistemic modals works is that we refer (de re) to the actual state of knowledge of the relevant person, then my explanation would work. For we are often ignorant of the set of propositions known by the agent in question.

So one might wonder how to decide between the two hypotheses. Consider the counterfactual “If I knew somewhat less than I now do, it would be that it might be raining outside”. On the first way of going, this is presumably true, since I am referring only by description to my knowledge-state, the embedded occurrence of “it might be raining outside” would express the proposition that, relative to my knowledge-state in a counterfactual state of the world in which I know less, it is consistent with my knowledge there that it is raining outside. But on the second option, where the contextual index is provided with the set of propositions that I actually know, the embedded sentence is false, and hence the counterfactual is false.

For what it’s worth, my judgement about the truth of the counterfactual “If I knew less than I now do, it would be that it might be raining outside” is that it’s false, which goes with the second of these two theories. If the second of these two theories is correct, then that supports the explanation I gave in the earlier post.

]]> By: Jason Stanley http://certaindoubts.com/epistemic-modals-relative-truth-and-contextualism/#comment-336 Wed, 14 Jul 2004 15:58:23 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=54#comment-336 Brian,
In your appendix, you say that there are two positions you could be taking. On one of them, which is the natural way one interprets you in the text,the referents of ‘that’ clauses determine functions from indices to propositions in the ordinary sense (sets of possible worlds). On this way of interpreting you guys, what you does not strike me as being correct.

The temporalist position about propositions is that the truth of propositions is relative to a world and also a time. On such a view, propositions determine functions from time-world pairs to truth-values. But this is not the view you are defending in your paper, at least on one of the two interpretations of your framework discussed in your appendix (and you guys are neutral on which interpretation to take). On this interpretation, which is how you talk in the text,you take the reference of ‘that’ clauses to be functions from contexts to propositions in the ordinary sense, viz. sets of possible worlds or entities that uniquely determine them.

The temporalist about propositions has no ‘genuine’ (i.e. eternalist) propositions in her ontology (unless named by eternal sentences). For her, the truth of genuine propositions is relative to times. On your account, you have genuine propositions as the values of the things referred to by a ‘that’ clause, relative to a context. You also have character-level entities that are the referents of ‘that’ clauses. This is not analogous to the temporalist position.

The position in philosophy of language it is analogous to is Nathan Salmon’s position in the appendix to *Frege’s Puzzle*. There, Salmon has temporal semantic values in addition to genuine eternalist propositions — temporal semantic values are treated like propositional functions. Salmon is certainly no temporalist. Indeed, the very purpose of his framework is to adjust Kaplan’s framework in order to *evade* temporalism about propositions.

Indeed, if we follow the temporal analogy, it again appears that you are explaining how to reject relativism in favor of contextualism, with genuine propositions as the values of the propositional functions named by ‘that’ clauses.

So, as I initially pointed out, your paper explains how to deal with some apparently problematic data in a contextualist framework.

On your last point. I agree that if people always followed the character of ‘it might rain’ rather than the content, that would be different than in the case of other context-sensitive indexicals (though, as I’ve emphasized, this would be equally a problem for the framework you defend, on one of the two interpretations you provide, since it’s just contextualism, and you also have genuine propositions available to be referred to). In standard vocabularly, the way you guys deal with this in your paper is to argue that for some reason, we generally refer to a character with some of its indices filled in assertion and belief reporting of other people’s uses of epistemic modals (rather than referring to the proposition they expressed, which is the value of that character, relative to that context of use, on your framework as well as any other non-relativist one).

Now if people *never* referred to the content, that would be a problem for you guys and your more openly contextualist allies. But even you must admit (“almost always”) that peple sometimes do refer to the content (the genuine proposition expressed). I think closer investigation of your examples in fact shows that one can often refer to the content, but it’s not often the natural referent of such a report, and you guys exaggerate the case a bit. But this isn’t important at all for the point I’m about to make. (Note the availability of the genuine propositional content to be referred to at all suggests that we need it around as a semantic value).

What I want to say about your final point is that I think you guys are right that we often refer to the character, and not the content, of uses of epistemic modals (or rather a character with some elements fixed). I think you’re also right that this is completely compatible with a thoroughly contextualist treatment (viz., the one you provide). One reason (I’m speculating here) that the natural reading of an assertion report involving epistemic modals is to take it as referring to the character of the sentence uttered (with some of its indices fixed) is that we often don’t have access to the knowledge-states of the person to whom we’re attributing the knowledge. In contrast, we often have ready access to the values of other context-sensitive expressions (e.g. the values of ‘I’, ‘here’, and ‘now’), so it’s much easier to refer to the contents of uses of sentences involving these indexicals.

By the way, this also may explain why we often seem to refer to the character of sentences containing comparative adjectives (since many of your tests would also suggest that truth is relative to comparison classes for such adjectives!). We sometimes are ignorant of the comparison class someone had in mind, so for the purposes of efficiency in communication, we refer to the character.

That’s just a speculation about the reason why the character of a use of an epistemic modal is an easily available semantic value in propositional anaphora and attitude reporting. I’m sure there are other factors too — as Richard Feldman pointed out in an old Phil Studies paper, it’s very natural to follow up John’s utterance of “I’m tired” with “Bill said that too”, when Bill uttered “I’m tired”. The character is just an easily available semantic value. I think in the case of epistemic modals and comparative adjectives, there are more conversation-particular reasons that lead us to talk about the character rather than the content. As you guys have shown in your admirable defense of contextualism, there is no reason to adopt a strange new relativistic position about truth to accomodate these facts. Within a standard contextualist framework, one can accomodate such facts by recognizing that we often refer, in attitude ascriptions, to the character rather than the value of the character, given the context, namely a genuine, non-relative proposition.

Now, you can apply these same arguments to defend contextualism about knowledge ascriptions against Hawthorne’s criticisms in chapter 2 of his book…I envisage a short, Analysis-style piece…;-)
