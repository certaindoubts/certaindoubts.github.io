False evidence

Posted on December 6, 2009 by John TurriDecember 6, 2009

Cal calculates that this circle’s area is less than 100m^2. He reasons as follows.

  1. A circle’s area = (3.141592654 exactly * radius^2). (Premise)
  2. This circle’s radius is 5m exactly. (Premise)
  3. So this circle’s area is 78.53981635 m^2 exactly. (From 1 – 2)
  4. So this circle’s area is less than 100 m^2. (From 3)

In virtue of this reasoning, Cal knows the conclusion. But 1, 2 and 3 are all false. Pi is not exactly 3.141592654, this circle’s radius is 4.9999998, and this circle’s area is not exactly 78.53981635 m^2.

The fact that 1 – 3 are all false doesn’t prevent them from either (A) being the evidence Cal bases his conclusion on, or (B) being good evidence for Cal’s conclusion.

No doubt it will be noticed that Cal’s premises, although strictly speaking false, are nearly true, and so nearly true that, for almost any practical purpose, we could treat them as if they were true. Nothing I say requires me to deny this.

It would be exceedingly implausible to claim that these premises don’t in fact constitute Cal’s evidence for his conclusion, and only ever so slightly less implausible to claim that they aren’t in fact good evidence for Cal’s conclusion. Absent a compelling argument that we should give up either A or B, I’d be unwilling to do so.

Fritz Warfield has already argued that cases like this refute the “No False Basis” response to the Gettier problem, and the attendant theory of knowledge. I suggest that they further demonstrate that evidence isn’t factive.* Something false can be your evidence for accepting some claim, and something false can even be good evidence for the claim, indeed evidence good enough to produce knowledge.

*In fact, Warfield notes that this sort of case undermines the view that good reasons must be true. Given the close connection between reasons and evidence, it’s probably more accurate to say that I’m simply re-emphasizing this fact in a slightly different context.

    Hey John,

    Why can’t we say that it’s because these things are nearly true it would be pedantic to say that these things aren’t part of someone’s evidence? They are, after all, for all intents and purposes true. (Nevertheless, and forgive me for being pedantic, these things aren’t evidence because these things are all false given the stipulation that we’re not to read these things as things that are true in the way Geoff suggested.)

    Two things about reasons and evidence. First, it’s not a consequence of the view that all evidence consists of truths that all reasons consist of truths because it’s possible that not all reasons are evidence even if every piece of evidence is a reason. Suppose evidence consists of what you know non-inferentially. If I infer q from p but know p non-inferentially, I’d say that p is my evidence and that p and q are both reasons for belief. This is true even if the inference is an inductive inference. What makes a derivative reason a reason is that it enables you to apply the support from evidence to further beliefs by means of further inference. It may well be that a false inductively supported proposition is a reason to believe a deductive consequence of that proposition and it might be a good one–the deductive consequences of this false but well supported proposition will be well supported.

    Second, owing to the fact that we speak of normative reasons, motivating reasons, explanatory reasons, the agent’s reasons as ‘reasons’ and it isn’t at all clear that these belong to some single ontological category, I think that someone can say that someone’s reasons for believing p were false because the kind of reasons here might not be normative but motivating. Further, these can be good if the (false) propositions led the subject to infer things that were reasonable, justified, known, etc… They were good because of what they led to in a rational way even if they were not themselves items of evidence, genuine normative reasons, etc…

    I don’t think this will sway you, but I think the factivity team can beat their paddles and keep their sinking canoe afloat a bit longer than you’d wager.

  8. Clayton Littlejohn on December 7, 2009 at 6:42 am said:

    There’s an interesting passage in Conee and Feldman’s discussion of McDowell. (I’m thinking of their paper ‘Evidence’ in the Q. Smith collection). Here’s what they say:

    “Here are a couple of reasons for doubting that it is PS1 [a factive mental state that one is in when one perceives a tree] instead of E1-En [the experiential states one could be in while hallucinating, I think] ultimately justifies B1 [the belief that there is a tree before you] in ordinary cases of knowing B1 by visual perception. First, PS1 has a justifying strength that intuitively derives from its relation to E1-En. It will be helpful to compare cases in which one sees a tree with cases in which one is deceived by a mere tree facade. If PS1 were ever one’s ultimate evidence for B1, then PS1 would be available as entailing evidence when one saw a tree and was in state PS1. When PS1 did not obtain, as when one was deceived by a tree facade, one would have no entailing evidence on behalf of B1. Yet, intuitively, one has equally good evidence for B1 in both cases. If the evidence is the same in both cases, then it is no wonder that it is equally good. If PS1 were what ultimately justifies in the veridical case but not the facade case, then there would be no straightforward explanation of the equal quality of evidence (100)”

    A couple of points. If we say that there’s ultimate evidence and derived evidence, they presumably wouldn’t want to treat _that there is a tree here_ as evidence of either sort given their insistence that we never have entailing evidence for the belief that there is a tree here. (I tend to think of evidence as ultimate evidence and UE as that which we can properly reason from for the purposes of deliberation without needing any justification for starting there, hence the reason to distinguish reasons from evidence. There can be some reasons you can reason from (properly or improperly) where you’d need evidence to properly reason from those.)

    I’d be interested to see whether people agreed with the sentiments in this passage. I don’t agree with them because I don’t accept the equal evidence claim or the claim that beliefs about the external world are supported just as well by hallucination and veridical experience, but (and this is why I stressed what I did) for those of you who like the idea that there can be non-factive evidence and agree with Conee and Feldman’s sentiments here, why would you say that there’s not entailing evidence for false beliefs? If you have a set of propositions that are true in one world and false in another, the same entailment relations among these propositions could hold in both worlds. How can we infer from the fact that some belief, B1, is false that there’s no entailing evidence for that belief unless we’re assuming that entailing evidence is, well, evidence and evidence consists of truths.

    (I take it that ‘entailing evidence’ can be understood in two ways. We could say that if h is entailed by e, h is true because e entails h and e is by its very nature true. We could say that e is entailed by h in the sense that it’s impossible that e is true and h isn’t. The only way I can make sense of this passage is to assume that they are talking about entailing evidence in the first way, not the second. Here’s some evidence against the evidence isn’t factive view. Many, many epistemologists seem to want to say that we cannot have entailing evidence in the second sense for our contingent beliefs about the external world and if you look at their reasons, they seem to make sense only when you take them to assume (implicitly, perhaps) to think of entailing evidence in the first sense. But, that would be a commitment to the factivity of evidence.)

    Second and unrelated point. Take the question, ‘Why is it that p?’ Responses include:
    (i) ‘There’s no reason why, it’s just a brute fact’
    (ii) ‘p because q’
    (iii) ‘Your question rests on a mistake, it’s actually not the case that p’.

    The first two responses entail p. The third entails ~p. ‘p because q’ is true only if p and q. ‘It’s a brute fact that p’ is true only if p. If you know that p is part of your evidence, don’t you know that the right answer is going to be of form (i) or (ii)? I think no one’s evidence has ever included the proposition that fish weigh less when they die because if someone asks ‘Why do fish weigh less when they die?’ the answer is that they don’t. I take it that those who say that evidence doesn’t consist of truths have to say that you can know that e is part of your evidence but not thereby be in a position to know whether the question ‘Why is it that e?’ rests on a mistake. That just doesn’t seem right.

  14. Gregory Wheeler on December 8, 2009 at 3:01 am said:

    There is quite a lot in your comment. I’ll focus on 3 and 1/2 points.

    (1) I had thought that, if forced, C&F would concede that (good) evidence is not necessarily factive evidence. I haven’t followed them closely in recent years, and I left Rochester around the time that the impact from Williamson’s book was still being absorbed, but I thought that (i) their particular brand of internalism was difficult to square with a factivity condition for evidence, and (ii) they wouldn’t be too bothered to square it. Again, I stress, this is an old impression, and I might well have had the wrong idea even then.

    (2) Disjunctions are tricky, and you are correct to observe that the example you mention would follow even in a normal modal logic, i.e., Xp -> X(p v q) is a theorem, for diamond modality X. This is not a theorem of minimal modal logics, but the class of systems that give you this flexibility are so weak that we (still) do not know how to do much with them. Even so, entailment is truth preservation, not factivity preservation. The truth values for the entailment relations might be the agent’s judgment, after all. The logical plumbing works the same no matter the philosophical interpretation of the semantic values one pours down the drain. So, if we distinguish true / false in the semantics for evidence from factivity / non-factivity, then, yes, I imagine that one could adopt C&F’s basic line while maintaining that non-factive propositions constitute evidence.

    Non-factive accounts are not anything-goes accounts; the issue is not to do away with the constraints that the world imposes on our beliefs, but rather to do away with the idea that those constraints show up as the content of our beliefs.

    (3) What about BIVs? What about them? The problem with BIVs is not to distinguish them from us, but to distinguish the error we presume them to be from everyday errors that we can mitigate. I can make headway with the latter question, and much of the reason why I can make progress stems from the fact that the former question is deterministic (by design) but, often, the latter is non-deterministic. In short, I can manipulate systems to learn about error and causal relationships in non-deterministic systems, but I am blocked from doing so by hypothesis in the former. I’ve yet to be convinced why I should worry about the paper doubts (Levi’s phrase) raised by BIVs.

    (1/2) Another thought that came to mind in my original reply was to poo-poo entailment talk altogether. Why think that the relationship between evidence and belief is logical (i.e., deterministic) rather than non-deterministic? One might instead think that Ramsey was right: beliefs don’t have truth values at all, so the logic of (partial) belief should be thought of as a logic of consistency rather than a logic of truth preservation. This fits together with the end of (3) and the end of (2), I suppose, but I thought that raising this would be too abrupt a change in subject.
