Since there’s not contradiction there, philosophizing can commence. If the problem was merely that you were assuming that the premises were both true and false, it wouldn’t be the same.

What we may be seeing is that your premises are both “nearly true” (which is to say, false but not useless). Rather, they are true *in one sense* and, not “nearly false” in another sense, but just false in that other sense.

They are evidence in the sense in which they are true. And they are not evidence in the sense in which they are not. By offering pretty good approximations of the values involved but then, falsely, qualifying them with “exactly”. So we “get” why it might be evidence and are forced to admit that it is not true. That’s how the puzzle works.

]]> By: Gregory Wheeler http://certaindoubts.com/false-evidence/#comment-10388 Tue, 08 Dec 2009 08:01:46 +0000 http://el-prod.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=1644#comment-10388 There is quite a lot in your comment. I’ll focus on 3 and 1/2 points.

(1) I had thought that, if forced, C&F would concede that (good) evidence is not necessarily factive evidence. I haven’t followed them closely in recent years, and I left Rochester around the time that the impact from Williamson’s book was still being absorbed, but I thought that (i) their particular brand of internalism was difficult to square with a factivity condition for evidence, and (ii) they wouldn’t be too bothered to square it. Again, I stress, this is an old impression, and I might well have had the wrong idea even then.

(2) Disjunctions are tricky, and you are correct to observe that the example you mention would follow even in a normal modal logic, i.e., Xp -> X(p v q) is a theorem, for diamond modality X. This is not a theorem of minimal modal logics, but the class of systems that give you this flexibility are so weak that we (still) do not know how to do much with them. Even so, entailment is truth preservation, not factivity preservation. The truth values for the entailment relations might be the agent’s judgment, after all. The logical plumbing works the same no matter the philosophical interpretation of the semantic values one pours down the drain. So, if we distinguish true / false in the semantics for evidence from factivity / non-factivity, then, yes, I imagine that one could adopt C&F’s basic line while maintaining that non-factive propositions constitute evidence.

Non-factive accounts are not anything-goes accounts; the issue is not to do away with the constraints that the world imposes on our beliefs, but rather to do away with the idea that those constraints show up as the content of our beliefs.

(3) What about BIVs? What about them? The problem with BIVs is not to distinguish them from us, but to distinguish the error we presume them to be from everyday errors that we can mitigate. I can make headway with the latter question, and much of the reason why I can make progress stems from the fact that the former question is deterministic (by design) but, often, the latter is non-deterministic. In short, I can manipulate systems to learn about error and causal relationships in non-deterministic systems, but I am blocked from doing so by hypothesis in the former. I’ve yet to be convinced why I should worry about the paper doubts (Levi’s phrase) raised by BIVs.

(1/2) Another thought that came to mind in my original reply was to poo-poo entailment talk altogether. Why think that the relationship between evidence and belief is logical (i.e., deterministic) rather than non-deterministic? One might instead think that Ramsey was right: beliefs don’t have truth values at all, so the logic of (partial) belief should be thought of as a logic of consistency rather than a logic of truth preservation. This fits together with the end of (3) and the end of (2), I suppose, but I thought that raising this would be too abrupt a change in subject.
