Ascribing knowledge in English and other languages

Posted on January 12, 2013 by Jennifer NagelJanuary 12, 2013

What can epistemologists learn from studying knowledge attributions in other languages?  This is the question behind the “Epistemology for the Rest of the World” conference upcoming in Tokyo this August (see the call for papers here).

I think it’s an excellent question, and I love the main idea behind the conference, which is, as Steve Stich puts it in the announcement, to try to answer this question by “bringing together philosophers, linguists and social scientists from Asia and the West”.

What seems more controversial is Stich’s suggestion that epistemologists who have relied on intuitions about hypothetical cases presented in English have been studying the properties of the English language word “know”, as opposed to studying knowledge itself. He suggests that since fewer than 6% of the world’s population are native English speakers, we have reason to doubt that the concept expressed by the English word “know” has important implications for epistemology.

One way of quelling those doubts, as Stich points out, would be to say that the concept expressed by “know” is universal, expressed by some word in every natural language. That’s actually a thesis endorsed by linguists who study the limits of cross-linguistic diversity (see for example Cliff Goddard’s website for details). Lexical universality is a really rare feature — for what it’s worth, Goddard’s research program identifies just 63 words with this status. Many words that you’d expect to show up in all languages (“eat”, “drink”) don’t actually (apparently Warlpiri and Kalam get by with one word meaning “ingest”).

KNOW is claimed to be universal (like BECAUSE, IF, LIVE and DIE).  Actually, to be more specific, it’s the sense of “know” that embeds a propositional or wh- complement that is universal — not every language does the confusing English thing of making “know” do double duty for “savoir” and “connaitre”.  But no language seems to get by without a word that precisely translates “know”, or so Goddard and company say.  I should say this is a bit rough, because what counts as a word gets complicated when we look at inflected forms, or at languages like East Cree which don’t have verbal infinitives –“lexical expression” might be more accurate than “word”.  (Curious about the East Cree knowledge-ascribing lexical expression “chischeyihtam” / “chischeyimeu”? You’re welcome.)

According to Stich, “there is currently very little reason to think that the universality thesis is true, since little or nothing is known about the meaning and use of epistemic terms in languages other than English.  What little is known looks to point in the other direction.”  He goes on to mention the fact that two distinct verbs are used in Japanese to translate “know” — “shitte-iru” and “wakatte-iru”.

My own suspicion is that quite a lot is known about the meaning and use of epistemic terms in other languages (although we could doubtless benefit from getting this knowledge shared around a bit more, as Stich’s conference helpfully promises to do). I think there’s also a fair bit of evidence that KNOW is lexically universal.  But I wonder what to say about Japanese. It’s not especially threatening to universality that there is more than one verb used for knowledge attribution in Japanese: we also use multiple verbs in English (to notice that, to be aware that, to see that…).  Assuming that the verb “to know” is special because it is the most general such verb, we can wonder whether there are languages in which this generality is not expressible.

According to researchers in Goddard’s program, it’s the first of Stich’s two verbs that expresses the universal concept KNOW (shiru – 知る), but they may be wrong about that. In general, the universality program is actually OK with the idea that in some languages a lexical universal has two words that are used to express the universal (multiple exponents of the universal).  These words themselves might not be fully interchangeable because of polysemy; what would matter is just that the key senses are the same (consider the English “think” and “believe” — I take them to be interchangeable for epistemological purposes, but just if we restrict ourselves to the sense that embeds a propositional complement).

In any event, I’m curious about what readers of Certain Doubts think about universality — either its empirical plausibility or its significance for epistemology.

I’m also curious about what value other epistemologists see in cross-linguistic work (if any).  I don’t like to think that I am (just) exploring the English word “know” when I think about hypothetical cases that I might or might not use that verb to describe.  My own feeling is that it can help me avoid getting hung up on peculiarities of English to spend some time looking at Aquinas’s use of “cognoscere” and “scire” (for example!).  Does anyone else share my sense that examining the commonalities in knowledge talk across other languages can help us see what is most epistemologically significant in our own patterns of knowledge attribution?

    Jonny,

    First, Professor Nagel has just pointed out that KNOW is in fact universal, and your message suggests an unwillingness to update on that information. You might want to follow up on the links she has provided. I did a bit of this work via Rutgers linguists on know wh-constructions, and I didn’t hear of any languages in which the propositional and question-embedding verbs were different (though my research was not as thorough).

    The situation is more complicated concerning translations of “know how to H”. For example, some languages, e.g. Greek (obviously), Hindi-Urdu, and German resist allowing infinitives in embedded questions (in her 2001 book, Susi Wurmbrand discusses a few exceptions in German, such as “Ich weiss nicht was tun”, but they are as they say the proverbial exceptions that prove the rule). As I discuss at length in my 2011 Nous paper, and Chapter 6 of my book, Know How, there are at least five different ways in which languages express something like what we express by “x knows how to H”. I argue at that the best account of the nature of the states in question that can capture all these different ways of expressing the state is the account of the nature of the state given in the Stanley and Williamson theory.

    Second, your claim that “Stanley and Williamson’s argument for know how being a species of propositional knowledge relies almost entirely on linguistic considerations” is false. Because I have heard it before, I should say again what we do. First, we argue that the central arguments that knowing how is not knowing that, given by Ryle, are thoroughly unpersuasive. So there is no prima facie reason to think that knowing how *isn’t* knowing that. Secondly, we produce an account of the nature of knowing-wh generally, and use it to formulate a theory of the nature of knowing how. Third, we defend our theory of the nature of states knowing how against a series of objections. Four paragraphs at the end of Section III are devoted to saying why our theory is better than other theories. In one of those paragraphs we say that our theory gives a better account of the linguistic facts than rival theories (a claim defended at length in Chapter 6 of my 2011 book). So when you say “virtually all” of our paper consists of that one paragraph, it’s a little misleading.

    The paper was published in 2001. Its aim was not to provide a conclusive case for a view according to which knowing how is a species of knowing that. It was rather to explain and develop the view, which at that time was considered prima facie absurd, and show that the objections to it were unpersuasive.

    It is true that in giving the account we do of the nature of knowing how, we draw on work that has been more influential among linguists than philosophers (though Groenendijk and Stokhof are philosophers). But it is a historical accident that work on the nature of states of knowing-wh ceased to be of central importance among philosophers. Though a lot of the work done by linguists is in the formal mode, if you take the quotation marks off it yields an account of the nature of such states.

    I do however think it’s a virtue of a view of the nature of some states that it is a plausible account of what we mean when we talk about such states. So that’s why I spend a chapter of my 2011 book on the cross-linguistic data, arguing that my account of the nature of such states is consistent with all of it.

    However, the basic argument that knowing how is a species of knowing that has nothing to do with language. It has to do with the fact that “the philosophical considerations that support the view that knowing how to phi is not a kind of propositional knowledge also entail that knowing where to phi and knowing when to phi are not kinds of propositional knowledge.” In fact, I think I show something considerably stronger in the book. I show that the philosophical considerations that have been employed to support the view that knowing how to phi is not a kind of propositional knowledge can be used to show that knowing that p is not a kind of propositional knowledge. In short, the main argument of the book is that it is only by having the wrong theory of propositional knowledge that one can argue that knowing how to do something is not propositional knowledge (see Stalnaker’s 2012 PPR commentary for a nicely succinct statement).

    (b) how universal is concept C?

    I take it that their underlying assumption is that that the more universal a concept is the likelier it is to be a natural one.     Dear Gregory Wheeler,

    As I explain at some length in my book *Know How* (see pp. 151-4), the AI distinction between declarative and procedural knowledge has nothing whatsoever to do with the putative distinction philosophers have drawn between knowing how and knowing that. In “Frame Representations and the Declarative-Procedural Controversy” Winograd does explicitly cast the controversy that way, but as I point out, he says many things are flatly and straightforwardly inconsistent with the view that there is any tight analogy between the declarative-procedural discussion in AI, and the (small) 20th century know how-know that debate as conducted e.g. by Ryle and some others. I won’t repeat all three pages here, but really it’s just wrong to think the debates are in any way analogous. The AI debate is an implementation issue; the knowing how-knowing that debate(s) are not. See for a nice, clear explanation of why the AI distinction has nothing to do with propositional knowledge vs. a putative non-propositional state, Stalnaker’s 2012 contribution to the PPR symposium on know how.

As a native speaker of a Romance language (a language that distinguishes between COGNOSCERE and SAPERE) I have always found that thesis particularly counterintuitive and I suspect that it is difficult to find it appealing unless you are a native speaker of English (or of some other language that uses the same word for COGNOSCERE and SAPERE).”

    I confess to being bewildered by this comment. The distinction between “Cognoscere” and “Sapere” has nothing whatsoever to do with the putative distinction between knowing how and knowing that. It is rather corresponds to the distinction between knowledge by acquaintance and knowledge by description. Virtually all Romance languages of which I am aware use the same verb to express knowing how to do something as they use to express propositional knowledge, the verb in the “sapere” family. As Ian Rumfitt explains in his 2003 JPhil paper, “Savoir Faire”, the distinction between romance languages and English in the expression of knowing how involves explicit question morphology. In English, it’s natural to use “how”, but in most Romance languages (more accurately, as I’ve discovered, in most dialects of I think every Romance language), you use the propositional knowledge verb with the bare infinitive. In Chapter 6 of *Know How* (specifically, pp. 138-41), I explain in detail that a perfectly straightforward compositional semantics for the Romance languages assigns just the state I say is knowing how to do something to Romance language expressions of knowing how, and I give a full explanation of all the data from French, that is fully consistent, and indeed straightforwardly predicted, by the Stanley and Williamson theory.

  24. Gregory Wheeler on January 19, 2013 at 12:38 pm said:

    Dear Jason,

    This will be a bit long; apologies in advance. I shall give two examples** of cross-linguistic variability. The first is between English and Polish. The second is between German and Italian, although I describe the issue mostly in English. The (broad) point will be that syntactic variability is a poor guide to understanding true variability in cross-cultural epistemic states.

    Consider these two sentences:

    (1) She knows that the bicyclist confessed (to doping).

    (2) She knows how to ride a bicycle.

    In Polish, the translation of (1) is pretty straightforward:

    (1′) Wie, że kolarz prezynał sie (do stosowania dopingu).

    But (2) presents a problem, because a straightforward translation of ‘know how’ into Polish runs into grammaticality problems: one does not say know-how in Polish. However, there is

    (2′) Umie jeździć na rowerze,

    which conveys that the subject has the ability to pilot a bicycle, and also

    (2”) Wie, jak sie jeździ na rowerze w Holandii,

    which conveys that she knows how to ride a bicycle in the Netherlands, meaning that she knows the rules for bicycle riding in the Netherlands and can manage to abide by them.

    The first point I would draw from these examples is that, despite the failure to have as clean a rendering of ‘know-how’ in Polish as one has with ‘know-that’, the underlying conceptual distinction that we mark by ‘know-how’ is immediately clear and easily rendered. The variability is largely a superficial issue that accounts for some slippage between English and Polish, but is no evidence for a deep difference in epistemic states.

    Contrast this case with an issue that exercises translators working between Italian and German, which is that German contains modal particles whereas Italian does not. To get the idea across, we can use `did’ in English for emphasis as in:

    (3) After a decade of denial, the bicyclist has publicly confessed that he DID dope.

    In German, the modal particle DOCH is used to express this emphasis:

    (3′) Ein Jahrzehnt lang hat der Radrennfahrer geleugnet, jetzt hat er DOCH öffentlich zugegeben, gedopt zu haben.

    Italian does not have modal particles, and the translator really needs to do some surgery to get this emphasis across, perhaps by laying down a sentence that expresses that the bicyclist confessed to doping after a decade of denial, and then looping around with an additional clause to emphasize the point. The effect is something like the following in English:

    (3”) For a decade the cyclist denied having doped, but now he has confessed to have done it,

    where it refers to ‘doping’ and there is an invitation extended to the reader to add sotto voce something like ”all along!” The point here is not that there are states in one culture that are missing in the other, but more that there really is a gap here between the languages (and, arguably, the cultures) in how such states are expressed. There is an economy in German that isn’t in Italian on this narrow score, and that has deep ramifications if the degree of emphasis is salient and the discussion is carried out primarily in German.

    To be brief, I think cross-linguistic data on knowledge attribution is more like the English-to-Polish case of rendering ‘know-how’ than the German-to-Italian case of rendering modal particles. The conceptual distinction between having an ability versus having the facts is pretty robust, I conjecture, and I predict that the linguistic variability observed in these cases will far more often be a surface feature that is easily navigated. By contrast, one really needs to understand both German and Italian to handle the modal particles case: the translator needs to first notice the emphasis in the German, which non-native speakers often miss, and then to figure out how (or even whether it is worth the effort) to rework the Italian to convey that emphasis. Here would be an area were things are often “lost in translation”, sometimes with significant consequences. In so far as proponents of cross-linguistic studies of epistemic vocabulary think that epistemic terms are toward the modal particle end of the spectrum, I don’t buy it.

    To be sure, these are empirical questions, and they are worth investigating. But the bar should be set higher than it apparently is (for some) before phrases like “epistemology for the rest of the world” are used.

    (**) Note: Data gathered at the breakfast table this morning from a native Polish speaker, who is also fluent in German and Italian.
