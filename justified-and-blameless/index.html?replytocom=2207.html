Justified and Blameless

Posted on July 22, 2005 by Kvanvig JonJuly 22, 2005

Epistemologists and ethicists often want a more subjective notion to supplement their objective accounts of justification (or warrant or reasonableness…) and moral rightness. A common strategy is to appeal to the language of blame and praise, distinguishing the above properties from that of being blameworthy.

I’m suspicious of this ploy, though more suspicious of it in epistemology than in action theory and ethics. So I’ll start with actions.

The idea is supposed to be, I think, that if your act is excusable, then you are not to be blamed for it (even if it was wrong). It is, of course, one thing to have done something wrong, and another thing to be held responsible or criticized for having done something wrong. That shows that wrongness and blame are distinguishable. The desired distinction, however, is not between blame and wrong, but between blameworthiness and wrong. If we think about blameworthiness, that looks like the idea of it being legitimate to hold a person responsible for, or to criticize them for, an action. How can holding a person responsible be improper if the action was wrong? Answer: they have an excuse! That is, they have a legitimate excuse; the wrong action is excusable. As the dictionary puts it, they have a defense or justification for the wrong done.

Put this way, we should be a bit suspicious. To get the distinction to do the work desired, we have to say that the act in question really is (ultima facie) wrong, but not blameworthy. That’s going to take an argument, however. If we’re told that an action is wrong but that you have a justification for doing it anyway, one natural way to interpret such a remark is this: the action is prima facie wrong, but your situation involves additional factors that render the action ultima facie permissible.

If we now translate the above discussion into the domain of justification for belief, we get the even more troubling idea of finding a belief that is unjustified, but where there exists, or we find, a defense or justification for the belief. Not, as one might expect, a defense or justification for the belief from our perspective, but a defense or justification for the belief from the very perspective from which the belief is unjustified. To avoid the air of incoherence here, the appeal to the distinction between prima facie justification and ultima facie justification is even more attractive.

Notice that if we talk in the domain of belief about the concept of truth rather than justification, we can distinguish between true beliefs and beliefs we’re not blameworthy for holding. Using the dictionary definition, that’s just the ordinary distinction between justification and truth.

Similarly, if we hold a strongly realist moral theory on which moral rightness is analogous to truth (rather than justification), we can make sense of the difference between moral wrongness and blameworthiness. For example, suppose moral rightness is defined in terms of actual utility. Then one could do a wrong action and yet not be blameworthy for doing it.

But when we are talking about the property of justification itself, whether applied to actions or beliefs, the distinction becomes problematic precisely because blameworthiness seems to be a property defined in terms of justification itself.

One might respond: there’s a difference between objective and subjective justification, and blameworthiness goes with the latter. I’ve already posted on this one, and there is a respectable approach of this sort: present and defend a subjective theory of justification along with a more objective one. As far as I know, only John Greco has attempted this (though others have tried something similar–for example, Goldman’s distinction between strong and weak justification is in the same conceptual neighborhood). My own view of the matter, which I’ve defended in print, is that once you’ve got a good subjective theory, there’ll be no need for an objective theory at all–any intellectual value for belief in the area of justification, knowledge, and understanding can be explained in terms of the value of truth and the value of subjective justification.

The bottom line, I think, is this. The temptation toward a distinction between justification and blameworthiness arises because one’s assumptions about justification are not subjective enough. Once one corrects for this bias, all that is left to explain is the situation where a person reflects carefully on his or her belief and comes to the incorrect conclusion that it isn’t justified. In such a case, one of two responses is appropriate. One option is to to remind ourselves of the long history of levels confusions in epistemology, and say that in such a case the person’s belief is justified in spite of having a justified meta-belief that it isn’t. The other option is to say that such cases are ones where meta-level information filters down to object-level justification, since the meta-level information provides (subjective) defeating information regarding the original justification. On this option, the proper thing to say is that the belief was justified before the person reflected on it, but once the reflection occurred, the justification disappeared for the object-level belief. The thing to notice is that in neither case do we need any distinction between justification and blameworthiness.

So I propose the following. Once we have an adequate theory of justification, there will be nothing left to explain in terms of some purported distinction between blameworthiness and justification.

  5. Clayton on July 22, 2005 at 11:20 am said:

    There is one thing I meant to mention but forgot to in the earlier post. We have to be careful when dealing with blamelessness and blameworthiness because while blameworthiness implies inexcusable, blamelessness does not imply excusable. Strawson (‘Freedom and Resentment’) distinguishes pleas that are exemptions from those that are excuses. An exemption is aimed at denying that blame is fitting because we are not really dealing with an agent whereas an excuse is aimed at showing that an agent shouldn’t be faulted if the product of agency is an instance of wrongdoing. So blamelessness really commits us to either denying that the agent should be faulted or that we should regard the act/attitude as something under the control of a rational agent.

    This distinction between excuses and exemptions matters, I think, because I’ve found that the reason some writers have insisted that (personal) justification is not merely a matter of blamelessness is that when we ascribe justification, we often see something positive in the agent whereas we need not see anything positive in the agent whenever we deny that we are dealing with someone who is a fitting object of our reactive attitudes. This is correct so far as it goes, but it also misses something important about excuses: if we didn’t see any positive qualities of the agent being manifested, we wouldn’t likely regard an excuse as in order. However, since excuses seem to fall short of justifications, seeing something positive in the agent on the occassion where she comes to believe or act would also seem to fall short of commiting us to thinking of the agent’s acts or attitudes as justified in any significant sense. This is one reason I’m very sceptical of the appeal to arguments from error against externalist accounts of reasons or justifications for belief. Of course we think that the agent is rational in erring, but that isn’t to say that there was a reason for the error at all.

    It is interesting to see that the link between rationality and the existence of reasons in the strict sense (i.e., as considerations that go towards determining which options count as correct, permissible, forbidden, etc.) has been questioned recently by writers like Mike Smith (I’m on his team) precisely because of the sorts of issues that force us to recognize the subtle differences between pleas that are justifications, excuses, and exemptions.
