]]> By: kvanvig http://certaindoubts.com/justified-and-blameless/#comment-2218 Mon, 01 Aug 2005 11:37:30 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=379#comment-2218 Mike, the claim isn’t whether there will be cases like this, but what notion of blame is at work in them. I’ve given several ways of understanding such claims: prima facie vs. ultima facie justification, epistemic justification versus some other notion of justification, such as moral or practical or all-things-considered justification, and even some other epistemic notion for which an independent theory can be provided. My complaint in the post is about thinking that one has somehow blocked objections to the lack of subjectivity in a view merely by saying that the belief is blameless even though unjustified according to the theory. That’s a ploy that simply doesn’t attend sufficiently to what the word ‘blameless’ means. Dictionary definitions are not always accurate, but they are a good place to start, so if the dictionary definition of ‘blameless’ in terms of having a defense or justification for a wrong done is incorrect, we should see some explanation of the error and some proposal to takes its place. If we go with the dictionary, however, the first idea that comes to mind here is the distinction between prima and ultima facie wrongs, but there are other possibilities. The more general point is to think about the factor that makes the action or belief excusable, and note which of the categories of items above the factor falls into (and if it doesn’t (assuming that my list is complete, which I haven’t claimed yet), the proper conclusion to draw is that a levels confusion has occurred). The result will be that blameworthiness won’t be any longer just a fallback for the absence of sufficient subjectivity either in moral rules or epistemic principles.

If we want the medication case to come out so the person is epistemically blameless, one can do that by arguing in favor of a kind of responsibilism about justification. Since the person did nothing irresponsible in terms of belief formation or inquiry, such an approach could make him out to be epistemically blameless since not irresponsible. But then the belief is not unjustified either, in spite of there being no good evidence for it. As with other approaches to justification, then, the temptation to use the language of blamelessness arises from adopting a position on the nature of justification that doesn’t take into account all the subjective factors one believes on reflection need to be taken into account. The distinction itself doesn’t do the trick, however.
