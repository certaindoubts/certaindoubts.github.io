The idea is to first identify 2 beliefs:
B(drawing)
B(drawing at 6).

Then assume that the deceptive world is a close world, and assume matching is safety plus sensitivity: if you believe it, it will be true in these worlds; if it’s false, you won’t believe it. Then the first belief is not knowledge because the belief isn’t sensitive. And the second belief is both safe and sensitive (since it isn’t held in the deceptive world).

So the mistake I made is this: I individuated the second belief, not in terms of its content, but in terms of it being a belief about the time of the drawing. In the deceptive world, your belief about the time of the drawing doesn’t match the facts of the matter in that world about the time of the drawing. But matching is defined in terms of safety plus sensitivity, so belief individuation has to be in terms of contents of belief.

So you need to talk about types of belief and make the type of belief match the facts in close worlds, whichever member of that type is tokened in that world?

]]> By: Keith DeRose http://certaindoubts.com/contextualist-closure/#comment-1973 Fri, 10 Jun 2005 18:46:59 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=350#comment-1973 Richard writes that I am committed to the view that there exists a class of propositions which can be known only when they are not being considered.

That’s not what I’m committed to. On my view, we do know-ord (know by ordinary standards) that we’re not BIVs and we know-ord this whether we’re considering the matter or not. We do not know-abs that we’re not BIVs, and we fail to know-abs it whether we’re considering the matter or not.

What’s closer to the truth (but still not the truth) is that I’m committed to this less wild thing: That though we know-ord that we’re not BIVs, we can never truthfully claim that we “know” we’re not BIVs (because any attempt to make this claim will raise the standards to the abs level at which we don’t count as knowing).

That’s still not right, though. It would be right if the “Rule of Sensitivity” invariably worked to raise the standards in the way it specifies. My actual position is that the standards *tend* to be raised in accordance with this Rule, but it doesn’t always take effect. In section 2 of “Solving the Skeptical Problem,” I explain that for ease of exposition, I’ll assume a very “skeptic-friendly” version of contextualism on which the standards are always successfully raised in accordance with the Rule, and explain how to adjust the explanations on other, less skeptic-friendly, versions.

Jon: This post raises very tricky issues, but (I’m of course sorry to say) I don’t think Stew makes the mistake you attribute to him. Hope to get back to this later.

]]> By: Richard Greene http://certaindoubts.com/contextualist-closure/#comment-1972 Fri, 10 Jun 2005 17:31:18 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=350#comment-1972 Despite the fact contextualism is put forth as an alternative to nonclosure theories in solving certain skeptical puzzles, I think things would actually be better for DeRose’s position if it did deny closure. In order to hold that knowledge is closed, if one holds that (in certain contexts) one can know that p (that some ordinary empirical proposition is true), then in those same contexts one must hold that that one can know that not-sk (that skepticism is false), provided that p entials not-sk, etc. Since consideration of skeptical counterpossibilities serves to raise epistemic standards (or on DeRose’s view increases the number of possible worlds through which one’s beliefs must track)the contextualist is committed to the view that there exists a class of propositions which can be known only when they are not being considered.
