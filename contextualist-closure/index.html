Contextualist Closure?

Posted on June 10, 2005 by Kvanvig JonJune 10, 2005

Stew Cohen, in “Contextualism, Skepticism, and the Structure of Reasons,” (Philosophical Perspectives 13, (1999)), claims in passing that DeRose’s truth-tracking version of contextualism will have violations of closure. That would be a very surprising and important result, but I think the example doesn’t work. Here’s the setup for the example:

The lottery drawing is set for 6pm, and it always has occurred then. But this time there’s a conspiracy between the managers and the reporters to fix the lottery. They plan to announce a winner without holding the lottery, but to deceive one scrupulous reporter not part of the plot, they also plan to say the drawing was held at 5 pm (since they know that this honest reporter can’t attend at that time). At the last minute, they get cold feet and go ahead with the usual drawing at 6 pm.

Reading the paper the next morning, you come to believe there was a drawing at 6 pm. In the ordinary context in which you find yourself, you can (apparently) know this on DeRose’s view, because your belief tracks the truth out to the nearest world at which there was no 6pm drawing. Presumably, you could track the truth out this far, so you can know that there was a drawing at 6 pm.

But what about the claim that there was a drawing? Do you track the truth out to this world in which there was no drawing? In this world where there was no drawing, it is reported that the drawing was at 5 pm. So if you are the kind of person who believes very simple consequences of your beliefs (i.e., so that you believe there was a drawing whenever you believe there was a drawing at a specific time), then your belief that there was a drawing does not track the truth appropriately.

That’s Cohen’s account of the case: you can know that the drawing was at 6, but not that there was a drawing.

There’s a simple mistake here, though. Travel out to the world where the drawing didn’t take place. That’s a world where it is reported that the drawing took place at 5. Now, either this world is close enough that your belief has to match the truth in it in order to know, or it isn’t close enough. Suppose it is close enough. Then your belief doesn’t match the facts in this world: you believe the drawing was at 5, but you are wrong. So, if the world is close enough, you don’t know that the drawing was at 6. So suppose the world is not close enough. Then you can know that the drawing was at 6. But, you can also know that there was a drawing, as Cohen argued. Either way, no closure violation.

Update: the counterexample works, I was mistaken…so counterfactual epistemologists will have to find a different account of what it is for a belief to match the truth in close worlds. I suggest one possible direction below, but thought I should retract the objection here as well.

    Richard writes that I am committed to the view that there exists a class of propositions which can be known only when they are not being considered.

    That’s not what I’m committed to. On my view, we do know-ord (know by ordinary standards) that we’re not BIVs and we know-ord this whether we’re considering the matter or not. We do not know-abs that we’re not BIVs, and we fail to know-abs it whether we’re considering the matter or not.

    What’s closer to the truth (but still not the truth) is that I’m committed to this less wild thing: That though we know-ord that we’re not BIVs, we can never truthfully claim that we “know” we’re not BIVs (because any attempt to make this claim will raise the standards to the abs level at which we don’t count as knowing).

    That’s still not right, though. It would be right if the “Rule of Sensitivity” invariably worked to raise the standards in the way it specifies. My actual position is that the standards *tend* to be raised in accordance with this Rule, but it doesn’t always take effect. In section 2 of “Solving the Skeptical Problem,” I explain that for ease of exposition, I’ll assume a very “skeptic-friendly” version of contextualism on which the standards are always successfully raised in accordance with the Rule, and explain how to adjust the explanations on other, less skeptic-friendly, versions.
