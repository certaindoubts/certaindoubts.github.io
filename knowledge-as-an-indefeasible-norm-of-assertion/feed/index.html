Williamson’s remark that it’s not that big a wrong also seems right. (But I laughed out loud when I read it.) Here are two other possibilities that might be worth some thought: When we make skeptical (and other philosophical) assertions we’re not really asserting things, but only pretending to assert them (as when I take the skeptic’s role in discussion with my students). It does seem to me that there is more than a bit of acting or role playing in much philosophical discourse, though I don’t want to deny that we are also often serious, or at least think we are. Another way out would be to say that we intend our assertions to be covered by an operator like “It seems to me that…” or “I’m inclined to say that…” or “These arguments seem to me to give us good reason to think that…”. If our assertions include those operators then we could reasonably claim to know what we assert. That leaves people like Descartes or Spinoza out however, since they apparently did think that their unqualified philosophical claims expressed knowledge. I don’t much mind saying that they shouldn’t have been so positive however. (And I admit that the qualifiers, which most of us use a good share of the time (see your last post and this one), give recent philosophical prose a disagreeably wishy washy feel.)

]]> By: jon kvanvig http://certaindoubts.com/knowledge-as-an-indefeasible-norm-of-assertion/#comment-221 Fri, 02 Jul 2004 12:30:23 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=40#comment-221 I guess I think the following about philosophical claims such as skeptical claims. Divide philosophical remarks into positive and negative (yes, I know there’s no clear way to do so, but I’m relying on our recognition skills (!) here to tell the difference between one philosopher finding fault with a theory and another philosopher proposing something that is a new theory). I don’t think positive philosophy is within the range of what we know at all. So when a utilitarian says that the right thing to do is what maximizes utility, the utilitarian is saying something that s/he doesn’t know to be true (even if, per impossible, the claim happens to be true).

So there’s one gigantic ad hominem lurking here, of the sort that is a legitimate criticism. If knowledge is the norm of assertion, only negative philosophy should survive (and, of course, some of that should disappear as well). Even more interesting, no should ever assert that knowledge is the norm of assertion. And since assertion is to belief (maybe…) as outer is to inner, no should believe it either. (An aside: who would think that knowledge is the norm of belief anyway? Maybe I’ll do a main entry on this…)

Such untoward implications can be handled simply by making the norms defeasible. That would still explain our students’ responses that the skeptic is being inconsistent (which s/he’s not) or doing something else that is wrong. They haven’t yet caught on to the special circumstances that surround philosophy that allow us to make what would be in ordinary contexts, outrageous claims.

Williamson and I emailed about this a couple of years ago, and his reply to the philosophy example (actually, I used the example of his own book, which contains an unusual percentage of original positive philosophy) quite amusing. He said something to the effect that violating the norm is not that big a wrong…

What I offer instead in that paper is an anecdote about my students tending to think that the skeptic is somehow being inconsistent (in some broad sense — the kind my students notice) in so saying. I claim that that’s evidence that we do require knowledge for assertion. The students are quite right in saying the skeptic is inconsistent, but they are relying on the general requirement of knowledge for assertion in seeing the skeptic as inconsistent (by something like conversational implication), not on any explicit claims by the skeptic to know (or logical implications). Of course that raises the question why philosophers think the skeptic’s claim that we have no knowledge should be allowed. Maybe it’s one of those obviously unacceptable assertions that we think philosophers are entitled to in pursuit of whatever it is we do. (Like Unger, in his book Ignorance, arguing that since knowledge is required for assertion, and we can’t have knowledge, we ought to remain silent. Not following his own advice was in some way consistent with the calculated outrageousness of his opinions I guess, however contrary to his explicit conclusions.)

]]> By: Steven Reynolds http://certaindoubts.com/knowledge-as-an-indefeasible-norm-of-assertion/#comment-218 Thu, 01 Jul 2004 21:15:15 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=40#comment-218 Well yes I do. I’ve got a moderately elaborate theory about all of this (See my Phil Studies paper, 2002, reference on the web site) which argues that the point of saying people know is encouraging better (more helpful) testimony. Roughly speaking, to say that someone knows that p is to approve of their testifying that p. It’s intended (now) as a rival to Williamson’s view (though I wasn’t aware of Williamson’s view of the relation of knowledge and assertion when I first began working on the idea some years ago), and also as a rival to the popular view that knowledge has some close relation to a goal of obtaining true beliefs.

I think that the requirement of knowledge is defeasible, if that just means that there can be reasons of other sorts that outweigh it, so that all things considered one may (and sometimes should) still assert that p even though one doesn’t know that p. But it’s not, if being defeasible means that given appropriate other reasons, there is nothing wrong with making an assertion without knowing.
