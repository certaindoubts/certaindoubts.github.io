The evidential value of pedigree

Posted on September 10, 2009 by John TurriSeptember 10, 2009

You’re on a search committee in the final stage of a hiring decision. It’s down to two candidates, D and U. D and U are indistinguishable in almost every respect: each earned the PhD last year, and has spent a year as a visiting assistant professor at a small liberal arts college; each has a glowing letter from an advisor who is very well respected in the field; each has a very strong letter from one very well respected scholar in the field, who is not affiliated with either the PhD-granting institution, or the candidate’s current appointment; each has impressive teaching credentials; each has three solid publications in excellent journals; each gave a great job talk, and got along well with the department and administrators on their visit; each has a promising research agenda. In all those respects, you are hard-pressed to discern any meaningful difference between U and D. Each is also of the same gender and ethnicity.

But they do obviously differ in this respect: D earned the PhD from a distinguished university with a world renowned philosophy department, whereas U earned the PhD from an undistinguished university with a serviceable but unrenowned philosophy department.

Question: at this final stage of the process, does this last bit of information provide you with good evidence for thinking that you should prefer either U over D, or D over U? If so, why?

Feel free to import standards peculiar to your university or department — or if you’d prefer, to some generic university or department of type X — if that’s needed to yield a definite answer.

  9. Jon Kvanvig on September 10, 2009 at 6:20 pm said:

    yes, Bryan, read the order of evaluation I note in comment 3 above. Publication record comes first, pedigree farther down the list.

    I note one further point though. Fairness here is a matter of adequate procedures, and procedural fairness will always leave open the possibility and often the actuality of individual exceptions. Fair trial arrangements will sometimes let the guilty go free, for example. So sometimes candidate X would have actually been better in the long run than candidate Y, and yet our fair procedures, indeed our best procedures, favor hiring Y. But there is no alternative to adopting procedures of evaluation here, and whatever procedures are adopted there will be cases of just such sorts. So pointing out the possibility or actuality of mistakes doesn’t help much unless accompanied by a suggestion about what procedures will work better overall.

  15. Clayton on September 11, 2009 at 11:25 am said:

    Oops, “During my early years, the department was not mentioned in the overall rankings of the Leiter Report in action theory, epistemology, moral psychology, and maybe meta-ethics” should have read, “the department was not mentioned in the overall rankings but was mentioned in the rankings for action theory, epistemology, and moral psychology”.     As for the general issue, it seems to me that:
    (i) Lack of pedigree is the absence of a positive and in this sort of market that might function as a negative, but I don’t think there’s any justification for using the absence of pedigree as a screen to exclude someone from consideration, particularly when there’s other evidence of ability on the CV.

    (ii) Lack of accomplishment doesn’t seem to me to be the absence of a positive, it seems that it is some indication of a negative. It shows that when you finished your Ph.D. you have not reached the point where you can publish or present papers at the APA. That seems like some evidence that you could use a little extra time to ripen. Since this isn’t Logan’s Run, you don’t literally get shot out of a tube into the radioactive wasteland when you don’t get a job the first year out. Given the glut of candidates that have been on the market or are leaving graduate school with pubs and/or conferences, I think it’s not obviously rational to pick someone with pedigree and no accomplishments over those who have accomplished something.
    (iii) Someone might say in response to the last remark that there are departments that encourage their graduate students to do nothing. Okay. Then it seems like there’s some evidence that there are some departments that do not do a good job training their graduate students to do what must be done to get tenure and contribute to a literature. That might undermine the justification for saying that the candidate is not qualified for a position, but it doesn’t seem to me that the response to this is to proceed as if we have great evidence that this candidate should get a job. They are something of an unknown and given the presence of candidates known to be of quality, it seems that a wait and see attitude might be the right one. And, by wait and see, I mean, wait and see what they do in the years to come working for someone else. It would be nice if we all got TT jobs straight out of graduate school, but that’s not possible. Some people just need to sweat it out for a few years before the tenure clock starts. It did me some good, I’m sure I’m not unique in this respect.
    (iv) There’s a really bad sort of inference that runs rampant in the anonymous discussions on some blogs. X counts as a better candidate than Y because X went to a better program than Y where ‘better’ is understood in terms of overall ranking of X’s department compared to Y’s. The justification for this is often that X received better training than Y and/or that X must have been better coming out of undergrad than Y was. I don’t think that we can assume that a department’s rank on the Leiter Report correlates with the kind of training that graduate students receive at those departments. That seems like the sort of thing that we’d want empirical evidence for. What would that empirical evidence look like? Who knows. I’d say go with publications, but that’s why I’d use publications to rank the candidates in first place and why I would only use pedigree if I had no other evidence (something that I suspect almost never happens in this sort of job market).
