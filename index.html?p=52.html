Testimony and Holistic Theories of Justification

Posted on July 12, 2004 by Jon KvanvigJune 20, 2012

In the discussion on testimonial knowledge here, the question of holism crops up (read the comments if you’re interested to find out why). In order for testimonial knowledge to require knowledge at its source, the knowledge must be “purely” testimonial (and even then, the thesis that testimonial knowledge requires knowledge at its source is problematic). But consider the more limited thesis about pure testimonial knowledge. On it, if the testifier had a defeater and hence didn’t know, and the defeater was communicated along with the claim at issue, then one knows only if (it is said) one has an overrider for the defeater.

The crucial move is this: if the overrider allows one to know, then one’s knowledge is no longer purely testimonial. This maneuver raises the question of holism, for in any case of knowledge, one’s belief system has to lack undefeated defeaters of one’s evidence for the claim in question. So everyone (restricted quantifier) holds, as far as I know, that justification (or whatever surrogate term you prefer) is holistic to this extent. So if the existence of an overrider in the testimony case renders the resulting belief not purely testimonial, does the negative condition also render all beliefs “impure” (i.e., there is no such thing as a purely testimonial belief nor a purely perceptual belief nor a purely memorial belief, etc.) If so, the thesis that pure testimonial knowledge requires knowledge at the source is trivial.

So what’s the difference between having an overrider and not having an undefeated defeater in this context? Is there a way to clarify the difference so that purity is lost by the first and not by the second that is not guilty of ad-hockery?

  2. jon kvanvig on July 15, 2004 at 7:30 am said:

    Fritz, I think there is no careful definition, but here’s two versions. One is coherentist-sounding: the justification of a belief depends on the entire belief system in which it is embedded (and all we need now is a clarification of “depends on”…). The other is simply a result of the defeasible character of evidential support: for p to be justified by q for S at t, the body of evidence for S at t must be free of ultimately undefeated defeaters.

    The second one gives some hope of clarifying the distinction between q justifying p as a positive relation, and the defeat relation as a merely negative one. If we can do this, then we can say that the positive elements are part of the justificatory base for p, but the negative ones aren’t.

    The problem is that this characterization is not theory-neutral–coherentists aren’t going to go for it. Maybe that’s too bad for them, but when we clarify the nature of testimony, the more theory-neutrality we can have, the better.

    Here’s some shameless self-promotion: I’ve argued in Coherentists’ Distractions that coherentists can identify subparts of the entire system that play a central role in the story of justification, even though these parts don’t convey any degree of justification on their own. One way to think about them is in terms of INUS conditions for warrant or justification. This way of thinking about coherentism allows the coherentist to explain what it is for a belief to be properly based without having to say absurd things such as that the entire belief system must cause the belief in question.

    *Maybe* something like this approach can be used as well to distinguish positive from negative elements of a belief system. If we take the class of INUS conditions for warrant in the system, we may be able to define “justificatory base” in terms of it. The rest of the system would play a role, but not as part of the justificatory base.

    So maybe this approach can secure more theory-neutrality for Audi’s distinction between justificatory and causal bases…
