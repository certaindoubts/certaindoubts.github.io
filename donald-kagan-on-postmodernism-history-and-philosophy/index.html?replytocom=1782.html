Donald Kagan on Postmodernism, History, and Philosophy

Posted on May 13, 2005 by Kvanvig JonMay 13, 2005

Kagan gave the 34th Jefferson Lecture in the Humanities last night, a lecture sponsored by NEH and which is the highest honor given by the federal government in the humanities. Kagan spent his time defending the preeminence of history in the Academy, and also bashing postmodernism and its influences. So he’s half-right; and the half-wrong part has my ire up. So here goes.

The mistaken part goes like this,

Although historians in universities have given far too much ground to such mindlessness promoted by contemporary political partisanship, as historians they are better situated than their colleagues in the other humanities to recover their senses. They know that the current fad of skepticism and relativism is as old as the Sophists of ancient Greece and had a great revival with the Pyrrhonism of the 16th century. On both occasions their paradoxical and self-contradictory glamour yielded in time to common sense and the massive evidence that some searches are more objective, some things truer than others, however elusive perfect objectivity and truth may be.

I applaud the denigration of postmodern tendencies, and thankfully inhabit a discipline that has well-entrenched opposition to such nonsense. But Kagan thinks history is best suited for “recovering our senses.” Such garbage!

First, let me introduce Kagan to a discipline that hasn’t succumbed and so doesn’t need to recover its senses. Second, the intellectual heritage of the view, as Kagan rightly notes, is philosophical and those whose senses need restoration will need a restoration of philosophical sensibility, not (just) historical perspective. Moreover, without resorting to philosophical argumentation to restore such sensibility, all the historian has to offer is the insipid idea that such viewpoints will “yield in time” to better viewpoints. A much better strategy is to give an argument that the view is implausible and has theoretically untenable consequences–but then, of course, one would be doing philosophy, not history.

Kagan goes on to defend his view of history as “Queen of the Humanities, standing between and slightly above her noble handmaidens, the muses of literature and philosophy.”

If we cannot look simply to moral guidance firmly founded on religious precepts, it is natural and reasonable to turn to history, the record of human experience, as a necessary supplement, if not a substitute. History, it seems to me, is the most useful key we have to open the mysteries of the human predicament.

Such thinking doesn’t pass the scrutiny we all use in assessing freshman writing in Intro to Philosophy! If it’s moral guidance we want, it is not clear that such falls within the purview of the Academy. But to the extent that the study of morality and the mysteries of the human predicament fall within the purview of the Academy, it falls squarely within the discipline of philosophy. That’s not to say that history isn’t relevant, but rather to make the point that this is the wrong way to defend the preeminence of history, and one quite good way to defend the preeminence of philosophy!

    Allow me to express an opinion between yours, Jon, and Kagan’s. You are right that the idea “this too shall pass” is pretty insipid. However, I think two other points need to be made. For one, historical awareness has a “softening up” effect; my Ancient & Medieval students were much more ready to question the skepticism and relativism they saw around them, when they realized that the world had seen this movie before. I expect a good class in modern philosophy (I’ve never taught one) could accomplish the same thing. But that’s teaching history (albeit history of philosophy).

    Second, if arguments were all that were needed, it would be difficult to explain why this syndrome flares up repeatedly (not just in Western cultures), and when it does. Did philosophers remember the anti-skeptical arguments of Augustine for 1200 years, only to forget them in the 16th century? And then did they remember them for another 400 years, only to forget them in the 20th c? I don’t think so. Skepticism and relativism have causes that are basically sociological and historical–exposure to multiple perspectives, too many competing theoretical strains, and recent untenability of long-established beliefs. Pointing that out– *with* a dose of philosophical argument–is worthwhile.
