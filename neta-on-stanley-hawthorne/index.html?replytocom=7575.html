    Here’s an obvious objection to the JBK-Reasons Principle: one doesn’t need to consider the question whether one knows P in order for it to be rationally permissible to treat P as a reason for acting. So, one doesn’t need to form the belief that one knows. If that’s right, then perhaps the JBK-Reasons Principle should be formulated in terms of propositional justification, rather than doxastic justification.

    How does this square with Case 2? Well, suppose you know P and you have justification to believe that you know P, but in fact you don’t believe you know P because you are neurotically diffident about your memory. In that case, I’m inclined to say that given your justification to believe that you know P, you also have justification (or rational permission if you like) to treat P as a reason for acting.

    Of course, given your neurotic diffidence, you wouldn’t be fully rational even if you did treat P as a reason for acting. But the same point applies if do you have a justified belief that you know P, but owing to neurotic diffidence, you fail to believe that you know that you know P, despite the fact that you have justification to believe that you know that you know. Irrationality at the higher order always brings with it an element of overall irrationality.

    So, why not do it all in terms of propositional, rather than doxastic, justification?

  5. Clayton on September 20, 2007 at 11:42 am said:

    [Pre-emptive disclaimer. The following question will anger the gods of epistemic internalism. Hopefully the pre-emptive disclaimer that acknowledges this fact will appease them to some degree].

    Suppose we combine Neta’s principle and a Reasonable Assumption:
    NP: Where S’s choice is p-dependent, it is rationally permissible for S to treat the proposition that p as a reason for acting if and only if S justifiably believes that she knows that p.
    RA: It is possible that S justifiably believes that she knows p and unknown to S, p is false.

    I think NP + RA = trouble.

    We’ll begin with a story:
    LOAN SHARK
    Moro is behind in his payments to Snake, the loan shark. Snake gave Moro a severe beating last week, with a warning that, if Moro missed another payment, he would be killed. The payment is due today but Moro doesn’t have the money. He borrows a can of mace and hangs out at Deffi’s Deli hoping Snake will leave him alone in public. He is shocked when he sees a man with a menacing look he takes to be Snake come in and walk straight towards him. Moro says, ‘I won’t let you get me Snake!’ and he pulls out his can of mace and takes aim

    The story might continue in one of two ways. First ending:

    A
    Deffi has a can of mace. He knows that the man Moro takes to be Snake is Snake’s twin brother. He also knows that while his twin looks dangerous, he isn’t a threat to anyone. Deffi knows that to disarm Moro, he’ll have to mace him. He does so, intervening on behalf of Snake’s brother.

    Concerning LOAN SHARK A, it seems natural to say two things:
    (1) Deffi is justified in intervening on behalf of the man Moro intends to Mace.
    (2) Moro has lost a right to non-interference he’d otherwise have if he didn’t intend to mace the guy.
    From (2), I think it makes sense to say:
    (3) Moro should have acted otherwise.
    But then, by NP it follows that:
    (4) Moro was in a position to justifiably believe that his intended course of action was not justified, permissible, such as to not be a course of action that went in a different direction, etc…

    But, (4) is false.

    Objection: You cannot infer from the fact that (a) someone was justified in interfering in a way that would predictably lead to injury that (b) Moro lost the right to non-interference because Moro acted impermissibly. Think of cases in which non-combatants have to decide whether to (a) take arms against just combatants who are about to perform an action that would lead to the deaths of such non-combatants as acceptable side-effects or (b) take up arms and fire on them in the hopes of defending themselves from being killed as side-effects in a just war. It seems that morality would allow someone in such a situation to choose between (a) or (b). Audrey, let us say, has to choose between firing at an incoming plane flown by a pilot that is part of a just, liberating force or do nothing, allow the plane to drop its bombs, and obliterate the apartment complex in which she’s living.

    Response: Fair enough. So, consider a second version of LOAN SHARK:
    B
    Deffi has a can of mace. He knows that the man walking in is Snake and that Snake is here to kill Moro. He also knows that Moro’s can of mace is empty, which means that Moro cannot defend himself. As Snake approaches Moro, Deffi maces Snake to protect him.

    Concerning A and B, it seems intuitive to say that the range of permissible options available to Deffi in A and B differ. So, consider

    ASYMMETRY: The best explanation as to why the range of permissible options available to Deffi is more limited than the range of permissible options available to Audrey is that Deffi was in a position to intervene on behalf of only one agent whose actions would be justified if carried out.

    It seems that unless you reject Asymmetry, you have to concede that there are at least some situations in which the correctness of a belief is a necessary condition on having acted on such a belief. But, then, either RA is false or NP is false. The obvious way of blocking the argument is to say that the permissibility of an action that is p-dependent is not itself a necessary condition on holding the belief concerning p. That’s not how I’d do it, but it’s a possible response.

    Anyway, I thought I’d run it up the flagpole and then run for cover…

  14. Declan Smithies on September 20, 2007 at 9:33 pm said:

    So, for all the participants in this debate, there’s an important question: what is it to take P as a reason for action?

    I agree that there is a metacognitive notion of taking P as a reason for action, which involves explicitly conceptualizing P as a reason for action. And it’s very plausible that metacognitive concepts are required for this kind of metacognitive act.

    But isn’t there another notion of taking P as a reason for action which applies whenever I act for the reason that P? This is what makes the difference between (i) the case in which I act for the reason that P and (ii) the case in which P is a reason that I have to act, and I do act, but not for the reason that P. In this sense, taking P as a reason for action is just a matter of performing your action on the basis of the reason that you have. It’s not obvious that this requires having any metacognitive concepts.

    I guess I’m wary of the assumption that performing an action on the basis of reasons requires conceptualizing one’s reasons as such. Interestingly, Stanley and Hawthorne seem to make this assumption when they write, “We need to distinguish between the existence of a reason for acting and appreciating that reason in such a way as to make it your reason for action.”

  15. kvanvig on September 21, 2007 at 7:44 am said:

    Hi Ram, I really like the Hannah-Mordecai example and your diagnosis of it. If I remember correctly, H&S had a Vietnam example they attributed to Jim Pryor of the same sort, except where the complaint that you don’t know was supposed to be legitimate. I remember thinking on reading the example that it wasn’t persuasive, that it depended on the kind of evidence the wife had that her husband had been killed. If the evidence is casual and of the hearsay variety, the complaint is warranted. But if there’s an involved story, testimony from fellow soldiers, pieces of jewelry returned with dogtags, etc., the complaint becomes bizarre.

    On practical reasoning, I think I’m sharing Declan’s worry, though. Here’s a short piece of practical reasoning. I’m wondering what to do this weekend. I think to myself: I could go to the football game, or I could go to that Spyro Gyra concert; I like both, but the opportunity to see S.G. is so rare compared with the opportunity to for football games, so I think I’ll go to the concert, since variety in pleasures makes life much more interesting. This reasoning is fairly explicit, and we can ask what has to be true about the premises for things to be OK from a rational point of view. But notice that I don’t employ, and don’t need, the concepts of knowledge, proposition, reasoning, premises, or conclusions to engage in this reasoning.

    This is no help to the knowledge account, though, and if accepted, only requires minor changes to the JBK account. One could adopt the view that one’s reasons for accepting the premises have to be strong enough to also justify the claim that one knows the premises to be true. That proposal differs from yours in two small ways. First, it substitutes propositional justification for the doxastic justification that your principle proposes, and second, it replaces the metabelief in your proposal with belief in the premises of the argument itself (and requires that this belief be justified). (A piece of self-promotion: I argue in my contribution to the “Williamson and his critics” volume that such an account handles all of the data that Tim uses to support the knowledge view: lottery sentences, Moorean stuff, etc.)

    Now, perhaps your proposal guarantees belief in the premises anyway, on grounds that you couldn’t justifiably believe that you know p if you didn’t believe p. I’m not sure that this will work, but if it does, then the only difference is the first one above plus the requirement that the belief in the premises be justified or rational. If it doesn’t work, though, then there is a common legitimate kind of criticism that your proposal isn’t able to block. Discouraged students often say to each other, “I’m never going to succeed at college, I’m just going to quit,” only to hear from a friend, “Look, that’s just discouragement talking–you don’t really believe that you can’t succeed.” Of course, this isn’t a case where the discouraged student justifiably believes that he knows he can’t succeed, so it’s not a counterexample to anything you said. But if you can satisfy the JBK principle without having the belief in question, it is hard to see how noting the metabelief would block the force of the criticism.

  18. Clayton on September 21, 2007 at 9:43 am said:

    Ram,

    What one is justified in believing is one thing — what inferences it is wrong for one to make is another thing. (I assume that “wrong”, as you’re using it, applies to acts, e.g., macing people and drawing inferences, not to states like belief — right?)
