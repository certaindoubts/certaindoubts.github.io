Lehrer’s Pain/Itch Example

Posted on March 5, 2005 by Kvanvig JonMarch 5, 2005

My son is taking an honors intro to philosophy course, and just encountered this example. He is completely unimpressed with the example, and that raised the question for me of whether the example is convincing at all and whether there are more convincing examples. The example is meant to show that we might be mistaken even about the contents of our own minds.

Here’s how the example goes, for those unfamiliar with it.

You go to the doctor complaining of an itch. He listens to your complaint, observes the location of the itch, writes down how the problem started, and the details about physical symptoms including duration and intensity of the experience. Then he tells you that he thinks he knows what the problem is. He tells you that it’s not really an itch, but a pain. People have confused these two in the past, but we now have a well-confirmed theory that distinguishes the two in a slightly different way than “the folk” do. The theory has led to two technologies. One is a machine for distinguishing the two underlying states, and the other is medicine for treating the two conditions. Your doctor tells you that one of the medicines will solve the problem if you’re experiencing a pain, but not the itch; and the other medicine will have the alternative results. You insist that you’re experiencing an itch, but he uses the machine and shows you the results: you’re in pain, it says. If you still insist, he’ll give you the itch medicine. You do, and he does; you return two weeks later, still suffering, and ask for the pain medicine. You take it and get well. So you say, “I guess I was wrong. It was a pain, not an itch, after all!”

So, two questions: is the example convincing, and if not, are there better examples to undermine the claim that we are infallible about our own mental states?

  7. Jonah Schupbach on March 6, 2005 at 10:29 am said:

    I agree with your son. I too am “completely unimpressed with this example”; in fact, I actually experience a bit of pain in the form of a headache whenever I attempt to understand how this argument by itself could possibly convince anyone. Judging from the list of people who do find this argument and similar arguments persuasive, I think that I must be missing something. Anyway, here is my response to Lehrer’s argument:

    First, I think that it is intuitively obvious that Lehrer is wrong. Try to imagine the situation as Lehrer depicts it: You feel an itch; thus, you believe B: “I feel an itch.” The doctor says, “No; bad news! I just ran the info through the pain/itch machine and it turns out that you actually are feeling pain.” Doesn’t it seem obvious that you would just know that the doctor is wrong? How would the doctor have calibrated the machine or tested its accuracy in the first place – presumably by matching its results with the first person reports of test subjects! What kind of info would the doctor plug into the machine in order to get results? – “How the problem started” and “physical symptoms”? But even if this info makes it immensely probable that you have a condition that would put you in pain, it is still the case that if you are experiencing an itch, then you feel an itch – not pain. Pertaining to the meds, as I ponder the situation, if the itch eventually was eased with the aid of pain medicine, I certainly would not thereby conclude that I must have had a pain because the pain meds worked. I would think, “thank goodness the itch is gone…that’s funny that the pain medicine took it away and the itch medicine didn’t.” Again, how would the medicines have gotten their respective names originally; presumably by matching which medicine relieves which first personal experience. Now I know that intuition is not an infallible guide to philosophical truth; however, given the persuasive power of such intuitions, I for one am at least prima facie suspicious of Lehrer’s examples.

    Second, one can deny Lehrer’s main point and still allow that people can sometimes confuse itches with pains – or other experiences. This point does not seem to me to establish Lehrer’s thesis that we can be mistaken about the contents of our minds. If I do state that I have an itch when I actually have a pain, my mistake reflects a mislabelling of that which I am experiencing; my mistake does not reflect the fact that I was actually experiencing pain although my first person experience was of itchiness. Thus, contra Lehrer, I don’t think that simply pointing out that people have confused labels of mental content is the knock-down argument against the view that we can have certainty about our mental content itself. Perhaps it is best to restate this point using the idea of indexicals: It may be the case that I cannot possibly be mistaken in my beliefs of the form, “I am experiencing this now” even if one can be mistaken about beliefs such as B. When one begins to attempt to label the “this,” he may run into mislabelling problems; however, this does not mean that he is thus uncertain that he is experiencing the “this.”

    Again, I may well be missing something here. The fact that so many people who are smarter than I am find the argument compelling makes me suspicious of my own views on the argument; however, these are my views at least for the time being.

  21. Kristoffer Ahlstrom on March 8, 2005 at 4:59 pm said:

    Jonah, you’re probably right in that the doctor (or rather the scientist responsible for the technology the doctor uses) has calibrated the machine or tested its accuracy on first person reports of test subjects. But I’m not sure this makes it impossible for him/her (i.e., the doctor) to correct the patient in Lehrer’s example.

    As Matt points out, we may very well want to correct statements based on the investigation of one property (Matt’s examples being felt solidity) in light of what we find out about the sub-structure of the stuff (Matt’s example being glass) in question. Analogously, the researchers responsible for the medical practice in Lehrer’s example may have found certain (perhaps striking) correlations between certain brain states with situations in which subjects report pain, and certain other brain states with situations in which subjects report itch. This does not, however, necessarily rule out borderline cases, i.e., situations in which some subjects report pain and some itch, even though they are all in the same kind of brain state.

    The question now, of course, is: what are we to make of such borderline cases? Are we to conclude that pain/itch does not correspond to one particular brain state, but maybe an amalgam of different brain states that we have haven’t yet been able to specify? Or are we to say that for certain particularly complex or elusive states, subjects are sometimes simply mistaken about their inner mental life?

    In Lehrer’s example, one might want to argue that the researchers have opted for the latter, and that the subject, furthermore, happens to be in one of those borderline cases. And if so, it is probably not enough to talk about a mere instance of mislabeling on the subject’s part.

    Does this make sense? It’s a very tricky example, and I’m not sure whether the above reasoning rests on a distinction that is applicable in the non-phenomenal case (e.g., solidity) but not in the phenomenal case at issue here (pain/itch). In any case, the above was my initial reaction to Lehrer’s case and Jonah’s/Matt’s reply.

Then I move out of New York City to go to college in Kansas, and I find out I’m not in NYC any more. Still, having made it to college, I am able to do the translations for how these mid-country dwellars misidentify animals. “When I think of cats, I think of those things that Kansas folk think of when they say they are thinking of dogs.”

    Now the trick, with Lehrer’s example, is the supposed 2nd or 3rd person authority of the machine. Assuming the machine cannot be mistaken, that it knows better than we do, the example might work.

    I think that a better example is a counselor example. You go to a psychiatrist to talk about some difficulty you are having. After weeks of “therapy” (actually paying large sums to have someone listen to you talk, hoping that at some point you’ll get some advice), the counselor “reveals” something about yourself that you were not aware of. It seems that the counselor had a better grasp of your own mental states than you did. Of course, if the counseling is to help with your difficulties, you will have to come to own or accept the counselor’s account of you. You have to come to believe that yes, she is right. But then, perhaps, you are back to 1st person authority.
