Still, I may not have missed the point. It might not at all provide evidence to undermine 1st person authority. Suppose I had mildly malicious parents who taught me that cats are animals that pee on fire hydrants and go woof woof, that cows race in the Kentucky Derby and go neigh, that–well you get the picture. Now the trick, with Lehrer’s example, is the supposed 2nd or 3rd person authority of the machine. Assuming the machine cannot be mistaken, that it knows better than we do, the example might work.

I think that a better example is a counselor example. You go to a psychiatrist to talk about some difficulty you are having. After weeks of “therapy” (actually paying large sums to have someone listen to you talk, hoping that at some point you’ll get some advice), the counselor “reveals” something about yourself that you were not aware of. It seems that the counselor had a better grasp of your own mental states than you did. Of course, if the counseling is to help with your difficulties, you will have to come to own or accept the counselor’s account of you. You have to come to believe that yes, she is right. But then, perhaps, you are back to 1st person authority.

I have a pain in my arm. Yet, it turns out this is a ghost effect after an amputation. Now am I infallible about having the pain in my arm? Clearly not, since I don’t have the arm.

I think the infallibilist will answer that we ought separate out the pain from the content of the pain. They might say that the content – say “pain in my arm” is inferred. I don’t think we experience it that way. We experience it as a whole. The other choice is to say that I’m infallible about having an experience that appears to be a pain in my arm, but really it isn’t. But that seems an odd choice to make. After all if the location of the pain can be an false appearance why can’t the rest of the experience also be wrong? Clearly I’m having an experience. But what kind of experience it is I’m not sure I necessarily can know infallibly. I don’t just mean the label I ought give it, but the actual type.

]]> By: craig http://certaindoubts.com/lehrers-painitch-example/#comment-1517 Thu, 10 Mar 2005 19:08:54 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=271#comment-1517 I am also unconvinced by the pain/itch example. If I feel itch, no instrumentation offered by a 3rd party is going to persuade me that I feel anything but itch. But I think there is a base problem that should be addressed first: there is an implicit assumption here that there is a unitary mind that is in agreement about what it believes / feels / etc. There are strong anecdotal indications that that isn’t so.

For one, the example of children who don’t know their own feelings. Similarly, autistic people are known to be unaware consciously about their own internal states. In common speech, a person is said to be “of two minds” on an issue, and from introspection I can see that there is a real feeling of having two warring factions within ones own self.

Similarly, failures of will (as when dieting or exercising or doing other chores), where you know, consciously, what you should be doing, and yet you don’t do it. What better explanation that that some part of you doesn’t agree that it’s the best course of action?

Have you ever felt “compelled” to finish an unpleasant act? I have. Frequently, my wife will tune into some hideous movie on one of the “women’s” channels, and although I don’t want to, and I know in advance I won’t like it, I can’t resist the urge to watch the wretched thing through to the end.

These and other data points, including the actual physical structure of brains, give the lie to the idea that your mind is a singular, atomic thing.

Such a viewpoint helps to explain the root canal example: your conscious mind is relieved by the laughing gas, but some other, subconscious part of your mind is still fully aware of the pain, and responding in the usual way.

Likewise, the feeling of fear experienced on roller coasters. You know, intellectually, that they are perfectly safe. But once on the ride, your heart races, and your adrenalin pumps just as though you were in danger.

Maybe researchers have discovered that the vast majority of what people call ‘pains’ correlate with C-fiber firings–and that sensation S, which everyone calls an itch, also correlates with C-fiber firings. If I wanted to make that point I would use something like Williamson’s arguments against luminosity–say, if you gradually dial up the sensation on someone, there can be a point where they’ll still insist they aren’t feeling any pain, even though in fact they are. ]]> By: Kristoffer Ahlstrom http://certaindoubts.com/lehrers-painitch-example/#comment-1514 Tue, 08 Mar 2005 21:59:16 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=271#comment-1514 Jonah, you’re probably right in that the doctor (or rather the scientist responsible for the technology the doctor uses) has calibrated the machine or tested its accuracy on first person reports of test subjects. But I’m not sure this makes it impossible for him/her (i.e., the doctor) to correct the patient in Lehrer’s example.

As Matt points out, we may very well want to correct statements based on the investigation of one property (Matt’s examples being felt solidity) in light of what we find out about the sub-structure of the stuff (Matt’s example being glass) in question. Analogously, the researchers responsible for the medical practice in Lehrer’s example may have found certain (perhaps striking) correlations between certain brain states with situations in which subjects report pain, and certain other brain states with situations in which subjects report itch. This does not, however, necessarily rule out borderline cases, i.e., situations in which some subjects report pain and some itch, even though they are all in the same kind of brain state.

The question now, of course, is: what are we to make of such borderline cases? Are we to conclude that pain/itch does not correspond to one particular brain state, but maybe an amalgam of different brain states that we have haven’t yet been able to specify? Or are we to say that for certain particularly complex or elusive states, subjects are sometimes simply mistaken about their inner mental life?

In Lehrer’s example, one might want to argue that the researchers have opted for the latter, and that the subject, furthermore, happens to be in one of those borderline cases. And if so, it is probably not enough to talk about a mere instance of mislabeling on the subject’s part.

Does this make sense? It’s a very tricky example, and I’m not sure whether the above reasoning rests on a distinction that is applicable in the non-phenomenal case (e.g., solidity) but not in the phenomenal case at issue here (pain/itch). In any case, the above was my initial reaction to Lehrer’s case and Jonah’s/Matt’s reply.
