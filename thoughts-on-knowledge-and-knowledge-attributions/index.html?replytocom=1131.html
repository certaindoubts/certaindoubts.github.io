Thoughts on Knowledge and Knowledge Attributions

Posted on December 14, 2004 by Kvanvig JonDecember 14, 2004

Sosa complains that contextualism is not a theory of knowledge, but only of knowledge attributions. The complaint arises because contextualists clarify their approach in terms of what it takes for a knowledge attribution to be true in a context.

Suppose we assume that a contextualist theory is developed by considering the following schema:
‘S knows that p’ is true in C iff X,
where each particular theory yields a different value for X.

The question I want to consider is how to assuage Sosa’s concern.

So consider an analogy, one developed on behalf of Sosa’s concern. I want a theory of the self, and the proposal is to offer instead a semantic theory of first person indexicals (since, whatever the self is, it is what is referred to by first-person indexicals). Such a theory would fit the schema:
‘I have characteristic P’ is true in C iff Y.
If the theory is a semantic one, the value for Y will presumably talk about the speaker of the utterance and the property P (if the predicative expression in question has a uniform semantic value). But no such semantic theory could give me what I want if I’m wanting a theory of the self. It is true that whatever the self is, it’s the sort of thing referred to by first-person indexicals, though perhaps also referred to by other linguistic expressions as well. But even if we supplement the semantic theory of indexicals with semantic theories of all ways of referring to selves, we won’t scratch the itch of wanting a theory of the self.

So, returning to contextualism, it looks as if no value for X in the above schema could answer Sosa’s complaint, if the value for X is chosen to yield a semantical truth explaining how our use of ‘knows’ is contextual. Is there some other way of filling in a value for X that would be both contextualist and assuage Sosa’s concern? I don’t know of any, and maybe that’s Sosa’s point. Maybe the point is that you can find a value for X if you wish, but you’ll have to do something else entirely to give a theory of knowledge. If that is right, then no value for X, and nothing that appeals to any instance of the above schema could assuage Sosa’s concern.

The question is whether this is right. Perhaps it is best thought of as a challenge, so instead of trying to answer Sosa’s complaint directly, contextualists ought to try to say how an instance of the above schema could constitute a theory of knowledge.

    Contextualism by itself just doesn’t give you a theory of knowledge, if I’m understanding what you mean by that correctly, so your challenge simply will not be met. There are contextualist as well as invariantist versions of most of the theories of knowledge out there. The claim I make for contextualism is not that it provides a theory of knowledge, but that he truth of contextualism is vital information in the search for a theory of knowledge, and also for solving various epistemological puzzles. Here’s how I put the general point in “Contextualism: An Explanation and Defense”:

    Contextualism, so understood, then, is a position about knowledge attributions (sentences attributing knowledge to a subject) and denials of knowledge â?? precisely, a thesis about their truth-conditions. This has been known to give rise to the following type of outburst: “Your contextualism isn’t a theory about knowledge at all; it’s just a theory about knowledge attributions. As such, it’s not a piece of epistemology at all, but of the philosophy of language.”
    Of the many things that can be said in response to this type of charge, let me limit myself here to just this. To the extent that contextualism/invariantism is an issue in the philosophy of language, it’s a piece of philosophy of language that is of profound importance epistemology. How we should proceed in studying knowledge will be greatly affected by how we come down on the contextualism/invariantism issue. For contextualism opens up possibilities for dealing with issues and puzzles in epistemology which, of course, must be rejected if invariantism is instead correct the correct position. And how could it be otherwise? Those who work on the problem of free will and determinism should of course be very interested in the issue of what it means to call an action “free”. If that meant different things in different contexts, and we didn’t realize this, then all sorts of problems could arise from a failure to recognize this shift in meaning. If there is no such shift, then that too will be vital information. In either case, one will want to know what such claims mean. Likewise, it’s important in studying knowledge to discern what it means to say someone knows something. If that can mean different things in different contexts, all sorts of problems in epistemology, and not just in philosophy of language, will arise from a failure to recognize such shifts in meaning. If, on the other hand, there is no such shift, then we’re bound to fall into all sorts of error about knowledge, as well as about “knows”, if we think such shifts occur.

    As for the relevance of contextualism to the problem of skepticism, I have some remarks about that, responding specifically to Ernie’s worries, in section 8 (“Contextualist Solutions to Skepticism”) of my “Sosa, Safety, Sensitivity, and Skeptical Hypotheses,” which is available on-line at http://pantheon.yale.edu/~kd47/OLP.htm

  3. Jonathan Schaffer on December 15, 2004 at 8:24 am said:

    I think Keith puts things well when he says that contextualism is “a piece of philosophy of language that is of profound importance to epistemology.” Indeed, the contextualist is committed to the following radical thesis about the knowledge relation: there is no single Ksp relation.

    Here’s why the contextualist has this commitment. What is constitutive of contextualism is the recognition of cases in which attributor1 may truly say “s knows that p”, and attributor2 may truly say “s does not know that p” (for the same s and p, concerning the same time t). If what attributor1 truly said was that Ksp obtains, and what attributor2 truly said was that ~Ksp obtains, the contextualist would be stuck with a flat contradiction: Ksp&~Ksp.

    What the contextualist must think is that there is some additional shifting parameter that prevents what attributor1 truly said from contradicting what attributor2 truly said. One way to do this is to add indices on K, so that attributor1 says K-sub-1 sp and attributor2 says ~K-sub-2 sp. This is a consequence of the standard contextualist treatment of “knows” as an indexical. It leads to epistemic pluralism: there are many binary epistemic relations of the form K-sub-n sp. A second way to add a parameter is to add a third argument place to K, so that attributor1 says Kspq1 and attributor2 says Kspq2. This leads to something like contrastivism: there are no binary epistemic relations, but rather a single ternary epistemic relation of the form Kspq.

    Either way, contextualists are committed radicals about the knowledge relation.

  6. Jonathan Schaffer on December 15, 2004 at 9:21 pm said:

    Keith: I’m not sure there is a standard usage of “contrastivism”. On my usage, the invariantist thinks that there is one binary epistemic relation: Ksp. The contextualist thinks “knows” is an indexical, and so winds up thinking there are many binary epistemic relations: K-sub-n sp. While the contrastivist thinks “knows” invariantly denotes a three-place relation, so winds up thinking that there are no binary epistemic relations. Instead there is one ternary epistemic relation: Kspq.

    So what might have seemed like a mere debate about the word “knows”, turns out to have profound implications for the structure of the knowledge relation(s).

  7. Keith DeRose on December 16, 2004 at 1:02 am said:

    I guess this is a question of what outflanks what. What I was thinking was that, according to the contrastivist (of either kind), A’s assertion “S knows that P” is true iff, for the selected q, Kspq. (So there’s your 3-place K relation.) On the invariantist version of the view, facts about S & her surroundings by themselves determine what contrast proposition gets selected. Thus, if Kspq holds for that selected q, then anyone, in any context, who asserts “S knows that P” asserts a truth, and anyone, in any context, who asserts “S doesn’t know that P” asserts a falsehood. On the contextualist version, S’s situation doesn’t, or at least doesn’t fully, select the q. Facts about A’s conversational context can affect which q gets selected. Thus, if Kspq1 holds, but Kspq2 doesn’t, and A1’s context (perhaps together with facts about S) selects Q1 and A2’s context (perhaps…) selects Q2, then “S knows that P” is truthfully asserted by A1 but falsely asserted by A2.

  8. Mikkel Gerken on December 16, 2004 at 6:08 am said:

    Perhaps Sosa’s worry can be recast as follows: Contextualism is not about knowledge but about attributive assertions of knowledge. The standard incompatibilist worry is then that the cases motivating contextualism are really about the pragmatics of assertion in general, not about the truth-conditions of knowledge in particular.
    I think I have a couple of interesting cases pertaining to this issue. I’d like to hear what people think of them. The background is familiar:
    In an airport case scenario we have two speakers, Al and Bob, in a hard and easy context respectively. There is an intuition that Al’s utterance ‘Ben knows that p’ is, somehow, incorrect. The attributer contextualist takes the intuition to be about truth-conditions of what is said. I.e., the intuition is taken to pertain to the truth conditions of the knowledge relation.
    The invariantist does not deny the intuition about the case. She merely differs in her account of what the intuition is about. The invariantist takes it to be about the propriety conditions of assertion in general. I.e., Al’s utterance is true, but conversationally inappropriate in a hard context.
    So the beef is not about whether there is an intuition of incorrectness in the airport case. Rather, it is about what the intuition is about. Roughly, according to the contextualist it is about the semantics of knowledge attributions, the invariantist takes it to be about the pragmatics of assertion in particular. Hence she doubts that we can learn anything about knowledge. I take the last point to be part of Sosa’s worry.
    I think that we can test, on linguistic grounds, whether our intuition that Al’s atterence is incorrect is about truth-conditions of knowledge or propriaty-conditions of assertion. Here are two test cases:
    Test 1: Enter Bill an airport passenger in a hard conversational context. It is important to Bill that p is the case, there are undefeated defeaters to p etc. Assume that Bill does not assert ‘Ben knows that p’, but merely ‘p’. But given his hard context (all the relevant defeaters to p etc.) we have an intuition that his assertion is somehow incorrect. Just like in the original case of Al. But there is no knowledge attribution to serve as the cause of our intuition. This provides linguistic evidence for the invariantist account.
    Test 2: Consider Jim, yet a passenger in a hard context. Like Al he utters ‘Ben knows that p’, but his utterance is not an assertion. Jim merely expresses his hope that Ben knows that p. In such a case we DO NOT have the intuition that his utterance is incorrect. It is OK to express one’s hope in the face of undefeated defeaters. Here the content of the utterance is the same and only the mode of utterance is changed. So it seems that our original intuition about Al’s utterance is generated by the fact that he ASSERTED (rather than by the fact that his assertion attributed knowledge). If so this test seems to favor an invariantist account of the cases as well.
    Of course, by subscribing to a de facto knowledge account of assertion DeRose has an account which is consistent with the verdict of the test cases. But many contextualists are reluctant to accept the knowledge account.
    Any thoughts?

  9. Jonathan Schaffer on December 16, 2004 at 8:10 am said:

    Keith: I think the invariantist version of contrastivism is unstable. If the contrast proposition q is a function of s’s situation (“subject factors” as you nicely call them), then q is a function of the s-t pair, in which case q is not an independent parameter. In that case the Ksp form (plus something like a world-time index) would suffice for the knowledge relation.

    In other words, in order for q to be a genuine third argument of K, q must be independent of s and p (and of the w-t pair). So something else must play a role in setting the value of q. This pretty much requires that q at least partially involve the attributor’s standpoint (“attributor factors”). So the only stable form of contrastivism (stable in preventing the third argument place for dropping off as redundant) is akin to contextualism in the sense that attributor factors play at least a partial role in determining q.

    Perhaps in your terminology this would make both theories forms of contextualism. So you might distinguish (i) indexical contextualism, on which attributor factors play a role in determining which of the many indexed binary epistemic relations is in play, from (ii) contrastive contextualism, on which attributor factors play a role in saturating the third argument place of the one and only ternary epistemic relation.
