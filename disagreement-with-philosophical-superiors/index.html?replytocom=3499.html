Disagreement with Philosophical Superiors

Posted on April 8, 2006 by francesApril 8, 2006

Suppose you think David Chalmers is wrong about some well-known thesis regarding phenomenal consciousness. Or, you think David Lewis got something fundamental wrong about the metaphysics of modality. Or, maybe you think Tim Williamson has got vagueness seriously wrong.

Are you just an arrogant dolt? Do you really think that you have bested *those* philosophers on *those* topics? Let’s suppose that you say P and they say not-P on those topics. If you have any self-knowledge at all, and you are a typical philosopher, then you should be disposed to admit all of the following regarding those philosophers on those topics and claim P:

They are generally more informed than I am on the topics involving P, they have more raw intelligence, they have thought and investigated whether P is true longer and in more depth than I have, they have thought about and investigated the topics surrounding P longer and in more depth than I have, they are just as or even more intellectually careful than I am, they are just as or even more intellectually honest than I am, and they have understood and fairly and thoroughly evaluated virtually all the evidence I have seen regarding P—and usually more evidence.

And yet here you are disagreeing with them on those very topics. What I’d like to read are opinions regarding (a) how often this situation arises amongst professional philosophers, and (b) whether the people in these positions are epistemically blameworthy in virtue of being in this position. For (a), I mean to ask how often it is that someone is disposed to admit everything in the previous paragraph and yet explicitly and knowingly disagrees with Chalmers/Williamson/Lewis on the truth-value of P. If you have time and answer (b) affirmatively, I’d be interested in hearing your opinion regarding the nature of the blameworthiness (is it irrationality, lack of justification (and if so, for which belief), etc).

I could say more as way of setting up the issues, but it’s probably wiser to let readers develop the issues in multiple ways. Thanks a million!

    Joachim, suppose you do come across an argument for P that strikes you as pretty good, where Lewis has held and argued for not-P for many years.     Dennis, I agree that whenever I find myself disagreeing with someone I admit to be my epistemic superior with regard to the topics surrounding our disagreement, I can often point to some other epistemic superior of mine who shares my belief. But why should that give me confidence that I should continue to accept P? Instead, shouldn’t I just be agnostic?

    Alex, yes, I’ve read Tom Kelly’s excellent article. However, he treats the case of epistemic equals, whereas I consider the case of my disagreeing with someone I explicitly admit to be my epistemic superior. That’s an important difference (for one thing, the notion of an epistemic equal is harder to characterize so that there ends up to be actual epistemic equals, in addition to mere possible ones). I also don’t think mere possible disagreement counts for much. Disagreement counts against me when it gives me reason to think that there is evidence against the actual truth of my belief.

    Timothy, I think that hero-worship is a big mistake in philosophy. But after one has had some conversations with people like Chalmers and Williamson, for instance, one sees that some people deserve their fame.

    Here is another way to think of the general problem:

    You say P, where P is some controversial bit of philosophy. Then your five-year old daughter says P. You’re not too worried that you might be mistaken. The fact that she disagrees with you gives you no good reason to doubt your belief. Then an undergraduate philosophy major of average abilities says P. You’re still not worried that your belief in P is mistaken. Then you discover that many of your epistemic peers regarding P and the topics surrounding P say that P. Now you’re a bit worried. Then you die, come face to face with God, and He says that the truth is definitely P. At this point only an idiot retains his belief in P. Surely at this point you have to admit you were wrong—or at least withhold judgment! It seems to me that there is a continuum of degree of blameworthiness. In the situation with the 5 year old, you’re not blameworthy (degree zero); in the situation with God you’re an idiot to retain your belief (just about the highest degree of blameworthiness); and people in the Epistemic Superior situation seem to be in a bad enough position among this continuum to be blameworthy. I don’t know whether peer disagreement is bad enough to make one blameworthy. It depends in part on the threshold for being epistemically blameworthy. In any case, all I want to discuss here is the person in the epistemically worse position, the one who recognizes the disagreeing superiors, and not merely peers.

    I don’t think it’s the mere fact that someone is merely in a better (even a much better) epistemic position than you are that is relevant here. Rather, I think you’re epistemically blameworthy for denying P only if the person you’re disagreeing with is in a particularly good epistemic position with regard to P. (Or maybe you need to have reason to believe that this is the case.) How good? Plausibly, good enough that, if P is true, they are in a position to know P, or are at least close to being in such a position.

    Are the very best philosophers in such positions with respect to their cherished controversial claims? I don’t think they are, despite their exemplifying all of the virtues you mention. Suppose modal realism is true. Did David Lewis know it? Did he even come close? I seriously doubt it. I have much more confidence in (K) than I do in any controversial philosophical claim I accept:

    (K) For all philosophers S and controversial philosophical claims P, if P is true, S is not even close to being in a position to know that P.

    So I think I’m blameless in disagreeing with claims made even by the most intellectually supercharged philosophers (provided, of course, that I don’t do something else epistemically irresponsible, like disagree with them for no good reason, or deliberately misunderstand their arguments, or what have you). Of course this doesn’t mean that I shouldn’t take their superiority into account, and it doesn’t mean that I’m not being irresponsible if I don’t — it just means that disagreeing with them isn’t in itself sufficient to make me blameworthy.

  22. Michael Huemer on April 10, 2006 at 2:12 am said:

    There’s lots to say about this interesting topic…

    (1)
     First, a plug (but a very relevant one!) for my paper, “Is Critical Thinking Epistemically Responsible?”, Metaphilosophy 36: 522-31. I argue that with controversial issues, it generally makes more sense to just take the word of experts, or to suspend judgement, than to form one’s own opinion by “thinking critically.” This seems to me very similar to what Frances is saying.

    (2)
     To Jon: Consider three methods of forming a belief about a philosophical question:
    a) Believe what the smartest expert (or the majority of the smart experts) believe.
    b) Think about the issue and then believe what seems right to you.
    c) Just suspend judgement.

    My argument in the critical thinking paper (which I think Bryan Frances will endorse) is that, ex ante, (a) or (c) seems better than (b); it’s hard to see how (b) could be more reliable than (a) (note: if neither (a) nor (b) is reliable, then you should choose (c)). Now, you make the point that since the expert is fallible, you’re not incoherent if you think that you’re right and that the expert is wrong due to some sort of miscalculation. But the point is that since you’re probably less reliable than the expert, you should attach a higher probability to the expert being right than to your being right. But that would require you to give up your opinion.

    (3)
     In some cases, we accept this: e.g., we defer to doctors, electricians, and so on. But we don’t commonly defer to philosophical experts. Why? Perhaps because we don’t accept that there are philosophical experts — that is, we don’t think anyone is sufficiently reliable that we can take their opinion as adequate evidence of the philosophical truth. But then it seems that we ourselves aren’t reliable enough to hold an opinion. Suppose that the threshold probability for rational philosophical belief is x (it is rational to believe a philophical claim only if it has probability greater than x). If you take your own reliability at identifying correct philosophical claims to be less than x, then it seems that you should not endorse your own philosophical beliefs. If your own reliability is greater than x, then the reliability of an expert who is smarter, better informed, and so on, must be even greater, so one should be prepared to endorse a philosophical claim made by an expert solely on his say-so.

    (4)
     As an aside, the examples of Lewis on modality and Williamson on vagueness are not the best for Frances’ case, because (a) the consensus of the experts in metaphysics seems to be that Lewis’ view of modality is crazy, so that surely outweighs Lewis’ lone testimony. (b) A weaker majority, but still a majority of experts on vagueness seem to regard epistemicism as being fairly crazy, too. Better examples would be ones where either there is no clear majority opinion among the experts, or one disagrees with the majority. For instance, I disagree with physicalism, even though that is the majority opinion in philosophy of mind.

    (5)
     There’s a connection between this issue and my principle of metacoherence (from an earlier CD thread). I think there’s a rationality constraint roughly to the effect that one’s first-order beliefs (/degrees of belief) should cohere with one’s assessments of how good one’s belief-forming methods are — so if you believe P (to a high degree), you must think that your method of forming that belief is highly reliable (you must also think your belief is justified, undefeated, fully grounded, etc.).
    There’s a threat of violating this condition if you assign a higher degree of belief to what seems right to you than to what seems right to person S, while you also hold beliefs that imply that S is more likely to form correct impressions about the matter in question.

    (6)
     Here (without evaluation) are some objections I have thought of:

    Objection #1:
    First, as others have pointed out, when it comes to interesting philosophical claims, for every expert there is an equal and opposite expert. For this reason, one might think that the testimonial evidence is essentially balanced, with respect to typical controversial philosophical claims.
    Second, however, one might say that one also has non-testimonial evidence, which can shift the balance of probability toward one claim or another. For instance, Berkeley’s testimony that there are no material objects is counter-balanced by the testimony of Locke. In addition, however, I have the evidence of observation, or intuition, etc., that there are material objects.

    Objection #2:
    The argument of Frances, myself, and Robin Hanson (see comment 9) seems to disagree with the opinion of the great majority of experts. Most intellectuals (judging from their behavior) seem to think that it is rational to stick to one’s guns despite the contrary opinions of experts and other smart people. Furthermore, if there are experts on this sort of thing, it would probably be the people who write critical thinking textbooks, and they seem to agree that it’s rational to use one’s own judgement rather than defering to others on controversial questions. Therefore, Frances, Hanson, and my position is self-defeating: if you accept it, then you should defer to the experts, whereupon you should then reject our position.

    Objection #3:
    We’ve been assuming that such traits as intelligence, knowledge, and time spent contemplating controversial issues are truth-conducive, i.e., higher levels of them increase your probability of attaining the truth. But this isn’t always true. In some cases, these traits may lower one’s chances of forming true beliefs. When would that happen? When someone is influenced by bias, so that their intelligence, knowledge, and time are directed towards rationalizing a prejudice, instead of toward an objective examination. Smart people are better at rationalizing false beliefs. Hilary Kornblith discusses this in “Distrusting Reason,” Midwest Studies in Philosophy 23: 181-96.

    Objection #4:
    (Related to #3.) An expert might have generally superior cognitive abilities and knowledge, yet be employing the wrong methodology. For example, I think David Lewis is wrong to assume that simpler metaphysical theories are typically for that reason more likely to be true. Given this fundamental methodological problem, his intelligence, familiarity with the literature, and years spent thinking about metaphysics are not going to avail him much in attaining true opinions in metaphysics.
    Of course, you could push the issue to whether I’m rational to disagree with Lewis on this methodological issue. But it doesn’t seem that Lewis’ expertise on this issue is particularly impressive. As far as I can tell, he doesn’t say anything about why he thinks simplicity is a virtue of metaphysical theories.

    When I admit that so-and-so is my epistemic superior I don’t do it based on anything like IQ. I do it based on the things Joachim mentioned, especially our track records. Just to be clear: by ‘going against’ I mean belief, so he believes P while they believe ~P. There is nothing whatsoever blameworthy in saying ‘Yeah, I know everyone–and I mean everyone–thinks that one thing can’t be identical with two or more things, but I’m going to pursue [without belief] that weird line anyway because such-and-such’.

    First case. Suppose you believe P but you have looked at all the reasons your recognized superiors have offered in print to accept P. You have given all their public arguments a fair, thorough, and expert examination. You have subsequently and expertly concluded that they aren’t very good; so you have retained your belief in P. So, even if the thorough, expert, and fair investigation of all the public arguments for P gets you off the hook, you’re still stuck for many of your other philosophical beliefs.

    Second case. You believe P and successfully trace the source of your disagreement with your disagreeing epistemic superiors such as Lewis to a single assumption, S. Lewis flags S, admits that his entire case against P relies on S, and sheepishly admits that although S strikes him as true, and thus good enough to rule out P, he has absolutely nothing to say in its defense. S strikes you as false, and you may or may not have supporting arguments. In this type of situation, perhaps you’re epistemically upstanding in sticking with your belief in P–provided this works with the other recognized epistemic superiors as well. For you know that the only articulated reason against P is S, and Lewis doesn’t have any articulated reason for S. I’m not sure about that, but let’s be generous and not challenge your epistemic status in that situation. Even so, this type of situation doesn’t happen very often.

    Third and final case. Pretend that most of your metaphysical superiors agree with Peter van Inwagen that there are no chairs. You think there are four chairs in your office. I suspect that for this type of case you’re epistemically blameless in your disagreement provided your excuse comes primarily from your knowledge of the considerations that have led those philosophers to that radical view. There are certain very sharp paradoxes that seem to generate only anti-commonsensical views. We can suppose that most of your many epistemic superiors with regard to these paradoxes conclude with the anti-commonsensical P, while you stick with common sense and affirm P. I suspect that if you decide to go along with common sense while admitting the extreme difficulty of the paradox, then you’re epistemically blameless in holding P. You know that it certainly appears as though every possible solution to the paradox is radically counterintuitive; you also know that that ubiquitous counterintuitiveness means that it is awfully hard to adjudicate among the solutions; and so you can be blameless in thinking that none of the proposed solutions is correct, as the real solution will mark a true revolution. You might as well stick with P and wait for the revolution, suspecting that common sense will win out over some very subtle assumption underlying the formulation of the paradox. At least I’m willing to allow that result for these cases.
