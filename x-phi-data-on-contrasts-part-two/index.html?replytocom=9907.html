X-Phi Data on Contrasts, Part Two

Posted on September 4, 2009 by J. SchafferSeptember 4, 2009

Joshua Knobe and I have just drafted a paper discussing the empirical evidence for a contrastive model of knowledge ascriptions. The paper offers a review of the existing empirical research, and introduces several new experiments of our own. It then describes a contrastive model of knowledge ascriptions, and concludes that the contrastive model may provide the best explanation of the data.

We wanted to share the paper with you, and invite your comments and criticisms. The paper is available here

We have already profited from a very useful discussion of our first round of experiments (link). So we are hoping that you will help continue the discussion! We are very much open to alternative models, and so we especially hope that you might help us to see how alternative models might try to account for the data.

  2. Josh May on September 7, 2009 at 10:42 am said:

    What a great paper, guys! Here are a few initial comments:

    (1) The Epistemic Side-Effect Effect

    From what I can tell (though I need to look at these papers in more detail), the effect that Beebe and Buckwalter found significantly altered subjects’ responses but didn’t push it over the mid-line. (And I’m assuming this is the same for Beebe and Jensen’s extension). That is, people still tended to attribute knowledge in both the help and harm cases and only something like their confidence was affected, not necessarily their judgment. So I’m not sure this calls out for the kind of explanation you’re envisioning, contrastivism or otherwise. The explanations you offer seem to be trying to explain why their judgments changed; but their judgments didn’t change, only their confidence in it (or something like that). This does call out for explanation, but not necessarily a shifty one.

    So, I’m not sure the epistemic side-effect effect can be used to motivate or provide additional evidence for contrastivism. But, as you nicely put it, this is a ladder that can be kicked away if it is problematic but the rest of your data do clearly support contrastivism.

    (2) On Experiment 4

    Your fourth experiment in very interesting and helpful. But it seems one might be able to legitimately complain that the salient contrast condition simply undermines the evidence a knower would otherwise have for the relevant proposition (and participants notice this). After all, in that condition, Sarah replies, “Well, banks do change their hours sometimes. My brother Leon once got into trouble when the bank changed hours on him and closed on Saturday.” First, the phrase “banks do change their hours sometimes” might be taken not as the obviously true claim that it’s possible that banks change their hours but rather the not-so-obvious claim that it happens fairly regularly. Furthermore, the next sentence telling the story about Leon doesn’t help. When she says “the bank” changed it’s hours on Leon, it sounds like she’s saying it’s *this* bank—the very same one they are considering going into. Now it may reasonably sound to participants like this very bank changed it’s hours in the past so that they were once no longer open on Saturdays but no are (but not again!). And that sounds like additional evidence that this bank will change its hours from time to time. Perhaps it would have been better to say “his bank” to make clear that it isn’t this bank.

    Anywho, if this is the case, an invariantist could hold that this is what explains the effect, not contrastivism. The story would be something like: Hannah has good undefeated evidence for p (=that the bank will be open on Saturday) in the control condition but this is undermined in the salient contrast condition. Of course this is just a difficulty with relevant alternatives views and the like—it’s difficult to separate mere salience of a possibility from additional evidence, defeaters of current evidence, etc.

    (3) Tying Things Together

    Furthermore, this would then provide for a way to explain the fourth study differently from the first three studies. So one could hold, for example, that something like shallow processing plausibly explains the first three and this evidence confound explain the fourth. This would then evade your objection to the shallow processing explanation and others like it (like my de re explanation!).

    With the addition of the worries about the purported support for contrastivism that the epistemic side-effect effect provides, it looks like there might be a non-contrastivist way out.

    But these are just some worries. I must say, the paper is great and the argument is quite strong. Thanks for posting it!

    Thanks for the great comments, John.

    (1) On your ‘if implemented, would have…’ moral valence test, I think our model predicts that the data would turn out differently from the way it did in the Beebe and Buckwalter experiment. We are suggesting that the moral valence effect is an indirect contrast effect, and so I take it you are suggesting that we try to vary moral valence while holding the contrast fixed. Cool idea. If a moral valence effect of the same magnitude remains, that would suggest that the moral valence effect in not an indirect contrast effect (assuming that the attempt to hold the contrast fixed really succeeded). Whereas if the moral valence effect diminishes or disappears, that would be further evidence that it is an indirect contrast effect. Is that a fair way to put your point? If so, this is an experiment we should run!

    (2) As to why we didn’t use a single vignette involving two reporters following different lines of inquiry, there were two main reasons. First, we wanted to structure our context sensitivity experiment to be as much like our other experiments as possible, so we naturally gravitated to using two sorts of vignettes. Second, we worried about getting performance errors from asking a single participant to juggle multiple contexts that differ in their guiding questions. Indeed I suspect that if we ran your suggested experiment alongside a variant that flipped the order in which the questions were introduced, there would be a strong framing effect.

    We actually did run a somewhat comparable experiment which didn’t make it into the paper. There we used our usual baseline vignette:

    “Last night, Peter robbed the jewelry store. He smashed the window, forced open the locked safe, and stole the rubies inside. But Peter forgot to wear gloves. He also forgot about the security camera.

    Today, Mary the detective has been called to the scene to investigate. So far she has the following evidence. She has been told that there was a theft, she has found and identified Peter’s fingerprints on the safe, and she has seen and recognized Peter on the security video, filmed in the act of forcing open the safe. She has no further information.”

    We then asked each participant to evaluate two clefted ‘rather than’ claims: (a) “Mary now knows that it was Peter (rather than anyone else) who stole the rubies” and (b) “Mary now knows that it was the rubies (rather than anything else) that Peter stole.” But we varied the order in which participants had to evaluate these claims. As expected, we found a strong framing effect. When participants were asked to evaluate (a) then (b), the means were 5.6 and 4.37; but when participants were asked to evaluate (b) then (a), the means were 3.04 and 3.36.

  9. James Beebe on September 11, 2009 at 10:27 am said:

    Jonathan and Josh (Knobe),

    I don’t think your contrastive explanation of the effect of moral valence on knowledge ascriptions is a ladder to be kicked away. I think it’s a great explanation (par for the course with contrastivism).

    I have one comment about the proper interpretation of null effects. When the mean responses in a pair of cases are different enough that the p value resulting from our statistical test is below .05, we can be confidence in rejecting the null hypothesis–i.e., we can be confident in believing that the effect is real and not due to chance. We can also be reasonably confident that the variables/factors we are testing are responsible for the effect.

    However, when we find no statistically significant difference between mean responses, as in Feltz & Zarpentine, Buckwalter, and Neta & Phelan, what can we conclude? Not much. We cannot be confident, e.g., that salience and stakes have no effect. All we can confidently say is that we didn’t find evidence that they have an effect. A p value below .05 warrants confident assertion that there is an effect. A p value above .05 does not warrant confident assertion that there is none.

    I think you effectively showed that the reason that some have not found a salience effect is that the probes they used were not designed in a way that would reveal such an effect. I have already found (unpublished results) that stakes do have an effect on knowledge attributions. Again, I think the general explanation of the difference between these findings and earlier ones is that some of the probes used were simply not able to reveal the effect.

    It’s been a while since I’ve looked at Feltz & Zarpentine, Buckwalter, and Neta & Phelan, so I don’t recall how these authors portray their results. But there are several times in your paper where you seem to treat their “null” findings as evidence for a lack of a certain kind of effect. I think these comments could be changed without substantively affecting the arguments of the paper.

    M1: Mary knows that Peter stole the rubies only if Mary knows that rubies were stolen (period!).
    M2: Mary does not know that rubies were stolen (period!).
    M3: Mary does not know that Peter stole the rubies (period!).

    M1 seems plausible. The fact that Peter stole the rubies entails that rubies were stolen. To know the former, one needs to know the latter. Even if we accept that one does not need to know all entailments of p to know that p, it seems implausible to reject M1.

    M2 seems plausible. To know that the rubies were stolen, one needs (i) a belief that the rubies were stolen and (ii) pretty good justification for that belief. Given the vignette, there is no reason to suppose that Mary believes that the rubies were stolen. I guess it is consistent with the vignette that she does believe that rubies were stolen. But, if so, her belief is entirely unjustified.

    M3 follows from M1 and M2 by modus tollens, of course.

    So, armchair reasoning suggests that Mary doesn’t know that Peter stole the rubies – (period!). If so, knowledge ascriptions that she knows will be invariably false. Now some questions:

    First: Do you think that knowledge ascriptions that Mary knows that Peter are not invariably false. (More specifically, are the knowledge ascriptions in the thief contrast contexts true?)

    Second: If so, which premise or transition in the argument above do you reject and why?

    Third: If not, why does the fact that any knowledge ascription is false (period!) not play any role in your assessment of the various accounts of the data?

  15. Jonathan Schaffer on September 14, 2009 at 5:24 am said:

    Mikkel,

    Cool argument. It actually has a similar structure to the following skeptical argument:

    M1′: Mary knows that she has hands only if Mary knows that she is not a handless BIV
    M2′: Mary does not know that she is not a handless BIV
    M3′: Mary does not know that she has hands

    The contrastivist endorses a unified dissolution of both the M1-M3 and the M1′-M3′ arguments. For the contrastivist what Mary knows is more fully articulated as:

    (a) Mary knows that Peter rather than anyone else stole the rubies
    (b) Mary knows that she has hands rather than stumps

    To know (a) requires eliminating the prospect that anyone else stole the rubies, but it requires (to paraphrase Dretske) little to no evidence that it was rubies that were stolen. Likewise to know (b) requires eliminating the prospect that Mary has stumps, but it requires little to know evidence that Mary is not a handless BIV. So the contrastivist denies both M1 and M1′, for parallel reasons.

    Of course both M1 and M1′ have intuitive force. So here the contrastivist needs an error theory. (We already knew we were going to have to deny something intuitive in the skeptical argument M1′-M3′–every view needs some error theory here.) One error theory that fits well with contrastivism is the theory that we can in some cases get confused by discourses where multiple covert arguments of the same sort take different values. Obviously it is a further question whether this error theory can be sustained. But it is at any rate a specific and testable hypothesis.

    Just to be explicit as to how this is intended to address your questions:

    1. The contrastivist does think that the knowledge ascriptions made in the thief-contrast contexts are true. (That is her explanation for why people tend to say they are true!)
    2. The contrastivist denies M1 for the reasons given above.
    [3. Does not arise since the contrastivist takes the other option.]

    The reason why this isn’t a case in which ‘philosophical knowledge’ may be used to guide or constrain anything, is that your claim of ‘philosophical knowledge’ is something that is being contested. We are trying to provide evidence that some things that philosophers have widely assumed might not actually be true!

  16. gerken on September 14, 2009 at 9:10 am said:

    Thanks for the clarifying reply, Jonathan!

    I’d like to try to defend M1, and thus classical invariantism, against your response. The parallel to the BIV argument is interesting. But I think that there are important asymmetries between the two arguments that speak against a unified account. At least, a Dretske-style relevant alternatives (RA) theorist may accept your treatment of the BIV case and reject your treatment of my argument.

    The two arguments are, in fact, not structurally isomorphic in all relevant respects (I guess that’s why you only say “similar structure”). The fact that the rubies were stolen figures in the antecedent of M1. In contrast, the BIV possibility does not figure in the antecedent of M1’.

    Similarly, the fact that rubies were stolen is part of Mary’s knowledge that Peter stole the rubies. In contrast, the fact that Mary is not a BIV is not part of Mary’s knowledge that she has hands.

    For a start, Mary can know, let’s assume, that she has hands w/o entertaining the concept of a BIV. In contrast, she cannot know that Peter stole the rubies w/o entertaining the concept of rubies.

    Likewise, Mary can know, let’s assume with RA theorist, that she has hands w/o any positive epistemic status (propositional warrant) that she ain’t a BIV. In contrast, she cannot know that Peter stole the rubies w/o any positive epistemic status (propositional warrant) that rubies were stolen.

    The (RA) theorist may regard the BIV scenario as an irrelevant alternative vis-à-vis S’s knowledge that she has hands. In contrast, it is hard to see how the fact that rubies were stolen may be argued to be irrelevant for the knowledge ascription that Peter stole the rubies. The knowledge-that clause takes scope over the rubies. It does so even in the theif contrast such as (a) of your reply. I thought that a contrastivist should say that anything within the scope of a knowledge-that clause is partly constitutive of the contrast.

    In sum, I think M1 is considerably more plausible than M1’. So, I think that the prospects for a classical invariantist account of your data (based on empirical assumptions about the nature of intuitive judgments) are more much better than those of contrastivism.

    Finally, re my third question: Your cases look a lot like cases in the literature of intuitive judgment in which participants in a particular condition make a judgment that is known to be mistaken. It is not a promising theoretical response to the data generated by, say, the Linda problem to say that given the experimental condition, it is true that Linda is more likely to be a feminist bank teller than a bank teller. We know that what’s wrong is the participant rather than the conjunction rule. The empirical investigation is guided by this knowledge. The proper explanandum is, in part, why participants overlooked the relevance of the conjunction rule in the experimental condition.

  17. Joshua Knobe on September 14, 2009 at 8:21 pm said:

    Mikkel,

    Thanks so much for your comments — I’m delighted to see that you are pursuing these issues and, especially, that you are developing an original hypothesis of your own.

    I was struck by your invocation of Kahneman & Tversky’s famous work on the conjunction fallacy, but I’d like to suggest a slightly different interpretation of what is going on there. It is not quite right to say that we simply knew a priori that the answers people were giving in those cases had to be wrong and then all the subsequent empirical work was just a matter of understanding the nature of this error. Rather, it seemed initially that one might be able to explain people’s responses in those cases by giving a purely pragmatic account (according to which people were correctly responding to certain complex pragmatic features of the questions they had been given). Part of the reason that many researchers came to reject this view was that Kahneman & Tversky were able to give a substantive and empirically powerful error theory — a theory that characterized people’s intuitions as the product of errors and then explained exactly how those errors might be arising.

    So it seems like the original hypothesis you are developing might actually be the best reply to our contextualist claim. We are giving an account that offers a substantive explanation of people’s intuitions on the assumption that their concept of knowledge is fundamentally contrastive; perhaps the best to fight back would be not only to give general reasons to suspect that there might be an error here but also a specific testable hypothesis about what exactly that error might be.

    It seems like you have something like that in the works right now — it would be wonderful if you could post something about it here at CD when it is actually available.

    Thanks for your encouraging remarks, Joshua! They are very helpful and we’re in broad agreement on the dialectical situation, I think.

    I agree that we don’t know a priori that thief-condition participants gave the wrong answers. For example, as you suggest, we cannot rule out know a priori that they really gave correct answers to a different question that it was pragmatically reasonable to suppose was being asked. E.g., T&K explicated the confounding pragmatic implicatures in their original article and participants still made the mistake.

    What we do know a priori, however, is that it is not more likely that Linda is a feminist bank teller than that she is a bank teller. And this is point generalizes: We know a priori that base rates shouldn’t be neglected, that affirming the consequent is an invalid argument even though its conclusion is plausible, that the correct answer to the Wason selection task is ‘A’ and ‘7’ etc.

    These a priori assumptions guide and constrain the empirically based theorizing. Now maybe M3 does not have the same “Archimedian point” status. To wit: Schaffer’s reply shows there is considerable wiggle room w.r.t. M1 for the contrastivist (see my response to him, though).

    Anyhow, I feel pretty damn good about making M1 and, hence, M3 my working hypothesis. Of course, even if the hypothesis is right, it does not explain why the participants in the thief contrast condition responded the way they did. That is an empirical question. I conjecture that they gave the wrong answer to the question asked because the thief contrast made them overlook (i.e., neglect) the relevance of the rubies.

    Now, “folks spending time in Canberra mall overlooked the relevance of the rubies” is not much of a theoretically satisfying account, of course.

    But I hope to develop this basic idea within dual processing framework so that it shares some features with stock cases from the literature on intuitive judgments and makes some testable predictions. For example, I hope that the dual system invariantist account can be developed to predict that participants in the thief condition could easily recognize their mistake, that high capacity participants would outperform low capacity participants (cf. Stanovitch 1999), that thief contrast participants cued to reason explicitly would outperform participants not so cued etc.

    But, of course (as Schaffer effectively points out), when I say ‘outperform’ and ‘mistake’ here, I already beg the question against you guys since I presuppose that the right answer in the thief condition is that Mary didn’t know…
