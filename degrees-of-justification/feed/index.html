Some logical truths are more complex than others and so more difficult to entertain. Insofar as one sees one truth more clearly than the other, I am inclined to think one can be more justified than the other. Additionally support for the justification of one logical truth might come by way of remembering having proved it and so the remembering might too confer more justification.

]]> By: jon kvanvig http://certaindoubts.com/degrees-of-justification/#comment-63 Thu, 17 Jun 2004 02:23:21 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=21#comment-63 I think the deeper issue here is not whether the representation in question is realistic, but whether, as an idealization, it offers sufficient benefits to allow it. The question of idealization is a very hard one, though. In empirical matters, we can explain the value of an idealization in terms of prediction and, maybe, explanation (though the latter is a bit more dubious). With respect to a philosophical theory, however, explaining the value of an idealization is a much harder task.
