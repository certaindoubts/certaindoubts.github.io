Arbitrary Actions and Arbitrary Beliefs

Posted on March 8, 2006 by Kvanvig JonMarch 8, 2006

Here’s a follow-up to the post on the Buridan’s Ass Paradox, and the natural response to it in terms of the falsity of the following principle:

Reason R is a reason to do A (or believe p) only if, for any B (or q) that competes with A (or p), R is a reason to do A rather than B (or believe p rather than believe q).

If this principle is false, we can define a sense in which both actions and beliefs can be arbitrary and yet rational: it’s just a case where the action or belief is rational without being contrastively rational in the sense required by the principle above. This point is connected with closure principles in epistemology, since affirming that beliefs have to be contrastively rational in order to be rational commits one to an implausibly strong closure principle.

Yet, there should be some other sense of ‘arbitrary’ in which actions can be arbitrary but beliefs not. The proper analogue of the animal caught between equally attractive bales of hay should be the inquirer caught between two equally attractive viewpoints, and even if the animal can rationally opt for one of the bales, the inquirer can’t rationally opt for one of the viewpoints, at least not in the purely cognitive sense of rationality.

So, any suggestions about a precise principle here?

  9. Michael Huemer on March 8, 2006 at 8:27 pm said:

    Jon,

    (I)

    I agree that one may have justified inconsistent beliefs. But how does that require epistemic pr. to violate the probability axioms?

    Here’s one way you might have in mind:
    1. If a person is justified in believing that A, then the epistemic probability of A is 1.
    2. There is a person and a set of propositions {A1 … An} such that the person is justified in believing each of {A1 … An} but A1 … An are inconsistent.
    3. If each of {A1…An} have probability 1, then, given the axioms of probability, P(A1 & … & An) = 1.
    4. But, if A1…An are inconsistent, then, given the axioms of probability, P(A1 & … & An) = 0.
    5. Also, given the axioms of probability, P(A1 & … & An) cannot be both 0 and 1.
    6. So the axioms of probability are not all true.

    The problem with this is that one who wants to treat degrees of justification as probabilities is going to reject #1. Instead, we should say that if a person is justified in believing that A, then the epistemic probability of A is high. In fact, this enables one to explain the preface paradox: S may be justified in believing each of {A1 … An} (in part) because the probability of each is high, even though P(A1 & … & An) is low, making S unjustified in believing (A1 & … & An) or even justified in believing ~(A1 & … & An).

    (Note: I assume that S is justified in believing A only if the epistemic probability of A is high, but I don’t assume that this is sufficient for S to be justified in believing A.)

    (II)

    On the principle offset in your original post (hereafter, “the Strong Contrastive Principle”):

    Suppose one likes to interpret principles of justification in terms of probability. Then there are two obvious interpretations of the Strong Contrastive Principle:

    (a)

    E raises the probability of H1, only if E lowers the probability of every proposition incompatible with H1.

    This is false. Consider a situation in which we have incompatible alternatives H1, H2, and H3, each equally probable. We subsequently acquire evidence E that rules out H3 but is neutral between H1 and H2. Then E raises the probability of H1 without lowering that of H2.

    (b)

    H1 is highly probable on E, only if each alternative is highly improbable on E.

    Comment #2 suggests that you would prefer this interpretation (though perhaps you wouldn’t accept either). And of course, (b) is true.

    (III)

    In answer to your original question, here’s a replacement for the Strong Contrastive Principle:

    It is rational to A only if there is no competing (action/belief) B such that it is more rational (or: there is more reason) to B than to A.

    This makes it irrational for the ass not to eat one of the bales of hay, since he has more reason to eat either than to sit there doing nothing. But it is rational to withhold judgement regarding P when the evidence is equally balanced, since one has more reason to withhold than to either believe or disbelieve P.

  13. Gregory Wheeler on March 9, 2006 at 9:35 am said:

    Jon’s right about the clash between the axioms of probability being in conflict with the basic idea of rational acceptance, which is not to say that we cannot toy with probability models to make them look something like a model of rational acceptance. The cost however is that one must give up probabilism (the idea that the structure of the calculus reveals the structure of the notion you wish to apply probabilities to, in this case, rational acceptance) *or* give up the idea that rational acceptance admits accepting propositions whose measure is less than one. There are things to be learned by various attempts to get around this, but what is learned is not going to amount to a refutation of this general point.

    Also the lottery paradox is not about lottery tickets or a kind of proposition or a type of context, but rather it is a short-hand thought experiment for capturing this point. The clash is rooted in properties deep in the calculus, namely general properties inherited by probability being a measure on a ring; so Kyburg was right to finger the rule of adjunction (aggregation) as the source of troubles rather than deductive closure in general. (And this feature is captured in the short-hand of the puzzle, too.)

    Igor Douvens and Tim Williamson have a forthcoming paper in BJPS exercising this point, and directing it against “structural” attempts to define a defeater. (A copy can be fetched from OPP.) One note of caution here that I would add is that “structural” in their account is used as a stand-in for “probabilism”, in the sense that the algebriac structures they are looking at are aggregative, which means that the result they have is at bottom a restatement of the paradox.

    It is also worth mentioning that a point they mention in a footnote early on about the generalized consequence relation they are studying being non-monotonic is due to a distinction between full transitivity and cumulative transitivity (although they use a different name for the latter), which was first noted by Gabbay in 1985 and studied by David Makinson (See Makinson’s recent ‘Bridges to Non-monotonic logic, King’s College Press, 2005). The preferential semantics (System P, studied by Kraus, Magidor and Lehman 1990), which underpins many semantics for non-monotonic logics and conditional logics, including probabilistic semantics for the latter, such as Pearl’s, satisfies cautious monotonicity which also satisfies cummulative transitivity. Williamson and Douven are a bit vague here, and I think the note was put in at the last minute before press, but it appears that they are gesturing toward this class of logics. That is my guess. The point to note is that all these systems are aggregative. (And they need this for their result to go through anyway.)

    There are plenty of weakly aggregative logics on the market (Horacio Arlo-Costa, Peter Schotch and Ray Jennings, Bryson Brown, Jim Hawthorne, I have one, and there is some nice work by Gilo, in Italy) which the authors ignore, which is fine, except that they suggest both by their title and in the text that their results are very general and tell against there being a “logical” or “formal” solution that models non-trivial rational acceptance. And, in so far as this suggestion is understood by readers as being true, it is misleading.

    The game here should be why probabilism rather than starting from probabilism and clubbing opponents with theorems one can already see in the algebra underpinning the language.

    Edit of [13]: the remark about measures on rings isn’t right, or it demands far too much charity from readers to make it close to right. The idea is that Pr, in virtue of being an extended real valued set function (generically, m), is monotone (i.e., If A has a measure, B has a measure, and A is a subset of B, then the measure of A is less than or equal to the measure of B, or if A is a subset of B then m(A) less or equal to m(B)) and subtractive (i.e., when A, B have a measure, A is a subset of B, and the set difference (A-B) has a measure and the absolute value of the measure B is finite, then the measure of the difference is equal to the difference of the measure, or m(A-B) = m(A) – m(B).)

  26. Chad Mohler on March 12, 2006 at 3:12 pm said:

    Jon,

    Your original post mentions that there is an important difference between actions and beliefs: the choice of one action from among equally attractive (but mutually exclusive) alternatives can be rational, whereas the choice of one belief from among (evidentially) equally attractive (but mutually exclusive) alternatives cannot be rational (at least in a cognitive/epistemic sense of rational). I think, though, that if we were to formulate carefully the comparison between equally attractive beliefs and actions, we would see the difference disappear.

    Let’s start with the case of belief. We might plausibly say that a belief that p is cognitively/epistemically rational if the belief is reasonable in light of our goal of now having true beliefs (and now not having false beliefs) concerning the subject matter of p. What is it, then that keeps belief in one of several (evidentially) equally attractive (but mutually exclusive) alternatives from being epistemically rational? Well, it’s that because we know the alternatives to be mutually exclusive, we know that at most one of them is true. But because our evidence does not reveal which one is true (they are, after all, equally evidentially attractive), believing one of them without additional evidence makes us run the risk of falling short of our goal of now having true beliefs and now not having false beliefs about the subject matter in question. So believing one of them is not epistemically rational.

    What’s the proper analog here in the case of action? It’s not, I contend, a Buridan’s ass-style case, in which the agent knows the value of each alternative, and that value is equal across the alternatives. The salient feature of the belief case is that only one of the alternatives had the quality we desired (namely, truth), but that we did not know which alternative had that quality. So in the case of action, we should construct our case similarly– one in which only one of the actions under consideration has the attractive quality (say, for instance, the maximization of some utility), but we are not sure which. Suppose, for example, each year you’re in charge of awarding a small scholarship to one of a group of students, but this year you do not personally know what the qualifications of each student are– you’ve not looked at their applications or letters of recommendation, you’ve never had them in class, and no one has revealed any relevant individual-specific info about them. Suppose, however, that one of your colleagues reveals to you that several of the candidates are remarkably well-qualified, but also that several of the candidates are likely to squander the opportunity the scholarship presents. Mysteriously enough, the colleague doesn’t reveal anything about which ones are which (perhaps wanting you to make up your own mind about the candidates). I contend here we have a case of action that is properly analogous to the case of equally epistemically attractive belief, with the moral being the same in each case. Just as we risk falling short of our epistemic goal of having true beliefs now and now not having false beliefs about the subject matter in question in the belief case, so we also risk falling short of the goal of maximizing utility by awarding the scholarship without further exploration of the candidate’s qualifications. In each case, the move in question– adopting the belief, performing the action– is irrational.

    Jim and Jon: What do you think about the following line:

    What separates your cleaned-up positions on rational acceptance is that Jon is thinking in terms of the syntax of rational acceptance, whereas Jim is thinking in terms of the semantics of rational acceptance. A person interested in a closure principle is interested in syntactic rules for how to manipulate collections of sentences labeled as rationally accepted, and to treat them as such *inside the object language*. Boolean conjunction + the probability axioms + allowing acceptance to take values less than unity add up to trouble, which I think we all can agree. There is nothing in here to carve out the models Jim is pointing to, in other words.

    Jim’s point is that we can isolate those models in which various combinations of rationally accepted sentences with high but less than total probability are sound. And, making some assumptions about the particulars of lottery and preface puzzles, we can pick all of them out. Then you can introduce a connective if you like that corresponds to that class of models that are sound with respect to a particular threshold point.

    I’ve argued that there is a tension between these two views of the matter. The kind of thing we want a connective to do in this case (e.g., to serve duty in effecting the application of a closure operation, not simply the meta-language description of one; to allow the encoding of rationally accepted statements in cases where we are ignorant of the probabilistic relationship between the events (propositions) which includes not having grounds for assuming that they are independent) and the result that Jim provides. To get the kind of modularity behavior that one would like, you lose completeness i.e., there may be collections of rationally acceptable statements that live in Jim’s model that our syntax cannot get to. Of course, all of the rules should yield only statements that live in Jim’s model. (If we don’t at least have soundness, then we don’t have much of a logic.)

    This kind of clash between syntax and semantics is familar in other branches of logic; it isn’t unique to the discussion here. It is common to find two proofs of a theorem, so both proofs ‘living’ in the same model, yet there be limits or quirks in exchanging the syntactic pieces of these proofs with one another and preserving theoremhood. All of this is not necessarily seen by the model theorist, since his job is to tell you “Yes, this theorem is sound” not “Here is the recipe and ingredients for making sound theorems.”

    Jim might insist he’s given us this, but describing procedures in the meta-language is very different than have those tools in the object language.

    And the puzzle behind rational acceptance concerns how to construct rationally accepted sentences from other rationally accepted sentences, i.e., to provide the structure of rational acceptance.
