Most good scientific theories are taken to be at least somewhat justified in that if they are true, they show how it is that our observations that lead us to them can be reliable. So physics shows how it is that our eyes are capable of absorbing the light reflecting off instruments in a way that is sensitive to what the instruments show, and evolution shows how it is that we might have minds that are shaped to comprehend complex theories like evolution. Each of these is taken to be somewhat better than a purely circular justification, like one for the law of excluded middle that shows why we can use the law by pointing out that every instance is true. However, the theory of the lying witness seems to undermine itself rather than justifying itself. I suppose that the explanation of a relatively unreliable witness can explain better why the witness utters IL better than either a theory of a totally reliable or an always lying witness does, but it still seems somewhat problematic. (Note that the witness saying “I always tell the truth under oath” gives merely circular justification to all theories.)

If all there is to my disbelieving p is my believing not-p, then I could believe both p and not-p and call it â??disbelievingâ?? both! Have those authors tacitly assumed that it’s impossible for a person to believe contradictories? What they call â??disbelieving pâ?? must involve _refraining from believing p_ (but not _withholding belief_ with regard to p). So, why not call _this_ (rejecting p) â??disbelieving pâ??? Moreover, for paradoxical propositions like EL and IL (among others), disbelieving must definitely not involve believing the contradictory. Otherwise, we get funny results.

In any case, there are also those who speak of disbelief as simply the act (or state) of rejecting a proposition. Priest (1987): â??One can reject something without accepting its negation.â?? (Who else?) This is what I had in mind when I spoke of disbelieving IL or EL in the post.

2. Suppose Ulysses comes to believe IL and then deploys his inductive argument. I suggested that he would then be evidentially required to disbelieve IL. But maybe he should then withhold belief in IL, given that he would then be caught in the crossfire between his evidence for IL and the counterevidence provided by his inductive argument. (There may be no precise way to decide the matter where intuitions are not clear one way or the other.) Be this as it may, the important point remains: If he believes IL for good reason and later deploys his inductive argument, he’ll be required to hold an attitude contrary to belief in IL.

]]> By: Claudio http://certaindoubts.com/the-inductive-liar/#comment-1650 Sat, 16 Apr 2005 23:07:34 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=302#comment-1650 “There seems to be a glitch in the example. You could reason: the witness asserts that he has lied under oath in the past. If this assertion is true, then he is unreliable. If it is false, then he has just lied under oath and so is unreliable. One doesn’t need to accept his assertion, to take his word for it, but just believe that he has so asserted on the basis of your own perception of him asserting it.”

Maybe not, Steven. To my mind, the hypothesis according to which the witness is lying is irrelevant. I know a priori that any assertor who is lying is a liar. If I have good, internally undefeated reason to believe the witness before he opens his mouth (which, by hypothesis, I do), I should just consider what follows from my believing what he asserts (that is, what follows from taking it as a truth). Will his assertion make me reconsider his apparent reliability? (Suppose the witness asserts EL. If he is telling the truth, he is unreliable, and I can’t rationally believe someone whom I believe is unreliable. I certainly need not believe him to see this.) The witness has now just asserted IL. If his assertion is true, he’s just ended his career as an under-oath liar, since he is now (by hypothesis) telling the truth and he is asserting that he used to be a liar. He has nothing to gain from admitting to being an ex-liar (and I have good reason to believe that he understands this and is not a masochist, etc.). So, he must be telling the truth, as rationally expected.

But then, again, I notice that there’s a good inductive inference coming. But, again, no inference without belief. So, I’ve just seen something about my future as a believer. But now I can’t become that believer. Yet, the evidential pressure to believe him remains the same. Etc.
