Evidence and two-dimensional belief

Posted on September 3, 2004 by Kvanvig JonSeptember 3, 2004

I’m going to take a big risk here. I’m home recovering from surgery (too much pitching on genetically inadequate knee joints), thinking about epistemology, and hoping the anaesthetic hasn’t made me so confused that the following is a complete waste of time… Anyway, here goes, and if it makes no sense, at least this time I have an excuse!

A post by John at Fake Barn Country, with an interesting and novel approach to gypsy-lawyer cases, got me thinking more about degree and strength of belief. Suppose we adopt a Bayesian framework of degrees of belief, supplemented with the notion strength of belief, as suggested here. Further, suppose we adopt as a sufficient condition for e’s being evidence for p that Pr(p/e) > Pr(p) (which, I’m assuming, is just Pr(p/e&K) > Pr(p/K), where K is background knowledge).

Given a two-dimensional framework for belief, should we also say that this probabilistic condition is necessary for e’s being evidence for p? Maybe not. If strength of belief is a measure of the tendency for a degree of belief to persist in the face of new learning, then it looks like you could acquire information that would have raised your degree of belief had you not known some of the things you do know. What the information does do is make your degree of belief more firm; make it more resistant to change by the acquiring of new information. So, it’s evidence, not because it enhances your degree of belief, but because it enhances your strength of belief.

This point would fit well with John’s use of the framework, since he proposes that if you rationally believe that p is true and a conclusive reason for q, then either your strength or degree of belief is enhanced.

But there is a measurement problem here. If we understand the concept of evidence to include the idea that evidence is a probability-enhancer or a strength-enhancer, then we can’t turn around and clarify the concept of strength of belief in terms of a measure of resistance in change of degree of belief on acquiring new evidence, on pain of circularity in the account. Instead, we’ll have to measure strength of belief in terms of resistance to change of degree of belief in the face of new learning, where the information acquired may or may not be evidence. But how would such an account work? It’s not as if resistance to change is a function of quantity of information. And any appeal to the quality or kind of information in question looks like it will slip in the notion of evidence itself.

One could claim that evidence has the enhancing properties proposed even though we shouldn’t define evidence in terms of these enhancing properties. If one doesn’t define evidence in terms of these enhancing properties, perhaps there is some other way to get a metaphysically necessary association here, but i don’t see how to do that. And without a metaphysically necessary connection, the two-dimensional framework for belief would be useful for purposes of psychological explanation, but not for Bayesian purposes, including that of defining the concept of evidence itself. Such a limitation would be unfortunate, I think. For one thing, it would give John’s principle only the force of nomological necessity at best (“at best” since I’m somewhat attracted to the idea that the only nomologically necessary laws apart from particle physics have heavily-laden ceteris paribus clauses, and the principle above doesn’t have one), and, besides, I think real epistemologists aim for metaphysically necessary epistemological principles!

But maybe there’s a way to avoid this conclusion. It is tempting to try to understand strength of belief in terms of probabilistic inequalities where background knowledge is pared of evidence for p. So, define a class K-, relative to p, where k- is a member of K- iff k- is K minus some e such that Pr(p/(K-e)) < Pr(p/K). Then we might understand strength of belief in terms of some member(s) of K-, something to the effect of the degree of belief remaining as it is through conditionalizing on the relevant members of K. (You might notice that this approach is roughly analogous to the approach used by Howson & Urbach on the problem of old evidence, and that should give some cause for suspicion, given the problems faced by that account; but I'll wait to see if anyone wants to shoot down the proposal from this direction before addressing this issue further.) I'm not convinced that such an account can work, however. Here's one simple issue. Suppose you've found a proof of a p, and a week later found a different, perhaps more elegant proof. Isn't it intuitive to think that finding the more elegant proof will add to strength of belief? I would think so, since finding a flaw in the first proof could leave a fully rational person with the same degree of belief if in the possession of the more elegant proof, but not without it. Call the first proof P1 and the second P2. The intuitive way to account for P2 being additional evidence is to note that Pr(p/(K-P1)&P2) > Pr(p/(K-P1)) (or if we assume that P2 is already a member of K, then compare dropping both P1 and P2 from K with just dropping P1). This approach is too atomic, however, for it assumes that P1 is the only thing in K that warrants the assumed degree of belief in question (besides P2). Since justification is holistic in some sense incompatible with this assumption, some way will need to be found to select the appropriate k- from the class K-, and I don’t see through the clouds on this one…

I have another worry as well. If we move past versions of Bayesianism that attribute a complete theory of evidence to a person in the form of conditional degrees of belief for any possible future learning whatsoever, then we encounter the problem of epistemic timidity. In the face of some unanticipated learning for which conditionalization or Jeffrey’s probability kinematics can’t be relied on for updating, some people assume the worst with respect to their degrees of belief and quickly drop them. Others display more of a “mountain man” mentality. If we could rely on conditionalization or probability kinematics for all updating, this character difference would be encoded in various degrees of belief; if not, then any measure on strength of belief should factor in this difference. Since the above account measures strength of belief on the basis of probabilistic inequalities, it looks as if this approach assumes a version of Bayesianism that attributes a complete theory of evidence to every person. Offhand, I’m inclined to share van Fraassen’s worry that this is a bit unrealistic.

    You know that there are 100 balls in the urn, and that each ball is either red or white. There will be ten draws from the urn, with replacement of course.
