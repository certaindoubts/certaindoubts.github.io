]]> By: Jamie http://certaindoubts.com/evidence-and-two-dimensional-belief/#comment-652 Fri, 03 Sep 2004 15:01:42 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=120#comment-652 Ok, but does that mean that the strengthening evidence is not (necessarily) evidence for anything? That was my point. When S strengthens a very high degree of belief in T, it seems natural and acceptable to say that S is evidence for T; but when S strengthens a middle-of-the-road credence, it doesn’t sound right to me.

Here’s an example. (It’s so predictable what sort of example a Bayesian is going to give at this point that I was sorely tempted to throw a curve ball, but then I remembered that your knee surgery was the result of a pitching injury so I thought it would be in bad taste.)

URN
You know that there are 100 balls in the urn, and that each ball is either red or white. There will be ten draws from the urn, with replacement of course. By indifference, you believe to degree .5 that the tenth ball drawn from the urn will be red. Now you learn that exactly 50 of the balls are red. Your credence doesn’t change, but your view is strengthened.

It is plainly wrong to say that you got evidence for the tenth ball’s being red. On the other hand, you certainly got evidence.

Can’t we just say that the evidence is evidence in favor of the first nine draws being white and the tenth red? (This sounds very strange, because the chance of this happening in light of the evidence is about .001, but the point is that your credence for it can go up by orders of magnitude when you learn the composition of the urn.) So the second dimension in fact supervenes on the first (which I think is consonant with your original suggestion).
