My claim is that your hypothesis equivocates on the meaning of “inferential knowledge”. (Again, ordinary, non-philosophical humans often claim to have knowledge.) So, I still fail to see that your claim is any simpler than mine — or that mine is any more theory-driven than yours.

]]> By: jon kvanvig http://certaindoubts.com/on-useful-falsehoods/#comment-1095 Sat, 11 Dec 2004 02:55:26 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=195#comment-1095 Claudio, the hypothesis about mentation in animals is too complicated if it requires the additional elements required by your theory. It is, I suppose, possible in some sense that animals have the reflective capacities you refer to, though I find it terribly implausible to suppose that they do. And I don’t think it implausible at all to think that they know things, both non-inferentially and inferentially. I’m inclined to think it a theory-driven response to require that they have reflective capacities with respect to skeptical questions in order to have knowledge. Think, for example, of experiments by cognitive ethologists on mentation in monkeys, especially on danger vocalizations, which differentiate predators on the ground and predators in the air. Such experiments surely constitute evidence of mentation, but provide no evidence of reflective capacities with respect to skeptical questions.

Surely, the fact that you wouldn’t get an answer in English (or any other known language) to the question “How do you know that p?” is philosophically insignificant. Since, by hypothesis, your horses have propositional knowledge, it seems safe to conclude that they conceivably could communicate their beliefs to you. So, why do you think they are in principle incapable of communicating an answer to a skeptical question (if you somehow manage to get the question understood by them)? I confess that I think your hypothesis about the mental life of your horses is much too complicated.
