Littlejohn on Justification

Posted on August 23, 2004 by Kvanvig JonAugust 23, 2004

Clayton Littlejohn sent me an interesting email about justification, and with his permission I’m quoting it here so that our discussion can be available to others as well. Clayton’s questions are about the relationship between personal and doxastic justification, as expressed in locutions such as “S is justified in believing p” and “S’s believing p is justified”, respectively. Since his discussion is several paragraphs long, I’m going to put the entire quotation plus my comments on it below the fold.

I was hoping I could ask you some questions concerning your discussion of the relations between propositional, doxastic, and personal justification in ‘The Basic Notion of Justification’. Your argument for reducing doxastic to propositional justification strikes me as correct, but I’m quite sure that I don’t understand the argument for reducing personal to doxastic justification. This is in part due to a lack of technical expertise.

You and Menzel claim that S is personally justified in believing p iff S’s belief that p is doxastically justified. This strikes me as unintuitive in part because in the case of actions, it strikes me as a mistake to say that I’m justified in acting a certain way iff there is a justification for my action and because I don’t see why there should be any difference between the case of action and the case of belief. It will facilitate my discussion if I help myself to an assumption Robert Audi defends in ‘Justification, Truth, and Reliability’ that crops up from time to time in his subsequent work [note: I think Audi is wrong in the conclusions he draws from it as will emerge]. Audi maintains that justification obtains in virtue of non-normative properties we could cite in giving a justification. He calls this the ‘process property integration thesis’ (PPI hereafter). Now, suppose Robert is driving 45 in a 55 zone and I say that he’d be justified in driving 10 mph faster given the circumstances. Suppose, however, that Robert is driving 45 because he saw a sign that said that the
speed limit was 45 and failed to see a sign further down the road saying that the speed limit has increased. Were Robert to drive 55 under such circumstances, I couldn’t justify his doing so as he’s ignorant of the fact that the speed limit had changed.

However, if asked whether there is a justification for driving 10 mph faster, it seems I could say truly that there is. If I’m justifying Robert, defending our late arrival, I can justify this retrospectively citing Robert’s beliefs (as well as Robert’s ignorance) knowing full well that there wasn’t any justification for driving as slowly as he did. In justifying prospectively Robert’s driving 10 mph faster, such considerations don’t factor into the justification-what factors into the justification are the legal facts (as well as facts about Robert’s driving ability, the road conditions, the condition of his car, etc.). It seems like Robert could be justified (because were things as he supposed, he should have only driven 45 mph) without there being a justification for Robert to drive as he did. It seems that driving at 55 mph can be justified (because of the legal facts of which Robert was unaware) when there is no justifying Robert’s driving at this rate of speed.

Following Scanlon (‘Intention and Permissibility’), it seems we should distinguish two processes of justifying. In the moral case, he points out that principles can be used in a critical fashion justifying either a past action or some hypothetical action or they can be used in an action-guiding way to answer the question ‘May I f?’ In some cases, we can successfully defend someone who f’d knowing full well that a justification for f-ing was lacking when, say, given what they non-culpably believed, were their beliefs correct, f-ing would be permitted. In giving correct answers to questions such as ‘May I f?’, however, we are less interested in the agent’s state of mind as we are not interested in moral credit but in permissibility. If there are two processes of justifying that are driven by different sets of considerations and can lead to split verdicts (S was justified in f-ing but there is no justifying S for f-ing; There is justifying S’s f-ing, but there really isn’t any justification for f-ing), it seems that there should be two properties of justification which supervene on different features of the case.

It seems something similar can arise in the case of belief. Focus on basing has drawn our attention to apparent examples in which there is a justification for someone to believe p in which there is no justifying the person’s believing p. It is harder to come up with examples of cases in which someone is justified in believing p where there isn’t a justification for believing p, but I think that the case of those deceived by Cartesian demons is one such example. It seems (to me) that if the dupe says ‘I should believe that I have hands’, what the dupe says is mistaken. This is perhaps due to the fact that the belief is false and the fact that if p is false, you shouldn’t believe it. I’d argue further that if you shouldn’t believe p there is no (adequate) justification for believing it. No one I’ve talked to has been persuaded by this example, but I’m not using the example to motivate the point, only illustrate it. The point is that in the abstract, I don’t see why if there is a gap between justifying an agent and justifying her action, there shouldn’t be a similar distinction in the case of belief and thus given PPI, modally separable properties of justification.

I’ll comment on each paragraph after the introductory one in turn. First, I agree with you in finding the the following claim troublesome and don’t think the view Chris and I endorsed implies it: “I’m justified in acting a certain way iff there is a justification for my action.” The reason our view doesn’t imply this claim is that it is ambiguous, since both sides if the ‘iff’ have both a type and token reading, and if you mix type/token interpretations of the claim, you end up with falsehoods. That point, however, is not relevant to your argument, which hinges on the driving example, and claims that when Robert is not aware of the change in speed limit, you can’t justify his driving faster, but there is a justification for it. I guess I don’t see this at all. You can justify it: just cite whatever justification there is for it. That doesn’t mean that were he to drive faster, his token of that action would be justified. It wouldn’t, since, presumably, the counterfactual situation in which he drives faster would include his ignorance. So I think your attempt to undermine Audi’s principle trades on a type/token ambiguity here. (Notice, too, that this type/token ambiguity is the analogue in the arena of action of the distinction between propositional and doxastic justification in the arena of belief.)

In the second paragraph, I think the same type/token ambiguity is occurring, with two or three extra complications. In the last sentence, you move to a more general type of action, driving 55, when the comparison should require the action Robert’s driving 55. I think that is a mere slip, however, so I won’t press that point. Another complication is whether, in the first sentence, you are justifying Robert’s actions or simply explaining them. Or maybe you’re not justifying his actions, but rather freeing him from blame for causing you to be late. I’m searching here for a better description of the case, since it doesn’t strike me as strictly accurate to take what you’re doing when you defend him as justifying his actions. Part of the reason here crops up in the last two sentences: you take things into account when talking about justifying Robert that you refuse to take into account when describing there being a justification for his actions. That raises a third complication for your description: when different factors are involved in two uses of the language of justification, that looks like a situation where we’d take the uses to trade on the ambiguity inherent in the concept of justification. In particular, when you say there is a justification for driving faster, you deploy a legal concept; but when you deny that you could justify Robert’s driving faster, you deploy a non-legal concept, since the speed limit itself plays little role in your remarks. It is instructive to note that you immediately turn in the next paragraph to a defense of the ambiguity claim, but if you adopt it, it undermines you argument here.

About the third paragraph, one could wish for a different schematic letter here! Anyway, I’ll comment on it and the application in the last paragraph. If there are two concepts of justification, that will be true both of justifying and of being justified. Moreover, not every statement involving an appeal to a justification for believing p is an instance of doxastic or personal justification. When you talk about the basing relation, for example, you say that this shows that it is possible for there to be a a case where there is a justification for a person’s believing p, but where you can’t justify the person’s believing the claim. That’s true, but the explanation is simple: the first involves propositional justification, and the second doxastic justification. No rejection of the property/process thesis is needed to account for this truth. The remainder of the last paragraph I take to distort the distinction between justification and truth, thereby relying on an ambiguity in the notion of what one “should” believe. In one case, one should believe the truth; in another, what one should believe is a what the theory of justification is all about.

    A question might help focus the discussion a little.

    Do you think it possible for someone to be personally justified in believing her belief that p to be doxastically justified but be wrong about that? I think this is possible. Suppose that doxastic justification requires that there are certain relations amongst propositions. A person who believes falsely that their beliefs stand in this relation may be rational in thinking this and intuitively personally justified in believing that their belief has the credentials of a doxastically justified belief where the relations amongst the relevant propositions aren’t as they’d have to be for the belief to be justified.

    Maybe we can’t make non-culpable mistakes about whether there are the right relations amongst the relevant propositions, but as I think we can make non-culpable mistakes about such matters , it seems possible for me for there to be a case in which S is personally justified in believing some belief to be doxastically justified but be wrong. I wouldn’t endorse:

    (JJ1) If S is PJ in believing that her belief that p is DJ –> S’s belief that p is DJ.

    [note: ‘PJ’ is personal justification; ‘DJ’ is doxastic justification]

    However, I would endorse:

    (JJ2) If S is PJ in believing that her belief that p is DJ –> S is PJ in believing that p.

    The motivation for JJ2 is that when it comes to PJ (a property our conception of which is tied to credit) someone’s being justified in believing that they are justified cannot be criticized for believing and thus the belief will be one that the subject is thus PJ in holding. So, if it is possible to make non-culpable mistakes about what one ought to believe and about what beliefs have DJ where some of these said mistakes will be cases where the subject is PJ in (falsely) believing a first-order belief to have DJ, by (JJ2) & ~(JJ1) we’d get the result I’m after.

  4. jon kvanvig on August 24, 2004 at 5:25 pm said:

    Clayton, a couple of comments here. First, the part of your view that is clearly correct is where you want to distinguish saying something epistemically positive about the believer from saying that a token belief is justified, or warranted, or rational. Where I find the discussion unconvincing and strained is where you want to find this difference different in certain syntactic constructions involving the language of justification. Why not just say, as you do, that the person can be rational, and even blameless, even though the belief isn’t justified?

    As to JJ1 and JJ2, I think they are both false. Any thorough-going fallibilism will imply that, since being justified doesn’t imply being correct, even if the object of belief is itself about justification.

    The point of our piece is to try to convince you that if you’ll seriously undertake the project of revealing the logical structure of syntactic constructions involving the language of justification, you’ll see that personal and doxastic justification are syntactic variations of each other, no less intimately connected than logical equivalence. All that is needed for this result is careful attention to the logical representation of each and an application of lambda-conversion. It is ineffective to respond to this argument by citing intuitions about assessments of agents rather than beliefs; the only thing you can do is to show that we’ve misrepresented the logical structure of the sentences in question (or show that the logical operation in question is invalid).
