Fordham’s “Varieties of Understanding” Project

Posted on February 4, 2013 by Stephen GrimmFebruary 4, 2013
2

The Philosophy Department at Fordham University is pleased to announce its three-year “Varieties of Understanding” project.

The project will sponsor research in psychology, philosophy, and theology that will examine the various ways in which human beings understand the world, how these various types of understanding might be improved, and how they might be combined with one another to produce an integrated understanding of the world.

For more details, and for information on how to apply for funding, please see the project website: www.varietiesofunderstanding.com

The project is supported by a 3.56 million dollar grant from the John Templeton Foundation, with additional support from the Henry Luce Foundation, Fordham University, and the University of California, Berkeley.

Belief through thick and thin

Posted on February 4, 2013 by Wesley BuckwalterFebruary 4, 2013
Reply

Some readers may be interested in this new paper. In it, David Rose, John Turri, and I distinguish between two categories of belief — thin belief and thick belief — and provide evidence that they approximate genuinely distinct categories within folk psychology, each of which often finds expression with ‘belief’. We use this distinction to make informative predictions about how people view the relationship between knowledge and belief.

Our main focus is to show that if the distinction is genuine, then we can make sense of otherwise extremely puzzling findings in recent experimental philosophy [discussed here on Certain Doubts] and [here on XPHI] questioning the entailment thesis (i.e. the widely held philosophical thesis that knowledge entails belief). Specifically, we argue that the entailment thesis should be understood in terms of thin belief. And as it turns out, when people are primed to employ the concept of thin belief, they respond exactly as we would expect if belief entailment was widely accepted. (We also suggest that future work might fruitfully apply our thick/thin distinction to other questions cornering degrees of belief, doxastic voluntarism, and internalism.) Comments and suggestions welcome!

Posted on January 16, 2013 by Jon KvanvigJanuary 16, 2013

Formerly called the Ammonius Organization, the Marc Sanders Foundation has sponsored younger scholars prizes for the last several years. They have a new website here.

Ascribing knowledge in English and other languages

Posted on January 12, 2013 by Jennifer NagelJanuary 12, 2013
25

What can epistemologists learn from studying knowledge attributions in other languages?  This is the question behind the “Epistemology for the Rest of the World” conference upcoming in Tokyo this August (see the call for papers here).

I think it’s an excellent question, and I love the main idea behind the conference, which is, as Steve Stich puts it in the announcement, to try to answer this question by “bringing together philosophers, linguists and social scientists from Asia and the West”.

What seems more controversial is Stich’s suggestion that epistemologists who have relied on intuitions about hypothetical cases presented in English have been studying the properties of the English language word “know”, as opposed to studying knowledge itself. He suggests that since fewer than 6% of the world’s population are native English speakers, we have reason to doubt that the concept expressed by the English word “know” has important implications for epistemology.

One way of quelling those doubts, as Stich points out, would be to say that the concept expressed by “know” is universal, expressed by some word in every natural language. That’s actually a thesis endorsed by linguists who study the limits of cross-linguistic diversity (see for example Cliff Goddard’s website for details). Lexical universality is a really rare feature — for what it’s worth, Goddard’s research program identifies just 63 words with this status. Many words that you’d expect to show up in all languages (“eat”, “drink”) don’t actually (apparently Warlpiri and Kalam get by with one word meaning “ingest”).

KNOW is claimed to be universal (like BECAUSE, IF, LIVE and DIE).  Actually, to be more specific, it’s the sense of “know” that embeds a propositional or wh- complement that is universal — not every language does the confusing English thing of making “know” do double duty for “savoir” and “connaitre”.  But no language seems to get by without a word that precisely translates “know”, or so Goddard and company say.  I should say this is a bit rough, because what counts as a word gets complicated when we look at inflected forms, or at languages like East Cree which don’t have verbal infinitives –“lexical expression” might be more accurate than “word”.  (Curious about the East Cree knowledge-ascribing lexical expression “chischeyihtam” / “chischeyimeu”? You’re welcome.)

According to Stich, “there is currently very little reason to think that the universality thesis is true, since little or nothing is known about the meaning and use of epistemic terms in languages other than English.  What little is known looks to point in the other direction.”  He goes on to mention the fact that two distinct verbs are used in Japanese to translate “know” — “shitte-iru” and “wakatte-iru”.

My own suspicion is that quite a lot is known about the meaning and use of epistemic terms in other languages (although we could doubtless benefit from getting this knowledge shared around a bit more, as Stich’s conference helpfully promises to do). I think there’s also a fair bit of evidence that KNOW is lexically universal.  But I wonder what to say about Japanese. It’s not especially threatening to universality that there is more than one verb used for knowledge attribution in Japanese: we also use multiple verbs in English (to notice that, to be aware that, to see that…).  Assuming that the verb “to know” is special because it is the most general such verb, we can wonder whether there are languages in which this generality is not expressible.

According to researchers in Goddard’s program, it’s the first of Stich’s two verbs that expresses the universal concept KNOW (shiru – 知る), but they may be wrong about that. In general, the universality program is actually OK with the idea that in some languages a lexical universal has two words that are used to express the universal (multiple exponents of the universal).  These words themselves might not be fully interchangeable because of polysemy; what would matter is just that the key senses are the same (consider the English “think” and “believe” — I take them to be interchangeable for epistemological purposes, but just if we restrict ourselves to the sense that embeds a propositional complement).

In any event, I’m curious about what readers of Certain Doubts think about universality — either its empirical plausibility or its significance for epistemology.

I’m also curious about what value other epistemologists see in cross-linguistic work (if any).  I don’t like to think that I am (just) exploring the English word “know” when I think about hypothetical cases that I might or might not use that verb to describe.  My own feeling is that it can help me avoid getting hung up on peculiarities of English to spend some time looking at Aquinas’s use of “cognoscere” and “scire” (for example!).  Does anyone else share my sense that examining the commonalities in knowledge talk across other languages can help us see what is most epistemologically significant in our own patterns of knowledge attribution?

Tim Cook: Apple CEO and friend of the a priori

Posted on December 9, 2012 by John TurriDecember 9, 2012
Reply

Brian Williams: What’s the role of intuition in your job?

Tim Cook: It’s critical. It’s extremely critical. The most important things in life, whether they’re personal or professional, are decided on intuition. I think you can have a lot of information and data feeding that intuition. You can do a lot of analysis. You can do lots of things that are quantitative in nature. But at the end of it, the things that are most important are always gut calls. And I think that’s just not true for me, but for many, many people. I don’t think it’s unique.

Brian Williams: Fascinating. Absolutely fascinating.

Cook {reaches into his bag and hands Williams a worn out copy of some book}: It’s all in here, man.
