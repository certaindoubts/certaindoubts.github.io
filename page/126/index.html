Structured Procrastination and Pollock’s Doxastic Assumption

Posted on December 11, 2004 by Kvanvig JonDecember 11, 2004
6

It’s that time of year again for structured procrastination, so I’ll engage in some. (If you haven’t seen this piece by John Perry, it’s fantastic and funny. The idea, applied to a research career, is that success is best achieved by using writing as a way of procrastinating with respect to more pressing duties, such as grading papers. I highly recommend the practice…)

Pollock claims that nearly everyone in epistemology prior to the last half of the twentieth century adopted the doxastic assumption that only beliefs can justify beliefs. I’ve always thought this misrepresented foundationalists, since a common way to characterize that view is in terms of a foundation in experience itself. Since the justification of everything is supposed to be generated from the foundations, such a position denies the doxastic assumption. Perhaps there are other versions of foundationalism that adopt the assumption, but it always looked to me like a very prominent version doesn’t.

I would think as well that one could find textual evidence that Hume or Berkeley and perhaps Locke held this prominent view, either about justification or knowledge, though I haven’t done a search for such passages. But maybe I’m missing something subtle here, a thought that always arises when such a good philosopher as Pollock makes a claim that seems so obviously false.

Progic2005

Posted on December 8, 2004 by Kvanvig JonDecember 8, 2004
Reply

Progic2005: second workshop on Combining Probability and Logic
Special Focus: Objective Bayesianism

Centre for Philosophy of Natural and Social Science, London School of Economics, 6th-8th July 2005

You are invited to submit a paper for presentation at the workshop and publication in a special issue of the Journal of Logic, Language and Computation.

There is a clear connection between probability and logic: both appear to tell us how we should reason. But how, exactly, are the two concepts related? Objective Bayesianism offers one answer to this question. According to objective Bayesianism, probability generalises deductive logic: deductive logic tells us which conclusions are certain, given a set of premises, while probability tells us the extent to which one should believe a conclusion, given the premises (certain conclusions being awarded full degree of belief). According to objective Bayesianism, the premises objectively (i.e. uniquely) determine the degree to which one should believe a conclusion.

The aim of this workshop is to explore the connections between probability and logic, and in particular to evaluate aspects of the connection forged by objective Bayesianism. The workshop is intended to be interdisciplinary: the themes of the workshop are relevant to mathematicians, logicians, philosophers, computer scientists, psychologists and engineers, for example.

Further details:
http://personal.lse.ac.uk/willia11/progic2005/.

Pragmatics of knowledge-attribution

Posted on December 8, 2004 by Kvanvig JonDecember 8, 2004
6

Here’s an issue that I’m not sure how to begin thinking through. If we think about theories of knowledge in terms of a semantics/pragmatics distinction regarding the use of knowledge ascriptions, we encounter the problem of determining which aspects of use to explain semantically and which to explain pragmatically. The same issues will recur if we wish to cast the discussion more in terms of the nature of knowledge rather than in terms of meaning, but I’ll just do things in terms of meaning for now.

Consider, then, the way in which knowledge and practice are related. Ascriptions of knowledge typically convey the idea of closure with respect to further inquiry, and thus when the practical stakes get raised, ascriptions of knowledge tend to be inappropriate. Contextualists and Subject-Sensitive Invariantists generate a semantic account of this feature of knowledge ascriptions, but I am unaware of arguments to the conclusion that this feature is semantic in character rather than pragmatic.

Austin-Berkeley Annual Formal Epistemology Workshops, FEW 2005

Posted on December 6, 2004 by Kvanvig JonDecember 6, 2004
Reply

The purpose of these workshops will be to bring together individuals, both faculty and graduate students, using mathematical methods in epistemology in small focused meetings. Topics treated will include but are not limited to:

* Ampliative inference (including inductive logic);
* Game theory and decision theory;
* Formal learning theory;
* Formal theories of coherence:
* Foundations of probability and statistics;
* Formal approaches to paradoxes of belief and/or action.

Besides papers with respondents, each workshop will typically include short introductory tutorials (three or four topically related presentations) on formal methods. These tutorials will be oriented particularly to graduate students.

The second workshop is scheduled for 26-29 May 2005 and will be held at University of Texas at Austin. The deadline for abstracts is February 28, 2005.

Those interested in participating, either by presenting papers, responding, or providing tutorials, or in helping with organization, should contact one of the organizers listed below.
