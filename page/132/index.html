sosa on foley

Posted on September 14, 2004 by Kvanvig JonSeptember 14, 2004
4

Sosa criticizes Foley’s theory here, but I think the criticism misunderstands Foley’s theory. Sosa says:

About Foley’s stance, it does provide a unifying connection to truth if only via the requirement of a deep commitment to the T principles, which speak of truth and not only of justification. This sense of justification, Foley-rationality, is plausibly epistemic. It amounts to conforming to one’s own deep epistemic standards. But a believer might conceivably have standards that are epistemically quite deplorable no matter how deep, as would someone deeply committed to the dicta of astrology. But the point can also be made without recourse to what is only hypothetically so, in some remote possible world. Deepest standards for belief regulation in our actual world can have functions that bear less on truth than on solidarity and coordination, as with some primitive myths, which seem plausibly to require nothing short of belief if they are to satisfy their latent functions. Can it plausibly be supposed that it is nevertheless their sheer depth that gives the relevant standards epistemic efficacy? Surely there must be some further sense of justification that matters in epistemology.

Lycan’s “No-Essential-False-Assumptions” Theory

Posted on September 12, 2004 by Kvanvig JonSeptember 12, 2004
4

As noted in the last post, Lycan not only thinks the anti-gettier problem chorus is off-key, but that there is relatively simple solution to the gettier problem: the NEFA theory. What’s interesting in this proposal is it’s rejection of some standard gettier cases: the fake barn case of Ginet and Goldman, and the Jill/assassination case of Harman.

In this respect, Lycan’s theory is a middle ground between those who take these counterexamples seriously, and Weatherson, who takes none of the purported counterexamples as actual counterexamples. Bill chastens Brian for not explaining away the counterexamples, and on his blog Brian responds by claiming that those who take the counterexamples seriously are just closet skeptics succumbing to focal stress on the use of the word ‘knows’ in the examples, a stress akin to those we find in skeptical arguments.

Lycan on the Gettier Problem

Posted on September 11, 2004 by Kvanvig JonSeptember 11, 2004
13

Bill Lycan has a new paper on the problem of the Gettier problem. It has become fashionable to denigrate the project of attempting to solve the Gettier problem, and Bill’s paper evaluates a number of attempted explanations of what is wrong with the project, showing why they are not good explanations. Along the way, he also proposes his own account, which is what interests me here.

Lycan’s proposal is the “no-false-assumptions” proposal (in the face of some examples, this proposal becomes the “no-essential-false-assumptions” proposal, but the difference isn’t relevant here so I’ll ignore it), a variant of the “no-false-lemmas” approach suggested by Clark. Lycan thinks that the NFA proposal was not entertained directly, but implicitly by the adoption of the defeasibility approach. In effect, Lycan claims, the NFA approach was bypassed by a theory that attempted to analyze what a false assumption is in terms of the notion of defeat. So what the counterexamples show is the the NFA approach is very hard to characterize in terms of the notion of defeat.

Then Lycan echos a point made here, when he says “Indeed, that difficulty was predictable, because (a) it was almost irresistible to start the further analysis with a subjunctive of some kind, and (b) any time any analysis of anything contains a subjunctive, irrelevant counterexamples will ensue. (b) is worth a paper of its own.” Brian Weatherson joins in the chorus here, saying “I always thought the best simple description of the “Conditional Fallacy” in Shope’s paper of the same name was the fallacy of thinking that the analysis you’re after contains (subjunctive) conditionals. Shope said something more subtle, but that was the easiest takeaway line. I’m glad to see someone else agrees!”

I’m going to post more on Lycan’s paper, but since the conditional fallacy is a pet peeve of mine, I enjoyed seeing some signs that awareness of the problem is becoming more common. If we’re lucky, it will become common enough that no paper with a subjunctive analysis of anything will be sent out for publication without an explicit defense that the analysis avoids the problems that plague nearly every such analysis.

An issue for propositional evidentialists

Posted on September 11, 2004 by Kvanvig JonSeptember 11, 2004
6

I’ve been thinking more about my own propositionalist view of justification, in response to the conversations here with ej, and some thoughts as well on Uriah’s view that consciousness as well as belief is self-intimating (discussed at Desert Landscapes, with references there to some papers of his on the subject). In my view, the fundamental kind of justification is propositional. The important kind for knowledge is doxastic justification, which is propositional justification plus proper basing. Doxastic justification is important in its own right as well, independent of its connection to knowledge, since one is not epistemically blameless when one believes things for reasons that don’t justify the content of the belief in question.

From this perspective, the first task for a propositionalist is to construct a theory of evidence. One would think that a propositionalist would want to develop a theory of evidence in terms of relationships between propositions, e.g., with some account of necessary and sufficient conditions for e being evidence for p. Then, if you believe e, you have evidence for p; and if your belief that p is based on your belief that e, then your believing p is doxastically, and not just propositionally, justified. A nice proposal along these lines assumes that evidence can be characterized in terms of probabilistic inequalities: e is evidence for p iff Pr(p/e) > Pr(p) (which, I will assume is the same inequality as Pr(p/e&K) > Pr(p/K), where K is the totality of everything you know). (This definition isn’t adequate, but it’s a good example of the sort of account that would appeal to a propositionalist; it’s also a good assignment in an epistemology class to ask students to find counterexamples to it for homework!). Once we have this definition in place, we can say that S has evidence for p just in case there is some e such that e is evidence for p and S believes e. (Since I incline toward subjectivism about justification, I want the probabilistic inequality to be explained by stuff in the head, the most beautiful example of which is the bayesian account on which the stuff in the head is simply conditional degrees of belief).

But it looks like there is a problem here. First, sometimes your evidence is in the form of experience rather than belief. Second, and related to this first point, sometimes it looks like it is not the content of the belief that provides the evidence for p, it is the belief state itself. So, for example, if p = I will succeed, then believing that you will succeed increases the chances that p is true, and thus might legitimately count as evidence that p is true. Here, the belief state itself is evidence for the content of the belief state; it would be ridiculous to suggest that it is the content of the belief that is evidence for p, since the content of the belief just is p itself. In this way, the belief state can be evidence for p, by being a belief state with content p, just as an experiential state can be evidence for p when it has as content p itself. In such cases, there is a temptation to describe the situation as follows: sometimes your evidence isn’t propositional at all, but is rather in the form of beliefs or experiences–and isn’t that a problem for the propositionalist?

Call for Papers: Contrastivism in Epistemology

Epistemological theories on which knowing a proposition always involves reference to one or more contrastive propositions can be enlisted under the heading of epistemological contrastivism. For instance, according to one type of epistemological contrastivism (the one developed by Fred Dretske), in order for a subject S to know that the animal in the pen is a zebra, S must be able to eliminate the contrastive proposition that the animal in the pen is a tiger. And according to another type of epistemological contrastivism (the one developed by Jonathan Schaffer), there is no such thing as ‘knowing that the animal in the pen is a zebra’, though there is such a thing as ‘knowing that the animal in the pen is a zebra rather than that the animal in the pen is a tiger’.

Social Epistemology plans a special issue on epistemological contrastivism for which paper submissions are now invited. Social Epistemology is interested in all papers addressing epistemological contrastivism, and is especially interested in receiving paper submissions which explore the connections between contrastive theories of knowledge and topics that are important in social epistemology. Suggested topics include (but are certainly not confined to):

1. How are contrastive theories of knowledge and testimony connected?
2. What is the relation between contrastive theories of knowledge and the
use / purpose of knowledge attributions?
3. Can contrastivist theories of knowledge shed light on the notion of
expertise?
4. Can the notion of trust be accommodated by a contrastivist epistemology?
5. To what extent are contrastivist theories of knowledge contextualist in
character?
6. Can contrastivist theories of knowledge respect the closure principle for
knowledge?
7. Can contrastivist theories of knowledge be defended by using such notions
as “contrastive explanation” or “contrastive causation”?
8. To what extent can contrastivist theories of knowledge be of help in
solving skeptical problems?
9. Is justification a contrastive concept? And belief? And rationality?

The deadline for paper submissions is April 10th 2005. Submissions can be e-mailed (in .doc, .pdf, or .rtf format) to the guest-editor of this issue of Social Epistemology, Martijn Blaauw, at the following address:

basing and arbitrary actions

Posted on September 9, 2004 by Kvanvig JonSeptember 9, 2004
7

Some hold that there is an important disanalogy between justified belief and justified action. When speaking of justified belief, we distinguish between doxastic and propositional justification, where the former obtains when there is propositional justification for the content of the belief, and the belief is properly based on the propositional justification in question. We might distinguish similarly between two kinds of justification in the arena of action, between the action-type being justified and the action-token being justified because it is done for the reasons that justify the action-type.

Here’s the purported disanalogy, however. Arbitrary actions can be justified in the way that is analogous to doxastic justification. Suppose you are choosing what to eat for breakfast, and you’ve got no reason to prefer Wheat Chex to Rice Chex, but arbitrarily choose to eat Wheat Chex. Your action is fully justified, but it need not be based on any reasons for preferring one kind of Chex cereal to the other, since by hypothesis, there are none.

I think this attempt to distinguish the role of reasons and basing in the arena of action from the arena of belief fails. There are reasons for eating Wheat Chex–you like it, it will fill you up, you’ll enjoy the experience, and it won’t cause cancer (we hope). If your eating Wheat Chex is rational, you will be eating the cereal for these reasons. What is not true is that there are reasons to prefer eating Wheat Chex to eating Rice Chex, so your action need not be based on such reasons. In slogan form, we can say that your action must be based on reasons, even if they need not be based on contrastive reasons.

So the difference between action and belief is not that basing is required in the latter but not in the former. It is, rather, that one’s reasons can leave open multiple options in the case of action but perhaps not in the case of belief. That’s the lesson of the Buridan’s Ass paradox: reasons for A-ing need not be reasons that would undergird contrastive explanations (though perhaps I should be pushed a bit on how this could possibly remove the paradoxicality here).
