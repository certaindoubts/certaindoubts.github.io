At least this will work for false and objectively likely to be false.

The point about laws and true generalizations is interesting, though I’m not quite sure what to make of it. I suppose the idea is that reliability should be counterfactual-supporting, and if it is, it may yet add something of value to mere true belief. There’s a view in the neighborhood of this idea, inhabited by a house built by another Sosa, that I like: when the reliability is explained by excellences or virtues of the person who has the belief, then value is added because the person deserves credit for getting things right (though I tend to merge the views of Sosa with Greco and Riggs on this point).

So if I beg the question against them (and who better suited to beg the question against!), then I’d say that one’s cognitive interests are those specified in terms of getting to the truth and avoiding error now. If I try to avoid begging the question, I think I have to do it negatively, by ruling out all the other interests that I say are purely cognitive: political, moral, aesthetic, practical, etc. And we might characterize the purely cognitive interests in terms of purely theoretical interests as well (in the sense of abstracting away from non-ivory-tower concerns), but of course that would raise just the same questions you raise, anyway.
