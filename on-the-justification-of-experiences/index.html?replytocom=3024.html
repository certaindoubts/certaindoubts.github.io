On the Justification of Experiences

Posted on December 7, 2005 by Kvanvig JonDecember 7, 2005

Here’s a datum (or so I think): beliefs can be justified but experiences can’t. Believing that it is raining outside right now is justified, or rational, for me to believe; my experiencing that it is raining is not justified or rational. Experiences just aren’t the right sort of thing to be justified or rational.

But this claim raises a puzzle: why aren’t they the right sort of thing to be justified? We might approach the question by considering what kinds of things can be justified or rational.

We ordinarily think of both actions (and omissions) and various mental states (or events) as capable of being justified. Most relevant here are mental states or events. Among these are both cognitive and affective states. In the former category are things such as thinking, assuming, postulating, presupposing, hypothesizing, etc.; among the latter are things such as hoping, fearing, desiring, wishing, etc. Closely associated with these are a special class of actions which includes inscribing, asserting, insinuating, etc.
Maybe the same is true of emotional states: fear, anger, sorrow, disgust, joy, surprise, pity, indignation, envy, shame, despair, gratitude, wonder, awe, remorse, worry, nervousness. And maybe the same holds for more complex states: pride, humility, the feeling of vulnerability, shyness, surprise, embarrassment, etc.

If all of these things can be rational or justified, why not experiences? Well, consider states of persons that cannot be justified or unjustified, such as digestive states, cardiovascular states, chemical imbalances, neurological disorders, nervous twitches, reflex actions, breathings, stress responses such as sweating and increased heartrates, etc.

Is the distinction to be explained in terms of something like an agent/patient distinction, so that agential states or events can be justified but patential states or events cannot be? How would that help, however? It’s hard to see such a distinction helps, since it is hard to see how, e.g., a dispositional belief is supposed to count as an agential state whereas a concious experience is a patential state. There is, for example, no interesting notion of control available for the first state that is not available for the second.

How about active and passive? Perhaps we might want to say that the believing is justified, but the proposition’s being believing isn’t. The looking is justified, but the experience that results isn’t. The hoping is justified, but the claim’s being hoped for isn’t. So can we think of something like the active/passive voice distinction in grammar as signalling something here?

    Jon,

    I think there’s a further complication here that arises from how one characterizes experience. If one assimilates experiences to sensations states then it’s very hard to see how sensation states can be justified or not. If, however, one assimilates experiences to appearance states then it’s not so clear that experiences cannot be justified. For appearance states have many of the same properties that belief states have. Appearance states like belief states are conceptual, capable of being accurate or inaccurate, and are propositional attitudes (e.g., it appears to me that it is snowing outside). If one looks at the reasons for thinking that belief requires justification it’s hard to see why appearance states are exempt from such a requirement.

    I may be off about the history here, but I think classical foundationalists wanted to assimilate experience to sensation states so as to end the regress of reasons in something that can justify but need not be justified. The problem, though, is that sensation states cannot justify belief, as the problem of the speckled hen shows. Appearance states are ideal candidates to justify perceptual belief, but classical foundationalists have shied away from such states because they seem to require justification. Some foundationalists try to get around this problem by introducing the notion of direct apprehension or acquaintance, which is like an appearance state in that it can have cognitive significance and like a sensation state in that it isn’t the sort of thing that can be justified.

  4. Ted Poston on December 7, 2005 at 11:49 am said:

    There are two issues here that need to be held distinct: (a) whether experience has accuracy conditions and (b) whether experience has justification conditions. Suppose experience has accuracy conditions. There’s still the further question of whether experience has epistemic appropriateness conditions. Of course, beliefs about experiences can be epistemically appropriate or inappropriate. But can experiences themselves be epistemically appropriate or inappropriate?

    Consider an analogy with inferential justification. Suppose S draws all the right inferences from a set of unreasonable beliefs. Are S’s inferential beliefs justified? No. Why? The non-inferential beliefs are not justified. Now, suppose instead of inferential beliefs we consider non-inferential beliefs formed on the basis of experiences. Suppose S forms all the right beliefs given S’s experiences but that S’s experiences are misleading. Are S’s beliefs justified? Yes (or so I think). But if this is right, then there’s a good reason for thinking that experiences don’t have epistemic appropriateness conditions. For misleading experiences can confer justification on belief.

  7. Brad Majors on December 8, 2005 at 1:40 am said:

    I assume we’re talking about perceptual experience here. I further assume that experience of the relevant sort is nonconceptual.

    If this is correct then we see already one reason why experiences do not admit of justification. The reason is that epistemic justification has to do with truth, and only propositional (conceptual) representations are true or false. Notice that many lower animals (rabbits, e.g.) indisputably have perceptual experiences, yet probably lack concepts altogether. So it cannot be the case that experiences are conceptual.

    I think I agree with Ted that we must be careful to separate experiences (sensations, etc.) themselves from our descriptions or reports of them. Presumably any description is conceptual. But some subject matters are not. So the fact that our report about some subject matter is true or false, or justified or unjustified, implies nothing whatever about whether the subject matter itself admits of these properties. This point seems to be lost sight of in the above example concerning sensations.

    More generally, all representations have veridicality conditions, but it would be a stretch indeed to regard all of them as being justified or unjustified (think of maps, e.g.). It seems to be a necessary condition upon admitting of epistemic justification that a state or event have conceptual (propositional) content. But it is not a sufficient condition. Imagining that p is a counterexample. I think that epistemic justification applies primarily to beliefs. But it is a difficult matter to say why precisely this is. Presumably the answer has at least partly to do with the unique role belief plays in our cognitive and practical economies.

  10. Brad Majors on December 8, 2005 at 11:50 am said:

    Jon, I take truth to be a subspecies of veridicality. All representations have veridicality conditions, but not all representations are truth evaluable. The term ‘alethic’, as I understand it, applies *only* to that which is truth evaluable. ‘Accuracy’ is a synonym for ‘veridicality’. So the fact that experiences have accuracy conditions does not imply that they are alethically evaluable. And I think that nothing which is not alethically evaluable admits of epistemic justification (at least strictly speaking).

    I won’t rely here on claims about non-human animals. But there are independent, positive reasons for thinking that perceptual experience is not conceptual (and therefore not propositional). Two human beings can share the same perceptual experience, even though one or both of them lacks concepts we would use to describe that which their experience represents. One can perceive a cricket match, without having the concept [cricket match]. If perceptual experience were itself conceptual this would not be possible.

    Of course I agree that experiences admit of some kind of normative evaluation. As you say, an experience can be (in)appropriate to that which it represents; it can be the product of malfunction, etc. There may well be a sense in which the normative does not supervene on the alethic, since a belief can be warranted even if false — and an experience can be appropriate even if nonveridical –, but I think that there is nevertheless an essential connection between the two. If a belief is not well placed to achieve truth, then it is not epistemically warranted. Presumably an analogue holds for experiences, veridicality, and the type of pseudo-epistemic norm at issue with them.

    I think that justification and rationality have to be treated separately. I follow Burge in viewing justification as requiring the possession of reasons. But a belief can be rational even where a subject lacks reasons, if certain other conditions are met. (Roughly, if the organism is functioning properly as a perceiving, or reasoning, being.) More generally, I think that epistemic warrant is a broader notion than justification; and it is broader than the notion of theoretical rationality as well. A belief that is not justified, in the sense that the believer has no reasons, and not rational (though not irrational either), in the sense that there is nothing in either the believer’s own reason, or reason itself, to support the content of the belief, can still be warranted — if the believer is functioning as she has been ‘designed’ to.

  11. uriah on December 9, 2005 at 12:33 am said:

    That is an puzzle, Jon! Interesting. My first thought is: how about the following position.

    There are two senses of “justified,” one broadly externalist and one broadly internalist. In the broadly externalist sense, there are theoretical reasons to say that experiences are justification-eligible after all, despite the counter-intuitiveness of it. They are justification-eligible because they are produced by certain mechanisms that are either reliable or not; or something like that. In the broadly internalist sense, experiences are not justification-eligible, and they are not justified because of the agential business. Experiences are something that happens to us, not something that we do. This is unlike forming or adopting a belief, which is something that we do rather than something that happens to us. Compare: when a thought that p just pops into my head, the thought is justification-ineligible, but when I form the belief that p, the belief is justification-eligible, even though the two states have the same content. The difference is in agency.

    The question is what to do with dispositional beliefs, because as you point out they raise a difficulty for the agency-based approach. I see three possible strategies:

    1. Bite the bullet and say that, in the broadly internalist sense, dispositional beliefs are not justification-eligible.
    2. Claim that, contrary to appearances, there is some agency involved in *acquiring* dispositional beliefs. (Perhaps sub-personal agency, if that makes sense? I’m just thinking out loud.)
    3. Apply 1 and 2 selectively to two exclusive and exhaustive subsets of dispositional beliefs. Consider John Pollock’s view that there are no dispositional beliefs other than beliefs that were originally occurrent and then were stored away. Suppose instead we say that there are two kinds of dispositional belief: Pollockian and non-Pollockian. We might with some justification apply 1 to the latter and 2 to the former.

    I like 3, but I can’t say that I *love* it.

    It’s a cool puzzle.

  13. kvanvig on December 10, 2005 at 5:50 pm said:

    Brad, it is important to note here that the kind of experience in question is that kind which bears on the justification of beliefs. The cricket match example doesn’t tell us about the nature of the perceptual experience, but rather gives an external account of it, and one which is irrelevant to the justificatory status of believing, e.g., that a cricket match is occurring.

    As to the distinctions between justification, rationality, and warrant, I take it that the basis for such distinctions is in terms of precisifying epistemological theory rather than recording distinctions present in ordinary language. When thought of in this way, I can’t sympathize: the best-developed version of proper function theory is Plantinga’s, and the prospects for such a theory are not good (for evidence to this effect, look at the volume of essays I edited on the view). Moreover, the claim that justification requires the possession of reasons seems strained. The concept of reasons and the concept of rationality are more natural bedfellows.

    One of the central questions here is what one takes the developed theory to be doing, and independent of that, I think the language of warrant, justification, and rationality is just too vague for any particular precisification to have much going for it over other kinds of precisification. One way to go here is to think of what one is doing in terms of a necessary condition for, or constituent of, knowledge. Another way to go is to think of what one is doing in terms of the egocentric predicament: how should I go about pursuing the purely cognitive goal or goals? There may be other ways as well, but these two are important ones, and take one in quite different directions when attempting to develop a theory using the language of justification or rationality or warrant.

  19. Michael Huemer on December 13, 2005 at 10:38 pm said:

    Jon,
    I actually discussed this in my Skepticism book (pp. 97-8). There, I offer as an initial explanation, that to say someone is justified or unjustified in doing x is to say, roughly, that they should or should not do x. We can say someone should or should not believe something, but we can’t say someone should or should not have an experience. I propose, further, that this is because we control our beliefs but not our experiences. (Now I would perhaps want to say: because we are responsible for our beliefs but not for our experiences; this is slightly different, since it doesn’t presuppose that responsibility requires control. It is controversial in the free will literature whether responsibility requires control.)

    About the dispositional belief problem: my view is that a dispositional belief is always grounded in an occurrent belief (past or present). This may be an earlier occurrent belief in the same proposition, or an occurrent belief in a proposition that is appropriately related to the proposition that is dispositionally believed. The idea of the latter possibility is that we might think q is somehow “implicit in” p; that by accepting p, I am implicitly accepting, or presupposing, q, or something similar. It is thus that we have responsibility for even dispositional beliefs.
