]]> By: Michael Huemer http://certaindoubts.com/on-the-justification-of-experiences/#comment-3014 Wed, 14 Dec 2005 03:38:23 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=476#comment-3014 Jon,
I actually discussed this in my Skepticism book (pp. 97-8). There, I offer as an initial explanation, that to say someone is justified or unjustified in doing x is to say, roughly, that they should or should not do x. We can say someone should or should not believe something, but we can’t say someone should or should not have an experience. I propose, further, that this is because we control our beliefs but not our experiences. (Now I would perhaps want to say: because we are responsible for our beliefs but not for our experiences; this is slightly different, since it doesn’t presuppose that responsibility requires control. It is controversial in the free will literature whether responsibility requires control.)

About the dispositional belief problem: my view is that a dispositional belief is always grounded in an occurrent belief (past or present). This may be an earlier occurrent belief in the same proposition, or an occurrent belief in a proposition that is appropriately related to the proposition that is dispositionally believed. The idea of the latter possibility is that we might think q is somehow “implicit in” p; that by accepting p, I am implicitly accepting, or presupposing, q, or something similar. It is thus that we have responsibility for even dispositional beliefs.

]]> By: kvanvig http://certaindoubts.com/on-the-justification-of-experiences/#comment-3027 Sat, 10 Dec 2005 22:50:19 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=476#comment-3027 Brad, it is important to note here that the kind of experience in question is that kind which bears on the justification of beliefs. The cricket match example doesn’t tell us about the nature of the perceptual experience, but rather gives an external account of it, and one which is irrelevant to the justificatory status of believing, e.g., that a cricket match is occurring.

As to the distinctions between justification, rationality, and warrant, I take it that the basis for such distinctions is in terms of precisifying epistemological theory rather than recording distinctions present in ordinary language. When thought of in this way, I can’t sympathize: the best-developed version of proper function theory is Plantinga’s, and the prospects for such a theory are not good (for evidence to this effect, look at the volume of essays I edited on the view). Moreover, the claim that justification requires the possession of reasons seems strained. The concept of reasons and the concept of rationality are more natural bedfellows.

One of the central questions here is what one takes the developed theory to be doing, and independent of that, I think the language of warrant, justification, and rationality is just too vague for any particular precisification to have much going for it over other kinds of precisification. One way to go here is to think of what one is doing in terms of a necessary condition for, or constituent of, knowledge. Another way to go is to think of what one is doing in terms of the egocentric predicament: how should I go about pursuing the purely cognitive goal or goals? There may be other ways as well, but these two are important ones, and take one in quite different directions when attempting to develop a theory using the language of justification or rationality or warrant.

]]> By: uriah http://certaindoubts.com/on-the-justification-of-experiences/#comment-3025 Fri, 09 Dec 2005 05:33:09 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=476#comment-3025 That is an puzzle, Jon! Interesting. My first thought is: how about the following position.

There are two senses of “justified,” one broadly externalist and one broadly internalist. In the broadly externalist sense, there are theoretical reasons to say that experiences are justification-eligible after all, despite the counter-intuitiveness of it. They are justification-eligible because they are produced by certain mechanisms that are either reliable or not; or something like that. In the broadly internalist sense, experiences are not justification-eligible, and they are not justified because of the agential business. Experiences are something that happens to us, not something that we do. This is unlike forming or adopting a belief, which is something that we do rather than something that happens to us. Compare: when a thought that p just pops into my head, the thought is justification-ineligible, but when I form the belief that p, the belief is justification-eligible, even though the two states have the same content. The difference is in agency.

The question is what to do with dispositional beliefs, because as you point out they raise a difficulty for the agency-based approach. I see three possible strategies:

1. Bite the bullet and say that, in the broadly internalist sense, dispositional beliefs are not justification-eligible.
2. Claim that, contrary to appearances, there is some agency involved in *acquiring* dispositional beliefs. (Perhaps sub-personal agency, if that makes sense? I’m just thinking out loud.)
3. Apply 1 and 2 selectively to two exclusive and exhaustive subsets of dispositional beliefs. Consider John Pollock’s view that there are no dispositional beliefs other than beliefs that were originally occurrent and then were stored away. Suppose instead we say that there are two kinds of dispositional belief: Pollockian and non-Pollockian. We might with some justification apply 1 to the latter and 2 to the former.

I like 3, but I can’t say that I *love* it.

It’s a cool puzzle.
