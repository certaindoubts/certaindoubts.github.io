Belief and Agreement

Posted on August 15, 2004 by Kvanvig JonAugust 15, 2004

Here’s a followup to the discussion that is occurring about the role of disagreement as a defeater of justification, focusing on the other side of the coin: the epistemic significance of agreement.

So suppose you believe a claim and have pretty good evidence for it. But your evidence is insufficient for knowledge: even if your belief is true, you don’t know that the claim is true. You hit on a strategy to correct your epistemic deficiency. You reason that if the view of some is correct about disagreement with peers or superiors constituting a defeater of justification, then agreement by your peers or superiors ought also to improve your evidential position. It matters to you a great deal whether you have knowledge in this case, so you plan your day. You spend the entire day visiting one peer or superior after another, hoping they agree with you. As it happens, they do. You never ask them why they believe as they do, however; you don’t have time for that. You’ve got things to do, places to go, people to see…

By the end of the day, do you now know that the claim is true? I think the intuitive response is “no” (especially if none of them has any better evidence for the claim than you do). If you think otherwise, imagine that all your peers and superiors were together at an Agree-ers support group, leaving you with only one place to go to complete your rounds for the day. You don’t know what the support group is for, and you’re surprised to find everyone you’re looking for there, but here they are! You congratulate yourself on your luck, and on how easy it was to advance your epistemic condition today.

I think this simply doesn’t work; you don’t come to know in such a case. That response is in some tension, however, with the obvious point that we can acquire knowledge by testimony. So if you agree with me that soliciting agreement won’t by itself turn your weak evidence into strong evidence, what’s the difference between the agreement case and cases where testimony imparts knowledge?

    Uriah, I like your way of separating out various possible cases here, and I think as well that the interesting cases are of the last variety you delineate.

    Here’s my worry about that kind of case, and it concerns whether the procedure followed is really one of gathering evidence. Let’s suppose that the subject is within your range of competence and that you’ve conducted what you take to be a thorough and complete assessment of the issue. So you’ve concluded that the evidence favors p, but not in a strong enough way for you to have knowledge. Because you view your inquiry as fair, honest, thorough, and complete, you don’t believe, and have no grounds for believing, that anyone you consult will have some special insight into the matter that you lack (though you know that such insight is possible and perhaps has some chance of existing). Some of them are smarter and some not, to be sure, and their quality of inquiry may vary, for all you know, from casual to complete as well.

    I haven’t got to the heart of what is disconcerting about agreement vs. testimony, and I’m still flailing around hoping to hit on what is bothering me here. Here’s a thought in that direction, though. To learn from testimony is to treat it as a source of information about a subject matter. In the typical case, that involves coming to learn something you knew little about prior to the testimony. If testimony has to be filtered through some sort of additional belief about the value and need of an additional source of information, we could appeal to that requirement here, but I’m trying not to impose additional beliefs about the value or need for testimony in order for it to be epistemically efficacious, since I think such requirements are incorrect. But even if such additional beliefs are not necessary for testimony to be a source of evidence, it can still be possible for a person to have additional beliefs and attitudes that prevent the word of others from having any epistemic effect.

    This last remark is obvious in one way: if you believe a testifier to be wholly unreliable, it would be irrational for the testimony to bolster your confidence. My idea is that seeking agree-ers should be handled within this part of the theory of testimony–that viewing oneself as competent and one’s inquiry as thorough, fair, honest, and complete is (close to) sufficient to undermine testimony from functioning as a source of additional evidence. So when our confidence level is increased in such cases, it’s just an attitudinal overgeneralization from the usual significance that the word of one’s peers and superiors usually has.

But, usually we’d find that S’s confidence boost comes from not just the agreement, but the credentials of the agree-ers. This then, becomes new evidence in the form of “This person, who is very credible on this subject agrees with me that P”, and in this way it is the same as testimony. Thus, I think that when S consults R and knows that R is quite smart and reliable, then it is a case of testimonial knowledge transmission. The only time it wouldn’t be testimonial transmission is when new evidence is not introduced either implicitly or explicitly, such as a case where S consults a total stranger R and R agrees with them, but S has no idea about R’s epistemic credibility or reliability. In that case, it also seems that only if S was epistemically inept would he/she gain a boost of confidence.
