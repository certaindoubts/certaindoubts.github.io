]]> By: jon kvanvig http://certaindoubts.com/belief-and-agreement/#comment-582 Sat, 21 Aug 2004 12:41:13 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=100#comment-582 Mike, I think if you say the boost in confidence is justified, and mean by that epistemically justified, you’re going to have to deny that you can explain the notion of justification in terms of evidence. But then it is hard to see what explains the justification here.

It is clear that if there is such a boost, it is justified in some sense or other by agreement in the usual case, but the justification would seem explicable by the value of cooperative actions and attitudes, which would make it a form of pragmatic justification.

In your last sentence you give a version of the principle I’m after: one that separates testimony from simple agreement. A couple of thoughts about it. When you qualify the agreement so that you know that the person is smart and reliable, that would be too strong a requirement on ordinary testimony. You don’t formulate the sentence as a necessary condition, but you do say that new evidence has to be introduced, and this is an example of it. But in ordinary testimony cases, the new evidence is the direct result of the testimony itself, without any obvious need for having a view about the quality of the source. In this way, testimony works like perception, where you also acquire evidence without needing to have a view about the quality of your faculties. So I think the principle that I’m looking for can’t distinguish testimony from simple agreement based on some view about the quality of the source.

If we are externalists about justification, the principle is easier to formulate, I think. We would start from the idea that testimony is transmitted from sources that have more evidence than you do, and simple agreement would involve sources with no better evidence than you’ve got already. There will still be counterexamples to this proposal, but some chisholming away ought to work.

But if your not an externalist about justification, then this line of thought isn’t available. And since I’m not an externalist about justification, the problem still seems to me to be very hard (and I’ll take comfort in the fact that most seem to agree that there is a real problem here! :)).

]]> By: Mike Sturm http://certaindoubts.com/belief-and-agreement/#comment-581 Sat, 21 Aug 2004 03:11:42 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=100#comment-581 I’m not sure if I trailed off of what was originally being argued, but I agree that there is a boost of confidence from agreement, but I don’t agree that this boost is an epistemic benefit itself or even leads to one. Jon’s comment back in 2 about agreement not pumping up evidence is more of what I was getting at. Like I said, I agree that in Chris’s examples, S was justified in his/her confidence boost, but not any more justified in believing that P. The reason is that if when S is questioned as to why he/she more strongly believes that P, he or she would only answer “because so and so agrees with me”. If that is S’s only reason, he/she is not any more justified than before the agreement. But, usually we’d find that S’s confidence boost comes from not just the agreement, but the credentials of the agree-ers. This then, becomes new evidence in the form of “This person, who is very credible on this subject agrees with me that P”, and in this way it is the same as testimony. Thus, I think that when S consults R and knows that R is quite smart and reliable, then it is a case of testimonial knowledge transmission. The only time it wouldn’t be testimonial transmission is when new evidence is not introduced either implicitly or explicitly, such as a case where S consults a total stranger R and R agrees with them, but S has no idea about R’s epistemic credibility or reliability. In that case, it also seems that only if S was epistemically inept would he/she gain a boost of confidence.

]]> By: jon kvanvig http://certaindoubts.com/belief-and-agreement/#comment-578 Fri, 20 Aug 2004 00:10:13 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=100#comment-578 Chris, I think it’s a mistake to try to model the notion of evidence on the notion of malfunctioning. Notice also that Al doesn’t consistently follow this practice: attend to his argument against evolutionary naturalism. When he considers what would and what would not be a defeater, he’s committed to claiming that those who see his argument and don’t abandon their EN beliefs are malfunctioning. But he never argues such. Similarly, on the other side of the coin, those who claim that e isn’t evidence for p shouldn’t be required to show that those who conclude p from e are malfunctioning.

Furthermore, the appeal to proper functionalism is misplaced here. There are easy explanations about the value of human cooperation and agreement that can explain the way in which agreement bolsters confidence that don’t involve malfunction. They just don’t cite a part of the design plan aimed at truth. So if you think this version of conservatism is true, and you assume proper functionalism, you have to claim that the response pattern is part of the design plan aimed at truth. It’s hard to see why that would be the case, in the absence of some sort of argument that most beliefs have to be true (or that the designer isn’t especially adept at designing systems that accomplish the intended purpose).
