My sense is that you are right about the history and the relation to RA theories. Here is something you don’t mention, but that I think is another salient feature of the history of the topic: Once contextualism did come clearly into focus, almost all of the early discussion was around its adequacy as a response to skepticism. In fact, in those days people hardly made the distinction between contextualism as a reply to skepticism and contextualism as a semantic thesis. As things developed, though, people talked less and less about the former and a lot more about the latter. So, for example, the Stanley and Hawthorne books. By now it is clear that contextualism might be right as a semantic thesis about “knows” even if it does not give us an adequate answer to skepticism (that is my view, for example). Another aspect of the discussion you might mention is that contextuali began to be offered as a solution to problems other than skepticism, so for example Cohen on the lottery problem and Heller on the generality problem.

But I’m wondering mostly about my general sense of how the topic progressed — and whether others have the same sense: contextualism existing in 70s in the form of contextualist versions of RA, but not really being disentangled from RA (which can also come in invariantist forms) so that the contextualism itself was a subject of evaluation; the view being identified and addressed on its own in the 80s, with considerations for & against starting to be identified; the topic taking off in the 90s.

It’s interesting to see how topics/views pass in & out of favor in our field. I don’t really have that good a sense of what things were like in the 80s: I wasn’t as plugged into the philsophical scene while I was in grad school (89-90 was my last year), as I was in the 90s, so I feel a little unsure about comparisons. But I do remember looking at one of those Blackwell philosophy reference works from the early 90s (91 or 92, maybe?): I think it was edited by Dancy, and was called something like the “Companion” to epistemology. Anyway, as I remember, it was a big ol’ book with mostly short little articles, so it had LOTS of articles — like hundreds, I think. But, it seemed, nothing on contextualism. That would have been unlikely of such a book if it were done at the end of that decade, I think.
