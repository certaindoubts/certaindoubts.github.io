I guess I’d let the theorist tell me which he or she is doing, and then evaluate based on the answers.

The only thing I’d point out is that you can have ambiguities with senses that are nonetheless logically related. Think of “necking”. OK, besides the meaning we all think of, there is a behavior among giraffes that goes by the same name. They spar with each other, in a way involving their necks. So necks are involved in both senses, even though the term is ambiguous. Or think of those who (mistakenly!) claim that ‘or’ is ambiguous between inclusive and exclusive senses.

]]> By: Keith DeRose http://certaindoubts.com/five-kinds-of-metatheory/#comment-1838 Fri, 27 May 2005 14:51:36 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=332#comment-1838 OK, but there’s amibiguity & there’s ambiguity (!), and I fear your way of dividing things up may overstate the difference between standard contextualism and the various “two senses of ‘know'” (2SK) theories. I take it you want (reasonaly enough) to classify the latter as ambiguity theories. If so, then it’s vital to notice the important differences between this & many other cases of ambiguity. On such 2SK theories, “knows” is very different from bank (financial institution) / bank (as in river bank). There’s no temptation at all in the case of bank/bank to employ character/content to explain what’s going on. (What could be the common character, which takes on different contents depending on context?) But if I’m understanding correctly what kinds of “ambiguity” theories you have in mind wrt “knows” (the 2SK theories, as I’m calling them) that’s a very different story. Typically, on such theories, “knowing” in one of senses the theory posits (high, strong, philosophical, etc.) entails knowing in the other sense (low, weak, ordinary, etc.), and what’s more, what it takes to “know” in the higher sense is recognizably some stronger version of what it takes to “know” in the weaker. Generally, higher “knowing” is like weaker “knowing” on steroids. Here, it’s not crazy to employ something like character/content explanations — though when there are only two “senses” being posited, it’s not compelling to do so. In fact, such 2SK theories can be fit into (or crammed into, if you take a darker view of it) pretty much the same basic story as standard contextualism, on which the character of “S knows that P” is that S has a true belief that P and is in a sufficiently strong epistemic position wrt P, and where context fixes content on a particular use by selecting how strong is sufficiently strong — it’s just that on 2SK theories, there are just two options for context to choose between, rather than a continuum of many possible epistemic standards.

Of course, we also shouldn’t understate the differences between 2SK theories & standard contextualism.
