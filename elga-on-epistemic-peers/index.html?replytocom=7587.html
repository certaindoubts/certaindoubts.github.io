Elga on Epistemic Peers

Posted on October 20, 2007 by Ted PostonOctober 20, 2007

In order to break the trend of announcements I’ll venture a comment on Adam Elga’s recent Nous paper “Reflection and Disagreement”. Elga argues for the equal weight view which, as a first pass, states that you should assign the conclusions of your epistemic peer the same weight you assign your own conclusions. I was surprised, though, that Elga characterizes an epistemic peer this way:

My use of the term “epistemic peer” is nonstandard. On my usage, you count your friend as an epistemic peer with respect to an about-to-be-judged claim if and only if you think that, conditional the two of you disagreeing about the claim, the two of you are equally likely to be mistaken (p. 499, endnote 21).

On this characterization the equal weight view seems to be a trivial consequence of thinking someone is my epistemic peer. The standard usage invokes the idea of general intellectual virtues, e.g., thoughtfulness, honesty, intelligence, freedom from bias, etc. Given the standard view it seems obvious that the equal weight view is false. My colleagues in the math department are my epistemic peers but if I disagree with them on some theorem in topology I should go with their judgment. A more relevant sense of an epistemic peer will invoke intellectual virtues and broadly evidential considerations. On this characterization your epistemic peer has roughly the same evidence that you possess vis-a-vis a target proposition and is roughly just as competent as you in evaluating the evidence. This view of epistemic peers doesn’t seem to trivialize the debate in either direction.

Here’s another consideration in favor of a more general notion of epistemic peer. It strikes me that no one would think it right to favor the result of one metal detector over another equally good metal detector when the circumstances were the same and they disagreed. So, to the extent you view another person and yourself as equally good truth-detectors in some domain and yet you disagree, it seems you should assign equal weight to each other’s conclusions.

  8. Matt Benton on October 22, 2007 at 1:54 pm said:

    I read Elga’s paper in manuscript; what I find interesting about Elga’s nonstandard notion of peerhood is that it depends on a subject’s *judgment* about who is a peer. But Elga also points out in other written work that we tend to overrate our own capacities, intelligence, our abilities to process data and weigh evidence, and so on. (See his “On Overrating Oneself… and Knowing It,” Phil Studies 123 (2005).) He acknowledges that even with the knowledge that we are subject to this distorting bias of generally overrating ourselves, it is nearly impossible for us to downgrade our own self-assessments in order to correct for it. These facts naturally support the idea that we are, and will be, quite poor at determining who ought to count as our epistemic peers.
    And if this is right, we might be poor peer designators in more than one way; I might make the wrong (inflated) judgment about my own capacities but then make the right judgment about who would be my peers; or I might the right judgment about my capabilities but then the wrong judgment about who is my peer (I might count my epistemic inferiors as peers so as to come out “on top” more often, thus confirming the overrating bias).

    John,

    For what it’s worth, I agree with Matt, so much so that I think this is a good example AGAINST the idea of “pragmatic encroachment”. But even if we grant that there is an epistemic upshot to the fact that a “metal” reading is practically more significant in the bomb case, I am not sure that this is an epistemic upshot that is illuminating in the case of disagreement. Suppose that the equal weight view is right, and so we should treat the readings of two detectors as equally significant, splitting the difference as the view suggests. Once we have the right credence according to the view, only then do we proceed to decide which practical or epistemic course of action is appropriate. Or so it seems the story should go. At that point, the situation is similar to the one in which we would be if instead of two peer detectors we had one detector that we knew to be not terribly reliable and were being encroached upon by the pragmatic as we are in the bomb case.

    The question of what notion of epistemic peerhood is more appropriate strikes me as lacking an unequivocal answer. But as far as peer disagreement goes, the right place to start looking for the right notion could be by considering what happens when we look at a putative case of peer disagreement from the outside. What makes us treat two people as each other’s epistemic peers (with respect to some domain D, which, incidentally could never plausibly consist of only one proposition if we allow that they share some deductive mastery)? What norms should guide our own judgments once we find them disagreeing about a proposition in D? (Incidentally, this may help us avoid some of the complications arising from the bias of self-evaluation.)

  14. Gregory Wheeler on October 23, 2007 at 9:45 am said:

    To play Devil’s advocate, what is the point to having a theory of epistemic peers?

    There are lots of interesting topics that the example raises: issues surround assessing another’s epistemic position with respect to a claim, or (better) with respect to a situation; issues surrounding assessing the practical interests involved in this assessment, and whether there is agreement in those interests between the agents; and, perhaps, there are issues about decisions an agent might make, even if only to revise or update. And the particular example of the metal detector raises even more interesting issues. Set those aside for the moment.

    It would seem that we already have rich mathematical theories that study the basic issues involved: game theory, is one, belief kinematics, another.

    So, perhaps the question(s) should be Is there new ground being worked over here? If so, what is it? Why should a game theorist, to take an example, be interested in this discussion? Or, put the other way around, why are epistemic peer theorists warranted to ignore the results and methods in nearby theories?
