Plantinga’s models and the death of epistemology

Posted on June 18, 2004 by Jon KvanvigJune 20, 2012

I posted the following on Prosblogion, but it is really an epistemology question as well, so I’ll post it here, too, with some emendations:

I’ve been reading Plantinga’s third volume and am puzzled by his account. As I understand it, Plantinga wants to block merely epistemic complaints about Christian belief–that is, he wants to block the kind of complaint where you bracket the truth of the claims in question and claim that whether or not the beliefs are true, there is something wrong, on purely intellectual grounds, with holding them.

The method used by Plantinga seems to be this: describe a model on which, together with his account of warrant, the beliefs would be warranted if the model is true. If Plantinga can do this, he thinks he has shown that no purely epistemic criticisms of the beliefs are defensible. The model, naturally enough for Plantinga, contains a number of elements that, because they are part of the model, are not defended. These include the existence of God, a special faculty he terms the sensus divinitatis, the doctrine of the fall, and other things. The strategy of building a model and getting to include whatever one wishes with no need of defense is the perplexing part for me.

Here’s why it’s perplexing. Try to describe a belief that is a paradigm case of an epistemically problematic belief. I submit that I can always construct a model on which, together with Plantinga’s account of warrant, the belief in question would be warranted. How could I fail, since I’m allowed to put in special faculties and any further features I wish to postulate, with no need to defend any of them–after all, they are just part of the model.

If that’s right, then the Plantingian method is the hired gun on behalf of the death of epistemology movement, isn’t it? If my jaded account of his approach is correct, then the only issues we have any reason to discuss are issues about the truth or falsity of a belief (as far as object-level epistemology goes; there’d still be the possibility of doing meta-epistemology, addressing such questions as whether there is any pragmatic encroachment in the theory of knowledge). Discussing the truth or falsity of the views we hold is certainly worth doing, but my concern remains that there ought to be cases where we can criticize a belief on epistemic grounds independent of the question of truth or falsity.

So my question is this: how can we understand Plantinga’s appeal to models without it having this untoward implication?

    So, roughly the problem is this: Start with some broad form of reliabilism. Then add that there is this mechanism x which is reliable. That x exists and is reliable is not open to dispute. And x happens to produce a belief that p. So,the belief that p is warranted.

    I think there are two things to say, which are related. First, there still are concerns of rationality and the like that need to be addressed with respect to p. Second, the plausibility of the entire enterprise will depend on the plausibility of the propositions from Christian theism it incorporates. So, we will have questions like: How plausible is Christian theism and how plausible is it to think that, given Christian theism, there is the sensus divinitatis. Though Plantinga isn’t so concerned with giving evidence for Christian theism, I think that there is (supposed to be) a difference between Al’s proposal and some other proposal where you take his account of warrant, tack on some crazy mechanism, say that it’s reliable, and say that therefore beliefs it produces are warranted (when clearly they’re not). Christian theism (together with the sensus divinitatis) as a world view is supposed to be reasonable in a way that your tacked-on mechanism isn’t. Furthermore, with Christianity we don’t have an easy reductio (or many would say we don’t) of the following sort: The sensus divinitatis gives rise to a belief that p (or better, a whole class of beliefs, p1, …,pn)). But there’s no way p1-pn are true, so we have reason to question the reliability of the mechanism (or, perhaps, its existence). But if you posit some mechanism and say it is reliable (as part of your model), and it is spitting out nutty beliefs, we have reason to question the model–either the reliability of the mechanism or the existence of the mechanism.

  3. jon kvanvig on June 18, 2004 at 4:53 pm said:

    Yes, Keith, I agree that this is much like the GPO. There are differences, however. Most important, the GPO is directly about what is properly basic and what is not. My worry is about absolutely every belief “made clean” with reference to the model, whether basic or not. What’s surprising is that the GPO was never compelling to my mind. But here, without some constraints on the model, I think there is no hope.

    So Matt proposes that we think of the model as having restrictions on it. In particular, the elements of the model must have a certain degree of “plausibility” to them. I like that proposal, but Plantinga can’t adopt it. The notion of plausibility here is an epistemic one, and if the model has to have elements that are plausible in themselves, then we need volume 2.5 from Al to explain how the elements in his model are plausible.

    I sound quite unsympathetic here to Al’s project, but there is something to be said for approaching the rationality of religious belief in terms of robust versions of theism rather than truncated ones. I think we do something similar with scientific theories–we don’t isolate the idea that the speed of light is constant and evaluate it on its own apart from its place in relativity theory. The question is how to do this.

    Viewed in this way, Matt’s suggestion sounds too atomic for my tastes. It looks like the evaluation ought to be more holistic than requiring that each element of the model pass some plausibility constraint before being allowed into the model.
