Having read Platinga’s trilogy I come to the conclusion that, while he doesn’t like the traditional normative notion of justification, he has managed to repartition that sense of “justification” out of “warrant” and into “proper function,” the second part of _Warrant and Proper Function_. That book in particular spends a great deal of time describing people with various bizarre beliefs (like the Great Pumpkin) that they believe have warrant but which he says “obviously lack warrant”. I don’t think we’re apt to disagree with that assessment. But why is it obvious to us and not to these unfortunates? Because we properly function. We have a properly functioning belief-producing mechanism which was implanted in us “by God or evolution or both)” that allows us to discern whether a belief is truly warranted. Theirs has been damaged somehow, by sin or disease, etc.

So Al seems to have produced a blanket immunity for epistemic complaint in a pure normative sense, while allowing for “epistemic pity” for those who don’t function properly. It looks like all bizarre beliefs that obviously lack warrant, if coming from a sensus divinitatis-installed person, can be attributed to malfunction. And if one were able to construct a “sensus voodoois”-type design plan, he’d argue it wasn’t credible or, possibly given his other scientific arguments, that the facts of nature are otherwise.

Well, obvious entailment ought to count as a right relation, and if a claim is subjectively credible, then the model could contain the element in question.

OK, that’s supposed to be cheating. If we follow Al’s lead, the model will have to contain subjectively credible accounts of a design plan and a belief forming module in accord with the design plan, along with credible circumstances in which the belief in question would be produced. That will help Al with the GPO, but it won’t keep an bizarre belief we care to imagine from being immune to epistemic complaint in some possible circumstance. Al may take comfort in the claim that such bizarre beliefs won’t, in actuality, be subject to epistemic complaint, since it may be that such a belief is never held by individuals for whom the needed information for constructing the model is subjectively credible.

]]> By: jon kvanvig http://certaindoubts.com/plantingas-models-and-the-death-of-epistemology/#comment-100 Fri, 18 Jun 2004 21:53:29 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=24#comment-100 Yes, Keith, I agree that this is much like the GPO. There are differences, however. Most important, the GPO is directly about what is properly basic and what is not. My worry is about absolutely every belief “made clean” with reference to the model, whether basic or not. What’s surprising is that the GPO was never compelling to my mind. But here, without some constraints on the model, I think there is no hope.

So Matt proposes that we think of the model as having restrictions on it. In particular, the elements of the model must have a certain degree of “plausibility” to them. I like that proposal, but Plantinga can’t adopt it. The notion of plausibility here is an epistemic one, and if the model has to have elements that are plausible in themselves, then we need volume 2.5 from Al to explain how the elements in his model are plausible.

I sound quite unsympathetic here to Al’s project, but there is something to be said for approaching the rationality of religious belief in terms of robust versions of theism rather than truncated ones. I think we do something similar with scientific theories–we don’t isolate the idea that the speed of light is constant and evaluate it on its own apart from its place in relativity theory. The question is how to do this.

Viewed in this way, Matt’s suggestion sounds too atomic for my tastes. It looks like the evaluation ought to be more holistic than requiring that each element of the model pass some plausibility constraint before being allowed into the model.

]]> By: Matt Davidson http://certaindoubts.com/plantingas-models-and-the-death-of-epistemology/#comment-99 Fri, 18 Jun 2004 20:35:47 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=24#comment-99 So, roughly the problem is this: Start with some broad form of reliabilism. Then add that there is this mechanism x which is reliable. That x exists and is reliable is not open to dispute. And x happens to produce a belief that p. So,the belief that p is warranted.

I think there are two things to say, which are related. First, there still are concerns of rationality and the like that need to be addressed with respect to p. Second, the plausibility of the entire enterprise will depend on the plausibility of the propositions from Christian theism it incorporates. So, we will have questions like: How plausible is Christian theism and how plausible is it to think that, given Christian theism, there is the sensus divinitatis. Though Plantinga isn’t so concerned with giving evidence for Christian theism, I think that there is (supposed to be) a difference between Al’s proposal and some other proposal where you take his account of warrant, tack on some crazy mechanism, say that it’s reliable, and say that therefore beliefs it produces are warranted (when clearly they’re not). Christian theism (together with the sensus divinitatis) as a world view is supposed to be reasonable in a way that your tacked-on mechanism isn’t. Furthermore, with Christianity we don’t have an easy reductio (or many would say we don’t) of the following sort: The sensus divinitatis gives rise to a belief that p (or better, a whole class of beliefs, p1, …,pn)). But there’s no way p1-pn are true, so we have reason to question the reliability of the mechanism (or, perhaps, its existence). But if you posit some mechanism and say it is reliable (as part of your model), and it is spitting out nutty beliefs, we have reason to question the model–either the reliability of the mechanism or the existence of the mechanism.
