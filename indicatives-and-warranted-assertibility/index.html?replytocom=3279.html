Indicatives and Warranted Assertibility

Posted on February 10, 2006 by Kvanvig JonFebruary 10, 2006

From Keith’s talk here two weekends ago, here’s an example (borrowed, I think, from Harper?):

You’ve got a deck of 101 cards, numbered from 0 through 100. (I can’t remember the names Keith used, so I’ll make some up.) Each player chooses one, and high card wins. Slimy Slim cheats by having two friends help see what his opponent, Billy Bob, holds. Turns out Billy Bob holds an 83. Friend 1 signals to SS that BB has an 83, and says to a bystander, “If SS plays, he will win.” He says this because he knows SS isn’t stupid enough to play and lose when he knows what his opponent’s card is. The second cheating friend is behind SS, sees friend 1’s signal, knows what it means and so knows what BB holds. But he has more information: he also sees that SS is holding 55. So he says, “If SS plays, he will not win.”

Both assertions are appropriate, but both can’t be true (without introducing contextuality into the account). So suppose you want to hold that friend 1’s assertion is false. Fritz and Keith talked about this possibility, with Keith holding (I think) that you can’t say that the first assertion is warranted and yet false. The principle he appealed to is something like this: warrant for assertion can’t be lost by learning something that you already take to be highly likely. For friend 1, it’s highly likely that SS’s card is lower than BB’s, so the principle implies that you can’t explain the conflict between the two cases by saying they are both warranted but only one true.

Earlier I posted about the following probabilistic defeater principle: the truth of p can’t be a defeater of a piece of reasoning unless the high probability of p is a defeater as well. Several counterexamples were noted to the principle there, but it’s not clear to me whether the counterexamples show that the argument above won’t work. We can restrict the claim so that we are not talking about epistemic modals or probability claims, though that still leaves Branden’s example in comment 9 from the above thread. This example still seems to me avoidable, because the example can be retained no matter how high the probability is for friend 1 that Slimy Slim’s card loses. So I expect that the likelihoods that Branden notes will threaten the principle in some cases but not in others, so not all version of the above example will be undermined, since we’ll still get some cases where both assertions are warranted and yet the probabilistic claim is a defeater if the claim itself is.

Here’s a somewhat different worry, and it concerns defeaters which are undercutters. Take a case where the undercutter is reference-class-relative. For example, suppose Joe tells you that p is true, and the undercutter is that Joe always lies to epistemologists. If you know that, you’ve got an undercutter for the warranting relationship between testimony and content. But now suppose you only know that it is likely that Joe always lies to epistemologists. This information is compatible with further information that makes it reasonable to believe that Joe is not now lying to you, even though you’re an epistemologist. In such a case, the mere likelihood that Joe always lies in this way is not an undercutter, but learning that it is true, rather than merely likely, would be (since it would then rule out that Joe is not now lying to you).
This example makes me wonder whether there is a version of the probabilistic defeater principle that can be used to show that Fritz’s account of the case, in terms of warranted but false assertion, is mistaken.

    The principle he appealed to is something like this: warrant for assertion can’t be lost by learning something that you already take to be highly likely.

    That’s not quite it. I don’t think I believe the above principle — though I haven’t thought much about it. Suppose you have warrant for asserting p, even though you take d to be highly likely, and d is evidence (presumably weak evidence) against p. Coming to learn that d is true, as opposed to just probably true, it seems, could reduce your warrant for p. And I don’t see why it couldn’t push you below the threshold for being warranted in asserting p.

    I was using a principle like this: You are not warranted in asserting something that is, from your own point of view, very likely false.

    The argument goes like this (it’s at least close to an argument Gibbard himself uses, or at least suggests). Since Friend 1’s assertion (“If SS plays, he’ll win”) is warranted, we shouldn’t judge that his assertion is false unless there’s some relevant underlying fact about which Friend 1 is mistaken or is ignorant of, and this error/ignorance can explain why he makes his false assertion. F1 is of course ignorant of this fact: SS holds the lower card. (There are other, related facts about which SS is also ignorant, but the argument to follow applies to them as well.) And it is extremely relevant: If F1 knew this fact, he wouldn’t assert what does. But this fact can’t make F1’s assertion false. F1 thinks it’s quite likely that SS holds the lower card: Its probability, from F1’a point of view is .83. So if it were enough to make F1’s assertion false, then, from F1’s point of view, his assertion would have a .83 probability of being false. Since you can’t properly assert what, from your own point of view, is that probably false, F1 wouldn’t be warranted in making his assertion. But F1 is warranted in making the assertion. So, the fact that SS holds the lower card doesn’t render the assertion false. What, then, does? Nothing. There are no good candidates. (All the initially promising candidates can be rolled up into one big supposition which F1 thinks is quite likely. Apply the above argument to that big supposition.) It’s true — or at least not false.

    Yeah, I don’t see why Greg Wheeler supposed F1 and F2 to be using different conditionals, rather than both using the material conditional. As others have noted, the problem goes away if you assume the indicative conditional is the material conditional — F1 and F2 are both right, as long as Slimy doesn’t play.

    Here’s my argument that the indicative conditional in English is the material conditional (caveat: this argument may be wrong):

    1. The indicative conditional entails the material conditional. This is uncontroversial; I’m just saying that if “if A then B” holds, then at a bare minimum, it can’t be that A is true and B is false.

    2. The material conditional entails the indicative conditional. Here is a story.

    You get a chance to talk to God one day, and you ask him, “God, am I going to go to heaven?” God says, “Well, I won’t tell you that, but I’ll tell you this much: you won’t be there without Mike.” You then reflect, “Hm, so if I’m going to heaven, Mike is too.” Seems like a good inference. But look:

    God told you: ~(U & ~M).
    You inferred: If U then M.

    But ~(U & ~M) is logically equivalent to the material conditional, (U > M). So it looks like the material conditional (U > M) entails the English indicative conditional (if U then M).

    3. Therefore, the indicative conditional is logically equivalent to the material conditional. And if you accept this, you might as well say they are the same.

  19. Michael Kremer on February 12, 2006 at 10:39 am said:

    Here’s Stalnaker’s argument, boiled down as much as I can.

    (1) The idea is that the inference from P or Q to if ~P then Q is reasonable in the following sense: in every context in which the premise could appropriately be asserted or supposed, anyone who accepts the premise is thereby committed to the conclusion. Stalnaker argues that reasonable inference does not imply valid inference, and shows that on his theory of conditionals the above inference is reasonable but not valid (as are contraposition and hypothetical syllogism).

    (2) Context here includes presumed common knowledge and assumptions. The speaker must believe that these assumptions are common to him and his audience. This fixes a set of possible worlds, the context set, among which the speaker is intending to distinguish. A proposition is compatible with the context set if true in some world in the context set, entailed by the context set if true in all worlds in the context set.

    (3) Stalnaker’s semantics of conditionals is determined by a “selection function” that selects, for each world w, and each possible proposition (non-empty set of worlds) P, a world in P (intended to be the “closest” world to w at which P is true — if w is in P, then the s(w,P) will be w). A conditional if P then Q is true at a world w iff s(w,P) is in Q. (Conditionals with impossible antecedents are automatically counted as true. Stalnaker imposes further constraints on s intended to insure that it establishes a linear ordering of worlds as more or less similar.)

    (4) The selection function to be employed in evaluating any assertion of a conditional is contextually determined, and the following constraint applies: If a conditional, if P then Q, is being evaluated at a world in the context set, then if there are any P-worlds in the context set, then the selected P-world must be in the context set as well.

    (5) This constraint is a defeasible presumption, but there is a conventional way to defeat the presumption, namely the subjunctive mood. For an indicative conditional, the presumption holds without exception.

    (6) Therefore it is appropriate to assert or suppose an indicative conditional only in a context which is compatible with the antecedent.

    (7) Finally, Stalnaker assumes that it is appropriate to assert or suppose a disjunction only in a context which leaves it open that either disjunct may be true without the other. (In any context not satisfying the condition, the assertion of the disjunction would be pragmatically equivalent to the asserting of one of the disjuncts.)

    (8) Now the argument: Suppose that P v Q is appropriately asserted or supposed in context C. Then both P & ~Q and ~P & Q are compatible with the context (by (7)). Hence ~P is compatible with the context.

    (9) Now suppose that the premise P v Q is accepted. This changes the context so that all worlds in which neither P nor Q are true are excluded. But there will still be worlds in the context set in which ~P is true, and in all such worlds Q will be true. The selection function will thus be constrained so that for any world w in the context set, s(w,~P) will have to be a ~P world in the context set, and so a Q-world. Thus, at any world in the context set, the conditional if ~P then Q will be evaluated as true. (Stalnaker argues a little differently. I think I’ve isolated the gist of the thing though.)

    (10) Therefore, given the suppositions in (8) and (9), the conditional if ~P then Q must be accepted as well. That is: in every context in which the premise P v Q could appropriately be asserted or supposed, anyone who accepts the premise is thereby committed to the conclusion if ~P then Q. That is: the inference from P v Q to if ~P then Q is a reasonable inference.

    NOTE: I think there is wiggle room at the last step — which Stalnaker doesn’t make fully explicit (his slightly different argument obscures this a little). Stalnaker implicitly assumes, I think, that if a proposition A is true at every world in the context set, then A must be accepted, and vice-versa. This may, I suppose, be debated.

    (11) Stalnaker concludes that the assertion and acceptance conditions of P v Q and if ~P then Q are the same. But he also claims that their truth conditions are different. Thus, if I know that ~Q, but I’ve kept this a secret, and I also believe P, and someone asserts the indicative conditional if ~P then Q, I can appropriately deny this conditional, although I will not deny the disjunction P v Q.

  23. Gregory Wheeler on February 13, 2006 at 9:20 am said:

    It is not unusual to for us to use indicative conditionals to express something other than the truth functional reading: “If it rains in Lisbon, the ground will get wet in Lisbon” is an indicative conditional that expresses something other than either it doesn’t rain in Lisbon or the ground will get wet in Lisbon. “If R is not transitive, then R will not be rational” doesn’t.

    We use natural language `if A then B’ sentences to express all kinds of things: to signal that B is entailed by A, that B is supported by evidence A, that A causes B, that if we suppose A to happen, we may expect B to happen…and this is without getting into the mood, tense and aspect of the constituents, A, B, which are not, typically, preserved when they are dropped into the gaps of `if…then…’ constructions. (Example regarding tense: ‘He stopped calling her’ is past tenst, but not when it is inserted in ‘If he stopped calling her, she would be more receptive’.)

    There is no reason to think that indicative conditionals are truth functional, or to suppose that they are intensional, or to maintain that they express conditional probabilities. It is the wrong question to chase after.

    On the contrary, the extensive literature on conditionals suggests that there are no precise features for indicative conditionals—or, conversely, that no class of expressions corresponding to a fixed set of logical features is co-extensive with the class of indicative conditional sentences in English.

    All that I’ve said here is not to say that linguists shouldn’t be working on the formal semantics of indicative conditionals in natural languages like English. Nor do I deny that logical and probabilistic tools are indispensable to them. But formal semantics is but an application of logic, and it is one that has almost nothing to do with figuring out issues involving entailment.

    The question at the top of this thread concerned warrant, a question of what follows from what, or perhaps a question of what evidence supports what, and what we are to make of reports of information passed between three agents during a card game. Each of these agents has only incomplete knowledge of the state of play at the beginning of the game and learns, through time from incomplete observations of the state of play and communicating this to each other. This would, or should, be the salient features of the example to focus on to unpick questions about what sentences F1 and F2 are warranted to assert. But the word ‘conditional’ was included in the lead, which, like the report from a starter’s pistol, set off a recitation of everyone’s PhD oral exam notes on conditionals. (And an advert for a forthcoming paper of mine 🙂

    But, in figuring out how best to interpret the sentences in this example, it is necessary to agree about what each of the agents SS, F1 and F2 knew when and from whom he learned it from, and at what time he learned it. Once we agree on that, then we can turn to how to model the information flow and, finally, whether the assertions made by F1 and F2 were good ones to that effect.

    Yes, I am suggesting that we upend the discussion. Or, rather, to start the inquiry at a different place. These are the relevant questions to settle, clearly, before getting into a discussion of two conditional expressions in the story. The problem with the fuss over conditionals is that it reverses the priority of what matters when thinking about epistemology: philosophers are too enamored of linguistic analysis.

    It is a noble and important aim to have a formal object to point to and say that you mean that when you say such-in-such. But the sentences of these models are, almost without exception, worthless for inference. And the fuss over conditionals, in so far as it encroaches on the peace of mind of epistemologists, is a prime example: to get anything remotely interesting, from a formal semantic point of view, of indicative conditionals is work with a representation language that is worthless as an aid to tell you something about warrant, to tell you something about what follows from what.
