]]> By: Gregory Wheeler http://certaindoubts.com/indicatives-and-warranted-assertibility/#comment-3287 Tue, 14 Feb 2006 16:18:56 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=514#comment-3287 All that I’ve said here is not to say that linguists shouldn’t be working on the formal semantics of indicative conditionals in natural languages like English. Nor do I deny that logical and probabilistic tools are indispensable to them. But formal semantics is but an application of logic, and it is one that has almost nothing to do with figuring out issues involving entailment.

The question at the top of this thread concerned warrant, a question of what follows from what, or perhaps a question of what evidence supports what, and what we are to make of reports of information passed between three agents during a card game. Each of these agents has only incomplete knowledge of the state of play at the beginning of the game and learns, through time from incomplete observations of the state of play and communicating this to each other. This would, or should, be the salient features of the example to focus on to unpick questions about what sentences F1 and F2 are warranted to assert. But the word ‘conditional’ was included in the lead, which, like the report from a starter’s pistol, set off a recitation of everyone’s PhD oral exam notes on conditionals. (And an advert for a forthcoming paper of mine 🙂

But, in figuring out how best to interpret the sentences in this example, it is necessary to agree about what each of the agents SS, F1 and F2 knew when and from whom he learned it from, and at what time he learned it. Once we agree on that, then we can turn to how to model the information flow and, finally, whether the assertions made by F1 and F2 were good ones to that effect.

Yes, I am suggesting that we upend the discussion. Or, rather, to start the inquiry at a different place. These are the relevant questions to settle, clearly, before getting into a discussion of two conditional expressions in the story. The problem with the fuss over conditionals is that it reverses the priority of what matters when thinking about epistemology: philosophers are too enamored of linguistic analysis.

It is a noble and important aim to have a formal object to point to and say that you mean that when you say such-in-such. But the sentences of these models are, almost without exception, worthless for inference. And the fuss over conditionals, in so far as it encroaches on the peace of mind of epistemologists, is a prime example: to get anything remotely interesting, from a formal semantic point of view, of indicative conditionals is work with a representation language that is worthless as an aid to tell you something about warrant, to tell you something about what follows from what.

]]> By: Gregory Wheeler http://certaindoubts.com/indicatives-and-warranted-assertibility/#comment-3283 Tue, 14 Feb 2006 15:10:10 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=514#comment-3283 The proof to offer is not that ~A v B entails A -> B; no one is disputing this for the standard boolean readings of ~, v and ->. The argument to offer is a reason for thinking that `if..then..’ in English behaves anything at all like the logical connective ->.

I am not disputing that we can, and very often do, use English conditionals to express that A only if B. Likewise, we may often represent things said in English by sentences from some formal language or other, including classical languages built from the boolean connectives v and ~. But don’t confuse applying logic to model a particular use of a natural language, which is an exercise in applied mathematics, with a theory that tell us about the logical structure of sentences in English simpliciter. ‘If…then…’ is not a truth functional connective in English; neither is ‘and’, ‘or’ or ‘not’, for that matter.

]]> By: Jamie http://certaindoubts.com/indicatives-and-warranted-assertibility/#comment-3282 Tue, 14 Feb 2006 13:48:25 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=514#comment-3282 Why think that It is not raining in Lisbon or the ground will be wet in Lisbon entails that If it is raining in Lisbon, then the ground will be wet in Lisbon, as the latter is uttered when signaling a connection between rain and wet streets?

I think MIchael Huemer gave the argument in comment [13]. Here it is applied to your example.

Suppose it is not raining in Lisbon or the ground will be wet in Lisbon.

{Now (for conditional proof) suppose further that it is raining in Lisbon. Well, either it’s not raining in Lisbon or the ground will be wet, and it is in fact raining. So, the ground will be wet in Lisbon.}

On the further supposition that it is raining in Lisbon, we concluded that the ground will be wet. That is: if it is raining in Lisbon, then the ground will be wet.

The entailment in the other direction is uncontroversial, I take it.
