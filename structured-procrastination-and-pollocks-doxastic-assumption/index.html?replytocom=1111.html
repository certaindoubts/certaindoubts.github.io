Structured Procrastination and Pollock’s Doxastic Assumption

Posted on December 11, 2004 by Kvanvig JonDecember 11, 2004

It’s that time of year again for structured procrastination, so I’ll engage in some. (If you haven’t seen this piece by John Perry, it’s fantastic and funny. The idea, applied to a research career, is that success is best achieved by using writing as a way of procrastinating with respect to more pressing duties, such as grading papers. I highly recommend the practice…)

Pollock claims that nearly everyone in epistemology prior to the last half of the twentieth century adopted the doxastic assumption that only beliefs can justify beliefs. I’ve always thought this misrepresented foundationalists, since a common way to characterize that view is in terms of a foundation in experience itself. Since the justification of everything is supposed to be generated from the foundations, such a position denies the doxastic assumption. Perhaps there are other versions of foundationalism that adopt the assumption, but it always looked to me like a very prominent version doesn’t.

I would think as well that one could find textual evidence that Hume or Berkeley and perhaps Locke held this prominent view, either about justification or knowledge, though I haven’t done a search for such passages. But maybe I’m missing something subtle here, a thought that always arises when such a good philosopher as Pollock makes a claim that seems so obviously false.

    I think experience may well include introspection for Locke. He often uses “reflection” synonymously with “introspection”. So in light of that consider the following passage from Book II, Chapter I:

    “2.All ideas come from sensation or reflection. Let us then suppose the mind to be, as we say, white paper, void of all characters, without any ideas:- How comes it to be furnished? Whence comes it by that vast store which the busy and boundless fancy of man has painted on it with an almost endless variety? Whence has it all the materials of reason and knowledge?   4. Michelle Jenkins on December 11, 2004 at 7:27 pm said:

    Of course, if you want to go waaay back in the anals of philosophy, the Epicureans held that one’s perceptions (which come prior to belief) were the foundation upon which all knowledge rests.* We have Epicurus saying, “We must have grasped what underlies our utterances, so that we may be able to judge the objects of belief or inquiry or puzzlement, by referring them to these…” (Epicurus, Letter to Herodotus, 37-38). Also Sextus Empiricus, when talking of Epicurean epistemology says, “So for these reasons all perceptions are true. But beliefs are not all true; they allow of a difference. Some of them are true and some false, since they are judgments of ours on our apperances, and we judge some things correctly and others badly…true are those that are confirmed and not disconfirmed by what is evident; false are those that are disconfirmed and not confirmed by what is evident.” (S.E., Adversus Methematicos 7)

    This is a foundationalist theory but there is certainly no doxastic assumption there!

    * Perception was one of four criteria of truth, the other three being preconceptions, feelings, and applications of the intellect to presentation (combining preconceptions with perceptions, roughly). All of these (except maybe feelings?), the Epicureans held, came prior to belief.

  5. jon kvanvig on December 12, 2004 at 8:28 am said:

    Michelle, welcome and thanks for the very useful references. The first Sextus sentences is a bit puzzling, however, isn’t it? How is it that all perceptions are true? This would require that perception have content, but also that the content be about the very appearing in question. If it perceptually appears to me that there’s a tree in my backyard, it is true that I’m perceiving in this way, but when I believe the same thing, it’s also true that I’m believing in this way. So to get a difference, we have to cite a difference in content. And the right sort of content will require that I misreported the content of my perception. My appearance is not simply that there is a tree in my backyard, but that I’m perceiving there to be a tree in my backyard.
