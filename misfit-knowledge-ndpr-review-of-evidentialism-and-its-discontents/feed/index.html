I think you’ll want to reconsider the above claim. ]]> By: Clayton http://certaindoubts.com/misfit-knowledge-ndpr-review-of-evidentialism-and-its-discontents/#comment-40558 Wed, 19 Jun 2013 10:13:39 +0000 http://certaindoubts.com/?p=4054#comment-40558 Hi Trent,

There’s a reductio in the neighborhood of yours that’s worth considering:

1. Suppose for reductio that knowledge does not entail evidential justification.
2. If 1, then possibly, S knows p though her evidence does not provide adequate support for believing p.
3. If all S’s does not provide adequate support for believing p after perfectly virtuous inquiry, then S should not believe p.
C. So if 1 — modulo the seemingly undeniable 3 — possibly, S knows p though S should not believe p.

By weakening in this way, it seems you can also avoid the worries discussed above. As an added bonus, you can avoid the worry that a sufficient body of evidence doesn’t have to mandate belief in order to be sufficient to justify belief. This is all consistent with your reductio succeeding, but the move from should believe not-p to should not believe p makes it all a bit less contentious. I do think it’s odd that there’s something S knows that S should not believe on purely epistemic grounds, so this still retains much of the force of the original.

(Of course, I think that (2) is trivial since E=K and (3) looks pretty good because you shouldn’t believe what you don’t know.)
