Disagreement, Rationality, Epistemic Dilemmas, and Buridan’s Ass

Posted on August 29, 2004 by Kvanvig JonAugust 29, 2004

Rich Feldman is one of my very favorite epistemologists. Some of my respect is self-interested: I agree with a vast majority of his expressed epistemological views. But there’s more to it than just self-congratulation involved when I read his stuff. I would say, in fact I will say–here I go, I’m saying it–I’ve never read something he wrote and not learned something philosophically important. I’ve just read another piece of his, on his website. As always, I learned a lot, most of it by becoming convinced by his arguments of points of view he defends.

This time, however, another kind of learning occurred as well, I believe. We might say that what I learned counts as learning in, as our continental brethren might say, the privative mode. I think he endorses the following principle:

If S and S’ are epistemic peers and have shared all their evidence concerning p (and both reasonably believe this conjunction), then it is not possible for S to rationally believe p while S’ rationally believes ~p.

Consider what he says:

After examining this evidence, I find in myself an inclination, perhaps a strong inclination, to think that this evidence supports P. It may even be that I can’t help but believe P. But I see that another person, every bit as sensible and serious as I, has an opposing reaction. Perhaps this person has some bit of evidence that cannot be shared or perhaps he takes the evidence differently than I do. It’s difficult to know everything about his mental life and thus difficult to tell exactly why he believes as he does. One of us must be making some kind of mistake or failing to see some truth. But I have no basis for thinking that the one making the mistake is him rather than me. And the same is true of him. And in that case, the right thing for both of us to do is to suspend judgment on P.

This passage commits Rich to the above principle. But I also think it commits him to something stronger:

If S and S’ are epistemic peers and have shared all their evidence concerning p (and both reasonably believe this conjunction), then an attitude toward p by S is (ir)rational iff that same attitude toward p by S’ is (ir)rational.

The reason the above passage commits Rich to this stronger principle is that the case for the premises of the argument do not depend in any way on what attitude is taken toward p; all that matters is that S and S’ are epistemic peers and that they have shared all their evidence with respect to p.

If all the above is correct, however, we get a paradox. Suppose S’ withholds with respect to p, and S believes p. They share their evidence and reasonably view each other as epistemic peers. As Rich applies the principle in the quoted passage, the proper response is to abandon one’s attitude toward p. So S should abandon believing p. If S follows Rich’s advice, then S should withhold about p. But what should S’ do? With respect to propositions one has considered, there are three possible attitudes: believe, disbelieve, and withhold. So if S’ drops the attitude of withholding, then S’ will either have to come to believe or disbelieve p.

The only way out of the paradox is to deny that one must take an attitude toward a proposition if one considers that proposition. But I don’t see how that is possible. It is possible to take no attitude at all toward a proposition if one has never considered that proposition. But once considered, Pandora’s box is open and there’s no putting things back. Moreover, if refusing to take an attitude were possible, the argument would show that when there was disagreement about whether to refuse in this way, one would have to abandon this approach to the question of the truth of p as well.

So, if Rich’s argument is accepted, it appears that the principle implies that there is the epistemic analogue of a fundamental moral dilemma: no matter what response you make in the presence of such disagreement, you’ll be doing something epistemically improper. Moreover, at every point, whatever your attitude is toward p, the epistemically proper thing to do is to change your attitude to a different one: if you either believe or disbelieve, you should withhold; and if you withhold, you should believe or disbelieve. So not only do we have fundamental epistemic dilemmas on Rich’s view, we have a fairly precise analogue of the Buridan’s ass paradox, but with a twist. The twist is that no matter which bale of hay the ass eats, the rational thing to do is to be eating the other one.

    On Rich’s behalf, though he may be committed to the principle you’ve bolded, it needn’t generate a paradox. Note that Rich says:
    “another person, every bit as sensible and serious as I, has an opposing reaction.”

    It seems to me that the bolded principle, combined with the things Rich says, yield that the correct thing for S and S’ to do is split the difference in their attitudes. If S believes, and S’ withholds judgment, then both should end up at a belief that’s half as strong as S’s original belief. Now, in Rich’s example, S and S’ have opposing reactions–so when you split the difference you wind up with withholding judgment. But in general, splitting the difference won’t yield that.

    And this does seem like the right thing to do if you accept Rich’s line of thinking here–if I withhold with respect to p, and I discover that one of my peers thinks that our evidence does prove that p, then I should worry that p may be true. (In the extreme, if all my peers think it’s proof that p, I am certainly being dogmatically timid to continue withholding judgment.)

    Of course, this raises the problem of whether these differences can always be split….

    Matt, good points here. I take it your revision of Rich would require, first, an appeal to degrees of belief, and second, that given the antecedent for the principles I formulate, a degree of belief is rational for S iff it is rational for S’.

    To disagree in a transparent way, however, is going to cause problems for the revised account as well. If you and I disagree in a situation where we both know the degree of belief of the other, then we will also be convinced that the other person’s degree of belief is mistaken (and that any degree of belief other than the one we hold is also mistaken). If we then acquiesce and split the difference, then we’ll have an incoherent belief set. So suppose we also require that these additional attitudes be dropped (or lowered significantly). And attitudes such as, “splitting the difference between our two attitudes would do nothing to calibrate either of our attitudes more closely to the truth.” But I don’t see why dropping any such attitude would be rational; in fact, it will be one of the few things we do agree on!

    Here’s another point. Suppose degree of belief n is required for justified belief. If I don’t like having unjustified beliefs, then if I’m short of n with respect to some proposition, I can go look for a peer that I regard as overconfident. Once we share our information, and if I still think of them as a peer, I’ve got it made. Their overconfidence makes it rational for me to adopt a higher degree of belief. Suppose I manage to do so, in spite of still viewing my peer as overconfident. I’ve now got a justified belief; what a relief!

    Well, the rule I was envisioning was something like this: The evidence S and S’ share must include the fact that they’re epistemic peers and the fact that they each have certain degrees of belief. (This’ll have to be put in the third person rather than the first for the evidence to be sharable–each can believe “S is rational and believes p,” “S’ is rational and withholds judgment.”) My idea was that this evidence requires S and S’ to adopt a new degree of belief midway between their old ones. So that requires adopting the attitude that any degree of belief other than the new midway belief is mistaken–in light of the new evidence each person acquires concerning the other’s degree of belief.* Since learning about the other person’s attitude provides new evidence, it’s not surprising that it requires dropping old attitudes concerning the appropriate degree of belief on the evidence.

    Suppose S and S’ both do have the attitude “splitting the difference between our two attitudes would do nothing to calibrate either of our attitudes more closely to the truth.” I think Rich would say that that attitude just is irrational and should be dropped–his argument is meant to show that it’s irrational! And, if S adopts the midway attitude and S’ does not, S isn’t obliged to meet S’ halfway again; since S’ isn’t responding appropriately to the evidence about the attitude S has**, S’ isn’t an epistemic peer any more.

I don’t think the self-trust amounts to a claim to the effect that I’m more trustworthy than you, just that I am in some sense trustworthy. This is not very clear.   6. jon kvanvig on September 3, 2004 at 4:11 pm said:

    Rich, good to see you here! And the example has just the right flavor for me–the evidentialists are perfectly rational…

    The self-trust issue is a hard one for me, too, but I think the key has to be that it is not a matter of some general trust, though that plays a role too. I think of it as very specific trust in (some) cases of disagreement, since if I lose trust in myself in the specific case in which I disagree, say, with you, then I shouldn’t hold my opinion prior to splitting any difference with those whom I disagree. The first thing I must do is find an attitude that I do trust myself about. And once I achieve that, I don’t attribute the same specific trustworthiness to you: after all, I take you to be mistaken on the issue. I do grant that you may be entitled to the same trust for yourself, but that’s just part of the egocentric aspect of rationality.

    So I think the self-trust issue is, in a way, unaffected by whether other people agree or disagree. If I take an attitude toward p and lack self-trust on the specific issue, then there is something incoherent in my total epistemic condition. When others disagree, it brings to consciousness the issue of whether I should have such trust, in a way that doesn’t arise for me when others agree with me. My inclination, though, is to treat both cases the same: if you agree with me, that doesn’t legitimate my self-trust on the issue nor is it evidence for the claim in question; and if you disagree, that doesn’t by itself make my self-trust illegitimate. It doesn’t introduce an explanatory gap in my overall understanding of things, since if I’m right, you’ve made a blunder of some sort, and that raise the question of why an ordinarily reliable cognizer has done so. So to complete my understanding of the issue, I’ll need to come to see why you’ve blundered. But lack of full understanding doesn’t undermine justification, or so it seems to me.
