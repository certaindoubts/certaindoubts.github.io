More on the Value of Knowledge and Being in a Position to Know

Posted on December 21, 2004 by Kvanvig JonDecember 21, 2004

As a followup to the earlier post on the idea that being in a position to know other truths explains how knowledge is more valuable than true belief, suppose the set of claims that you are in a position to know is not insular, so that by coming to know some of them that you don’t already know, the class of things you are in a position to know might expand. Let C be the initial class of things you are in a position to know in virtue of knowing p, and C+ the class of things you’d be in a position to know if you come to know everything in C. We also assume that believing p itself does not put one in a position to know all of C. Furthermore, if we imagine you coming to know everything in C, and compare it with believing everything in C, we still get a difference, because we assume that believing everything in C does not put one in the position to know everything in C+ (as knowing everything in C does).

Defending the position to know idea will require stopping the argument contained in Patrick Hawley’s comment on the first post. Here’s how it works. Begin with knowing p, and then assume knowledge is expanded to include all of C. Then assume that this knowledge is expanded to include all of C+. The end state of this process is knowing everything that is knowable for you, and once you reach that point, there is nothing you are in a position to know that you don’t already know. And if we compare knowing all this with merely truly believing all of it, we won’t be able to explain the difference between the value of truly believing and knowing in such a case by appeal to the idea of what one is in a position to know.

Once you start down this slope, it’s pretty slippery. But why can’t a defender of the position simply grant the argument but question its relevance?

Imagine, that is, the following reply: once the class of things known gets as big as possible, there isn’t any additional value in knowing over believing the truth. But for lesser states, there still is a difference, and the difference involves what one is in a position to know. So suppose that the lesser state is short of knowing all that can be known, but only involves knowing everything that knowing p puts one in a position to know. The defender of the position to know idea could even grant that, in itself, knowing all this is no better than believing it when the class of things is assumed to be insular. Even so, the defender of this idea could maintain that this point is irrelevant. Your actual condition is one of being in a position to know these things, whereas one who only believes these truths is not. Changing the example to involve actually knowing the entire set vs. believing it doesn’t affect this point.

But consider the following. Does believing the truth p put one in a position to believe the truths contained in C, just as knowing p puts on in a position to know what’s in C? I think the answer must be “yes” and obviously so (in fact, believing the truth p is likely to put you in a position to believing a much larger body of truths than C, something of which C is a proper subclass). If that’s right, then the position to know idea, even at the very first step, is presupposing that knowledge is more valuable than true belief rather than explaining it.

    Jon, in response to the last point, I cannot see how truly believing p could put you in a position to truly believe more other things than would knowing p, because knowing p implies truly believing p. (Actually, I can think of one potential exception: The proposition that I merely truly believe p. But even this I have reservations about, for reasons elaborated in the last paragraph below.)     It’s worth reflecting a bit on the notion of being in a position to truly believe. This is not, as far as I can tell, familiar to us from ordinary language, whereas being in a position to know is. How does believing something put one in a position to truly believe other things? I would think that, for any given true proposition, one is in a position to truly believe it just in case one understands it–all one has to do is come to believe it in order to truly believe it. So it seems that truly believing p could put one in a position to truly believe other things in either of two ways: (i) by making certain other propositions true that weren’t true before, e.g., the proposition that one truly believes p, or the proposition that someone truly believes p, etc., (ii) by enabling one to understand various true propositions that one did not previously understand.

    If we settle on metaphysical possibility as the relevant modality, it’s hard to see why we would limit the class of knowables to propositions that are actually presently true. It’s metaphysically possible that I know exactly 337 logical truths; it’s also metaphysically possible that I know exactly 338 logical truths. There’s nothing in my nature, or in the nature of either of those propositions, that renders my knowing them impossible.
