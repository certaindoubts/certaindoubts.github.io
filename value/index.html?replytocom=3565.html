Platonic Epistemology, Evolutionary Epistemology, and the Value of Truth

Posted on April 21, 2006 by Kvanvig JonApril 21, 2006

Suppose one is attracted to the idea that the primary concern of epistemologists ought to be those aspects of successful cognition that are valuable from a purely theoretical perspective. That is, suppose one is interested in value-driven epistemology. Suppose one is also impressed with evolutionary theory. One might then begin to think about epistemology from both an evolutionary and value-driven perspective. The question is what would result from such a perspective.

The first thing to notice is that the two aspects of the perspective might conflict. The evolutionary aspect would lead one to focus on features of cognition that either enhance survival value or piggy-back on other features that enhance survival value. Successes from this point of view do not count as successes from a purely theoretical perspective. They are successes from a practical point of view instead.

The connection could be obtained indirectly, however. Perhaps survival value is enhanced by cognitive states that are valuable from a purely theoretical perspective, and perhaps this enhancement occurs as a matter of nomological or scientific necessity. In this way, evolutionary epistemology would be a competing perspective to the more standard Platonic conception in which the fundamental value is happiness or well-being, and cognition that is valuable from a purely theoretical perspective obtains its value in virtue of its connection to the fundamental value of human well-being.

In neither case would the value of getting to the truth and avoiding error (or the value of knowledge or the value of understanding) be intrinsic, but it wouldn’t be instrumental either. It would be extrinsic without being instrumental, in the terminology of Korsgaard (though without intending to adopt everything she says about the distinction).

This conclusion leaves us with the question of what philosophers, myself included, mean when they say that truth is intrinsically valuable. Do we mean to deny both the Platonic perspective and the evolutionary one? I think the answer is “no,” and that the reason the answer is “no” shows that we maybe shouldn’t be saying that truth is intrinsically valuable. I think what I mean to be calling attention to when I say that getting to the truth is intrinsically valuable is the following. I want the truth even if it provides no practical benefit to me that is caused to obtain because I have found the truth. And, most definitely, I want the truth even if it generates for me no enhancement of survival value or reproductive fitness. But the satisfaction of these desires is itself part of human flourishing, and so contributes to the Platonic idea that things are valuable because of their connection to well-being. The satisfaction of desires is partially constitutive of well-being, not causally contributory to it, and so to say that getting to the truth is intrinsically valuable is not to say that it is valuable because it contributes to well-being. It is, instead, to say that it is the object of a desire or motive or interest or something on the affective side of our being and the satisfaction or realization conditions for these aspects is partially constitutive of human fulfillment. But that characterization is a particular instance of the distinction between instrumental and extrinsic value, so the proper characterization should be that getting to the truth is extrinsically but not instrumentally related to human flourishing. In saying that getting to the truth is intrinsically valuable, what we intended to communicate was a denial of the mere instrumental value of truth. What we ignored was the distinction between instrumental and extrinsic value.

I think the reason I missed this point is that when I have distinguished between extrinsic and instrumental relationships, I did so distinguishing effective means to the truth from intentional means to the truth. The latter involves following steps aimed at getting to the truth, but not necessarily effective in doing so. Truth is valuable, however, not because it makes us happy or fulfilled to find it, nor because in seeking it, we are aiming at our happiness or well-being. But there is no reason to think that extrinsic relations are exhausted by purposive relationships of this sort, and so these facts give us no reason to think that the value of truth is not extrinsic.

But maybe I’m caving too quickly on the idea that truth is intrinsically valuable?

    Hi Jon,

    I’m guessing that the “not” should be removed from the last sentence of your post. If so, I think the answer is yes, you’re caving too quickly.

    In the second-to-last big paragraph, you say that what it means for getting to the truth to be intrinsically valuable is just for it to be the object of a motive, interest, or something on the affective side of our being and for the satisfaction of that affective element to be partially constitutive of human flourishing. If I understand the distinction you’re borrowing from Korsgaard, this would make the value of getting to the truth extrinsic but not instrumental because getting to the truth would have value only by relation to something else, where that relation is not a means-end one.

    However, there is another way of understanding the intrinsic value of getting to the truth. It could be something that is of value even if we don’t want it and it isn’t relevant to any motive we actually have. In other words, it could be something that we *ought* to desire, value, and pursue, even if we don’t actually desire, value, or pursue it.

    Of course, there are serious questions that may be asked about this sort of view–e.g., where does this sort of “ought” come from? Still, it is at least consistent with the view that getting to the truth has intrinsic value. One possible answer to that question might be that there are two distinct, though closely related, things that are each partially consititutive of human flourishing. One is the satisfaction of the affective element (e.g., desire, motive, or interest) aimed at getting the truth; the other simply is getting the truth. That the latter is independently important seems to follow from the intuition that someone who actually gets the truth on a regular basis, despite the fact that she does not regard it as possessing any value (perhaps for bizarre religious reasons), is still flourishing to a greater extent than someone who neither gets the truth nor values it.

    As I said, you may have reasons for rejecting a view of this sort. But pausing to reject it would at least entail “caving” a bit less quickly!

If so, then the account of the value of human flourishing cannot appeal, in part, to the value of truth; in which case there’s no threat to the intrinsic value of truth.

  8. kvanvig on April 25, 2006 at 8:22 am said:

    Mike, you wrote,

    Suppose the truth about the truth is that it does not contribute to human flourishing. Plato and company were wrong. I’d bet that you’d find getting to *that* truth very valuable. I know I would (but obviously not because it contributes to my well-being). But then getting to the truth does not derive it’s value from its constitutive relationship to human flourishing.

    Neat little dilemma! I’m not sure the conclusion follows, however. I think what you need to get to the conclusion is the additional premise that it is possible that Plato and company are wrong. I think we reason both counterfactually and countermodally, and the above is an example of the latter. E.g., “suppose God were not omniscient; then he wouldn’t be omnipotent either, since we can clarify what is known by God in terms of a display of certain cognitive powers;” and this reasoning doesn’t require that it is possible for God to fail to be omniscient. The premises of your argument work like this example: they don’t require that what is supposed is possible. But the conclusion does require that they be possible, unless constitution is some kind of hypermodal relation that I don’t see and would somehow be threatened by this reasoning.

    Though you didn’t quite say this, maybe your argument is like Descartes’s argument for dualism? You might be asking whether it is conceivable or imaginable that Plato is wrong, and then try to draw a metaphysical conclusion from premises about conceivability. The main difference from Descartes in what you say is that I think you are relying more on our sense of fallibility: given our fallibility, Plato might have been wrong, so suppose he was. But the ‘might’ here is an epistemic one, not a metaphysical one, and the metaphysical one is needed, I think.

  10. Chase Wrenn on April 25, 2006 at 4:52 pm said:

    For basically the reasons Jon gives (and some others), I am skeptical about the claim that truth is intrinsically valuable. I also think Jon’s right that we cannot conclude “truth is intrinsically valuable” from “truth is valuable even when its useless.” Truth could have contributory value or what I call “telic value,” and neither of those is intrinsic value.

    So far as I can tell, the suggestion on the table right now is that truth has contributory value. Having true beliefs is a constitutive part of human flourishing. I don’t think this is an entirely satisfactory account of truth’s value, though. Human flourishing is an organic unity. Although it surely comes in degrees, it is not as if a person flourishes just a little bit more with each new true belief. Rather, human flourishing has many constituents, and they work together synergistically so that their combination is more valuable than the sum of their individual values.

    I think this matters because it draws attention to the fact that something has contributory value only insofar as it is actually making a contribution to an organic unity. The beauty of my wife’s face is an organic unity; its aesthetic value exceeds the sum of the aesthetic values of its parts. Her nose is a constitutive part of that organic unity, and it has contributory value. But, if you were to remove the nose (DON’T DO IT!), the organic unity would be destroyed *and so would the nose’s contributory value*.

    It thus appears that whatever contributory value true belief has as a constituent part of human flourishing, only the true beliefs of those who are flourishing actually has that value. If you think the true beliefs of the wretched masses are valuable beyond their instrumental value, you need to think they have value beyond their contributory value as well.

    What sort of value does truth have, then, if it is not intrinsic, not only instrumental and not only contributory? My answer is that truth has *telic instrumental value*. A thing has telic value if valuing it is valuable. It has telic intrinsic value if valuing it is intrinsically valuable, telic instrumental value if valuing it is instrumentally valuable, and telic contributory value if valuing it is contributorily valuable. You might think that it is not only useful to value truth, but that doing so has intrinsic or contributory value as well. That wouldn’t worry me right now. What matters to me is the possibility that we might be able to account for truth’s value by shifting our focus to the value of valuing truth.

  11. Michael Lynch on April 26, 2006 at 9:00 am said:

    Great post Jon.

    I think Jon is right that it doesn’t do much good to say that true beliefs have intrinsic value. Maybe they do, but it is difficult to give an argument for that thesis. Giving an argument that x has intrinsic value is much like trying to give an argument for the claim that x looks red. Things either look red or they don’t. There are objective facts about whether they do, but I am not going to be able to convince you that something looks red if you don’t see it.

    That said, Chase and Jon suggest, and I agree, that there is another possibility: something can be more than instrumetnally valuable by being constitutively valuable- that is, by being an essential part of a whole that is itself of more than instrumental value. This is the thesis I’ve defended about true beliefs in a number of places recently, including in True to Life (see the “summary” and “replies to critics” in the Phil Books’ symposium for a short statement). Having true beliefs has extrinsic but non-instrumental value because that is a constitutive part of human flourishing.

    But Chase argues that contributory value fails to do the needed work. His reason seems to be that x can have contributory (constitutive) value at time t only if x is, at t, part of a greater whole that has value at t. Since few if no humans ever really have completely flourishing lives, having true beliefs can’t be of contributory value for most humans.

    Consider a parallel argument: fair democratic elections are a constitutive part of a just society. (They have extrinsic but more than instrumental value). But few if any societies are just, therefore fair democratic elections can’t have contributory value.

    Neither argument works because what we are talking about justifying is not individual true beliefs or individual elections, but the goal or ideal of having (all and only) true beliefs and the goal of having fair elections. Likewise, when we talk about flourishing we are talking about a goal. Unachieved goals can have worth. Indeed, they better be able to have worth, or there isn’t much point in pursuing them. Likewise with the goal of having true beliefs — the end of inquiry. Why is that goal valuable? Because having true beliefs is a constititutive part of another goal — having a flourishing life.

    (Note: if you dislike talk of unachieved goals having worth, then just go counterfactual: having true beliefs is good because if one’s life was completely flourishing, then having true beliefs would be an essential part of that flourishing).

    Having said that, I agree that it is a difficult question as to how to argue that difficult to achieve goals like truth have even contributory value. My own view is that we must start with the value of our attitudes toward having true beliefs, and then bootstap the value of the goal from the value of those attitudes. But I suspect that Chase does not find that convincing either!

    A final note: As Marian David (and I) like to insist it is important to keep two questions seperate in such discussions. The first is: what is the value of an individual belief’s being true? The other is: what is the value of having (all and only) true beliefs? I think that one you secure one of these, you can get to the other, but that is another story.

    Best
    Michael

    Thanks for your kind words about my work, Michael!

    Here’s why I think we shouldn’t count something as having contributory value unless the valuable whole to which it contributes actually exists. Some states of affairs can be essential to EITHER valuable or disvaluable states of affairs. It seems to me that they have positive contributory value when they are part of valuable states of affairs, and they have negative contributory value when they are part of disvaluable states of affairs.

    For example, Hitler’s health would have been a constitutive part of his flourishing, had Hitler led a flourishing and virtuous life. But that’s not enough to give Hitler’s health contributory value. As a matter of fact, Hitler’s health was a constitutive part of a vicious life, and so it had negative contributory value.

    (I assume Hitler was healthy. If I’m wrong, pick someone else who’s vicious but healthy, like George W. Bush.)

    More to the point, true beliefs can be a constitutive part of a life that is virtuous or vicious, flourishing or wretched. So can caring about truth. (Consider the Nazi doctors whose search for the truth was a necessary part of their viciousness). Although true beliefs and concern for truth have positive contributory value when they contribute to something good, I don’t think that’s enough to explain the non-instrumental value of truth. The wretched and vicious ought to care about truth too.

    Now, what about the fair elections argument? Don’t fair elections have contributory value even though most societies aren’t just?

    Nope. The fair of elections of just societies have positive contributory value, to the extent that they are part of the justness of those societies. But the fair elections of thoroughly unjust societies do not have positive contributory value.

    Now, we might think that justness is a matter of degree, and so any society with fair elections is ipso facto a little bit just, and so fair elections have contributory value wherever they occur. That’s fine by me; it doesn’t require rejecting the idea that something can have contributory value only when it is making an actual contribution.

    We might think of human flourishing in the same way, and say that people flourish a little bit simply in virtue of caring about truth, no matter what their other circumstances are. For some reason I can’t really articulate at the moment, that seems implausible to me. It seems more likely that some cases of caring about truth have positive contributory value, and other cases have negative contributory value, but it just doesn’t make much sense to talk about the the contributory value in general of truth or of caring about truth.
