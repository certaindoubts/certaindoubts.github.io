It seems to me that they’re saying that “might” sentences have an indeterminate content. When Jon makes his remark, it might or might not (those are metaphysical ‘might’s!) wind up being true, depending on what its context turns out to be. When Brit speaks up, that settles that the context is one in which Brit is to be taken as part of the group, and so Jon’s remaker is settled to be false. But they certainly understand the view better than I do, so I’d be overjoyed to hear from one of them!

One thing I’m unsure about is exactly how vF&G’s view of retreats works. They say that often the speaker can reply to “You’re wrong” by saying “I didn’t say he had to be in Missouri” or something like that; and I think their account of this is that the original utterance is indeterminate among a range of contents, and when the speaker retreats she’s insisting on one of the weaker contents in this range. What happens if Brit says to me “Jon’s wrong,” I later say to Jon “Brit said you were wrong,” and Jon says, “But I didn’t say he had to be in Missouri! I said he might have been. Sheesh”? (cf. 10c in the “CIA Leaks” paper.) Does Jon’s retreat make Brit’s statement false?

]]> By: Brit Brogaard http://certaindoubts.com/an-argument-against-relativism/#comment-8614 Mon, 29 May 2006 10:08:20 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=588#comment-8614 Hi Jon. I agree that there is a danger in taking the “group route”. But this is a danger the contextualists are facing as well. In fact I am tempted to say that the danger is more acute for the contextualists, as they allow for more inclusive groups.

As for the issue of testimony, I agree that both sides need to say more about which theory of testimony they presuppose. But some of the evidence can be evaluated independently of such a theory. Take, for instance, the “woman-in-the-bushes-case”. Jon: Joe might be in Missouri. Brit (hiding in the bushes): Jon is wrong. Joe is in Frankfurt.

Thony and Kai would want to say that Brit and Jon are part of the same group because they are both interested in the same “investigation”. Since Brit knows that Joe is not in Missouri, and Brit and Jon are part of the same group, Jon is wrong to think that Joe might be in Missouri. The relativists, on the other hand, would want to say that Brit and Jon can both be right. No need to appeal to any special theory of testimony here.

I agree that going the group route will help with many of the problems for relativists, but it is a move we ought to be suspicious of. There is a danger that such views can always find some group or other to appeal to in generating the right results, but it is often hard to see why this isn’t just theoretical gerrymandering. What would be nice is to have a straightforward account of what makes a given group contextually salient.

My point here wasn’t to question either the objection or the plausibility of relativism, but rather to express a worry that an account of how testimony functions is going to be presupposed by the discussion. If that is right, one will need to pick cases and examples in which the role of testimony can be controlled for so that it doesn’t cloud the primary issue of the plausibility of relativism. It is probably too much to expect defenders and critics of relativism to be responsible for attaching a theory of testimony to their theories, but a way needs to be found to hold fixed any issues about testimony in the arguments being used.

]]> By: Brit Brogaard http://certaindoubts.com/an-argument-against-relativism/#comment-3661 Sat, 27 May 2006 08:44:39 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=588#comment-3661 Jon, I think you are right that any account of epistemic modals will need to explain exactly when the testimony of others should be taken into account. The special problem for the relativists is that they claim to do better than the contextualists with respect to cases like the following. A: Jon might be in Missouri. B (a CIA agent in New York talking with Jon): That can’t be right. Relativists explain such cases by saying that the context of assessment is different in the two cases. Here B is right to trash A’s assertion. The problem then is to explain why the Boss shouldn’t also trash Jack and Zach’s assertions. On Thony and Kai’s account, the relevant knowledge is knowledge possessed by the members of a contextually salient group that includes the speaker (where the group may be surprisingly big). Andy Egan likewise thinks that the relevant knowledge is knowledge possessed by the members of a contextually salient group that includes the evaluator. If we follow Andy in making this move, then I think many of the problems raised by Thony and Kai disappear.
