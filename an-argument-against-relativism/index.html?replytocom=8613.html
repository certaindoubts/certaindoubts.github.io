von Fintel and Gillies Against Relativism for Epistemic Modals

Posted on May 24, 2006 by Kvanvig JonMay 24, 2006

One of the joys after the semester is getting to read. So, since Brian recommended “CIA Leaks”, I got to read the paper this morning. It’s full of neat examples and powerful arguments, but there is one argument that I don’t quite get, so I thought I’d see if someone can help me see the argument here.

Here’s the argument (‘CIA’ is code for the relativistic semantics that generates a truth value for a utterance in a way that depends on a context, an index, and an assessor):

Boss has two informants, Jack and Zack. There is a meeting of spies in a room, and The Boss, Jack, and Zack know that one and only one of their (conveniently named) comrades P, Q, R is a turncoat. Jack looks through his peep hole and sees clearly that it is either P or Q who is the turncoat, and Zack looks through his peep hole and sees clearly that it is either Q or S who is the turncoat. Each slips The Boss a note informing him:
(22) a. [From Jack]: It must be that either P is the turncoat or Q is the
turncoat.
b. [From Zack]: It must be that either Q is the turncoat or R is the
turncoat.
The Boss gets the messages, concluding that Q is the turncoat. But is there room for this in the cia theory? Not as things stand. Let a be the point of assessment The Boss occupies. He has no information at a about who the turncoat is, and hence the modal base will contain P-worlds, Q-worlds, and R-worlds. But then it is immediate that Ccia theories predict that Jack’s and Zack’s reports are both false at a… It is a bad idea for The Boss to conclude something he thinks is false on the basis of reports he thinks are false.

Here’s what I don’t understand about this argument. My initial reaction is to say the following. Where Boss is the assessor, there’s what Boss knows prior to the assertions in 22, and there is what Boss knows as a result of those assertions. He’s not only the assessor from whose standpoint a CIA theory will generate a truth value, but he’s also learning by testimony. He learns from Jack that it is P or Q and from Zack that it is Q or R. So while it is true that he initially had no information about who the turncoat is, there’s a question about which knowledge base is the relevant one for a CIA theorist to use in evaluating (22). So my failure to grasp the argument is that I don’t see why a CIA theorist is forced to ignore the learning the Boss achieves as a result of Jack’s and Zack’s assertions. What am I missing?

Before the boss hears from Jack, he is inclined to reject this proposition, since he doesn’t know if the turncoat is P or Q.

    So on the CIA account but not contextualism, it seems as though Jack is correcting a false belief of the boss’s. Maybe this means that CIA theorists owe us an account of why the Boss shouldn’t reject Jack’s testimony; if they start with conflicting beliefs, why is Jack a better authority than the Boss about that particular proposition?   6. weiner on May 25, 2006 at 11:58 am said:

    Sorry if I go on for a bit here — I want to try to work out the competing issues and set up a moderate position.

    Let’s assume that at the beginning the Boss has thought about what he knows about the turncoat (which seems plausible, since he’s worried about the issue). He decides that he knows that it is P or Q or R, but that he doesn’t know anything more. So, if “It must be P or Q” expresses a proposition that is true at a point of assessment iff “it is P or Q” is entailed by what the assessor knows, it is false as assessed by the Boss. I think that’s the answer to your question, Brit: Since “must” is the dual of “might” here, “It must be P or Q” is inconsistent with the Boss’s antecedent beliefs. If you like, at the beginning the Boss believes “it might be P” and “it might be Q and “it might be R.”

    (By the way, relativists may not accept that what’s relevant at the point of assessment is what the assessor alone knows. Andy Egan has done some work that depends on the idea that what’s relevant is sometimes what all the participants in the conversation know, or what one of them could find out by asking the others. That might nip this problem in the bud; then when the Boss starts talking to Jack he should suspend his belief in the negation of “It must be P or Q.” But there’s a potential problem with eavesdropping, which I’ll get back to possibly in a followup.)

    Now, take this as a rough conversational rule: When you believe that not-A and someone tells you A, you have the option of changing your mind or of sticking to your old belief. Whereas when someone tells you A and you had no previous belief on the subject, ceterus paribus you should accept what they say. This is very rough, and depends on questions of authority, which I’ll also try to get back to.

    If these are the rules, then the relativist accounts seems to predict that the Boss might reject Jack’s testimony, as conflicting with his previous belief. Here I’m disagreeing with what Thony said, that it predicts that he will trash the message; but even the prediction that he might trash the message seems to be wrong. We should expect the Boss to accept Jack’s testimony, as information about a proposition on which the Boss had no previous opinion. And that’s what the contextualist theory predicts: If the proposition Jack expresses is “What Jack knows entails that the turncoat is P or Q,” then the Boss will accept that (since he didn’t have any opinion about this before), and then he gets to “It is P or Q” by a familiar route.

    I think the relativist can address at least some of these problems, but I’ll leave that for a followup.

  10. weiner on May 25, 2006 at 4:03 pm said:

    Ok, first of all I’ll say upfront that I don’t think there’s a relevant difference between being told something and overhearing it here. The same issues would arise if the Boss overheard Jack and Zack as in the case where they tell him. (That’s the problem I mentioned in the parenthesis in my comment #6.)

    Now, for a very crude model, let’s say that when you believe not-A and someone tells you A, then you should trash their testimony if you are a bigger expert on the topic than them, accept their testimony if they’re a bigger expert than you, and suspend judgment if neither is a bigger expert than the other. On the contextualist model it seems clear that Jack is a bigger expert on the topic than the Boss; the proposition Jack expresses is about what Jack knows, and he’s in a better position to know that than the Boss is. (Note that this isn’t always true; if the Boss knew something about some error that Jack was likely to make, he’d be a bigger expert than Jack about what Jack knew; but that’s not the case in this example.)

    For the relativist, the truth-value of “It must be P or Q” as assessed by the Boss depends on what the Boss knows. And we might think that the Boss is a bigger expert than Jack on this, so that relativism predicts that the Boss should trash Jack’s testimony. If Jack had said “You already know that it’s P or Q” then the Boss could say “No I don’t.”

    But it’s not at all clear that the relativist should take the topic of Jack’s testimony as assessed by the Boss to be the Boss’s knowledge, or anyone’s knowledge; the proposition being expressed is a relativistic proposition that in itself doesn’t refer to anyone in particular’s knowledge. It seems not unreasonable to treat the topic of sentences of the form “Might A” and “Must A” as A itself. In this case that would be the identity of the turncoat, and Jack is a bigger expert on that (since he has eyewitness evidence). In which case the Boss should drop his previous belief in “It might not be P or Q” for Jack’s testimony “It must be P or Q.” Which is the desired result.

    In 3 Thony says that, if the relativist gives a story as to why the Boss takes on board testimony that is false from his point of assessment, he has to say why this story doesn’t apply in the cases of modal disagreement that originally motivate the CIA view. But here, if we look at the “Bond might be in Zurich” case, it’s clear that Bond is a bigger expert on his whereabouts than Blofeld; so when he hears Blofeld say “Bond might be in Zurich,” he should reject this rather than accept it. So the relativist does seem to have recourses to answer that problem. I’m not sure that this addresses Thony’s point (ii) in comment 3, though.

The Boss, Jack and Zack obviously belong to the same group (maybe partially for reasons you point out, Matt).
