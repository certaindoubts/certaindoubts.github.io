Minimalism about Intuitions

Posted on June 3, 2005 by lynchJune 3, 2005

Thanks in part to work by Weinberg, Stich and others, there is an important debate going on about intuition, namely a debate over the validity of a certain sort of philosophical methodology. What is as stake is whether appeals to intuition – in counterexamples, or supporting a theory – are trustworthy. If we want to make progress on that debate, it would be helpful to know what counts as an intuition.

I believe that we can get reasonably clear about what counts as an intuition without having to give anything like a High-Church Conceptual Analysis in terms of necessary and sufficient conditions. An alternative method – there are still others, obviously – is to appeal to common marks displayed by the things that we typically call intuitions. These marks or platitudes can serve to jointly constrain our understanding of intuition sufficiently to determine what, in at least a wide range of cases, counts as an intuition.

I propose that at least two important marks of intuitions are these.


Believability: If I intuit that p then I either believe that p or am inclined to believe that p. An “intuition” is a proposition I either believe or am inclined to believe.

Source-opacity: An intuition’s believability does not knowably derive from memory, perception, inference or any of the usual sources of evidence.

Let’s call the conception that results from taking these two principles as definitive marks of what counts as an intuition the minimalist conception of intuition. It has several distinguishing features.

First, we don’t need to appeal to a propositional attitude other than belief. Some philosophers (like Sosa) think we need to appeal to a distinct form of attitude because we don’t actually believe everything we intuit. (I can have the intuition that the naïve comprehension axiom of set theory is true while believing it is not). But believability incorporates this fact: I need not believe everything I intuit, but everything I intuit is at least believable. Thus intuition is explained in terms of belief.

Second, the account is pleasingly liberal. Unlike other accounts, such as Sosa’s or perhaps Bealer’s, it doesn’t restrict intuitions to those propositions that are, for example, necessary or self-presenting. Hence it allows, for example, that the following is an intuition, as it clearly is:

Objects continue to exist when unperceived.

This is knowable, if it is knowable, a priori. It is a paradigmatic intuition; yet it isn’t necessarily true. (For more examples, and for general criticisms of Ernie’s account see my forthcoming “Trusting Intuition” here).

Third, source-opacity leaves the nature of the source of any particular intuition’s believability open. An intuition’s believability may derive from our conceptual competence; or from the Natural Light. Or it may derive from a standard evidential source. Moreover, depending on how we interpret the force of the modality in “knowably” it may allow for the possibility that further empirical discovery may reveal the source of an intuition. That might further allow that what I may now regard as intuition I may later regard as the product of theory, or unconscious perceptual mechanisms, or neural hardwiring.

Fourth, the account is at least consistent with naturalism. It requires the positing of no new mysterious faculties.

And fifth: together, the two marks gel with a pre-theoretical intuition many people, including philosophers, have about intuitions. Namely, my intuitions are those propositions that I find believable without knowing why. They are what we just find them believable as it were. That is exactly what our two marks tell us about intuitions.

The minimalist conception of intuition may seem too minimal. Our two marks of intuition don’t seem to distinguish between the detective’s intuition that the butler did it and Kripke’s intuition about the necessity of origins. This is not a vice but a virtue. We should take seriously the fact that we use the same word to cover both sorts of cases. Moreover, the liberalness of the account doesn’t imply that we can’t distinguish between different types of intuition. Some intuitions may have probative force and others not, for example.

Nonetheless, we may wish to say more about philosophical intuition, since what is at stake in the debate over intuitions is their use in philosophical argument. Two facts are salient about how such intuitions are used by philosophers. First, as Tim Williamson has noted, use them to refute or defend some particular philosophical thesis. Yet second, philosophers who don’t believe them still typically grant that they are believable, or intuitively appealing. They thus try to explain the proposition’s believability away, by arguing, for example, that their believability is the product of bad theory. A rabid phenomenalist, for example, might argue that while strictly speaking, it is false that unperceived objects exist, it is understandable that people find the proposition so intuitive, since (they might say), talk of unperceived objects can be translated into subjunctive conditionals concerning what people would perceive in certain conditions. Accordingly, I think we can add to our two marks above:

Theoretical Relevancy: Philosophical intuitions are of positive or negative epistemic theoretical relevance.

So in sum: Intuitions are those propositions we find believable without knowing why. And philosophical intuitions are those propositions we just find believable which play a particular role in philosophical inquiry.

The account is rough of course. But if something like the minimalist conception is correct, then the skeptical doubts of Weinberg, Stich, Cummins etc. are well worth taking seriously. But if it turns out that epistemically speaking, we can’t help but rely on intuitions so defined, they may also be misplaced.

    A follow to my comment on your comment Carrie. You wonder why I think that it might follow that “skeptical doubts” are misplaced if minimalism about intuitions turns out to be right.

    Well, I confess I am not sure it *follows*. But minimalism does, I think, suggest several ways of showing that intuitions are trustworthy. One method invokes the familiar notion of entitlement. To this I think the answer may well be “yes” — we might be entitled to believe some propositions just by understanding them. And we might be entitled to believe others because if we don’t believe them, mass amounts of other things we take ourselves to know (and even our standards of evidence) would fall into doubt.

  10. Michael Lynch on June 5, 2005 at 9:35 am said:

    Hi Tedla — some quick replies to your two comments.

    1. What is the relationship between intuitions and beliefs on the minimal account? I think it is helpful to approach this question by first asking about the relationship between believing and intuiting. Both are attitudes we can have towards propositions, but to intuit isn’t the same as to believe. For I can intuit what I don’t believe (i.e. I can intuit a solution the semantic paradoxes that I don’t actually believe because I know it to be incorrect). Nonetheless, believing is the more basic notion, and the intuitive attitude is defined in terms of it. Why? Because on the minimalist account, everything I intuit I either believe or I find believable.

    The general strategy is familiar: explicate one attitude in terms of a more familiar one, such as belief or desire.

    What then, is an “intuition” — well, like “belief”, “intuition” is act/object ambigious. Often when we refer to beliefs in the count-noun sense, we are referring to propositions believed. Likewise, when we refer to “intuitions” we are often referring to propositions intuited. But of course, sometimes we might be referring to the attitude, or the composite attitude + proposition. It depends on what you wish to talk about — the key is to be clear about what you are referring to, and in the post, I stipulated that by “intuitions” I mean the propositions intuited. But of course, one can also use that term, on the minimal theory, to refer to tokens of the attitude of intuiting. Perhaps that is to be preferred, I’m not sure.

    2. Like you, I don’t want to rule out (completely) that intuitions are the causal products of a special discrete faculty. But I don’t have evidence that they are either; nor do I see much hope that there will be any empirical evidence for what appears to be an empirical hypothesis.

    Hi Carrie — thanks for the comments. First, let me say that you are right I think, that the epistemic status of intuitions, and their nature, are two distinct issues.   16. Jonathan Weinberg on June 6, 2005 at 10:53 am said:

    One thing that’s generating some confusion here, I think, is some unclarity as to just what Michael is trying to do with his account. What I do not take him to be doing is a bit of old-fashioned conceptual analysis on either “intuition” or “intuitive”. Rather, he is marking out an interesting epistemic kind: we find outselves with seemings that p, which we then frequently cite in defense of claims that p, but for which we have not even a workable folk account (let alone a scientific account) of how they work, and therefore are such that we cannot see clear reasons for why such seemings ought to be trusted and cited in that way. It’s easy to see why such seemings would be of interest in epistemologists, and all the more so once we consider that they present not just an epistemological puzzle, but indeed a metaphilosophical one as well, since it is us philosophers who are doing a lot of that citing.

    So, if I am right about what Michael L is up to, then a number of Michael H’s comments turn out to be a bit beside the point. Given ML’s interest, it’s appropriate for things to stop counting as “intuitions” as we come to understand where they come from, and in particular the epistemic status of such origins. Similarly, the point of framing in terms of believability is (again, if I understand ML correctly) to make it consistent both with the theorists that MH cites, as well as consistent with those who might deny what theose theorists claim (including earlier time-slices of Sosa, I believe). There may be lots of ways to have a seeming/inclination to believe, and one such way is if we have a special propositional attitude of “intuiting that p”. But that does not need to be the only way, and ML’s framework seems meant to beg as few questions as possible at the outset.

    Thanks Mike and Jonathan for the helpful points.

    First off, let me say that Jonathan has got my motivations and general strategy exactly right. I am certainly not offering an old-fashioned conceptual analysis of “intuition”. And JW is also right that the account is at least meant to leave room for the possibility that propositions that we find believable without knowing why are in fact the causal products of a special attitude/faculty called “Intuition” (or the Natural Light, or whatever). Personally, I am skeptical about discovering such a faculty or attitude, but I don’t think an account of what intuition IS should necessarily rule out one causal basis in favor of others.

    So I am not inclined, qua minimalist, to treat talk about “what I am inclined to believe” as anything more than shorthand for what I find believable.

    (As a side note, the general strategy being invoked here can also be found in P. Greenough’s “A minimal theory of vagueness” in MIND (2004) and in Crispin Wright’s work on truth, and in my own work on that subject.. I admit that I am unhappy with the term “minimalism”, since given Paul Horwich’s success in using that term as a name for deflationary outlooks, it is apt to be misleading. Perhaps “liberalism” would work, “neutralism” seems dull…perhaps harking back historically, we might say “topic neutral”…hmmm)

    Having said that, let me try to respond to some of MH’s excellent comments on source-opacity in a bit (although not full) detail:

    Three comments. First a quibble of sorts: I don’t think that it is likely — although it is certainly possible — that future neuroscience will discover a single source of our intuitions.

    Second, and putting that aside, I confess I don’t feel the pull of MH’s counterexamples (which may show I am just trapped within my intuitions about intuitions). In fact, I agree: if we found out once and for all (really, truly actually) that the causal source of our ethical or mathematical intuitions was an heretofore unknown perceptual organ X in our body, and that our capacity to have such intuitions was precisely modulated by the functioning of said organ, I wouldn’t think we would or should call beliefs caused by X “intuitions”, anymore than we call beliefs formed on the basis of visual perception “intuitions”.

    Indeed, I would hazard that one of the most important reasons we use the word “intuition” to mark out certain of the things we find believable is precisely because we are unsure of their source.

    Third, MH asks:

    “In fact, I think I do know, at least roughly, why I have the intuition that the Gettier cases are not knowledge: because (i) in fact they are not knowledge, and (ii) I understand the concept of knowledge. Does this mean that I donâ??t actually have the intuition?”

    No. I would say that you don’t know the CAUSAL source of your intuition unless you know precisely how your grasp of the concept causes you to believe that S lacks knowledge (in some Gettier case). I at least don’t have a good grasp on the causal particulars in such matters. Nonetheless, I would say that your belief that S lacks knowledge may well have a positive epistemic status due simply to your understanding the concept of knowledge. Upshot: talk about our being entitled to our intuitions “because” we grasp certain concepts contained in the intuition shouldn’t be construed as talk about the causal source of the intuitions in question, but about their normative epistemic status.
