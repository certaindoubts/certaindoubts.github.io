I just read your paper (I was lazy when I first put it up because I remembered talking with you about the paradox, and thought I’d check it later–and the result is a perfect example of why my practice is to read things immediately when I first get them, knowing that putting things off leads to not following through even in cases where I’m really interested and really intend to…).

Anyway, I *love* the paper. I talk about these kinds of cases in the first chapter of my value of knowledge book and in my contribution to the Williamson volume here: http://www.missouri.edu/~kvanvigj/pdf/knowledge,%20assertion,%20and%20lotteries.pdf. I use such cases to argue that rules of assertion must be defeasible rules, using the examples of Stich, Churchland, James, Pascal, and the like. But I hadn’t seen the extent of positions that are affected by the point, which your paper displays. Very nice indeed!

So, a quick story about this: When corresponding with Tim about his view that knowledge is the norm of assertion, I tried to get him to say the rules were defeasible (after his Mind paper “Knowing and Asserting” came out). He was resistant, so I decided to appeal to my view that we don’t know our positive views in philosophy (justified by the pessimistic metainduction among other things). So I told Tim that if he was right, he should never publish anything except criticisms of other people, so he shouldn’t have published the Mind paper. He was honest in his reply: he said, “You’re right, but it’s not that big of a wrong!”
