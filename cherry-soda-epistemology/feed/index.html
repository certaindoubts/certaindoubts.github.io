]]> By: Claudio http://certaindoubts.com/cherry-soda-epistemology/#comment-3554 Thu, 20 Apr 2006 03:12:01 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=571#comment-3554 Although I’m given the choice of editing my post as extensively and as often as I please, I don’t like the idea of making any changes in content after the post is made. I want the time stamp to mean something. Since posting on Sunday, I made about a dozen very minor revisions, all concerned with improving the language a bit. But I now feel the need to clarify a point. And that’s why I’ll do it here.

It just dawned on me that some may be disturbed by the way I use the term “truth connection” in the first point (8.1) on section 8 above. The use of the term may seem ambiguous when I mention Chisholm. The suggestion, before I mention Chisholm, is that an internalist conception of justification will fall prey to the Gettier problem, which is implicitly identified as the problem of the absence of the expected truth connection for internalism. But, since Chisholm does try to account for the appropriate connection between belief and truth with an anti-Gettier condition, it may seem that I’m now giving new meaning to the term “truth connection”. After all, his conception of justification remains as internalist as any and the problem of the appropriateness of the relation between belief and truth is supposed to be taken care of by the anti-Gettier condition. (The problem is in saying that Chisholm “rejects the truth connection”, which is slippery.)

In fact, there is no confusion on my part. Before an anti-Gettier mechanism is brought into the picture, internalism faces the problem of accounting for the relation between belief and truth, that is, the problem of (the surprising complexity of) the truth connection, which is what Gettier problems show. After the internalist theory is equipped with such a mechanism, the very same problem remains, since the addition of the anti-Gettier mechanism has no bearing on the (at least apparent) disconnection between what makes a theory internalist (an evidentialist condition) and the truth of the belief.

It remains that the truth connection is the conceptual core of the notion of justification since time immemorial. What’s new is the indictment of _internal_ justification. It doesn’t seem to do what was expected of it all along.

No inconsistency; no confusion. Just clumsy language.
