Distressing Epistemic Motives

Posted on February 17, 2007 by Kvanvig JonFebruary 17, 2007

Suppose equal weighters are right: when two epistemic peers share all and only the same evidence regarding some claim and disagree (i.e., one thinks the claim is true on the basis of the evidence and the other thinks it false on the basis of the evidence), both should turn agnostic about the claim. Suppose also that the mere possibility of such a disagreement isn’t a reason to turn agnostic. And add one final refinement of the view: that disagreement where it is unknown whether, or that, it is by an epistemic peer with the same evidence doesn’t rationally compel agnosticism.

So then, with the (very slight?) emotional imbalances characteristic of the subfield, two epistemologists are talking. One thinks knowledge is important and that it is the most general mental state; the other disagrees. Since the definition of a philosopher is someone who will talk about nearly nothing almost forever (that’s from my father-in-law. . .), they begin the cherished activity. No problem yet from the first paragraph, since they only know that they are peers who disagree. But as they talk, they are both aware that the body of evidence each has is converging toward a common collection.

But they also know the information in the first paragraph. As long as they don’t know that there is disagreement by a peer with the same evidence, everything is fine. But. . . they are approaching that. And now both of them rationally believe that their epistemic lives, their understanding of their subdiscipline, will be impoverished if they keep talking, because then they’ll be rationally compelled to become ignorant of something they presently think they have really good reasons to believe. And the additional information they acquire between now and compelled ignorance won’t undermine or strengthen any of the arguments on which the truth of the issue turns, and they know it.

So, sad as it is to say, they have a motive for murder. But all is not lost. They may quit talking about anything so arcane (but, remember, they are philosophers as defined above). They may change the philosophical subject instead (though, remember, they are epistemologists). OK, this is looking bad, but maybe epistemologists are just characteristically of high moral charac–yeah, OK, that won’t work either. Maybe all we have to hold onto here is the hope that they aren’t from Texas. . .

    You’re right that “Evidence for evidence is evidence” is much too hasty. For instance, I could have evidence that you have evidence that p while I have a defeater for your evidence, which you don’t have. For instance, I just had a letter sent to you from me postmarked College Station, but I’m still in Lubbock; so I have evidence that you have evidence that I’m in College Station, but I don’t have evidence that I’m in College Station. Here you’re not my epistemic peer with respect to this question.

    But I think that whatever restrictions we have to make on the principle won’t leave you justified in believing p in the case you’ve described. I have good reason to believe that, if I keep talking to you, we’ll be epistemic peers with the same evidence who disagree on this topic. And it doesn’t look like I’m in possession of anything that would defeat the counterevidence to my view that this would provide. So it looks to me as though (on equal weight views) I should lower my confidence to the extent that I feel confident that once we share our evidence we will still disagree.

    I guess I’m rejecting the last two suppositions of your first paragraph, or at least saying that they should be taken to be a matter of a degree. The mere possibility of disagreement doesn’t seem threatening—but if you’re pretty sure that your peers would disagree if they saw the evidence, that does seem threatening. (How threatening depends on how sure.) And even if you don’t know whether the disagreer is your peer and shares the evidence, the disagreement still threatens your view to the extent that you believe they’re your peer and to the extent that your evidence overlaps. (If we share almost all the same evidence, and I have no reason to think that the little bit of evidence I have and you lack is the decisive bit, it still seems to threaten my view.)
