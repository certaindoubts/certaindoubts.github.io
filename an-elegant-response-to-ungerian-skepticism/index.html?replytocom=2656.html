An Elegant Response to Ungerian Skepticism

Posted on October 5, 2005 by Kvanvig JonOctober 5, 2005

There’s a way of formulating a response to Unger’s skepticism that characterizes the failure of the position in terms of a scope fallacy. This point may already be in the literature, so feel free to point it out if it is.

The argument I’m thinking about generates a skeptical conclusion from the idea that when you know something, you’ve got adequate evidence for thinking that further investigation isn’t necessary. Some, including Unger, formulate this idea in terms of have adequate evidence that no evidence could be acquired that would undermine one’s present belief. That claim is too strong. A more cautious and accurate statement is that you’ve got adequate evidence for thinking that any information you might acquire that would rationally undermine present belief would be misleading evidence.

The next step in the argument tells us that the above commits us to the absurd position that if we know, we should then ignore any evidence we might gather in the future that counts against what we presently believe. It’s this step that contains the scope fallacy. As stated above, here’s what we can say. If you know that p, then

you are justified in believing that: if you were to acquire evidence against what you presently believe, it would be misleading evidence.

Notice that this claim isn’t the one needed to get to the conclusion that you should ignore contrary evidence. To get to that conclusion, you need the following. If you know that p, then

If you were to acquire evidence against what you presently believe, you’d be justified in believing that it is misleading evidence.

The difference between the two is that the justification operator takes wide scope in the first case and narrow scope in the second. So, a neat explanation of the failure of this argument for skepticism is that it contains a scope confusion.

  33. kvanvig on October 10, 2005 at 6:48 pm said:

    Stephen, you’re absolutely right in the first paragraph. Remember, though, that the issue isn’t about justification itself, but rather about knowledge.     What puzzles me, Jon, is that your response to Unger’s argument seems to put me in the position of both legitimately believing in the present that any contrary evidence I may come across in the future would be misleading, and yet knowing in the present that I may take a very different attitude toward such evidence should I actually come across any in the future.
