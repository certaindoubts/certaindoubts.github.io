]]> By: Stephen Fogdall http://certaindoubts.com/an-elegant-response-to-ungerian-skepticism/#comment-2673 Tue, 11 Oct 2005 00:59:38 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=443#comment-2673 What puzzles me, Jon, is that your response to Unger’s argument seems to put me in the position of both legitimately believing in the present that any contrary evidence I may come across in the future would be misleading, and yet knowing in the present that I may take a very different attitude toward such evidence should I actually come across any in the future. It seems to me less puzzling just to say that while knowledge may legitimate an attitude of closure to further inquiry, it does not legitimate any present attitude toward contrary evidence that may be uncovered later.

I think this discussion may raise issues analogous those raised by Van Fraassen’s principle of reflection, according to which your present degree of belief in a proposition should match what you know your future degree of belief in that proposition will be. Bill Talbott discusses some pretty compelling counterexamples to that principle in a paper titled “Two Principles of Bayesian Epistemology” (Phil Studies 1991). So perhaps the sort of assymetry in attitudes toward future contrary evidence that you are suggesting is the right approach to take.

]]> By: kvanvig http://certaindoubts.com/an-elegant-response-to-ungerian-skepticism/#comment-2668 Sun, 09 Oct 2005 22:20:16 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=443#comment-2668 Mike, 2′ isn’t clear enough to be assessable. First, it is ambiguous between a present attitude and one in question once has acquired new evidence. The former is granted, the latter is the only one in question, so the consequent of 2′ ought really to be a conditional. Second, as noted before, once one has evidence, the question is about what beliefs are justified even in the presence of having that evidence. So the consequent of 2′ is a claim that if we acquire the evidence in question, we’d also be justified in believing that the evidence is misleading. But then 2′ is just: JB(A -> B) -> (A -> JB(B)). There’s easy examples to show that this principle is false: Let A be “Canada is secretly planning to become part of the U.S.,” and B be “Edmonton will soon be part of the U.S.” The point here is what leaves room for the G/H line on Unger’s argument: being justified in thinking that A implies B simply doesn’t tell us what we’re justified in thinking when A is true.

]]> By: Mike http://certaindoubts.com/an-elegant-response-to-ungerian-skepticism/#comment-2667 Sun, 09 Oct 2005 20:40:45 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=443#comment-2667 This is the one that is obviously mistaken:

JB(A -> B); A; therefore JB(B)

This says:

1. JB[All of the closest A-worlds are B-worlds].

2. Our world is an A-world

3. :. JB[Ours is a B-world]

Since our world is closest to itself, I can be pretty sure it’s a B-world. So I don’t think you want to assume that our world is a A-world (premise 2). It’s not worth pursuing further, but the argument is supposed to conclude that I should ignore new evidence against my belief from premise (1). So, a valid version might look like this:

1. JB[In all of the closest A-worlds are B-worlds].
2′. If JB[In all of the closest A-worlds are B-worlds] then
I should ignore additional evidence against my beliefs.
3′. I should ignore new evidence against my belief.

Now the question turns to soundness and the truth of (2). And that raises the interesting question about whether I should ignore evidence on the basis of what I’m *actually justified* in believing or ignore evidence on the basis of what I *would be justified* in believing. Hard to know. In the car/explosion case, it seems like what matters is what I’m actually justified in believing. In any event, settling that question does not seem to be a matter of a simple error in counterfactual reasoning.

The two versions of my reconstruction are:
JB(A -> B); A; therefore JB(B)
A -> JB(B); A; therefore JB(B).
It’s very easy to construct counterexamples to the first form, so I won’t bother here. The second is obviously valid, but only the first argument has the appropriate first premise.

Then there is Ram’s expanded argument, and it may be that you are thinking more of it than the above two arguments. Regarding it, as I said above, and leaving minor quibbles aside (such as acquiring evidence without being in a position where believing that one has such evidence is justified), I think the G/H line is the proper one.
