]]> By: Fritz http://certaindoubts.com/fritz-on-eating-dead-animals/#comment-3318 Sun, 26 Feb 2006 01:00:19 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=531#comment-3318 The animal rights literature is pretty weak on the question of how to argue from the premise of the wrongness of the treatment of animals being raised and processed for food to the conclusion that (to a first approximation) eating and purchasing non-vegan friendly products is morally forbidden. The “I don’t kill or otherwise mistreat chickens, nor do I pay someone to kill or mistreat chickens” is more powerful than Singer, Regan, their followers (and others who have written on the topic) have admitted.

In my talk, I explored attempts to argue from that premise to that conclusion. Some arguments are “future directed” (focusing on alleged future consequences / market influence of meat eating/purchasing behavior). Some arguments are “past directed” (focusing on past mistreatment of animals). Some arguments are symbolic only (e.g., suggesting that meat eating constitutes endorsement of animal mistreatment). No shock that arguments of each of these types tend to either over- or under-shoot the target conclusion.

I suggested that two relatively neglected philosophical issues may help advance this discussion:
(a) it’d be good to have a fairly general account of what is required to dissociate oneself from the wrongdoing of a group to which one belongs
(b) the entire discussion might be be reframed as an issue about collective agency and collective responsibility (if it is, issue (a) become even more theoretically important and pressing.

The visit to Missouri was enjoyable and the discussion was both lively and helpful.
