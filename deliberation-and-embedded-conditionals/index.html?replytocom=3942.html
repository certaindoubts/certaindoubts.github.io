Deliberation and Embedded Conditionals

Posted on September 21, 2006 by Kvanvig JonSeptember 21, 2006

Suppose you are deciding whether to do A or B, which are mutually exclusive. Suppose also that you believe the following:

1. If p, then I should do A.
2. If ~p, then I should do B.

But, being a philosopher, you think further, and realize that the truth of these conditionals is contingent. And, further, you believe that the contingency is based on some further claim q for the first claim and r for the second. So you believe:

3. If q, then (if p, then I should do A).
4. If ~q, then ~(if p, then I should do A).
5. If r, then (if ~p, then I should do B).
6. If ~r, then ~(if ~p, then I should do B).

Suppose then that you learn whether or not p is true. Since you believe 1 & 2, it looks like that would settle the issue of what to do. But you also believe 3-6, and we may suppose that you haven’t learned whether or not q is true and you haven’t learned whether or not r is true.

You have available to you the following line of reasoning: “the truth of 1 and 2 is dependent on information I don’t have, and so I shouldn’t be confident about either 1 or 2. So I shouldn’t use the truth or falsity of p as a guide to what to do in this case.”

Is this reasoning sound?

If you think it is, then paralysis ensues. Paralysis ensues except when the conditionals that are used in deliberation are necessary, which of course is at best rarely true. For notice the argument doesn’t depend on knowing exactly what the contingency of 1 & 2 depends on. Instead, all it requires is knowing that it is dependent on something, we know not what. But if you know that 1 & 2 are contingent, then you already know, in virtue of knowing what ‘contingent’ means, that there is some claim which is such that a variation in its truth value implies a variation in the truth value of 1 or 2.

So suppose you think the above reasoning must be rejected. You think instead that all that matters is whether 1 & 2 are true or whether you believe them. That’s all that matters to proper decision-making. But then reflection on one’s deliberative processes of the sort imagined above is, we might say, completely epiphenomenal from the point of view of rational decision-making. But that can’t be right either. If you learn that your assumptions about which conditionals are true depends on information you don’t yet have, it looks like you shouldn’t yet use those conditionals in deciding what to do.

So perhaps we should say the following. If you believe 3-6, you should rationally withhold on 1 & 2, and so not use them in deliberating, because not knowing whether q and not knowing whether r undermines any reasons you’d have for believing 1 or 2.

Consider, though, a generalization of each of 3-6, replacing the reference to q and r with an existential quantifier and a variable. If 1 & 2 are contingent, that logically implies that there is some proposition such that if it were true, 1 would be true and if it were false, 1 would be false; and there is some proposition such that if it were true 2 would be true and if it were false 2 would be false. You may not know which propositions have that feature but you know there are such propositions.

So what difference does it make to rationality when you can identify the particular content in question? Why would the mere fact that you can specify the ground of contingency make such a difference?

    Parfit puts forward an example that is analogous to the issue of whether, if it will land heads, you ought to bet heads. 100 miners are trapped underground in either shaft A or B. In the time available before they die, you can either dig a rescue tunnel to A or to B but not to both. If you get it right you will save all, but otherwise all will die. Alternatively, you can dig an intermediate rescue tunnel, call it C, which will certainly save exactly 90 of the 100 lives, whichever of A or B they are in. Clearly, this is not a case in which you can refuse the bet, so what ought you to do? Under outcome consequentialism, if they are in A you ought to dig A and if they are in B you ought to dig B and it’s not the case that you ought to dig C (since there is an outcome under which digging C is worse, so a maximising teleological principle cannot imply that you ought to dig C). Under prospect consequentialism you ought to dig C (a propect need not be simply the expected outcome of an action, but the disparity between certainty of saving 90 and a significant chance of losing 100 is going to favour digging C under most accounts of a prospect).
