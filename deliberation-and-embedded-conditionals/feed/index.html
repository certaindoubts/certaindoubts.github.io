In the time available before they die, you can either dig a rescue tunnel to A or to B but not to both. If you get it right you will save all, but otherwise all will die. Alternatively, you can dig an intermediate rescue tunnel, call it C, which will certainly save exactly 90 of the 100 lives, whichever of A or B they are in. Clearly, this is not a case in which you can refuse the bet, so what ought you to do? Under outcome consequentialism, if they are in A you ought to dig A and if they are in B you ought to dig B and it’s not the case that you ought to dig C (since there is an outcome under which digging C is worse, so a maximising teleological principle cannot imply that you ought to dig C). Under prospect consequentialism you ought to dig C (a propect need not be simply the expected outcome of an action, but the disparity between certainty of saving 90 and a significant chance of losing 100 is going to favour digging C under most accounts of a prospect).

Moore-paradoxical propositions have the feature in question (some feature like the one you have in mind): the existentially quantified form is OK but some instances are not, and they are all contingent. So, “Somebody has no thoughts” is fine; “I don’t have any” is not. (I hold that that’s a Moore-paradoxical proposition, have argued that it is. But this will work for the historical forms too.)
