Primary/Secondary Propriety

Posted on July 10, 2005 by Kvanvig JonJuly 10, 2005

Jennifer Lackey sent me a really interesting paper on the norm(s) of assertion over the weekend, though I don’t think it’s up on her website yet. Among the interesting parts of the paper is some discussion of the topic of this post, which concerns the fact that, in defending accounts of the norm of assertion, theorists often resort to a primary/secondary distinction regarding the satisfaction of a particular norm.

For example, Keith says in “Assertion, Knowledge, and Context,”

While those who assert appropriately (with respect to this rule) in a primary sense will be those who actually obey it, a speaker who broke this rule in a blameless fashion (one who asserted something she didn’t know, but reasonably thought she did know) would in some secondary sense be asserting properly.

Matt Weiner uses a similar distinction to defend his truth account. He says in his forthcoming Phil Review piece,

if assertion is governed by the truth norm, an assertion is secondarily improper if the speaker does not have reason to believe that it is true.

There is surely something correct in these remarks, and I think finding what the truth of the matter is will be useful. It will also show us something about the plausibility of various proposals about norms of assertion.

So here’s my thought on the matter. Suppose we have an evaluative term such as ‘proper’, ‘warranted’, or some such notion, a notion found in a proposed norm of assertion. A proposed norm of assertion can then take any of the following forms, where N is one’s favored evaluative term:

An assertion of p is N only if X.
An assertion of p is N if X.
An assertion of p is N if and only if X.

Most will recognize the first form here as the one under discussion in disputes between the knowledge account of assertion and alternatives to it, such as Bach’s belief account, my own justification account, and Weiner’s truth account.

The reason I put all three up, however, is that the third one can teach us something important about the appeal made by DeRose, Weiner, et. al. to the primary/secondary distinction. Suppose X is either truth or knowledge in the third kind of account, and when faced with the inevitable counterexamples in terms of justified false assertion, a theorist resorts to the primary/secondary distinction. Such an appeal, in the context of the third kind of account, would lead to absurdity. Consider a truth account along these lines, for example: An assertion is proper iff true. But in the face of a counterexample, we say, “Yes, in the primary sense it is proper iff true, but there is a secondary sense in which it is proper iff it is reasonable to believe it is true.”

Such a remark is simply absurd. From the account itself, we can infer that any false assertion is improper; there is simply no room whatsoever for some secondary sense here. The only possibility is that there is some other evaluative notion that covers cases of justified false assertion, and that notion better not imply propriety on pain of contradiction. (For a clear attempt along these lines, see Williamson, who reserves the term ‘warrant’ for the property of an assertion that satisfies the norm of assertion, and tries to use the notion of reasonability to account for problem cases. This strategy becomes less plausible, of course, the more stipulative one becomes about the key normative notions, but my point isn’t to evaluate the strategy here but just to point it out and note its difference from the appeal to a primary/secondary distinction.)

These remarks apply only to instances of the third kind of norm of assertion, but they teach us something about the more typical instances of the first kind as well. The first kind finds a necessary condition for normatively adequate assertion in terms of some condition such as knowledge or truth. When defending such views in the face of apparent counterexample by appeal to the primary/secondary distinction, one way to take the appeal is the same as above: we might take the distinction in terms of recourse to some other normative concept other than the one used in expressing the norm of assertion.

This way of taking the distinction makes the choice of the language of primary and secondary somewhat misleading, however, unless some explanation is provided as to why one of the notions is primary and the other secondary. If we take Williamson’s approach as a model, how could we argue that warrant is primary and reasonability secondary? He doesn’t make such a claim, and it’s not easy to see how to defend such a claim. He simply reserves the notion of warrant for assertions that satisfy the norm of assertion, and uses different normative language to explain away troublesome cases. One can then say that warrant is primary with respect to satisfying the norm of assertion, but that claim is true only to the extent that it is made true by stipulation.

So if we want to make sense of the language of primary and secondary, we need a different understanding. We might try to talk like undergraduates who distinguish between objective and subjective truth, talking here instead in terms of objective and subjective propriety, but I think we should reject such talk. The same reasons for resisting the idea that there are two kinds of truth here are reasons for resisting the idea that there are two kinds of propriety here. (In brief, objective truth is just truth and subjective truth is nothing more than believed truth, and hence is not a kind of truth at all; something similar should be said about subjective propriety or justification or warrant.)

Here’s a better idea, I think. In distinguishing primary and secondary propriety, we could take such language as indicating two claims. The first is that there is another necessary condition on normative adequacy of assertion besides the primary one offered, and the second is that this other necessary condition is to be explained in terms of the primary norm. Without the second claim, there is no sense to be attached to the notion of secondarity, so it is essential to defending that the problem cases don’t simply refute the original proposal.

The bottom line, then, is that there are two ways to proceed here. The first is to try to use some other normative notion to explain away problem cases. Such an approach is successful only to the extent that it includes some story as to why this other normative notion is somehow second-class in comparison to the notion at work in the stated norm. It is interesting to note that examples of this approach tend toward, let us say, the enthymematic side of the spectrum of argumentation.

The other approach is to posit separate norms of assertion, and to argue that one is explanatorily fundamental. If one goes in this direction, however, one ought to say what the other norm or norms are and show how they are explicable in terms of, or depend on, the primary norm the theory posits.

The “primary” and “secondary” type of propriety — others might quite naturally want to use “objective” and “subjective” here — is a way of capturing that. To my thinking, this comes before we settle on a particular rule for assertion. Any theory is going to want to account for both types of (im)propriety. Different theories will propose different Cs in rules of the form: “Assert that p only if condition C is met.” Whatever C you choose, your theory predicts that assertions of p are appropriate in the primary way when C is in fact met, and appropriate in the secondary way only when the speaker is reasonable in thinking that C is met. (Though there may be questions about the exact relation between primary & secondary propriety. But it will be at least something like “reasonable in thinking that C is met.”)

    For many accounts of assertion (including mine), you can beef that “when” up to “when and only when” by adding an appropriate clause limiting what it’s an account of. We are seeking the rule that demands that one assert only what one is well-enough positioned with respect to. There are of course other rules governing assertion — like ones requiring us to only assert what’s relevant. Being an advocate of the knowledge account of assertion, my condition C is that the speaker knows what she asserts, and I put that forward as both necessary and sufficient for proper assertion so far as asserting only what one is well-enough positioned to assert — though one can meet this condition and still be asserting inappropriately by violating some other rule that governs assertion. So, on my account, so far as asserting only what one is in a position to assert goes, one asserts properly in the primary way when and only when one knows that p, and one asserts properly in the secondary way when and only when one is reasonable in thinking one knows that p.
