Phenomenologist’s such as Louis Sass criticize cognitive science and analytic philosophy for having no clear unambiguous definition of belief. If we take Huemer’s norm as providing a clear definition of belief, then this is a very useful result. He will then be able to dismiss the Ichiro example as being equivocal in that Ichiro does not “believe” that he will hit the ball in the sense of “belief” defined by the knowledge norm. But unfortunately, in my opinion, the knowledge norm for belief does not pick out the dominant usage of “belief” in English. As Pierre says, there is nothing wrong with saying that I believe something but that I don’t know it. What I want to do is to clearly define these two types of belief and how they relate to each other. The best way in my view is to talk about non-factive “knowing” to mean Huemer’s variety of belief, and “thinking” to mean the kind of belief that doesn’t warrant a knowledge claim. So Ichiro has got an epistemic reason to “think” that he will hit the ball, but lacks a justification for “knowing” he will hit the ball. But it seems that many say things with the implication that you don’t have a reason to even “think” that P if you “know” that not P is more probable that P. But this assumption is arbitrary, since you can have good reason to think that something is going to happen when it is highly unlikely that it will happen, especially in cases where your information is limited. In a five horse race where you have a tip that horse number one will win, you have a reason to think horse number one will win even if your degree of belief that horse number one will win is 0.3. I just meant that in Ichiro’s case, his past hit rate doesn’t give him a good reason to believe that he will hit the next ball or that he will miss it. It only gives him a good reason to believe that he is fairly likely to hit the ball, but more likely to miss it. The statistics on his past hit rate either give him a justification or they don’t. If they don’t then it seems you can’t say that he knows he is not epistemically justified. If they do, then you have to admit that a .7 past frequency of an event happening is an epistemic reason for believing that it will happen. In which case, why is a .3 probability of something happening not a good reason to believe it will? Neither warrant a knowledge claim. I’m not sure what traditional epistemic terms are, but if traditionally knowledge is true justified belief, then a justified belief will warrant a knowledge claim. In this case, statistical information will provide no justification whatsoever. This may be fine for what you want to say, since in this case Ichiro still doesn’t have an epistemic justification for believing that he will hit the next ball. But then the past hit rate is irrelevant, since he doesn’t have an epistemic reason for believing he will miss the ball either. Without some kind of threshold above which a probabilistic reason becomes a traditional epistemic justification, then there is nothing epistemically unjustified about his believing he will hit the next ball. If you reverse the situation, so that a terrible pilot finds out he is less likely to crash on missions where he believes he is going to crash, it might seem rational for him to believe he will crash, even though he only crashes 3 out of 10 times. But then it would seem epistemially justified to believe that he was going to crash even if your belief would have no impact whatever on the outcome.

It seems to be an empirical truth, or at least a much expressed idea, that you can improve your performance in a task by visualising success. In this way you are concentrating your mind on the details of a successful action. If you were recruiting for a fresh business venture, you would want to recruit people who “believed” in the project, even though statistically it might be a long shot. When in “Peter Pan” the audience are asked to “believe” it is to save Tinker Bell’s life. This meaning of “belief” is like faith, hope, commitment and loyalty, and is closely related to action. There is no way that Ichiro could increase his past hit rate by believing that it was better. Visualising successful action is focussing your desire, and is much akin to rehearsal and practice. It has little to do with assessing whether a future proposition is true. It has more to do with making that proposition true. In order to succesfully achieve a certain difficult and complicated goal you have to believe that that goal can be achieved and see a way to achieve it. A natural language short hand for this is to say that you believe you will achieve it.

]]> By: wheeler http://certaindoubts.com/the-knowledge-norm-for-belief/#comment-2325 Thu, 27 Oct 2005 19:36:27 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=394#comment-2325 I’m not sure which part of the dialectic you have in mind. I first toyed with the Ichiro example last summer as an argument against Michael Huemer’s Knowledge as the Norm of Belief proposal, outlined above. But I’ve since worked on a more general version of this argument, directed against epistemic constraints on rational belief that are much weaker than Huemer’s. I might post on this in the future, but I’m a bit too swamped at the moment to do it now.

This said, the theory of action you mention is not presupposed in my example. Rather, the idea is that Ichiro does a little bit better at the plate believing he’ll get a hit than believing otherwise, so he has a practical reason to believe he’ll get a hit but not an epistemic reason to believe he will get a hit. Indeed, he has good epistemic grounds for believing he won’t get a hit.

The content of Ichiro’s belief is that he will get a hit, not that he knows he’ll get a hit, nor even that he’s justified to get a hit.

Finally, we are restricting ourselves to whether the (positive) epistemic status of a belief is a necessary constraint on rationally holding that belief; we are not discussing the decision problem of whether Ichiro (or anyone else) should take a low probability gamble. Settling this issue, as you rightly mention, depends upon the utilities involved.
