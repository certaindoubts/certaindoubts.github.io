I’m not denying that he argues against it, or that he sometimes denies it categorically. But I find him saying, apparently summarizing the two arguments you mention, on p. 31, “On quite general grounds, one would not expect the concept knows to have a non-trivial analysis in somehow more basic terms.” and on p. 33 “The working hypothesis should be that the concept knows cannot be analysed into more basic concepts.”

“One would not expect…” “the working hypothesis” It sounds to me as if Williamson is supporting the relatively modest claim that previous attempts to make belief conceptually prior to knowledge, so that knowledge can be analyzed in terms of belief plus other conditions, have all been subject to counterexamples. A successful analysis of ‘knows’ in the sense of one that doesn’t have counterexamples, will probably seem ad hoc, given the apparently ad hoc character of the attempts currently on offer, and we will probably react to it by being puzzled about why knowledge should seem important, etc. For these reasons we ought to try regarding knowledge as basic, and see if other interesting concepts can be characterized in terms of it.

The arguments on 30-31 have an empirical character, and the conclusion is treated as only probable.I agree that these are “bad arguments” for the conclusion that knowledge cannot be analyzed, if that just means that they don’t deductively establish that conclusion. But it seems to me that they are still persuasive arguments for trying a different line about knowledge — for trying the “working hypothesis” that knowledge is a basic concept.

]]> By: Ralph Wedgwood http://certaindoubts.com/williamsons-argument-against-the-analyzability-of-knowledge/#comment-15 Sat, 12 Jun 2004 04:14:50 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=4#comment-15 I agree with Chris that it is very important to distinguish the 3 kinds of “account of knowledge”.
But type (2) accounts can also come in two forms: some make a very strong claim of “meaning equivalence”, or at least claim that the biconditional is at least implicitly known by everyone who possesses the concept of knowledge; others merely claim that the biconditional is in some way guaranteed to be true simply by the nature of the concept of knowledge, or that it is knowable by means of reflection on the concept of knowledge. I would regard the latter claim as much more plausible than the former.
As for Jon’s question about the second argument, I think that there is a way of defending that argument even as an attack on a type (1) account. We can take the argument, not as a psychological challenge (How can the “account of knowledge” in question explain our attitudes towards knowledge?), but as an axiological challenge (How can this account of knowledge explain why knowledge matters as much as it does, or why it is important in the way that it is?)
The idea is, if something is in a certain way intrinsically important, or something worth caring about, then a type (1) account of its essential nature should be able to explain why it is important or worth caring about in that way.
If I may be forgiven the shameless plug, that was why in the last section of my paper “The Aim of Belief”, I tried to show that my account of knowledge does explain why knowledge matters. (I was drawing on the brilliant discussion in chapter 2 of Bernard Williams’ Descartes book about why anyone who wants the truth has a reason to want knowledge: if you want to get something, then you also have reason to want to get it by means of your attempts at getting it succeeding in the way that you rationally assumed that they would; and knowledge is, very very roughly, getting at the truth because one’s attempt to get to the truth succeeded in this way.)

]]> By: Chris http://certaindoubts.com/williamsons-argument-against-the-analyzability-of-knowledge/#comment-14 Thu, 10 Jun 2004 13:57:22 +0000 http://fleetwood.baylor.edu/certain_doubts/?p=4#comment-14 There seem to be three sorts of things one might be trying to do by offering an analysis of knowledge.
(1) a metaphysical analysis: knowledge is actually metaphysically “built up” out of more basic stuff; the property or state of knowing just is identical to the property or state of believing truly, justifiable, and whatever else.
(2) a conceptual analysis: the concept of knowing is a conjunctive concept that includes the concept of belief, etc.
(3) a statement of necessary and sufficient conditions for knowing

(3) is by far the weakest; as we know, it may be necessary and sufficient for something to be a renate that it be a cordate, but the concepts are different and the terms do not pick out the same property. In addition, Williamson argues that the first two are independent of each other as well. To paraphrase p. 30: if the concept KNOWS is mental and the concept TB is not, then it follows that knowing is a mental state (given that S is a mental state iff there could me a mental concept of that state), but it does not follow that tb-ing is not a mental state, since perhaps there could also be a mental concept of that state. So lack of conceptual identity does not imply lack of metaphysical identity. If knowing is a mental state and tb-ing is not, then the concept TB is non-mental, but it does not follow that the concept KNOWS is mental, since there could be a different concept of the state of knowing which was mental. So lack of metaphysical identity does not entail lack of conceptual identity.

So there are these three things one might be up to when one seeks an analysis. Williamson’s claim that knowing is a mental state is consistent with the success of (3), is not consistent with the success of (2), and (hence) calls into question the success of (1). He argues that BELIEVES TRULY is not a mental concept, since if S is a mental concept, then all of S’s constituent concepts (if any) are mental concepts as well, and TRULY is not a mental concept. If these arguments are successful, then BELIEVES TRULY plus whatever else is not a mental concept, either. Since KNOWS is a mental concept on his account, (2) is doomed. (More carefully: any type (2) analysis that includes BELIEVES TRULY is doomed.) I think that is the version of analysis that is his real target, and that this sort of argument is the objection he gives to the claim that knowledge is analyzable in the sense of (1) or (2).

He goes on to say that although KNOWS being conceptually distinct from BT+whatever else does not *entail* that the states picked out are distinct, it at least calls it into question. So (2) is out of the question for Williamson, (1) very dubious given the hopelessness of (2), and (3) is consistent with his account.

I think the arguments cited in the post are part of Williamson’s case against *the hunt* for an analysis of type (3). I don’t think they are meant to be arguments against the analyzability of knowledge. He thinks that knowledge is unanalyzable in the sense of (1) and (2), but for the reasons stated above. He says his account is consistent with the success of (3), but the induction based on the history of epistemology and the argument from “uninterestingness” seem best construed as arguments for the conclusion that we should give up the hunt for a type (3) analysis, not that no such analysis is possible.
