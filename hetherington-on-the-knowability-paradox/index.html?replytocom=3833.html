    Thanks much for the clarification, Joe. Let me think. JTB is not problematic, if considered alone. It is paradoxical only insofar as we identify it with knowledge. The case with JTB, I suggest, is:

    (*) If K=J+T+B and K=J+T+B is paradoxical, then one between J, T and B must be paradoxical in the same way.

    But then, by modus tollens, we can discharge K=J+T+B (which seems to be quite reasonable by the way). We didn’t show that it is not the case that, in general, if some complex property F is paradoxical, then some of its component must be paradoxical in the same way.

  6. hetherington on August 18, 2006 at 5:39 pm said:

    1. Julien,

    Maybe you are right that, on my thinking about the supposed paradox, the issue could become one of whether there is a paradox of justifiability — and thereby one of knowability. (If there is a paradox of knowability only via the true-belief aspect of knowing, then there is not really a paradox of knowability at all — given, again, that there is none about true belief as such.)

    And is there a paradox of justifiability? There isn’t one of fallible justifiability, because the needed reasoning doesn’t go through for this notion. And there isn’t one of infallible justifiability, because the needed premise won’t be widely accepted for this notion.

    Would there be a paradox of knowability, once we add the Gettier factor X into our account of knowing? In the paper (at note 4), I implicitly take this into account. The issue remains one of whether the resulting knowledge that p would, or would not, be fallible — in effect, whether the justification plus X provides only fallibilist support for the truth of the belief that p. And then the argument can simply be repeated, mutatis mutandis.

    Here, for simplicity I’m trying to work with the usual assumptions about knowledge’s being a kind of belief and its having to include justification. For the record, I’ve argued elsewhere that knowledge doesn’t have to be a kind of belief (or acceptance either): see my paper in the book Epistemology Futures, where I try to reduce knowledge-that to knowledge-how. (And so I am also doubting the Williamson thesis you mention, that knowing is “a primitive mental state”.) And I’ve argued (in the book Good Knowledge, Bad Knowledge, and in a paper forthcoming in PPR) that, at least conceptually, knowledge doesn’t have to include justification, let alone any more complex form of warrant.

    I mention those other options not because they must be correct, but because IF they are, then they might both narrow and open up the debate a little, concerning (as they do) both knowledge’s components and its modalities.

    2. Joe,

    That brings me to your question about the paper. I’m not sure that I need as strong or general a principle as you’re describing. In note 7 of the paper, I run through a few possibilities as to what aspect of knowledge would be responsible for the supposed paradox (still assuming for simplicity, in the standard way, some usual sort of analysis of knowledge).

    And the property mentioned by you, of being Gettier-problematic, would fall under that brief discussion. It is a property that pertains to how the other components of knowledge (specifically, belief, truth, and fallibilist justification) are to be combined. Is not-being-Gettier-problematic thereby a constitutive property itself of knowledge? Whether or not it is, it is A property of knowing (on the standard view of knowing, even if not on mine); and once that is acknowledged, the same main question recurs — which is that of whether, with that extra property being instantiated, the resulting knowledge would be fallible. And with this question, we return to my argument in the paper. If the putative knowledge would be fallible, this is because whatever is added to true belief to constitute the knowledge is not strong enough, in the relevant way, to be constituting infallible knowledge. So, wherever, at present in the paper, I talk of fallibilist vs. infallibilist justification, just substitute talk of fallibilist vs. infallibilist warrant — with this being taken to include the property of not being Gettier-problematic (quite possibly some structural property).

    Stephen

  16. hetherington on August 20, 2006 at 4:49 pm said:

    I’m following these comments with great interest (because they are substantial and intriguing in their own right). But I’m not sure that all of them bear upon the strictly epistemic dimension of my argument’s focus.

    That focus is upon knowledge AS AGAINST mere true belief. Is there a paradox of knowability AS AGAINST a paradox of mere true belief? Even if we accept (as I don’t; see entry #6 above) that knowledge must include true belief, that question is reasonable. There is quite a difference between these two theses: (1) Any truth is believable; (2) Any truth is not only believable, but justifiably believable. And insofar as (again for simplicity, I’m assuming here; but again, see entry #6 above) knowledge is NOT merely true belief, the following obtains: If there is no paradox of (2)-knowability, then there is no knowability paradox about knowledge as such. Or (to state the latter consequent more helpfully) there is no knowability paradox about knowledge in any sense that touches upon what epistemologists routinely ask of, or expect from, knowledge qua something BEYOND mere true belief. There would be no knowability paradox about the strictly epistemic dimension of knowledge.
