Welcome to formal epistemologists

Posted on February 24, 2005 by Kvanvig JonFebruary 24, 2005

One of my interests is to see greater interaction between traditional approaches to epistemology and more formal approaches to epistemology, including Bayesian epistemology, confirmation theory, learning theory, and whatever other stuff is done by the really neat group of contributors we now have in this area: Fitelson, Wheeler, Hawthorne, Joyce, Armendt, Gillies, Roush, Weiner and others (I’m going from memory here, and may be leaving some out, so please feel from to complain about my sorry memory…).

So far, the experiment is a hit. Just look at the interest of such posts in terms of numbers of comments, both by the formalists and the traditionalists. Very neat, I say!

If you’re a contributor and would like to email me with suggestions to include more people who work on the formal side, please do so and I’ll contact them.

    Hi Clayton,

    Does your university offer a course in discrete mathematics by chance? There are several good textbooks pitched at different levels, but a good intro text (first year/second year undergraduate) is ‘Discrete Mathematics and its Applications’ by Ken Rosen, McGraw Hill. A course covering the topics in this book would give you a gentle and broad introduction with an emphasis on how logic and probability are applied, which, in my view, is very important since much of the interesting philosophy falls out from evaluating the appropriate fit (if any at all) between various mathematical notions or models and our philosophical intuitions about how particular concepts or processes should work. The nice thing about a discrete math course is that it is useful to see how modeling works on concrete problems that aren’t contrived before turning to philosophical applications which are, by their nature, less clear cut.

  8. Vincent F. Hendricks on February 25, 2005 at 3:35 pm said:

    The interaction between mainstream and formal epistemology is on the rise. At least two meeting points seem to stand out so far:

    (1) Epistemological “Forcing”: The idea of forcing is to delimit the set of possibilities over which the inquiring agent has to succeed: If the agent can succeed over the relevant possibility set then the agent may still be said to have knowledge even if he commits grave and many errors in other but irrelevant possibilities. The heuristic principle of forcing is this:

    Whenever knowledge claims are challenged by alleged possibilities of error, the strategy is to show that the possibilities of error fail to be genuine in the relevant sense.

    The forcing strategy may be found in mainstream proposals like from reliabilism, counterfactual, and contextual epistemologies. In formal epistemology, like epistemic logic the same strategy is enforced since algebraic constraints on the accessibility relation between possible worlds may screen off possibilities of error. The more algebraic constraints, the stronger the knowledge system all the way up to, say, S5, the price is of course stronger forcing assumptions. The cognitive rationale of epistemic logic must be something like this: The more properties the accessibility relation is endowed with, the more access the agent has to his epistemic universe, and in consequence the more epistemic strength he will obtain, and the less room for the skeptic to move around citing possibilities of error. Bayesian epistemology may also be read as a formal forcing paradigm: It requires the agent to succeed in possible worlds with high prior probability and disregard sets of possible worlds with low probability. In case the truth is in a world ascribed $0$ or infinitesimal prior the truth is never to be found. Bayesian epistemology does not alter the nature of the forcing relation. On this construal Bayesian epistemology simply provides yet another argument for dumping error-possibilities. For a final example “computational epistemology”, better known as formal learning theory (Kelly 96) focuses on the intrinsic solvability of inductive epistemic problems for both ideal and computationally bounded agents. Faced with an epistemic learning problem the basic idea is that the problem as such determines a set of possible worlds over which the agent has to succeed to solve the problem and acquire knowledge. This is also forcing. Brains in vats sever the connection between knowledge acquisition and reliable inquiry, but short of that agents may reliably have quite a bit of inductive knowledge. Being a logical paradigm in that it utilizes tools from mathematical logic it is also a procedural or effective paradigm concentrating on learning and knowledge acquisition issues rather than modal operators, axiomatics and validity as epistemic logic initially does. For much more on this meeting point, see “Forcing Epistemology” (forthcoming 2005, Cambridge University Press, see , and the forthcoming issue of Phil. Stud. “7 Bridges between Mainstream and Formal Epistemology” and the joint project with Duncan “New Waves in Epistemology”.

    (2) The Dynamics of Epistemics and Doxastics. One thing is to determine what knowledge is, its mode of justification and its potential resistance to skeptical challenges, another is to study how knowledge or information may or should rationally change in light of what is already known and new evidence. Van Fraassen recently outlined the tenets of what he calls a `new’ epistemology suggesting a shift in epistemological focus from the `defensive’ of the classical definition of knowledge to the offensive dynamics of epistemics and doxastics. For Van Fraassen the means are probabilities but they might as well be learning, strategies for winning games, belief revision and other formal proposals. See for instance “Forcing Epistemology for more.

    In arbitrary order the following formal epistemologists also work in the intersection, besides the ones already mentioned:

    – Krister Segerberg (epistemic logic and belief revision)
    – Heinrich Wansing (doxastic logic and doxastic voluntarism)
    – Joe Halpern (epistemic logic, rationality and game theory)
    – Robert Aumann (epistemic logic, rationality and game theory)
