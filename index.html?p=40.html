Knowledge as an indefeasible norm of assertion?

Posted on June 30, 2004 by Jon KvanvigJune 20, 2012

Tim Williamson holds that knowledge is the norm of assertion, and though I think this is wrong, let’s assume it for present purposes. My interest is not so much in what the norm(s) of assertion are, but rather whether they are defeasible.

Williamson, I recall, holds that they are indefeasible. That means when skeptics tell you of their skepticism and let you in on the philosophical issues that lead them to that conclusion, and then assert, “I don’t know anything,” they violate the norm of assertion. So too do the Churchlands and Stich when they say they have no beliefs (assuming belief is a necessary condition for knowledge). And when a follower of Pascal or James follows their pragmatic advice, and comes to believe God exists in some way or other, if they say “God exists, and I am pragmatically justified in so thinking,” that person too does something wrong.

I pick these cases intentionally, of course, to try to persuade that the norm(s) of assertion are defeasible. I’m interested to see how persuasive others think such cases are.

One more consideration, of a more theoretical sort. Norms that interest philosophers–epistemic, aesthetic, moral, pragmatic, etc.–are defeasible norms, aren’t they? Maybe I’ve been thinking about epistemic norms too much, of the sort Pollock describes, for example, but this seems to me to be true; and it certainly fits with my W.D. Rossian inclinations in ethics. If so, why would we think that the norms for assertion are different?

I don’t think this latter consideration counts for much, even if I’m right about norms generally being defeasible, so the weight of my complaint rests more on the cases above. One additional item, though: what does a theory such as Williamson’s lose by making the norm defeasible rather than indefeasible? One cost is obvious, since you’ll then incur some need for further explanation in order to say when defeat occurs. This cost is balanced, though, by the fact that if you insist on indefeasibility, you’ll have to explain away apparent counterexamples.

  3. jon kvanvig on June 30, 2004 at 9:33 pm said:

    The idea that the norm of assertion is defeasible is this: without some additional explanation to justify an assertion when one doesn’t have knowledge, the assertion is improper. But, since the norm is defeasible, there can be extenuating circumstances that make it OK to assert p even though one does not know p.

    Think of the idea in terms of defeasible reasons: a defeasible reason R for p is a claim that confirms p in the absence of undermining information, or grounds for doubt. Formally, such a reason for p is a reason even though R together with some additional piece of information need not be a reason for p.

    The remarks in the first paragraph presume that knowledge is the norm of assertion; if we’re talking about other views about the norm of assertion, we’d need to adjust the above remarks to the new view.

I’ve got a moderately elaborate theory about all of this (See my Phil Studies paper, 2002, reference on the web site) which argues that the point of saying people know is encouraging better (more helpful) testimony.   14. jon kvanvig on July 2, 2004 at 7:30 am said:

    I guess I think the following about philosophical claims such as skeptical claims. Divide philosophical remarks into positive and negative (yes, I know there’s no clear way to do so, but I’m relying on our recognition skills (!) here to tell the difference between one philosopher finding fault with a theory and another philosopher proposing something that is a new theory). I don’t think positive philosophy is within the range of what we know at all. So when a utilitarian says that the right thing to do is what maximizes utility, the utilitarian is saying something that s/he doesn’t know to be true (even if, per impossible, the claim happens to be true).

    So there’s one gigantic ad hominem lurking here, of the sort that is a legitimate criticism. If knowledge is the norm of assertion, only negative philosophy should survive (and, of course, some of that should disappear as well). Even more interesting, no should ever assert that knowledge is the norm of assertion. And since assertion is to belief (maybe…) as outer is to inner, no should believe it either. (An aside: who would think that knowledge is the norm of belief anyway? Maybe I’ll do a main entry on this…)

    Such untoward implications can be handled simply by making the norms defeasible. That would still explain our students’ responses that the skeptic is being inconsistent (which s/he’s not) or doing something else that is wrong. They haven’t yet caught on to the special circumstances that surround philosophy that allow us to make what would be in ordinary contexts, outrageous claims.

    Williamson and I emailed about this a couple of years ago, and his reply to the philosophy example (actually, I used the example of his own book, which contains an unusual percentage of original positive philosophy) quite amusing. He said something to the effect that violating the norm is not that big a wrong…
