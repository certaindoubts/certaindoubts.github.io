I guess one might think that, given the title of my post, but I was just giving the title of the Cullen paper I was calling to peoples’ attention. I have my questions and worries about some of what’s been done (as should be expected), and some of them do overlap with some of the issues that Cullen discusses. But I think x-phi is a promising development that has already given philosophers plenty that they should be thinking about. I wish your movement (if that’s what it is) well.

On my “pet peeve” (well, my real pet peeve concerns the x-phi work on free will, but that’s not my area nor the area of this blog: so I guess I mean my pet peeve concerning x-epistemology): You write:

The answer is that we wanted to try to make it more likely that our subjects would answer the question using a suitably epistemic construal of “knows”, of the sort that epistemologists are interested in, and not other standard usages in English like believes-with-subjective-certainty or “exam knowledge.”

This is a case of “you can’t please everyone,” I think. I myself have little sympathy for those who would make such “that’s not the right kind of ‘knowledge'” moves. I don’t think there are good grounds for thinking there are separate “believes-with-subjective-certainty” or “exam knowledge” senses of “knows.” As for the “suitably epistemic construal of ‘knows’, of the sort that epistemologists are interested in”: It isn’t clear to me what “epistemic” is doing there (one way to understand that adjective is “having to do with knowledge,” which yields: a construal of ‘knows’ on which it has to do with knowledge), and I think there’s plenty of room to suspect that what the epistemologists in question are interested in is an awful mess: they are moved by some aspects of our usual practices of using “knows” but not others. I guess this goes pretty deep into differences in “basic philosophical orientation,” but I think that what makes knowledge important is its connections with other concepts and terms (like between “know(s)” and “It’s possible that P[ind],” or with “It’s certain that P[ind]”, or with the connection between “know(s)” and appropriate assertion), and that the best way to get at those connections is via ordinary language uses of “know(s)”. But, at any rate, by trying to please those who might write off results as merely getting at “non-philosophical” uses of “know(s)” by the use of such correctives as inserted “really”s, x-phi-ers will fail to please me. By my lights & given my purposes, it would be much more enlightening to know how people respond to knows/doesn’t know. “Really knows” muddies the waters in roughly the way (though not nearly so badly as) that “Really, truly, honest-to-God K*N*O*W*S, or merely believes” would. (& I’ll bet there are some epistemologists out there who would like that proposal, because they’ll think that’s the way to get at the kind of knowledge that’s philosophically important.)
