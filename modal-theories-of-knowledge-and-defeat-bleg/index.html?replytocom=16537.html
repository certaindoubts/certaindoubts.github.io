Modal Theories of Knowledge and Defeat (BLEG)

Posted on June 18, 2011 by Trent DoughertyJune 20, 2011
In talking with John Hawthorne about the puzzle in the last post, he suggested a role for safety which I’m still thinking about, but it made me wonder about two things (which, again, I’m sure have been discussed somewhere). 

1. Safety must come in degrees, and this must raise lots of questions (confession: I’ve been focused on two chapters of KAIL in almost total exclusion of all others for several years now and I don’t recall Greco or Pritchard or Sosa talking about this (though I may well have missed it), and they are the main modalists I read: I’ve got my own internalist puzzles to puzzle over!)  So I suppose this is simply a bleg for a pointer to the literature.  Tim says knowledge needs a margin of error, but what’s the margin of error on this margin of error? (Traveling and don’t have my library with me.)

2. There must be a defeat problem akin to that for standard reliabilism.  I.e. a belief is safe (or what have you) but all your evidence strongly suggests that it isn’t [true].  A. It seems like you don’t know, and B. It seems like knowledge looses a lot of normativity.  I.e. The epistemic ought seems to go with the evidence here, not the knowledge.  Do people just say it’s impossible to be safe and yet have your evidence all point to it not being safe?  [UPDATE:]I now seem to recall asking Duncan this question at the MEW a few years back when he presented a doubly modal theory.  It seems like he appealed to methods of belief in the answer, but that’s all I can recall.]

I’m so convinced there is no such condition of knowledge that I haven’t thought through the details much, but these seem like interesting questions, and I wonder what people have said about them.

    Williamson talks about question 2 in “Very Improbable Knowing” (available on his website). In general, his strategy involves appealing to distinctions between different epistemic levels. Cases in which a belief is safe but your evidence suggests that it isn’t will involve failures of KK—you’ll know that P, but you won’t know that you know. In extreme cases, you might know that P, but it might at the same time be arbitrarily improbable (given your evidence) that you know that P—hence the title of the Williamson paper. I’m not happy with this kind of level-distinction, but if we grant that it’s possible, I’m not sure I see any further problem with using it to give an account of what’s going on in cases of defeat. Another good paper to read to see how somebody sympathetic to the safety picture can handle defeat is Maria Lasonen Aarnio’s “Unreasonable Knowledge” in the most recent Philosophical Perspectives.

    As for question 1, I’m not quite sure I see the problem. Suppose we think of the degree of safety of a belief as determined by the distance of the nearest world in which the belief is false. So the further we need to travel in modal space in order get to a world where you are wrong about whether P, the safer your belief about whether P. This is rough, and if we wanted to make it more precise, we might have to say something about belief-forming methods (to deal with cases where you could’ve easily failed to know something because you could’ve easily had different evidence).

    Maximal safety would amount to infallibility across all possible worlds concerning whether P–we never have that. Maybe the worry is that the claim that knowledge requires some particular sub-maximal degree of safety would be arbitrary? (That seems related to the issues you raise in your previous post.) The issues regarding epistemic levels are relevant here as well–in the Williamsonian picture, the higher the degree of safety of your belief that P, the more iterations of knowledge that P you can have–if you’re REALLY safe, you can know that you know that you know that P, rather than just knowing that P. So the safety picture does have a way to say what’s good about getting safer and safer, even once you’re already safe enough to know that P.

  7. Martin Smith on June 19, 2011 at 12:43 pm said:

    On most characterisations, safety will satisfy an agglomeration principle like the following: If S safely believes that P based on E and S safely believes that Q based on E, then S can safely believe that P & Q based on E. This will hold, for instance, if safety attributions have the following truth condition: S’s belief that P based on E is safe, at w, just in case P is true at all E-worlds that are very similar to w.

    This agglomeration principle should allow us to construct cases of safe beliefs that are arbitrarily unlikely given one’s evidence. A suitably modified preface paradox case – in which the author safely believes every proposition in the book – has the right kind of structure I think.

    On 1, in a paper from a couple of years ago, I suggested that safety was a comparative notion and that comparative attributions of safety could, roughly speaking, be understood along the following lines: A belief that P based on E1 is safer, at w, than a belief that P based on E2 iff there is a world w1 at which E2 is true and P is false and which is more similar to w than any world in which E1 is true and P is false. You don’t get *degrees* of safety out of this unless you’re willing to make some additional assumptions – like the assumption that the comparative similarity ordering of worlds is a well-ordering.

  8. julien dutant on June 19, 2011 at 5:58 pm said:

    Hi Trent,

    On whether safety comes in degrees: it’s far from straigthforward. Safety (in a case) means absence of error in most or all “close” (similar enough) cases. So there are two models for comparative or graded notions of safety:

    1. The greater the amount of error cases among close ones – or the greater the proportion of correct vs error cases among the close belief cases -, the less the belief is safe.
    2. The closer the the closest error case is, the less the belief is safe. (That’s the one Martin appeals to in his comment.)

    The first notion requires that you can measure “how many” error cases there are among the close cases. But it looks as if the number of correct and error cases is almost always infinite. (Take an arbitrary case, change the height of the subject continuously from -1mm to +1mm, for instance.) So you have to assume that there is some measure on the modal “space” such that you can measure the relative size of “areas” of cases. It is far from obvious that there is such a measure.

    Two further remarks on the first notion. (A) it implies that safe beliefs are maximally safe. You don’t get “safer than safe”. (B) it gives you a notion of probability *if you keep the proposition fixed* (i.e. you look at the truth or falsity of a fixed proposition p among the belief cases – or the belief on a given basis case: then the measures p and -p add up to 1). Sosa and (former) Pritchard’s safety keep the proposition fixed, but Williamson’s and (recent) Pritchard’s safety do not.

    The second notion requires – as far as I can see – a four-place similarity relation: X is at least as similar to Y as X’ is to Y’. If X and X’ are belief cases and Y and Y’ are their closest error cases, respectively, you say that X is at least as safe as X’ iff Y’ is at least as similar to X’ as Y is to X. It’s not obvious that we have a determinate relation of this kind throughout the modal space. If we don’t, some cases will be incomparable, and so you couldn’t tell that one is safer than the other or not.

    DeRose’s comparative conditionals are intended to make such comparisons. But they give clear results only when comparing very similar cases. Ex: case A I’m seeing a glass at arm’s length and believe it is full; case B is just like A but the glass is 1cm closer. Then the comparative conditional “if I know in A then I know in B” is clearly true. And correlatively it seems that the error case closest to A is at least as close to A than the first error case closest to B.

    But take case A = I ate a pizza two days ago and believe that I did, case B = I’m watching a live speech by a famous politician and believes that he is presently talking. Try and evaluate: “If I know in A I know in B” and “If I know in B I know in A”: at least I can’t. And I don’t feel like giving a lot of detail about the cases would help (except success rates perhaps?).

    As Martin points out the comparative similarity would give you comparative safety but not degrees of safety. But it’s not even clear that can rely on there being comparative safety.

    On the question of whether all beliefs can be compared for their safety…Suppose we generalise the characterisation of comparative safety that I gave above: A belief that P1 based on E1 is safer, at w, than a belief that P2 based on E2 iff there is a world w1 at which E2 is true and P2 is false and which is more similar to w than any world in which E1 is true and P1 is false.   19. julien dutant on June 20, 2011 at 6:58 pm said:

    Martin, on the question of whether all beliefs can be compared by safety:

    First, a minor point: your definition only gives comparisons for beliefs held at the same world. (One may think that we only care about one world after all, the actual one; but that’s false, for instance you may wonder if you’d improve the safety of your belief by doing such and such thing that in the end you won’t do. So you’d then have to compare your beliefs at the actual world with ones at counterfactual worlds.)

    Second, and I think more important: it’s not clear to me whether the similarity relation we use in safety should literally be between *worlds* or between *cases* (centered worlds in Williamson’s Knowledge and its limits; I actually prefer pairs of a centered world and a proposition). People often write things like “in a close world the agent”, or “in a close world at that time”, which indicates they have a designated agent and time in mind, e.g. centered worlds. And Williamson explicitly defines safety as a matter of similarity between cases. Now you might try to reduce similarity between cases to similarity between worlds: say two cases are similar if they involve a similar basis at a close world. But it’s not obvious that the reduction would work. For instance, you may think that some error *cases* happening in the actual world, but in the distant past, in the distant future or on the other side of the galaxy don’t matter to whether you know. Yet since the actual *world* is close to itself, if you formulate safety as a matter of similarity between worlds then you have to rule them out in some other way (e.g. they don’t involve the same basis, or the same agent). Another issue is that the kind of similarity and closeness between worlds we use for counterfactuals (same history up to a point, little miracle at some point and rolling on according to laws again) might not be of the kind we rely on when judging of the similarity of possible error cases. Maybe some error cases are very similar to one’s actual case but wouldn’t happen at close worlds measured in the counterfactual way. So it’s not obvious to me that we can get comparative safety by free-riding on Lewis’ assumption of comparative similarity between worlds.
